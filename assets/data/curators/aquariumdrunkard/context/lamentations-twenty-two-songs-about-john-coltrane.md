## Body

No American artist has inspired as much music as John Coltrane. His colossal stature in jazz, his mentorship of an entire generation of players, the overtly spiritual import of his later works, and his early death at age forty from liver cancer all together practically conferred sainthood on the saxophonist almost immediately after his passing in 1967. Of course, composers were penning tributes to Coltrane while he was still with us on this plane—tracks like Grant Green’s wonderful “Talkin’ about J.C.” The irresistible homonym of his nickname in tracks like Duke Ellington’s “Take the Coltrane” or Woody Shaw’s “The Moontrane,” served as an easy shorthand for the commendation. But since his death, John Coltrane has haunted modern music. 

The better part of whole careers in late twentieth-century jazz were subsequently spent working through the uncompromising immensity of his musical legacy. And more than a few of his disciples eventually had to call upon him by name, like the invocation of a saint, as much as in prayer as in memoriam. Indeed, one could claim the whole of what would come to be called ‘spiritual jazz’ was but a set of footnotes to Coltrane. But what is remarkable is how far beyond jazz the cult has spread. Prog weirdos like Magma were as forthright in their debts as soul poets like Gil Scott-Heron.  Steve Hillage and Miquette Giraudy’s post-Gong electronic chill unit System 7 found themselves speaking his name. Even the apex 1980s Paisley Underground outfit The Dream Syndicate knew that a bottle of wine in the freezer and “some John Coltrane on the stereo” was a solid plan for getting some. (Or “just play me John Coltrane,” as Lucinda Williams once snarled.) And hip-hop namechecks of the saxophonist would probably add another hour to this mix. But we focused here on the expansiveness John Coltrane bequeathed to music. Some of the cuts assembled here climb way into double-digit run times as if honoring the saint in anything less would be more than a little indecorous. Together they make an almost-cubist portrait of Coltrane’s legacy, with various planes of his vision and influence superimposed upon one another at odd angles. It is no substitute for listening to the man himself. But it begins at least to outline the magnitude of his absence. | b sirota
