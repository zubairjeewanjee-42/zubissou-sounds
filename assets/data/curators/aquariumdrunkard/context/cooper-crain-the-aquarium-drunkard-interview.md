## Body

Cooper Crain is a bandleader, band member, producer, engineer, mixer, songwriter, improviser, and a player of organs, synths, guitars, and much more. First coming up as a member of the psychedelic, grooved-based Cave and then gaining more prominence with the hypnotic, meditative, and powerful Bitchin Bajas, Crain concurrently emerged as a go-to producer/engineer/mixer if a band or an artist seeks a specific sound for their music. Recent collaborations include Ty Segall, Bill Callahan, Bonnie “Prince” Billy, Circuit des Yeux, the recently announced return of Stereolab, and many more. Crain’s ability to successfully capture the ideal sound for his clients is like the musical equivalent of a shooter’s touch, to use a basketball analogy. 

Crain’s work in Bitchin Bajas is centered in collaboration as well, which can be seen in the band’s recent reunion with Natural Information Society. After the joint release of Autoimaginary in 2015, the two groups repeatedly talked about working together again before a rewarding live gig prompted them to finally spend a day in the studio. Crain and Natural Information Society’s Joshua Abrams took their time over the course of several years to edit and mix the material before finally emerging with Totality. A beautiful work of exploration and discovery, the album is a mosaic of musical ideas, which blend electro-acoustic contributions from the seven musicians who comprise Bitchin Bajas and Natural Information Society. The four tracks on Totality move through drone-based soundscapes and grooves that transform and dissipate like puzzles without a solution, culminating in the charged, check-to-see-if-the-brakes-still-work finale of “Clock no Clock.” 

The release of Totality provided motivation for a conversation with Crain that touched on the latest collaboration with Natural Information Society, the art of mixing and editing music, and a selection of the musical projects that he’s worked on over the past several years. | s bunn

Cooper Crain: Unfinished business. Ever since that last one came out in 2015, we would do one show, and then we would always say, “Oh, we should do that again.” And then, after the downtime of 2020 and 2021, we had a residency at Mike Reed’s venues, the Hungry Brain and Constellation, and so we were trying to think of some things to do. And I was like, “Well, let’s do another show with Josh [Abrams] and Natural Information Society, and then let’s try to book a session.” After years of getting back to it, on and off, editing it with Josh, and getting it mixed, it eventually got done. Everybody had a good time doing the first run, so we wanted to see what could happen again. 

AD: Was there anything different about this time from the previous project 10 years ago? 

Cooper Crain: Greg Norman recorded it because it’s hard for me to perform and record. I remember, on the first one, I was going back and forth — it was at a much smaller studio, so it was easier. But at [Chicago’s Electrical Audio], it would have been a pain to go back and forth, and I wouldn’t have enjoyed it as much. Greg’s a friend, and he’s a longtime collaborator with Josh and the Natural Information Society albums as well, so he was an easy choice. But some of the personnel had changed in Natural Information Society. A few of the members from when we first did the collab are not involved anymore and Jason Stein joined. So, it was a slightly smaller group, but just as versatile. Everybody’s great at listening. It never sounds like it’s too much. That’s what I loved about the first [record]. On the first track “On No Fade” is like nine people, but at no point does it sound like there’s too many cooks in the kitchen, or there’s someone who should be edited out, or anything like that. Everybody gave space. And I think the same thing happened [with Totality].

AD: And you could focus on your own playing too. 

Cooper Crain: Yeah, which, if the ideas are very loose and a lot of improvising is needed, it’s easy to do. But to jump in that headspace and then have to worry about everybody else’s sound — I’ve done that enough to know that if I don’t have to do that [laughs] then it’s better for all. 

AD: “Always Nine Seconds Away” is the only song on Totality that was composed, and the rest are improvisations, is that right?

Cooper Crain: Yeah, there were some loose ideas [going in]. For instance, the track “Clock no Clock” was something that Dan [Quinlivan] and Rob [Frye of Bitchin Bajas] and I brought into the mix. It wasn’t really going as we thought, so we took off the thing we brought in because we were in a jam already, and then we just stopped that idea we had and then we did a take on its own, and that was what made it. “Always Nine Seconds Away” was obviously a composed piece and features Josh and Jason and Rob, and then me and Dan and Lisa [Alvarado] and Mikel [Patrick Avery] are all kind of hovering around. The ending was very open-ended and loose. 

AD: With so many of these musical voices in both bands, how do you determine which one takes precedence in any moment or any song? 

Cooper Crain: It wasn’t a lot of carving out of instruments. It was more, “This jam is 18 minutes, and it should be half. Let’s find the dull moments.” These were whole sections or chunks. It wasn’t so much, “Let’s take these instruments out and loop these,” or anything like that. It was more like, “This sounds good. How do we get from this point to that point? Let’s make this edit. Let’s hang out.” And then we would need time to sit with it and live with it for a while every time we did that. 

There was one percussion of Mikel Patrick Avery and he and Josh have a good rhythm thing going. And then Dan and Lisa and me were on the keys. The synths, the organs, and the harmonium — they have a texture. Because when you have synths, organ, and harmonium, that’s a palette that’s going to blend. But we also wanted the clarity, so we had to decide a few moments between those choices. Rob was playing a flute through a synth or sometimes just the flute, and Jason was playing the bass clarinet, which we processed a bit afterwards. But otherwise, everybody had their own space. We took the chance and booked the day and got Greg, and then it worked out, but it doesn’t always work out to pull a whole album out of a day [of recording]. 

AD: I have a theory that you can tell me is dumb: Mixing a record is similar to film editing, which famed editor Walter Murch said: “The editor is the only one who has time to deal with the whole jigsaw. The director simply doesn’t. To actually look at all the film the director has shot, and review it and sort through it, to rebalance all of that and make very specific notes about tiny details that are sometimes extremely significant, this falls to the editor.” I was wondering about the musical equivalent of Murch’s quote. Do you think it’s comparable to mixing? 

Cooper Crain: Yeah, it can be. There are ways you could probably edit a lot of shots in a film, and it could be this version or that version. But what evokes the emotion or the spirit of the scene kind of works in a musical way too. It’s hard sometimes to decide what’s the best route to go. Sometimes you just have to stop because it can keep going. 

I like all types of edits. I like hard edits. I like things like Can or something where it’s like, “Whoa, that’s just a tape cut and it’s now a totally different thing, but it’s the same song.” I also love when it’s smooth. If you’re going the route of being open-ended and you’re recording and jamming and you have a structure, but it’s not exactly tight, and you’re not sure — that’s going to require editing. That takes some work to get to a point where you feel good from the start to finish. 

It depends on the style of music. Is it a song? Is it an arrangement? It can go in every way. And I’ve done it in every way. But me, personally, when I make music, I leave a lot to chance in the moment of improvising. Sometimes for Bitchin Bajas during the mixing is when things start to appear to us to make more sense. And that might involve [saying], “Let’s take this and loop it. Let’s make a whole new thing out of this. Let’s overdub over this. Wow, this is actually the song right here.” And that requires a lot of different various stages of editing and remixing. I felt like that was a long-winded answer, but I don’t know if I answered your question [laughs].

AD: I think you did! There’s a certain quality to the records in which you serve as an engineer or produce that’s hard to describe. There’s a warmth. Do you sense that too and what’s your approach in achieving that feeling?  

Cooper Crain: I feel like over the years this warmness has varied, and that’s probably because when I first started, I was using whatever gear. It had a lot of noise or something. I’m just cutting high end. But also, I like there to be a mysterious element, especially in Bitchin Bajas. It’s not a haziness. I like to have clarity. But there’s definitely something about the top end that doesn’t exist so much to us. Now we’ve learned what we like about that, and we started to incorporate that on Bajascillators and this new one that’ll be out in the fall. At the same time, we like the mellowness of stuff. 

As far as when I’m working with other people, there’s certain ranges of frequencies that my ears aren’t into that much. It’s not that I’m using a lot of EQ. I might just tone down an amp or use a darker microphone or apply anything I need to do to fix it while it’s recording. But I do like top end. I like clarity. I like hi-fi stuff. I get to do that like on this new Stereolab album. I got to be as hi-fi as I wanted. At the same time, they like warm sounds as well, so it was a good mix of hi-fi and very warm and analog and organic. There’s so much these days that has a synthesized digital harshness. I like the way a lot of music is going. Abstractness seems to be very normalized now, in a way [laughs]. A lot of it doesn’t have much soul or warmth in it. I’m not going against that. I just like that I’m not a part of it.

AD: How do you compare producing and/or mixing someone else, say Ty Segall who you’ve worked with a lot, with Bitchin Bajas’ own music? Is it easier with other bands than your own music or vice versa?

Cooper Crain: It’s a different thing. My main goal is to get whatever the artists have in their head out. Some people are better at helping me understand that than others. I think that’s the case with any type of collaboration. Open communication or an understanding of the other person is helpful. It’s fun for me to work with all types of artists. I can reference a lot of things. Honestly, I think if somebody asked to make a record, and they wanted it to sound like Bitchin Bajas, I wouldn’t know how to do that. That’s something I do with Rob and Dan because of the way we interact and communicate and the understanding we have with one another in a musical sense. But when I’m working with another artist or band, I try as hard as I can to get into their head, into their world, and get what’s in there out. As long as everybody’s happy and satisfied, that’s the number one goal. I’m not always making a mix to appease a certain type of audience, or radio or Spotify, or your phone, or your speakers. I’m just trying to work with whoever and all of us to be happy in the end.

AD: With someone such as Josephine Foster with Godmother, did you go in with preconceived notions or did you have agreed upon experiments? Because that record seemed like a sonic departure for her. Previously, her music was naturalistic or organic and Godmother was more synthetic or processed.

Cooper Crain: That’s why she reached out to me. She’d worked with Andrija Tokic for a long time. When she wanted to make [Godmother], she had it in mind to make it on this keyboard and she had all the songs. I drove out there with some gear and we just did it in her house and then she came [to Chicago] to mix it. I wasn’t expecting that [shift] either because I liked her music. I loved her voice. I’m glad it wasn’t a departure from her great voice. But it was definitely musically and instrumentation, a departure. She had these improvised moments, and she didn’t want to overthink anything too much. She had it very much in her head how she wanted it to be laid down. I think in the end, she was happy. I thought it was a really cool sound. It’s this weird synth, baroque-y pop moment. I think it’s a great album. 

AD: You’ve engineered/mixed/produced a number of projects with Ty Segall. How has your working approach changed with him, if at all?

Cooper Crain: The only things we’ve worked on are usually albums where he is the main instrumentalist. He’s bouncing around all the instruments. Every now and then, Emmett [Kelly] will play bass, or Charles [Moothart], who used to play drums in [Segall’s] band, would hop in on a few songs, playing drums while Ty played bass. But, for the most part, it was Ty on everything. So that approach can only happen in one way, where you just stack. We’d get the drums and bass down and instruments and then voices. We would build on that track until it was done. With Harmonizer and Three Bells, those albums are different as far as the extent of what he can do on an instrument. He really pushed himself on Three Bells. Everything — guitar, drums, vocals. — I felt like it was like a step up. He had really dug in there on his own personal ability. I think that was a change in approach right there, what our reach was. Harmonizer had a lot of synths on it and synthetic sounds and Three Bells didn’t. So that was a shift, but it was a texture tonality shift, getting more room mic sounds, getting some more space in a different way. Whereas Harmonizer was a pretty direct sound with a lot of the instruments and synths and stuff. That was the point to be more upfront and in your face. I think the music itself led to a different type of approach. I hope to work with him again and figure out a new way.

AD: I need to ask about BCMC — Foreign Smokes is one of my favorite records from the last few years. You’ve produced/mixed/engineered Bill MacKay for a number of his solo projects and then you ended up starting a band. What was the synthesis of BCMC? Was it an outgrowth of you recording him? 

Cooper Crain: I mean, we were neighbors, so that led to us just playing in my living room. We had worked on a couple of his records at that point. Honestly, he’s such an amazing guitar player and his ear is so quick. He can latch onto anything almost instantly. I felt like I wanted to hear him on a different road or in a different color. The idea was I would have these beds, or I would have these scales, or I would pitch something, and, without even saying much, he could latch on and then we were doing takes in my living room. Then it was like, “Okay, this is an album.” We’re working on a new [record] now, and it’s ever-evolving. I love the murkiness and the soundtrack-y-ness of it all, but, at the same time, it’s another way to hear Bill. It’s different from his records, and it’s different from other collaborations he’s done. I think he really shines in a few moments. 

AD: And it’s different from Bitchin Bajas.

Cooper Crain: I don’t think either one of us wanted to make a Bill MacKay solo record with each other or a Bitchin Bajas record without the other guys. So it was, of course, a different thing, but it wasn’t anything that we had to say out loud. We would just meet up and turn the amps on and roll the tape and it would just happen. 

AD: Do you have a wishlist of people you want to collaborate with or is there someone you’re dying to produce? 

Cooper Crain: There’s a lot of great artists out there, but then, if I already like their albums, I don’t know what else I would do. I’ve gotten to work with some great folks. The upcoming Stereolab album that’s coming out, that was a dream to get that call to help them. That wasn’t really on my list because I didn’t know that was a real thing for them to make a new album. I’m nervous to say these things out loud because, honestly, when it works, it works. I’m very open to things. I do tend to work with friends a lot and people I know because I think that flow, that communication is already there, and I think that’s key for how I work. I’m not the kind of engineer who can go in without any info, and just do a good job. I’m not so technically-inclined in that way. While I’m confident and I know what I’m doing, I really love to dig in and understand what that artist wants, or if they’re open to suggestions, or if they want to go this way or that way without having to dig in and find that out a week into the session. As far as dream clients, I don’t know if I want to say any out loud, but I admire a lot of artists these days. I would love to work with more eccentrics or more interesting folks that I love their work already. I just let things happen in an organic way.
