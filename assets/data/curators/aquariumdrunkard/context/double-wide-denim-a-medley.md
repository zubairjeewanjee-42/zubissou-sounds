## Body

Music’s where you find it. I spent the last two summers of high school working at a warehouse off I-285 in Atlanta. Within two weeks I was the forklift operator which, at 16, was much cooler than my back-of-house restaurant gig the previous summer. I was the only kid on the floor, the other guys were roughneck dudes in their early thirties. The manager, a middle-aged guy named Skip, mainly sat in his air-conditioned office reading the New York Times while listening to the score to Kubrick’s 2001: A Space Odyssey on compact disc. He rarely bothered us. The floor boss, a proud Busch swilling redneck named Dodge, ran the actual show. Dodge looked straight out of central casting for a bar fight, commuted in from Doraville, chewed Levi Garrett straight from the pouch, and would regale my young mind with tales of tree stands, cleaning deer and chasing women.

There were three other guys on the floor: an affable born-again acid casualty who had dropped out of the Lovett school at age 17 to follow the Grateful Dead for 10 years, a KISS obsessed ex-Marine, and a strange mustachioed misanthrope. But, like I said, it was Dodge’s show and Dodge’s rules, and one of those rules had to do with the floor’s radio protocol and it was unbendable: every other day would alternate between the local FM classic rock and country stations. As Walkmans were not permitted, I would have to listen to Pavement and A Tribe Called Quest on my own time.

I worked in that hot and humid 20,000 square foot space for about six months between 1993 and 1994. This was my baptism in modern country and southern rock as dictated by the commercial radio of the day, and while I was not ready to trade in my green Adidas Gazelles for a pair of Ropers, I began to get a feel for it. Growing up in the south, country music was as common as the ambient cacophony of junebugs and cicadas, but this was the first time I had paid much, if any, attention to it.

A few summers ago I began compiling a playlist of what those long, muggy days sounded like. The result, a weird amalgam of Mel McDaniel, David Allan Coe, Molly Hatchet, Dickey Betts, Don Williams, ZZ Top and beyond, is Double Wide Denim — a tracklisting that evokes Coors Lite, charcoal grills, ice chests, lakes and pontoon boats.
