## Body

In Richard Linklater’s 2001 film Waking Life, a character driving a boat car states the following maxim: “The idea is to remain in a state of constant departure while always arriving.” The Necks have created conditions for themselves as a band that embraces a constant pattern of departure and arrival. Every 40-some minute live set they play is entirely improvised as they never perform songs drawn from their extensive catalog. Similarly, their studio recordings never sound like anything they’ve done before yet can always be identified as only having been created by The Necks. 

Their new album Bleed once again demonstrates The Necks veering away from expectations. While their previous album Travel contained four tracks suffused with a creeping uncertainty, Bleed sees The Necks returning to a single-track album with a lean, sparse sound. It’s as if there’s a numbness on Bleed, the kind that’s needed for survival after accepting that darkness has already arrived. Yet Bleed is a stirring piece of work because of the evident commitment to inventiveness by the band.

In the leadup to the release of Bleed, Aquarium Drunkard interviewed Lloyd Swanton (bass), Tony Buck (drums, percussion), and Chris Abrahams (piano, keyboards) of The Necks by email, comparing and contrasting how they work in the studio as opposed to performing live, the band’s decision-making process while recording, the mountains of live recordings in their archives, and more. | s bunn

Aquarium Drunkard: There are inherent restrictions when The Necks play and improvise in a live setting: the audience, the venue, different piano and bass for each gig, the 45-minute sets. Do you set conditions or parameters for yourselves when recording in the studio, or is it an open-ended process when you begin?

Lloyd Swanton: No, we just go into the studio, get set up, then ask each other “OK, who’s got an idea?” We discuss that, and start pursuing the directions that it suggests, laying some tracks and some individual events down, and over the next few days of recording (and on into the listening and mixing processes – which may be many months later) we’re discussing where it’s been going over time and where we might take it.

Tony Buck: I’d actually say there are quite specific restrictions when we play and record. We are improvising, but there are things we definitely do and don’t do within the framework of what we have set out to explore in The Necks’ music. These to me are the inherent restrictions. For myself, there are things I do in other improvising contexts I would never do in The Necks. In The Necks, from the beginning, everything I play relates to cyclic material and how it can change and develop over time.

Chris Abrahams: In the studio we afford ourselves the option to overdub and, especially in the mixing/editing stage, shape and refine parts, ending up with a considered and “composed” result. The choice of material and how a record takes shape is, as Lloyd says, a fairly open process within the realm of The Necks’ aesthetic, inspired by something as simple as, “Why don’t we try a ….. kind of thing.” How that developed over the days and weeks is anyone’s guess. If there are conditions or parameters, they tend to be very open. Sometimes, prior to going into the studio, we’ve set out to record something “sparse” or “up”—it’s never anything more descriptive than that. At other times we’ve decided to record long pieces or shorter twenty-minute pieces—something dictated by the format we want the release to be on. There have been premeditated ideas to incorporate other instruments (we recorded a horn section on Next and guitarist Michael Sheriden on Hanging Gardens) but these are rare events. On the most recent recordings (hopefully our next release after Bleed), we made a multi-rhythmic piece based on a premeditated idea from Tony. But mostly we enter the studio with no pre-planned, verbalized ideas and, if there is a concept, we allow each member to interpret (including to ignore) the concept as they feel fit, as the piece develops over the days we’re making it. The passage of time is a compositional element: it might be a year between recording sessions, and we allow for that time span—and the wealth of new ideas that, hopefully, come with it—to provide fresh material. It’s fortunate that, after 25 albums, we’re still able to work communally and with respect to each other.

AD: What’s the communication between the three of you after a set or a performance? Do you critique yourselves? Do you go back and listen to the tape? How does that differ with studio recordings?

Lloyd Swanton: We only ever talk in the most general terms. We really don’t want to start getting prescriptive about our live performances. It’s generally a little more specific than “Wow, I thought that was a really good one!” – “Did you? I didn’t feel I could get anything much happening” – “Really? I thought you played some amazing stuff!” etc. Yes, we go back and listen individually to some of our live recordings, but not in a critical how-can-we-optimize-our-key-performance-criteria way, more just to keep in touch with the sort of areas the band’s getting into, and also to consider the track in question for a live release.

Tony Buck: As I mentioned above, the studio does offer the chance to reflect and refine what we’ve been playing and consider, with a certain distance, how different parts work together. Of course, live in concert, we are listening to each other and responding but it all unfolds in real time, no second chances to fix up things that might not go as we might have hoped.

Chris Abrahams: Experience has taught me that subjectivity is immensely changeable: what I thought was unfortunate, might, on a second listen, sound very good and then revert to unfortunate on the third listen. I tend not to dwell too much on what I may have played in a set. However, if something extraordinary happens, we will talk about it after the set. We try to record every concert, something that’s become a lot easier over the years—recordings in the 90s were mainly stereo; these progressed to 8-track then 16-track. Nowadays you can take a USB feed straight from the desk and, within 15 minutes, set up a 24-track recording. We have amassed quite a backlog of live recordings. At the same time, we’ve developed a much stronger filter for what gets released. It’s been over a decade since we released a live album.

Having all these recordings may have forced us to examine what a live release should or shouldn’t contain. Our live performances use the acoustic qualities of the material context—the PA, the instruments, the architecture—to create an audio experience that can give the impression of transcending the confines of an acoustic trio. This is something very hard, if not impossible, to replicate in the stereo field of an album release. As the band has progressed, so too has our awareness of the gap between the live sound and the stereo replica. A released recording is something that heavily implies it should be listened to more than once. The re-hearing of a slow-building crescendo, for instance, is an experience very different to the original hearing, once the timespan and dynamics of the crescendo have already been experienced. I feel I’ve tended to pathologize these differences, to think of them as detracting from the memory of a concert. Lately, I’m more open to seeing a live album as something to be interpreted on its own terms, not totally as an attempt at representing a great show. 

Our studio recordings are a different matter. We tend not to make wildly dynamic studio recordings with big, sustained crescendos—maybe we will in the future. We also don’t try to recreate the “transcendental,” aural-hallucinatory dimension we often go for live. We also expand our instrumental options with guitars, organ, synths, and samples. We multi-track and edit. Some studio pieces never have us all playing live together at one time. We move material around in time. We generally spend a week to 10 days recording something, then we mix it for the same amount of time. Sometimes the mix happens a year or so after recording. It’s important for the studio albums to be made with us all present all the time. It’s rare for pre-recorded things to be brought in by individuals; on the whole, we record everything with us all present in a professional, purpose-built studio. The same goes for mixing. Our albums are, like our concerts, a group effort.

AD: When going in to record a studio album, do you always start with the three of you improvising as if you are playing a live performance?

Lloyd Swanton: Sometimes. We start recording with whatever approach we feel might be a fruitful way to begin.

Chris Abrahams: It depends on the record. Travel was recorded this way, but it’s a sort of hybrid studio / live album. Our earlier studio records chiefly involved overdubbing over a base of improvising. When we started, there was really only magnetic tape to work with—Sex was recorded on a 15-inch reel of 2-inch tape at half speed, allowing for an hour of non-stop recording time. I don’t recall us ever getting the block and razor out to temporally edit anything. We progressed to hooking up DA88 machines to the Studer to bolster the track count, but we still made records by overdubbing parts on to a real-time improvised performance. But the improvising was different to that in a concert performance: instinctively, we left room for overdubbing, and this tended to make the “base” not as dynamically varied or dense as a concert performance. With the advent of Protools, our way of doing things was opened up to a new way of dealing with time and instrumentation. Our studio albums now can have large track counts and are often built using non-chronological chunks of material. 

AD: Is there ever a musical idea or expression that one or all of your play live and you say after the performance, “Let’s return to this in the studio”?

Lloyd Swanton: Only in a very general sense. Someone might say “I’d love to try doing something like that next time we’re in the studio.” But, as I say, that’s as prescriptive as we’d get, and we certainly wouldn’t be breaking it down and analyzing it and trying to replicate it. Or, maybe, once we’re in the studio, one of us might, in the process of putting down some ideas as overdubs, the others will get on the intercom and say, “How about trying that thing you sometimes do on gigs where you go etc. etc. etc.?”

Chris Abrahams: Yes. Although whether we do or not is debatable. The way the band has always operated is that we spend a great deal of time apart and then come together for a very focussed few weeks. All our studio albums have been recorded in Australia and have all happened in combination with touring. Sometimes the studio time will be during a tour; sometimes at the end. We may not have seen each other for six months before a tour starts and this tends to imbue each tour with its own identity. This “identity” survives into the recording process.  

AD: What’s the editing process like in the studio? Do you all have free rein? Is there an approval procedure? Do you build consensus between all three of you? 

Lloyd Swanton: Communal. Consultative. Gradual. Ongoing. Everything has to have a 100% consensus, not just a two out of three majority.

Chris Abrahams: On the whole, we tend to stick to our own contributions when it comes to omission of material, but not all the time. An intuitive understanding of the equality in the group makes for an easy decision-making process. There have been disagreements, but they’ve always been resolved amicably. There’s no leader in the group; there’s no composer with a premeditated idea of how the music should sound. We believe that what’s created, in the end, will be greater than the sum of its parts. 

AD: Do you go out of your way to ensure that the music will be different in tone or approach from record to record? 

Lloyd Swanton: We tend to just try to make sure each record sounds different to the one immediately previous. So far, we haven’t fallen into a checkerboard pattern, I’m happy to say.

Chris Abrahams: I believe that the music will be different because time has passed, and we’ve all been pursuing different musical projects apart from the band. These experiences are, hopefully, brought back into The Necks’ way of making music. Making it a priority to be different each time seems like a bit of a distraction.

AD: I feel as though I heard more ambient noise on Bleed than on previous studio albums. I thought I heard one of you breathing, the squeak of an instrument as well as someone shifting in their seat. Am I imagining that? If not, was that intentional or simply happy accidents? 

Lloyd Swanton: Happy accidents, intentionally left in. I think we’re all getting to a point where we actually enjoy real life in the recordings we listen to, so unsurprisingly our own releases might be reflecting that.

Tony Buck: I think sometimes a studio recording can end up with a kind of sterile, surgical purity that can negate the humanity out of a performance. For me, a squeaking chair, or an audible breath can give a performance context and help a piece of music come alive. 

Chris Abrahams: It’s not difficult to remove those sounds from a production nowadays. We’ve made albums that have been very thoroughly de-noised, and I guess we don’t see this as that much of a priority now. An album is a collection of documents of time, of movement, and living—among other things. We have no issues with including the “noise” of humans going about making music. 

AD: Do you have unreleased studio work, or do you use everything? If yes, are they recorded improvisations or more sculpted, edited pieces that didn’t fit the tone or approach of the record? 

Lloyd Swanton: We have very little in finished form because we’d rarely get that far into a project and then decide against releasing it. We do have one very lovely track which is ready to go, which, in the end, simply didn’t fit onto a release we did a few years back. That will come out soon, at least as a digi. Obviously, we’ve got reams and reams of material on some of these session masters that didn’t make the final mix, but we’re not really into doing more than one mix of each of our releases.

AD: Knowing that you have hundreds of hours of live recordings, what’s the process for deciding when to release a live album? Do you all inherently know that it was a good performance? 

Lloyd Swanton: The process is very haphazard, and, as you may have noticed, we haven’t released any live tracks in a very long time, despite us currently being about the busiest we’ve ever been with live performing. There are actually so many archival tapes it’s getting very unwieldy, and we are taking steps to put some systems in place so we can go through them methodically and economically. 

AD: Speaking as a fan who wants to listen to as much Necks music as possible, have you ever thought of doing a subscription service website in which people can listen to your unreleased live recordings? Or do you want more quality control before releasing a live performance rather than having everything available? 

Lloyd Swanton: That’s definitely something we’ve considered. But we wouldn’t just be just releasing unmixed desk tapes. We really feel like a lot of our live recordings don’t do the performance justice without some serious po-pro engagement, which is maybe one reason why we don’t release a lot of our live material.
