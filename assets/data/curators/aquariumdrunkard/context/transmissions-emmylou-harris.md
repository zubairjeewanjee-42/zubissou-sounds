## Body

Welcome back to Transmissions, a weekly interview podcast created and curated by Los Angeles online music magazine Aquarium Drunkard. This week on the show, host Jason P. Woodbury speaks with a living legend, and one of our all-time favorite vocalists and songsmiths: Emmylou Harris. 

Transmissions :: Emmylou Harris

On November 7th, New West Records will re-release an expanded edition of her 1998 live album Spyboy, ⁠back in print after 27 years⁠. Recorded in the wake 1995’s Wrecking Ball, an LP that redefined Harris for a whole new generation, Spyboy finds Harris and her band—Buddy Miller, Brady Blade and Daryl Johnson—on the road and stretching out into feverish new territory for the storied singer.

Harris released her first album in 1970, and along the way, she’s collaborated with artists like country rock pioneer Gram Parsons, Dolly Parton, Linda Ronstadt, Bob Dylan, Neil Young, and countless more. But as she settled into the ‘90s, she felt that country radio no longer made space for voices like hers—prompting a shift into a new direction with producer Daniel Lanois, who crafted a spectral, haunted sound for Wrecking Ball, placing her voice at the dreamy center. The resulting era introduced Harris to new ears—and we were thrilled to speak with her about it for this episode.

Aquarium Drunkard: I’m really excited to speak with you specifically about this record, Spyboy, and Wrecking Ball, which are two of my favorites. Right from the beginning of your career, you’ve been someone who doesn’t stick too closely to formulas or seem all that concerned about what rules might come alongside any given genre, be it folk or country or whatever else. But even so, Spyboy is especially eclectic and adventurous. What has it been like revisiting this project for you?

Emmylou Harris: Oh, it’s been wonderful, taking me back to when I was younger. I’ve had wonderful bands from the very first one, The Hot Band. I love playing live with musicians and performing, and to have this time capsule of Spyboy has just been a wonderful surprise—at the time—and then to have the chance to have it revisited and give other people a chance to experience it? Yeah, that’s been wonderful.

AD: This version has been expanded to include stuff that wasn’t included on the original version. Your cover of Tom Petty and The Heartbreakers, “A Thing About You,” and there’s a wonderful Dylan song, you’ve got Bill Monroe, Lucinda Williams, all these great songwriters. So it’s nice that we’ve got this expanded version of Spyboy. As I understand it, Buddy Miller, your collaborator on this thing, he wanted to record all of this stuff. He recorded something like 25 shows, so I imagine there’s even more music out there from this era.

Emmylou Harris: Well, it’s probably the same material. You are out there, you sort of have a repertoire of songs that you’re drawing from, so I don’t think there’ll be a Spyboy 3.

AD: Spyboy grew out of your 1995 collaboration with Daniel Lanois, who’s one of my favorite producers, Wrecking Ball, and that’s one of my favorite records of all time.

Emmylou Harris: Thank you. I think mine too.

AD: It’s such a beautiful album. There’s so many things that it inspires in me. It’s a record that I can’t listen to without feeling very deeply. But at the time, it was sort of a little bit of a departure for you. 

Emmylou Harris: I would say it was a big departure.

AD: What was it about that moment that made you so willing to take a left turn the way Wrecking Ball is?

Emmylou Harris: Well, I had still been courting country radio. Not that I depended on hit singles, but I’d sort of realized that I needed to go somewhere else, that I wasn’t exactly being invited to the party. But in a way, it was good because it gave me a chance to free me and just do what I wanted, just record songs that I loved. The record company was being very supportive of me and they said, “What do you want to do next?” Literally, and I had only been listening to Dylan’s Oh Mercy album and Daniel’s first solo record, Acadie. They said, “Who would you like to work with?” And I said, “Well, Daniel.” Apparently a phone call was made. And the next thing I knew, Dan was in my living room and we were just talking about songs.

AD: Did you get on with him right away? He’s an interesting character. 

Emmylou Harris: Oh, yeah. Right away. We were definitely on the same page. Well, I won’t say we were on the same page creatively—I was just open and I had a few songs. I had “All My Tears” because I had just become acquainted with Buddy and Julie Miller and a few others. But Dan brought songs and so did Malcolm Burn was very important on making in the making of Wrecking Ball. Dan had brought him in from another Canadian, those darn Canadians. They’re so creative. It must be that nice clean air up there or something.

AD: Something. Yeah.

Emmylou Harris: And in fact, he’s the one who came up with the song “Wrecking Ball.”

AD: Malcolm was the one who brought that to the table? That’s a song written by the great Neil Young…

Emmylou Harris: Another Canadian.

AD: Yet another creative Canadian. You really are onto something with that. He’s somebody who you have collaborated with many times over the years. But again, there’s just something, I mean, gosh, it’s a haunted sounding recording, especially that song. I think it’s such an incredible one. Were you already familiar with that number?

Emmylou Harris: I actually wasn’t. And I’m a huge Neil Young fan. I probably have almost every one of his records, but he’s done such an amazing big body of work. That was one song that I was not familiar with. Of course, I’d been a fan from his first record. Loved that first record, and I had actually sung with and met Neil and sung on “Star of Bethlehem.” So it was great to have that in there. But once again, I think just the combination of dances, he has those wonderful, he brought those turbulent rhythms and those atmospheric sounds connected to songs that I was quite eager to sing. And what you sing is inspired by what you hear, and so Dan’s production had a great deal to do with the emotional effect for me on the record. And of course, we wanted to get live vocals while the molecules were still moving around in the room. I think that also contributed to the emotional impact of the album.

AD: When we first started this podcast, one of the very first conversations that we taped was with Daniel about his work, and we did it there at his studio in Los Angeles. But the era that you’re talking about…you also appear on Teatro, the Willie Nelson album that he produced around the same time. And I think that he was really onto something in terms of this notion of, let’s take this American songcraft, folk, country, and roots sounds. Let’s take that as our core, but then let’s not be afraid to get more abstract, to get more atmospheric, to get more sort of, let’s say, moodier in terms of the way that it’s presented. It was so ahead of its time. There’s sort of a whole other field of stuff that is maybe to the left of center when it comes to country music, and it’s called Americana. But you guys were sort of blazing that trail a little bit in terms of did you feel like there was, I guess, let’s say alternative quality to what you were doing too, that was appealing to you?

Emmylou Harris: I don’t know if we were thinking about that. Once again, I approach everything as from the standpoint of the singer. It’s always about this song for me. But then when I listened to an album, when I heard the Oh Mercy album, not that I’d ever stopped being a Dylan fan, but Dan’s record, brought me full full back into the Dylan fold. I loved the writing and I loved the sound. Dan says, “A sound can evoke an emotion, and when you put that together with the emotional lyrics in a song being sung, it’s incredibly powerful.” So I mean, it was just two worlds coming together exactly the right time on Wrecking Ball.

AD: Yeah. You mentioned you’ve got The Hot Band, you’ve got The Nash Ramblers. You’re a musician who collaboration is a huge part of your whole body of work. Going all the way back, you’ve always been somebody who seems to have that to be rooted in that tradition of singing with people and sort of creating things together. But it was clear, obviously, that when you finished Wrecking Ball, you realized, okay, this is probably a record that I might need an entirely different band for, which is sort of what prompted the whole Spyboy thing to come into existence. But so I’m curious, what was it when you were thinking about how you wanted to approach going out on the road for this record? What was it that you wanted this new band to be able to bring to the stage?

Emmylou Harris: Those sounds, the rhythms and the atmosphere sounds that were on the record, I realized were a really important part of the emotional impact of the songs. I will say that while we were recording, I was thinking about that, and even that far back, I had recently met a buddy and Julie Miller and was aware of his virtuosity, and I was thinking about him, but even more fortunately, Daniel said, “Well, I’m going to show you how to do this.” He pretty much put the rhythm section together, because we had Daryl who had been on and from New Orleans, he had done some of the work on the record. Of course, we couldn’t get Larry Mullen. 

AD: He was busy. 

Emmylou Harris: He was with another band, and Dan just remembered hearing a Brady Blade. I think it had been several years he hadn’t seen, and it’s just amazing how that rhythm section came together. And Dan then with his playing, and we did a short tour, that sort of set showed me the blueprint the way, and then I thought, well, I think that Buddy Miller, who is not Daniel Lanois, but Buddy is equally extraordinary as a guitar stylist, and was able to step into Dan’s shoes, but add his own Buddy Miller touch, so that we were able to replicate live what we had been doing on the record. 

AD: You’re taking it in new directions too. It seems to me like it bloomed in this way where it’s one thing to sort of recreate the tones of the record, and you guys do touch on that, and you do make that, you take that sort of, I think, Lanois stuff. Obviously, it’s maybe not always the easiest thing to replicate live because of how layered and nuanced and atmospheric it is. But you not only get that, you also, I mean, you guys really rock at times on this.

Emmylou Harris: I mean, we had to. When you go out on the road, you have to do more than play songs on the record. A tour also incorporates material from your previous albums. And so with Buddy and that New Orleans rhythm section, we were able to add a new flavor to the old songs so that we had a seamless sound going on. Everything, whether it was the stuff from Wrecking Ball or stuff from things far back from another decade, because it became all of a piece. And Buddy, of course, was very comfortable in the Daniel world and in the country world of my earlier material.

AD: Yeah, he’s kind of a perfect collaborator in that way.

Emmylou Harris: Extraordinary. And of course, he’s the one I didn’t even know he was recording. He just wanted to do this on his own, which is probably good because I was not self-conscious at all. I just went out there every night and rocked out the I normally do. So it was a wonderful surprise after we got to 2001 or whenever it was, and he had all these shows that he had recorded and put the record together. And then of course, in its resurrection that there were songs that had not been on Spyboy, especially the Tom Petty song, which we’d both forgotten that we had done because it wasn’t a regular part of our set. In fact, we can’t even remember if we did it more than that one time.

AD: I’m so glad that it was recorded. That’s one of my favorites. I’m a huge fan of Tom, and I feel like he’s one of these guys where he’s almost sneaky, in my opinion, because you don’t even realize how many incredible songs he’s written. There’s just so many of them. You almost take it for granted. But to hear that one, I mean, you guys tap into that heartbreaker zone with such a sort of attack, and it’s really, really exciting. But it’s also in the liner notes, buddy talks about how this was the days before iPhones and Pro Tools and stuff, so recording these shows was no small feat. He’s talking about racks of gear and all of this stuff, but you didn’t even really realize that he was up to all that. 

Emmylou Harris: I wasn’t paying attention.

AD: Well, I’m glad that he did it, because like you said, maybe if you had even been thinking about recording, it would’ve changed what you were doing, but I’m so glad…

Emmylou Harris: There might’ve been an element of self-consciousness to it.

AD: I’m so glad there isn’t. Maybe I’m just prone to hearing music this way or thinking about it along these lines, but I have always found that there is a holy or spiritual quality to this era with The Millers. That’s an are lyrically they’re focused in on—faith and existence, in this powerful way. Obviously, you talked about Brady Blade, his brother Brian is on this record, and they’re associated with the Zion Baptist Church and came up deep in that tradition of spiritual music. But with songs like “Deeper Well,” “Where Will I Be?” I think those all tend to sort of nod towards faith and expression of spiritual ideas. In addition to that creative connection, was there sort of a spiritual connection with this group?

Emmylou Harris: I’ve always, from the very beginning, been drawn to songs that are sort of secular spirituals, I think there can be a spiritual element in a secular song, because I just feel like we’re all connected. Our secular lives in our spiritual lives, our spiritual journey through this world, whatever it is. So I have always been drawn to those songs, especially the ones that are, well, the ones that are very particular, like the old gospel songs that I’ve done, older songs, but the ones that sort of intimate something without actually coming out, I’m particularly drawn to that. And so I don’t think it was a conscious effort. I think that these were just songs that just spoke to me, and that’s always the way that I certainly, even though I’ve had a little bit of charts access over the years, I’ve never had to live and die by the radio. I’ve been very lucky that way. It gives me a chance to do exactly what I want to do, and fortunately, the fans of mine hear something in what I’m doing that speaks to them.

AD: I feel like I got into your work through your collaborations with artists. Maybe it was Bright Eyes specifically for me—that record, I’m Wide Awake, It’s Morning. At that time I’m interested in indie rock and things like that. But you bring both tradition and that unexpected sort of adventurous spirit to your collaborations, and so I was introduced to your music through that lens, and then working my way back, and of course, hearing all the incredible stuff you did with Gram, it’s almost like you’re right there at the beginning of whatever, we would eventually come to call alt country or…

Emmylou Harris: “Americana.”

AD: Americana, whatever term wants to get used, you’re sort of there right at the very beginning of it. But it doesn’t sound like you had a master plan…

Emmylou Harris: I actually definitely don’t have a master plan. For me, it’s been one song after another. It’s always been song after song, except in a few instances, like when I did Roses in the Snow back in 1980, it was conscious effort to keep the parameters strictly into bluegrass, and there were reasons for it. There’s no point that would be a whole ‘nother podcast…And oddly enough, it became one of my more successful, as far as record sales, oddly enough, it was the one that the record company thought would bomb, and it was the one that, because there were all these bluegrass people out there that nobody knew about. It was always an underground thing that had been there for a long time.

AD: You wanted to shine a light on that stuff.

Emmylou Harris: Yeah, but it was also because I had Ricky Skaggs in my band, and Emory Gordy at the time was a huge, had been in my band. Albert Lee, Emory, and I used to insert Louvin Brothers acoustic things into our Hot Band sets. It had always been a part, if not on the records, it was always a part of what we sang all the time. And so that was what was happening to me, what was inspiring me, especially because of Ricky being in the band, and I just felt like somebody was going to do a bluegrass record, somebody from a more mainstream, and I wanted to be the first. So it was out of ego, I suppose, in a way, but from a creative standpoint too.

AD: One dash of ego and one dash of creative inspiration. I think that that’s a pretty good combination. Well, you talked about people at the label maybe being worried that that record was going to miss the mark or not connect.

Emmylou Harris: You mean Roses in the Snow? 

AD: Right. 

Emmylou Harris: Yeah, but I will say it was a very creative, interesting label, and I’ve actually been in the Warner family my entire career, because none such, well, New West is a different kind of an offshoot here, but for most of my career, I’ve been under the Warner’s umbrella, and once they said, “Well, she’s determined to do it, let’s get behind it.” 

AD: So Wrecking Ball came out on Elektra/Asylum.

Emmylou Harris: It came out on Elektra/Asylum. Yeah. In fact, the album before that, Cowgirl’s Prayer, was the first one in Elektra/Asylum, and I was still courting country radio at that point, but eventually I ended with Red Dirt Girl after Spyboy. I ended up on Nonesuch, which is once again, a very interesting part of the Warner’s umbrella that had always sort of focused on artists that are hard to categorize.

AD: When I would see that logo, and still to this day, when you see that on a record, you’re like, “This is going to be interesting.” Were there people who, at the labels or people who you worked with who were nervous that Wrecking Ball was too different from what people associated you with? Did you get any pushback from people?

Emmylou Harris: I’m trying to remember. I think that there was, maybe at this point, I wasn’t working with the Nashville folks who had been very supportive on Elektra. I think there might’ve been some pushback from some on the West Coast, I can’t remember exactly, but there were people who were very, very excited about it, and so it was a wrecking ball took a while to become what it became for people, and so in a way you like that you realize that it happened organically, for lack of a better word, it was a word of mouth. There were people who were interested because Daniel’s name was on it, and my fans were always zigging and zagging with me. I think I lost a few of them maybe, but I got newer fans coming through Dan’s portal of his involvement, and I do think it happened on a level of true excitement, word of mouth, one person after another, which it’s a wonderful thing. I’m not saying the record company didn’t have anything to do with it, but it seemed to me that it happened on its own.

AD: As you alluded, Wrecking Ball opened you up to a lot of new fans. How did the audiences at the shows change in that Spyboy-era, and were you aware, “Hey, there are some people here who might not have been at my gigs a year or two ago.” 

Emmylou Harris: Well, there might’ve been for Spyboy. I remember that we did a show at the Exit-In, which can be  found on a wonderful film that accompanied this album, a wonderful film, one of our first performances. You could tell. There were just a lot of young people crowded into that. It was just what was basically a rock & roll club.

AD: Did you feel that energy? 

Emmylou Harris: Oh yeah. I could definitely feel the energy and it was wonderful every night. I mean, this band Spyboy, I mean, I hope that people will be able to see the Exit-In show and actually see this band in action. Even now, I look at it and I say “Geez, I was there? I was right in the middle of that?” 

AD: Does it happen for you that when it’s really working musically, you almost feel like you disappear and the music is just coming through? I mean, I think I’ve experienced that a few times where I’ll start playing a song and then realize that the set’s over and it doesn’t feel like any time passed. You know what I mean? You kind of slipped into it. Does that happen to you and did that happen with this band?

Emmylou Harris: Oh, well, I will say it’s happened with all my bands, really good musicians. You have really good musicians and you have songs that you’ve recorded because you had to record them because you felt them. They spoke to you, and so really, hopefully you try to reach that zone every night, and sometimes you reach it on a higher level than others, but even on a bad night when for whatever reason you don’t have what you think is your best stuff, you’re carried by the songs and by the musicians that you have surrounding you.

AD: Emmylou, it’s an absolute honor to speak with you and I’m such a huge fan of your work on your own and in collaborative form. Obviously right after this era, you went and you did another Trio record with Linda and Dolly, which is fantastic, and so there’s, looking back at your body of work, there are so many incredible moments, but Wrecking Ball, Spyboy, it really does feel like that was a particular moment and probably a moment that helped a lot of people start hearing you in a new light, and so it’s been a real pleasure to explore that era with you, and thank you so much for taking the time with us.

Emmylou Harris: Well, thank you for giving it some love. I appreciate it.
