## Body

Lagniappe (la ·gniappe) noun ‘lan-ˌyap,’ — 1. An extra or unexpected gift or benefit. 2. Something given or obtained as a gratuity or bonus.

Phoenix songwriter Caleb Dailey’s voice is a warm, enveloping thing. On his solo debut, 2022 LP Warm Evenings, Pale Mornings: Beside You Then, he applied it to a survey of country and western music, covering songs by Gordon Lightfoot, Waylon Jennings, Blaze Foley, Chip Moman, and more. But the album was far from an exercise in retro country—in these timeworn songs, Dailey finds a site for experimentation, ambient textures, and smoked-out soundscapes. For his Lagniappe session, brings a similar sense of vision and mystery to selections by Skeeter Davis and NRBQ, Leroy Van Dyke, and Lucinda Williams. Dailey illuminates his connection to these songs in the words below. 

Caleb Dailey :: Things To You (Skeeter Davis & NRBQ)

“Things To You” was my intro into both Skeeter Davis & NRBQ (She Sings, They Play), together, as well as their individual catalogs. I’m now a super fan of them both. The writing is sweet and perfectly simple, and the performance is playful—almost flirtatious. I also love how shockingly good the texture of the recording is itself. The moment I heard it, I thought, “Ummm…this might just be my favorite song now?”

Caleb Dailey :: One Minute More of Lonely (Leroy Van Dyke)

“One Minute More Of Lonely” is such a great song. My good friend, Seth Kasselman, sent me some videos of Leroy Van Dyke songs that landed in a more niche zone (“Auctioneer”). We both thought it ruled! But, when he sent me “One Minute More Of Lonely,” I was taken by it. It feels dramatic and kinda dips into a crooner vibe in the best possible way. I feel like he’s just saying, “Give me a minute. Let me be sad for a bit longer. I’ll be fine, but just give me a quick sec.” 

Caleb Dailey :: Sweet Old World (Lucinda Williams)

“Sweet Old World” holds such a special place in my heart for so many reasons. It is really quite difficult to piece a few sentences together to explain the nuances of my personal relationship to it, so I better not try. We might be moved to tears just relating the lyrics to the ones we have lost, and I very much do. Recently, the song has taken on a new life in me, as I start singing it to myself now. It’s quite easy for me (and many of us) to regularly feel a deep guttural hopelessness, and when I sing this song, I’m pleading with myself to remember and see the beauty in the little passing moments. I added a sample of my Mom playing the chorus at the end of the track. I immediately feel a rush of comfort; sitting in one of those little moments, feeling gratitude.
