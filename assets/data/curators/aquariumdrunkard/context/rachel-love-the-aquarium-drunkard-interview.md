## Body

Rachel Love’s musical career began in Cambridge, England in 1978, helping to form the pioneering all-female trio Dolly Mixture. With the group’s singles and early material collected in the classic, 27-track compendium Demonstration Tapes, the band’s post-punk DIY spirit, shambolic pop songwriting and penchant for ’60s girl groups like The Shangri-Las made them a trailblazer in the lineage of indie/twee pop, the riot grrl ethos and beyond. The group’s story was recounted last year in Teenage Daydream: We are the Girls Who play in a Band, penned by Dolly’s own Debsey Wykes (also of the later chamber pop band Birdie). Into the eighties, the trio also contributed vocals on some major UK hit singles by Captain Sensible, the solo moniker of Raymond Burns of The Damned.

Some forty plus years later, Rachel Love established a post-pandemic solo career with Picture in Mind (2021) and Lyra (2024). Both records are being repressed by underground pop purveyors Slumberland next month, including their first vinyl offerings. The pair of albums exhibit a striking and pastoral, melancholic beauty – Lyra created as a tribute to her late husband Steve Lovell. As expanded upon by Rachel in our conversation, Lovell was a prolific producer with a long career working with the likes of Julian Cope, Blur’s early records and a plethora of others. A unique family affair in composition, Lyra was created by Love and her three sons. Layered with lush, downright dream-like soundscapes accompanied by Love’s cello and hypnotic melodicism, the record is as stunning as the bittersweet, deeply personal subject matter conveyed within. There are more current projects as well: Slumberland will also release the new record from her new band Railcard in the fall, a four piece featuring fellow veterans of the formative English guitar-pop scene.

From her home in Brighton, Love joined us to talk through the impetus for her late career solo surge, some surprising Dolly Mixture influences, early Cambridge days with Robyn Hitchcock, her instrumental outfit with Martin Newell of Cleaners from Venus inspired by an old BBC radio program and much more. | m neeley

Rachel Love: So, I did a gig for Chickfactor in London, and they interviewed me. I met Mike [Schulman] there, and he was just talking to Birdie about releasing their album. Then I met him again when Lightheaded were playing with Birdie, they put on a launch for that album in London. Mike said he’d like to put out my albums as well, which was lovely. 

AD: I’ve been a fan of Dolly Mixture for about as long as I can remember, but I discovered Lyra from my friend Tati who has a radio show on NTS called Cosmic Peekaboo. She had sent me the song “Why”, and I listened to it so much that year.

Rachel Love: Oh, that’s great.

AD: When it comes to Dolly Mixture, I think a lot of people think of the DIY spirit and the influence of ’60s girl groups and the Shangri-Las. Were there other influences in your orbit that were perhaps never publicized?

Rachel Love: Oh, what else? Well, we did have a broad listening range. We did like some of the pop that was around at the time, so yeah. Definitely, punk was a big influence. We loved The Stooges and MC5, all sorts of things that my brother was listening to. So we listened to that sort of thing, and then got into the Velvet Underground. That’s perhaps a bit obvious. We liked the kind of seventies pop with lots of harmonies, it was a bit trashy but we listened to an Irish girl group called The Nolans. [Laughs] I don’t know what it was about them, it was really kind of trashy pop but we liked their harmonies and how it was sisters singing together. What else … any harmony group, we liked. We really liked Sister Sledge.

AD: It kind of reminds me of having a soft spot for the kind of pop sensibilities that was coined “bubblegum” in the sixties, a song like “Green Tambourine”. That band was from my hometown.

Rachel Love: Oh, really? With my husband, that song used to turn up a lot on compilations. My son actually played it the other day and asked, do you remember this one? 

AD: I recently read an excerpt from Debsey’s book that Robyn Hitchcock onced tuned your guitars, and he was the only one you ever let do that.

Rachel Love: [Laughs] He was very good to us. He was a real character in Cambridge, you know. We knew him for quite a lot of years. He was really supportive of us. I stayed in touch with him over the years because he wrote lyrics for Captain Sensible. We saw a fair bit of him at one point in time, and I’m still in touch. He was very sweet with us. 

AD: There’s a distinct personal story behind Lyra, but what was the impetus for making Picture in Mind, your first solo record back in 2021?

Rachel Love: Well, a lot was going on. My husband and I had made three albums together and just put them out ourselves. Didn’t do much with them. The third one he’d written most of it, and he kind of encouraged me to start writing. I had never really sat down with a guitar, you know. I would just join in with what he was doing. So he taught me to use GarageBand and sort of started me up again. He was really encouraging, and he also said I should use the name “Rachel Love”, so I’ve stuck with it. It was his idea.

AD: Lyra was made as a tribute to your later husband, the producer Steve Lovell. I was reading about his work with Julian Cope – would you mind sort of summarizing his career in your own words?

Rachel Love: He was in bands in Liverpool when he was in his late teens and early twenties, so he met a lot of people. One particular band he was in was with Hambi Haralambous, and all the members of Hambi’s band had big careers, but Hambi himself didn’t do so much with music. So in his band he had Mike Score from A Flock of Seagulls, and the list goes on – a load of people from Liverpool. That’s where his music career got going, and he got into producing people’s demos. He produced demos for A Flock of Seagulls when they were just starting out, and loads of people. He knew of Julian Cope when he was in Liverpool, but didn’t actually have a connection with him. He moved to London to follow a career as a producer, and there wasn’t much happening. He was busking in tube stations just to earn enough money to pay rent and that’s how he made a living. 

He was busking down in the tube station one day and someone threw him some money. It was Julian Cope. Julian said, I had just been talking about you with my management upstairs because I really like your guitar playing and I want you to play on my record. He told him his management would call him soon, but he didn’t think anything would happen. But they did. So he went into the studio to play guitar, I forget who it was but the other person who was producing but they were completely burnt out and kind of gave up on the job. So Steve said he could do it, he’s been producing people’s demos. And the record company let him. Suddenly he was in a studio with a 40-piece orchestra, and he was producing. He jumped in the deep end. In one year, he did Julian Cope’s first two solo albums and the Flock of Seagulls album. And then his career really got going. He produced all sorts of people: Blur, Longpigs with Richard Hawley. And so, when I was in a band in the nineties, he actually heard a demo of ours and was thinking of starting his own production company. Nothing ever came out [Laughs]. But we got together, so that was a good result. 

AD: It’s a true family effort on Lyra as well. Don’t your sons play on the record? 

Rachel Love: David [Lovell] especially. He played a lot on there and really produced it. I used to nab him when he came home from work and say, David, I just did a bunch more, can you sort this out? He really created the sound, I think. That is Steve’s son, so it was fitting that he was so involved. And then it was Syd who is Captain’s youngest. So I had three children with Raymond Burns [Captain Sensible], and one with Steve. 

AD: You also have an instrumental project with Martin Newell of Cleaners from Venus called The Light Music Company, including a Christmas EP. How did that collaboration come about?

Rachel Love: It’s very English [Laughs]. BBC used to have a “light music” channel, which turned into BBC Radio 3. In the fifties and sixties it was just for light music, people like Percy Grainger. Very much what you’d think of when you hear that. So we just and I just sent things back and forth to each other. It was just a bit of fun, really. 

AD: Could you see making more of that in the future?

Rachel Love: Oh, possibly. Martin is very busy but he even said not long ago that we should do something else. I think it was fine anyway, we enjoyed it. 

AD: Are there performances planned around these new reissues? I also saw that Monorail in Glasgow is helping distribute them. I’m such a fan of the Pastels and that whole scene past and present, it’s a goal to get to that store one day.

Rachel Love: We played in Glasgow last November. We stayed for the weekend and went to Monorail, that was great fun. Yeah, we’ve got a couple of gigs lined up. My son David’s band plays with me, so we have to slot in things when they’re free. We just got one in Wales and we’re playing a small London gig at the Betsey Trotwood, a small launch party kind of thing. I wanted to say, I also have another band that has a record coming out this year called Railcard. 

AD: Right, Slumberland is putting out the Railcard album as well?

Rachel Love: Yeah, they put out a CD of ten songs in January and then we’ve got an album of twelve songs coming out in September. The four of us have had quite long musical careers between us. There’s Ian Button from Penrose Webb and Papernut Cambridge. He’s been with Thrashing Doves, he plays with a lot of people. And then there’s Peter Momtchiloff who plays in Heavenly with Ian. And he plays in Would-Be-Goods, do you know them?

AD: I don’t. 

Rachel Love: Worth checking out. They’re fabulous. And then Allison Thomson who plays trumpet, she’s played in all sorts of bands. She played in Trashcan Sinatras and had a band called Heist. She also plays a bit with Love, do you know Love?

AD: The original Love, from the sixties? Arthur Lee and Forever Changes?

Rachel Love: Yeah, she plays a bit with them too. And she’s a full time member of Railcard, so it’s very exciting.
