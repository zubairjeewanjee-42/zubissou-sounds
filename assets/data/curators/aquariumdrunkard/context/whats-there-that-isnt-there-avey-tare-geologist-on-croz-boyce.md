## Body

Dave Portner and Brian Weitz are finally getting back to basics. After nearly three decades of pushing the concept of “rock music” to its absolute limit in Animal Collective, the duo better known as Avey Tare and Geologist recently dropped their debut collaborative release, Croz Boyce. A breezy, bountiful collection of instrumental jams, the record recalls Campfire Songs-era AnCo while simultaneously striking out in playful, surprising directions that almost verge on pop.  

Recently, we sat down with Portner and Weitz to unpack where Croz Boyce came from, who Janis is, how Animal Collective was never even supposed to be a band, and much more. Our conversation, which has been edited for length and clarity, is below. | i grant

Aquarium Drunkard: Hopefully we’ll get a little bit of both your perspectives here. Brian—how’d the project come together?

Brian Weitz: There’ve been a couple Animal Collective projects where it just ended up being [Dave and me] because Josh and Noah weren’t able to for scheduling reasons. In those projects, we’ve generally had this instrumentation: Dave playing mostly acoustic, and sometimes electric, guitar, and me on synthesizers. The most recent one was a track called “Brown Thrasher.” It was for a compilation called For The Birds, a big box set for the Audubon Society. Dave sang on that one, but the vocals were a bit more in the background. It was a sound we wanted to keep going with at some point when we had the time. We thought there was more there, and we really enjoyed it.

After the next year or two of Animal Collective being in cycle with records and touring, we started [the project that would become Croz Boyce]. Dave sent me a track or two with his guitar parts and was like, “Do you wanna get into this?” It took like two and a half years, just returning to work on it whenever we were inspired. Usually the songs were passed back once, twice at most.

Dave Portner: With Brian and me, part of our friendship has been being fans of a lot of contemporary classical music, artists like [György] Ligeti or [Krzysztof] Penderecki, many of whom combined electronics and classical instrumentation back in the 40s, 50s, and 60s. So I think maybe it’s always been destined for us to try and do something like that, though obviously, we don’t have the musicianship to do something entirely compositional.

For me, at the time of making this record, what I was playing a lot of was acoustic guitar, so doing something that was electro-acoustic in a sense felt [cool]. I don’t know if I’d ever heard anything that fully did that. I’m not saying it doesn’t exist or that we’re breaking any kind of mold, but it just interests me in that way. We like these kinds of records, we like this sort of composition, we thought maybe we could try this with acoustic guitars and electronics.

AD: It’s funny you mention Ligeti: I interviewed Bruce Hornsby for his new record recently, and he also mentioned that Ligeti is one of his all-time dudes. Him being an influence on both 70-something Hornsby with his crazy new record and then this record from you guys—it’s funny how they sound nothing alike, and yet you both have the same influence point.

Brian Weitz: The passage I was just reading in the Alice Coltrane biography [Cosmic Music] last night was about how when she was working on Universal Consciousness, she was also really inspired by Ligeti.

AD: I gotta get into this cat, can’t say I’ve ever listened to him.

Brian Weitz: You’ve seen 2001: A Space Odyssey.

AD: Oh, is that him?

Brian Weitz: That’s him, when he’s going through the light at the end—all that trippy stuff.

AD: Word, I’m gonna throw that on Youtube as soon as we’re off here. 

It’s interesting the way the record came together. The interpersonal dynamic between you two, Noah [Lennox], and Josh [Dibb] strikes me as a pretty unique working relationship. Animal Collective has been going for over a quarter-century at this point, and it seems very fluid. Sometimes everyone’s doing it, sometimes it’s just some of you guys. Then there are side projects that are also you guys, but it’s not “Animal Collective.” Talk about how that relationship has evolved.

Dave Portner: Our relationships are really important to us, and probably the most important thing about being in the band. It’s kinda what makes the band work. Part of staying friends with some people, I’ve learned, is you gotta give people space. As much as it’s nice to be together, it’s also nice to not be together and go experience your own life. There have been a lot of moments along the way where one of us has needed some space, or needed to do something else, and I think we’ve all taken that path. That’s just become a big part of the way we work.

It was just sort of accidental that the four-piece thing became recognized as “Animal Collective.” I think this idea, Croz Boyce, is going back, in a way, to how we wanted it to be originally, where we were just four friends who shared musical interests and liked hanging out together and playing music together. Going back to like, Campfire Songs—that’s an Animal Collective record, but it was originally a project Noah and I talked about that was just supposed to be this acoustic thing. It wasn’t necessarily supposed to be an album by Animal Collective. But because of the trajectory at the time we made that record, it just made sense to release it as Animal Collective so it would be noticed. 

In the beginning, we wanted to do all these different kinds of records. We amassed minidisc after minidisc of different kinds of improvisations and songs, much of which has yet to be released. They were all just their own special little thing, y’know? We never really thought of ourselves back then as this one band that was going to have all these songs that we perform as this [single unit]. It was more of an industry thing that led us that way. If we’d been left to our own devices, it would’ve probably always been this way, where we were putting out different records called different things.

AD: Makes sense. I guess it’s right there in the name, right? “Collective.”

Dave Portner: Right, yeah.

AD: “Animal Collective” has become the name of the band, but “collective,” in other contexts, can just be a group of people who are related and work with each other in various arrangements, but it doesn’t imply the same four-piece lineup for each album over time.

It’s funny you mention Campfire Songs too, because that was one of the records that sprang to mind immediately when I started listening to this record. Then I went back and listened to it again and took a look at the credits, and I forgot that you weren’t even part of that record, Brian. Or I guess you recorded it, but you’re not playing on it?

Brian Weitz: I would say I was an “operator.” [Laughs]

Dave Portner: He was there hanging out.

Brian Weitz: I mean Dave might not totally remember this, but in the late 90s, the first time Animal Collective songs were ever played in New York, Dave had a solo show. Some friends of his at NYU were playing a show with this guy who played in Cibo Matto. They asked Dave to play some songs—I don’t know how they even knew you were working on a record, Dave—but it was before Campfire Songs had been recorded, when Dave was still not quite finished with [Spirit They’re Gone, Spirit They’ve Vanished].

We played some of the Spirit songs, and at least one from Campfire. Dave asked me to help him out with the show, and I was doing a course at the old Columbia-Princeton Electronic Music Center at the time—more like computer music stuff. Before the show, Dave asked me, “Could you make some electronics and some synth stuff and join me on stage with a couple other friends?” And I did it, and then when it came time to record Campfire Songs—I probably didn’t have the parts anymore, but I was like, “It was really fun to play along with these electronics.” And I was rejected! [Laughs] They were like, “No no, we’re keeping it all acoustic, all outdoors acoustic. [The electronics were] just a one-time thing.”

But before [Campfire] was even recorded,  we had a live recording of it. It got stolen in a car theft incident, but we had a recording of it where Dave was just playing acoustic guitar and I was playing computer music stuff that was in the key of the song.

AD: So like proto-Croz Boyce.

Brian Weitz: I guess, yeah. I had kinda forgotten that until you brought up Campfire Songs just now.

AD: You guys have been working up to this for decades, I guess. You said you were passing the songs back, but really just once or maybe twice. What does that look like? I’m kind of amazed at how cohesive the whole thing is when it sounds like you guys were trading emails.

Dave Portner: A lot of the time—and Brian, correct me if I’m wrong—I would do a voice memo recording on my phone first, just to get it in memory, basically. I would do that and send it to Brian and just sorta gauge whether he thought it was interesting…

Brian Weitz: No, you only sent me one, for “Banana Pudding.” I didn’t realize that that was what you were doing. I just dropped the voice memo into Pro Tools and started working on it. I was even like, “Odd choice that Dave didn’t record this more clearly…” But then we kept it, because it worked.

AD: So “Banana Pudding” is a legit voice memo? That’s what’s on the record?

Dave Portner: Yeah. I just felt like I enjoyed that [recording] for what it was, and sometimes that’s just what it takes. I was worried I wouldn’t be able to nail it [if we recorded it again]. And I wanted Brian to affect it anyways, so in that sense it didn’t really matter what the quality of it was. It definitely did matter a little later on in terms of getting the cohesion going, but we got there…

Because this record happened over such a long period of time, almost three years, I think that’s what eventually led to the cohesion. Amassing songs and tunes and little parts. Some of the songs are a little bit shorter, and I would just make that part up on the acoustic guitar, and just be like, “Oh that’s cool. It’s kind of nice to have some shorter songs.” Me personally, I tend to write very long songs…

AD: You’ve been known to do that.

Dave Portner: [Laughs] I’m working on shorter songs these days, it’s definitely a hard thing for me. And it came down really early on, we decided there wasn’t gonna be singing. I actually never planned on there being vocals on any of it, though we had never really discussed that. When it did come up, I told Brian I thought it could just be instrumental—leave the space for the acoustic guitar and the synths and the other little things we put in there. So that was definitely part of the cohesion.

For every Animal Collective record, there comes a time when the songs start coming together, and I listen, and I think to myself, “As a full record, or as a group of songs, what is this missing?” And that leads to something like “Father Karras,” or “Steven’s Sunshine Rejected.” Those were two later songs. I think we felt that in order to have a full record, we needed songs like that, just to give some variation. We like a lot of variation in Animal Collective. We like a good journey.

An album should feel like a journey for us. That’s how we listen to music. The records that we really like a lot, going back to our high school days, have always been these incredible journeys. Hangman’s Beautiful Daughter by The Incredible String Band, or even Piper At The Gates of Dawn—these are the psychedelic records that got us into creating records like this. 

Brian Weitz: One interesting part, when I do receive the files—sometimes it’s very clear when the initial composer hears something as an A section, and then it moves to a B section, and my sounds have to help delineate the journey within a single song. But then there are some times where it’s not super clear, and you just sorta guess. 

I feel like the one I worked the longest on was “Towson Acid.” Partly because I had a lot of other projects in the middle of it…

AD: Yeah, you just put out your own record a few months ago.

Brian Weitz: Yeah, but I had recorded that a couple years ago—or at least started it. So “Towson Acid” had to take a backseat for a long time. But I spent like a year on it. I was like, “I think this song has seven parts,” but I never actually asked Dave. I was like, “I think I’m just gonna guess.” And there were a few parts where I was like, “Oh, and the C part—” And Dave was like, “This is still the B part, man.” 

But since we didn’t really go back and forth a ton, it was just like, he sent the track to me, just layers of acoustic guitars. And a year later, when I felt like I was done, I sent it back with all the synths and stuff, and we were like, “Does it need anything else?” And then there was one more pass of the drums, and maybe electric guitar.

It was fun to not have a whole lot of conversation. Like, “Oh, where do you see the delineation of parts?” Or “What should set one part [pause] apart…”

AD: “Set a part apart”—I got it [laughs].

Brian Weitz: “Set a part apart from another part,” yeah [laughs]. 

That doesn’t always happen with Animal Collective. Usually there’s a whole lot more discussion. And because there are vocals and lyrics, you can kinda tell where the intention is. And for this, the intention was often up to interpretation, and I found that really fun and freeing. And I guess we were pretty in sync, because it wasn’t like we planned on there only being one pass back and forth—like Dave would send me the guitars and then I would add the synthesizers and we’d call it a day. That wasn’t really planned, it just ended up that way.

AD: That method of working strikes as something that you can only get to through reps—by putting in the time—and you guys obviously have been working together for decades. It sounds like there’s an implicit degree of trust between you two to understand like, if Dave starts off with something and then just shoots it over to you, Brian, even if it takes a year to get to the finished product, it’s gonna come out right.

Dave Portner: I’d say it’s one of the best parts of the process too—the surprise element, awaiting a new demo. Whether it’s in Animal Collective, getting demos from Noah or Josh, or in this, where I have absolutely no idea what Brian’s gonna do. I might think one thing about the song, but then get it back and—bringing up “Towson Acid” again, like, I remember being a little concerned that he wasn’t into it, or wasn’t sure that it worked. But then just getting it [after a year] and being like, “Oh, it’s perfect.” It’s such a treat to feel like I’m getting a whole new song back. 

AD: I don’t make music myself—I only talk about it, I have no skills—but it strikes me as similar to shooting a roll of film, especially one you have sitting on your shelf for like six months. You finally take it in to get developed and then you get it back, and you’ve kinda forgotten what you even did in the first place, but inevitably, some stuff is gonna delight you. It brings back a rush of memories, but it’s a new experience at the same time.

Dave Portner: Yeah, exactly.

AD: You guys have talked about this a little bit already, but what exactly are we hearing on this record in terms of instruments? I got guitar, I got drums, obviously synthesizers, I think some organ. What else?

Brian Weitz: There’s one organ in there. There’s a hurdy-gurdy on two tracks, I think. There’s guqin, which is like a seven string Chinese zither.

AD: Is that the thing that’s got sort of a cello-y sound?

Brian Weitz: Yeah, that’s on “Father Karras” and “Eternal Dream Drone.” It’s not supposed to be bowed. I’ve looked into lessons but I can’t find a teacher [here] in D.C. It’s one of those instruments that like, the fingering technique is part of the performance. The notation includes what kind of finger strike you do. My attempts to try and figure it out have been poor so far. I’ve not really gotten very far with it.

I really like records with the guqin, and I’ve just been waiting for this [opportunity]. There’s a used instruments shop in the complex where my personal studio is. I’ve gone in there for years and been like, “You have one yet?” And then one day there was one there, and it was right when Dave had sent “Eternal Dream Drone.” So I just bowed it, because I didn’t know how else to get it to do anything.

AD: It sounds great.

Brian Weitz: That and gong and various synthesizers I’m using—I think that’s kind of it. Also contact mics on things, to make noise and textural stuff. Dave played a Buchla on one song.

Dave Portner: Buchla Music Easel, yeah.

AD: So Dave, you’re mostly guitars and the Buchla?

Dave Portner: Acoustic guitars, yeah. The Buchla was just a drone I did for “Eternal Dream Drone,” the underlying drone of that song. And some electric guitar, and some bongo percussion on “Towson Acid.” And some floor tom and kinda like maraca shakers on “Steven’s Sunshine Rejected.”

AD: You guys are really pulling all the stops out on this one. I was trying to look at credits on Bandcamp or something, and I couldn’t find any. I don’t know if it’s gonna be there on the physical copy of the record, like who does what, or all the instruments—

Brian Weitz: Actually it’s not, because I think we thought it would be on Bandcamp. I haven’t looked at our Bandcamp page. Maybe we never included it…

We go back and forth with that kind of stuff. I remember when we were teenagers in the 90s and getting Sun City Girls records—I think it was 330,003 Crossdressers From Beyond the Rig Veda, they list every single instrument that’s on the record. And then I remember there’s that Incredible String Band movie [Be Glad For The Song Has No Ending]. They’re going on tour in America, and they’re reading off the list for customs of all the instruments they’re bringing. It was really informative—it was the first time I was like, “What’s a gamelan?” Like learning about instruments I had never heard of. But then also seeing a Yamaha DX7 and being like, “Oh we have one of those.” And they’re combining it with all these other instruments we’ve never heard of. It was just inspiring. 

There’s always a part of me that wants to provide that for somebody else. Realize you’re part of a tradition, and you’re just passing on these moments of inspiration. And then there’s a part of me that’s like, “It’s more fun if people don’t know what they’re hearing.”

AD: Yeah, I couldn’t tell. It seemed to me when I was trying to find the credits that it might have been an intentional decision on your part to just be like, “Don’t worry about it.” 

Brian Weitz: Domino always asks us for credit sheets that are pretty detailed, and sometimes we’re like, “We don’t want this information out there.” And they say, “Don’t worry, this is just for our internal files.” But it’s gotten to the point now where I kinda forget to ask, and I don’t really know if that information’s gonna get out there or not.

Dave Portner: And we do like a lot of mystery. We like the Sun City Girls, we like a lot of bands that didn’t say a lot. So I think that’s one side of it. Now at this point it’s like record to record and what we feel like—letting people in or not.

Brian Weitz: Just like it was for Sun City Girls. Not every record of theirs listed what everybody was playing. That’s why 330,003 Crossdressers sticks out, because I was just like, “Whoa, I had no idea what these guys were doing, but now I have a roadmap to explore.”

AD: On the note of no vocals, it sounds like the decision just came naturally? Anything more?

Dave Portner: For me it was a time-of-life thing, what I’d been going through. I’ve been very internal about a lot of stuff the last few years, and doing a lyrical record can be very egotistical in a lot of ways, y’know? Lyrics can be self-indulgent—not to say there’s anything wrong with that. People go through what they go through and say what they have to say. 

But this stuff just came about at a time where I wanted to break free from a lot of that, the more egotistical, narcissistic side of music. I wanted to make something that felt a bit more detached, but also personal—personal in a musical way, which reflected more on taste and what I wanted to hear.  

I’m a very visual person when it comes to music. We talk about music within Animal Collective in visual terms a lot. And even when I’m writing lyrics, I want people to maybe not always think about what they mean so much, and just have it be like an automatic listening experience. Like whatever comes to mind comes to mind. For Brian and me, that was always the fun part of listening to music—even more intellectual, experimental music. It was cool to be playful about the listening experience.

Even the song titles don’t really have a specific meaning. People have been asking about them, and it’s just like, “Well no, why does everything have to have a meaning?” Interpret it how you will.

At one point, Brian was like, “I’m trying to leave space for the vocals.” And I was like, “Well no, I’m not gonna have any vocals.” It was a challenge for us too, because we’ve never really done a record like that before. In terms of my guitar playing, I like the microtones, and that’s what minimalism is about for me. What’s there that isn’t there, y’know? When there’s singing and lyrics, that’s not the kind of thing you can really focus on, because you’re focused on the singer and the vocalist the whole time. 

I wanted it to be more about the microtones and the frequencies—the ghost frequencies that you don’t always get a chance to hear in an Animal Collective record. Even though they’re there, we overcrowd things a lot. Not to say there’s anything wrong with that, it’s just the way we go about making things. It was nice to focus on making a record that felt a bit more minimal, where there could be more space.

AD: When I heard the first song [“Hanging Out With a Blueberry Pop”], and it’s got no vocals, and it’s got the textures, it’s got the microtones, I was expecting the record to be something more… not esoteric, necessarily, but more active listening. But “breezy” is one of the words that popped into my mind while listening. It’s very tuneful, very melodic. I was shocked at how approachable a lot of this music is, even though it has so many hallmarks of music that isn’t approachable.

Dave Portner: In the more recent years of Animal Collective, Brian’s played a lot more into the melodies that you’re hearing. And that hasn’t always been as obvious to people. So I think it’s also nice to have made a record where it’s hard to tell who wrote the song, because both of our parts are adding to the melodic elements. Again, like on “Towson Acid,” my parts weren’t as melodic to me. Then Brian brought more of the melody, more ear candy, in a textural way.

Brian Weitz: Thank you. [Laughs]

AD: [Laughs] You’re bringing the ear candy, Brian.

Brian Weitz: Rare. [Laughs]

AD: I know you guys have been doing the listening events [for the record launch]—is this the kind of music you foresee playing live?

Brian Weitz: Yeah, we’re gonna tour in the fall. We’ll announce the dates at some point. 

I mean, we have to figure out how we’re gonna do it. There’s a lot more going on on the record that just two sets of hands can do, and we don’t wanna over-rely on backing tracks. Figuring out how to do it live will be a whole other challenge, but one we’re looking forward to.

AD: The name, “Croz Boyce”—I’m not gonna ask you to explain that, but any sort of logic behind the decision to give this its own unique name instead of just an “Avey Tare & Geologist” record?

Dave Portner: We were totally ready to call it Avey Tare & Geologist.

To us, the heart of it was “Croz Boyce,” and we would have loved—or we did, obviously—to put it out as Croz Boyce. But for a while, we just assumed that Domino and the industry way of thinking would lead us toward calling it “Avey Tare & Geologist: Croz Boyce.” But then our manager Zoe was like, “No, it’s great. Why would you call it anything else?”

Brian Weitz: Domino said the same thing.

Dave Portner: So we were just like, “Alright, if you think that’s fine, we’re totally cool with that.”

Brian Weitz: Going back to what Dave said about high school and when we became friends and started playing with Josh and Noah—there were so many different bands and projects we had, and they all had their own name based on their identity. As he explained, we thought we would operate like that the whole time, until we were told that we needed a single name in order for people to find us. But internally, we often refer to things as the project name, y’know?

So as Dave said, we kinda thought that was what we were doing. That was the name of the Dropbox folder, that was what we called the project, but we were resigned to it being called something else. When you’re in the system, you just assume there are gonna be rules. Luckily, the people around us—when they heard the record, they were like, “No, it makes sense that this is what it’s called.” 

I guess there’s something intrinsic to the name that makes sense with the music.

AD: Shoutout to Domino for letting you not constantly optimize for Spotify searchability.

One last question. I think I probably know the answer, because you were saying earlier that people are asking about the names of song titles and you’re like, “It doesn’t have to mean anything.” But Janis does show up twice…

Dave Portner: That’s funny, somebody asked at one of the listening parties—

Brian Weitz: I asked! [laughs]

Dave Portner: Again, it’s just fictional… Even “Croz Boyce”—to me, they’re just two fictional people, y’know? Like a fictional duo.

AD: Sure.

Dave Portner: A lot of the other names on there, Janis in particular, they’re just fictional. It’s sort of a nod to old psychedelic 60s song titles, I think.

AD: I’m thinking Janis Joplin, Janis Ian when I see that. I love “Croz” too, because the first thing that pops into mind with my classic rock background is white-haired David Crosby and his giant mustache—which obviously, this is not David Crosby-type music.

Brian Weitz: We’re huge fans!
