## Body

Lagniappe (la ·gniappe) noun ‘lan-ˌyap,’ — 1. An extra or unexpected gift or benefit. 2. Something given or obtained as a gratuity or bonus.

Built from repetition, negative space, and instinctive interplay, there’s something fitting about The Sleeves taking on CAN. Coupled with the twisted wild card of their cover of early-’00s British girl group Sugababes, this Lagniappe Session feels less like a set of covers than a slow dissection, with Jack Cooper and Tara Cunningham pulling the songs apart until only fragments remain. The pair’s notes on their selections below . . .

The Sleeves :: Overload (The Sugababes)

I’m not sure that I had relistened to, or even thought about The Sugababes since I threw away my (heavily scratched) Now That’s What I Call Music CDs in the early 2000s. That is, until Jack brought this song back to my attention earlier this year, when he was on aux duty in the car on the way back from our Modern Nature show one snowy night in Chicago. He said that he had always wanted to cover it, approaching it from the angle of it feeling like a CAN song; groovy and sparse and understated. And I thought – bloody hell! I’m sold! | Tara

I loved it at the time and I love it now. I think all the vocalists on the original are ace but the opening verse by Siobhàn Donaghy has such a great feel. Like Tara notes; it’s always reminded me of CAN in an odd way and the way the two chords modulate gives it a similar feel to Olé by John Coltrane. Perhaps not comparisons you’d expect with a very mainstream pop song from 2000. | Jack

The Sleeves :: Thief (CAN)

It seems like CAN is a recurring theme with us – and this one instantly came to mind when we were deciding on songs to play. That simple, catchy guitar line as the thread throughout, and the rasping, intentional, at times pitch-less vocals from Malcolm Mooney make it so strong – and for that reason I thought, this can afford to be taken apart and looked at in 3D, brought into a quieter, more minimal world – to be ‘Sleeves’ed..! The ‘too late’ refrain we loop at the end of the song was sort of like an ode to the overlapping ‘heeere’s in the fourth song off our album, ‘A Door Swings’, and that incidentally felt like the way CAN tend to approach musical ideas – sit on something, and expand on it. | Tara

Just the coolest band ever… When Tara and I first met and played together, I clocked she was doing a show with some friends playing CAN songs and it was one of the things that made me wonder about asking her to join Modern Nature. They’re probably the band we talk about and reference the most, so this made perfect sense. The Sleeves come at songs in a completely different way to Modern Nature, in that the melodies are woven around improvisation and in terms of rhythm, we essentially play in free time. Our playing is almost entirely subconscious, so covering a song like this, it just seemed to happen. | Jack
