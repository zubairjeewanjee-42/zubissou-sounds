## Body

Sandro Perri is a patient excavator of musical possibilities. For the last three decades, the Toronto based musician has put out meticulously crafted toy adventures marked by hypnotic loops and heartfelt deliveries, in songs that feel refreshingly un-derivative and that carve a distinctive space in the landscape of contemporary experimental pop. What unifies the cerebral techno of Polmo Polpo, the imaginative funk of Impossible Spaces, or the seemingly infinite mosaics of the more recent records, though, is the piecemeal lacing of cell fragments by the game of restraint and discovery of his artistic research.

For this interview, we decided for a piecemeal approach as well, by doing it in two parts, or actually doing two interviews that can then sit alongside each other like weird modular blocks: first a series of written questions sent by email, then a casual conversation commenting on those responses. With effect, the reader should notice that, throughout both exchanges, certain themes will reappear: an attention to process instead of product (Whitehead would be proud!); composition as an end in itself (the value of losing track of time?); art as an ethical practice (Perri insists he is “in the service industry”); and a mode of thinking about music as a series of living-breathing elements with emergent properties, a fauna and flora of its own, to be charted by the musician instead of intentionally constructed by them (a fantasy that takes music-making from the claws of authorial control to a mode of contemplation, attention, and mutual release). Here is devoted work, and the devotion shows. | r moraes

Aquarium Drunkard: From the gritty ethereal techno of Polmo Polpo to the post-rock and freakish folk of the first few records with your name and then to yet even more unbound projects like Off World, of… leftfield electronic jazz?… What is the essential identity that is kept through all those records, if there is one? Do you start those projects with a concept or style in mind, or do they arise in the process of creation and collaboration?

Sandro Perri: Essential identity…I think I probably resist the idea. Or if there is one, it’s not for me to locate. Anything I think of to say, I can also come up with something that contests it. If others are involved then it becomes even more complex, of course. But I have no consistent formula. I like this thing that Ursula Le Guin said -“The creative adult is the child who has survived.” Play is an important part of what I do.

AD: You’ve been compared to Arthur Russell and Jim O’Rourke and other experimental pop explorers, and you’ve covered Russell yourself (in “Kiss Me Again”), and worked with Peter Zummo, who’s a Russell longtime friend. There’s also your frequent collaborator Dan Bejar, who we might group with those experimental pop pioneers too. Do you agree with such comparisons? Is it just because you are all dudes who venture into a wide ranging musical fauna, or is there something really concrete about creative processes or overall sensibilities that brings you all together? Meaning: do you see yourself continuing the legacy of people like Russell and O’Rourke?

Sandro Perri: I think that might be grounds for having my music license revoked – let’s hope I never see myself that way. I’m not convinced we can make meaningful comparisons between people. There are so many potential points of resonance and dissonance, it only gets thornier the deeper you go. I think of a strong work ethic when I read all of those names. And probably a high tolerance for failure. I have a high tolerance for failure – I can work and work and rework, long past the point that is probably advisable. I love the process. Finishing is not as rewarding for me. Maybe that’s a common thread. I’ll say this: if something excites me and it sounds like something else, I don’t worry about it too much. I know I’m a thief. I know “In Another Life” sounds like “Bizarre Love Triangle” and “In My Secret Life”. That was part of the intention. The question is always: what am I gonna do with this thing?

AD: I’ve been paying a lot of attention to the Toronto scene of indie music recently, with Thom Gill, Joseph Shabason, Cici Arthur, Dorothea Paas and some others. You’ve produced for some of them. How do you relate to them? Do you feel like your sound opened a space for them to emerge? And I know Canada has some good governmental funding for the arts, and their records have taken advantage of that too. What would you say of this interaction between music-making/musical imagination and forms of statecraft? Or I guess part of my question is: is there a way to generate Sandro Perris on a large scale?

Sandro Perri: I know and admire all of those people. How my work relates – it’s hard to say. I’m not sure I’ve opened up any space they wouldn’t just open for themselves. Everybody is trying to do their own thing. There is some history here of off-center song-making, if you want to think of Toronto that way. Who opened the first door – we have to go way back. Mary Margaret O’Hara is there. John Oswald, Bob Wiseman, Deep Dark United, The Silt, Eric Chenaux. Many others. There is a relationship to irreverence and play. That might be the common thread. However funding or resources come into the equation, it’s different for each person and not really my place to comment on. Government funding – not as easy as you might think. Most people don’t get it. I haven’t in years. It takes a special skill. You have to put a frame around your work before you’ve painted it. And know how and to whom you’re going to sell it. Most creative people struggle with that.

AD: Your use of the vocals is very particular to me, the kind of nonchalant intonation and soft polished timbres of bossa nova plus the sort of negative melodies you employ, which are so particular to you, and the rhythmic inventiveness, the sense of unexpected or playful prosodic turns in the singing, which breaks with any sort of stale expectation of the listener. Who are you inspired by in terms of vocal technique? Are you usually bending your voice to the lyrics or fitting the lyrics in a pre-written melody? How do you maintain this strong personality in your ways-of-saying no matter what you’re saying and in what genre?

Sandro Perri: That’s kind, thank you. I’m too close to my own singing to have much insight. It’s hard to say which qualities in a voice make it work in a given context. I have no technique. Try to stay in tune. And as you say, try to bend to the lyrics. Don’t hold the notes too long. Melody, words and phrasing are always being adjusted to the environment. It’s a constant balancing act. But I’m cooking without a recipe. Too acidic, too bitter, add a little sugar. Speaking of which – Curtis is probably my all-time favorite. Caetano, June Tyson, Jorge Ben. Phil Lynott, Van Morrison, Rupert Holmes, Alicia Keys. Gene Ween pitched up a bit. Mononeon. It’s a long list. I know I sound nothing like any of them.

AD: The other facet of your music that really calls my attention every time is form. What I hear in a bunch of your compositions is a suite of hooks or bridges, or in-between moments that rarely ever complete themselves. There is this ongoing tension, a very subtle one, but one that is always there. Like you have a theme and you’re toying around with it, taking it from varied angles, slowly testing modes of extraction for the musical nectar. What is your relationship with improvisation in that context? The jazz tradition is clearly influential to your guitar playing, but the songs all seem quite structured, even neurotically structured at times, as if you’re following a set of very strict abstract rules and contorting yourself around them. How do you visualize open forms for pop songs like these?

Sandro Perri: It’s true, the forms can be strict. I like to make something that feels open, but that’s not necessarily how it’s made. There is a lot of restraint. Most decisions are about what not to do.  My relationship to improvisation is for generating ideas and discovering variations. It’s part of the compositional work. Trying things, saying no, trying again until you say yes. Improvisation as a way to fill up the space in a song – that’s not what I do. I’ll explore the materials of a song, as a form of research about the song itself. How a performer behaves in that song is a different problem to solve. I’m more curious about how the musical materials behave with each other, and what the variations are doing to the song. I try to enable those relationships, let them do their thing. Musicianly impulses are often coming from another place, outside of the song. It’s a complex subject. Jazz is live-action painting, self-expressive and egalitarian. The song is the canvas. What I do is write, research, experiment and present musical materials in the form of songs. So many versions and edits go into what I do. I’ve said this before – they are more like song mosaics.

AD: Your two more recent records are centered on long, non-teleological compositions, almost inert circular tracks, that remind me of Reich and of post-minimalists like Gigi Masin or Blue Gene Tyranny, with the gradual phasing in and out of sonic cells. What is the goal with this method that you’ve called somewhere “infinite songwriting”? It seems like we access a different time with them, a different durational parameter, where it’s not about particularly cinematic moments in a song anymore but about the whole of it, the sense of some long construction, that extends to life itself. Or where it’s not about the experience of music qua music anymore but about a meditative or contemplative experience with time. Is that a fair representation of your recent sound?

Sandro Perri: That is a fair representation. I love Blue Gene. And maybe one of my goals is to lose track of time. It happens to me while I’m working, but creating music for a listener to lose track of their time is another thing. Thankfully there is no formula for this. And opinions will vary wildly. Most people are hitting skip. You have to be in a headspace. If I discover something I want to sit in for a while, I’ll do that until it tells me how to proceed. Timing and tempo are important. If something is too long it’s too long. And I’m not trying to be the longest. I used that term ‘infinite songwriting’ once because of the enormous pressure to say something about music when you put it out. But I regret it. In case anybody thinks I sit down and rub my hands and go “ok, time to get infinite!”. It’s absurd. I would generally say don’t trust what artists say about their own work – especially on release day.

AD: Is your new record going in the same direction—slow-burning pop meditations? I know it’s been in the making for a while now, and we are now more distant from your last release as Sandro Perri than your Polmo Polpo debut is from Sandro Perri Plays Polmo Polpo (2006). Could you be changing again (to quote a song of yours)? I mean, about twenty-five years have passed now since your first appearance as a musician! How do you see yourself from this distance, this shadow in the mirror? What are you planning to do with it next? How has your own personal life affected the new record, maturing and moving around and forming stronger human bonds and so and so on?

Sandro Perri: There are more long songs, yes, you got me there. And some short ones. Some instrumental, some vocal. Same as always. I don’t think I’ve changed much. I think the new music might be a little more absurd. Life is more absurd when you’re older. Personal life as it relates to music making – I don’t go there. I’ve been roped into it before and it feels cheap. Not to denigrate the work of music journalism – I know the job is tough and those details provide some meat. I’m just not oriented that way. I might get somewhat interested in an artist’s personal story after I’ve been hooked by their music if it’s incredible with no explanation needed and I can’t help being obsessed with knowing every detail of its origin. But to front-load the way we do now…it pushes me away. I get it though. People want to be seen.

AD: Your songs are very dependent on studio techniques, overdubbing, splicing, and mixing magic tricks. They seem to create this kind of cartoonish world that you then calmly populate with your themes. But even though I’ve never seen you live I’ve heard that it’s even more of an experience with the core aesthetics of your music. How can that be? How do you translate those headphone-fantasy tracks to the live concert environment? Any specific preoccupation is major to you at that point?

Sandro Perri: Someone went out of their way once to tell me they were disappointed at how different a show was from the record. At first I was a bit insulted, then I thought it was sweet. They cared enough to be disappointed. You can say anything to me now and I’m like – wow thanks, you listened ? Most people have told me they like our live sets. Something I think about a lot in both situations is pacing and tracklist/setlist. Maybe that translates. But I’m happiest in the studio. If I had a budget I would have a bigger band, but then everybody would have to play less. And you gotta pay people more to play less. We used to always open with the song “Impossible Spaces”. On record that could only ever be a last song, and live it only ever worked as the first. I can’t explain it.

AD: Apart from music, I’m interested in visual and literary resonances with your music, the sort of expansive cosmos that makes up its overarching vibe. I always think of how Andre Ethier’s music sounds exactly like his drawings, for example. And I’ve heard you’re really into Robert Altman. What else do you like, and what artists do you think are sort of analogous to your own music in other fields? Plus, what have you been watching, seeing, and reading these days, that we might imagine would come through the new record? Or how do other arts influence your new compositions and general presence as an artist? Do you see them all pushing each other around in the same flat level or do you try to keep your music fairly autonomous from these other realms?

Sandro Perri: I remember wanting to make a record that felt like the movie Shortcuts, which seems like a ridiculous and presumptuous idea to me now. It’s not how I think anymore. Though I still come up with ways to avoid staring into the void. I like to organize things thematically. Sometimes I think about film editing as it relates to records – those two Walter Murch books were inspiring to me. So yes, the resonance between mediums affects me too. But at the end of the day, I’m most interested in music on its own turf. I know this sounds like an old-fashioned way of thinking, but for me it’s a way forward. It’s more about decluttering; removing the relentless encroachment of other elements. Getting closer to the source. It’s a holistic concern for me. I find videos, and visuals in general, when applied to music, mostly one-dimensional. Sound on its own is much more 3-D. The eyes aren’t busy so the mind has more room to play.

AD: There is a non-zero probability that Impossible Spaces (2011) is the record I’ve listened to most in my life. Which is kinda crazy. The thing is it became deeply tied to my partner of 14 years, and marked landmark moments of our adolescence together, so I kind of developed a para-social relationship with it, like it’s an entity. It’s an album of pure love for me, for mnemonic reasons, obviously, but also for what I like to believe are ingredients of the sound itself, its elasticity to those specific affects it invited for me. So thank you for that. Random curiosities, though, just to disguise this fanboy note as a question: what do you think of Marcel Proust? And what do you think of J Dilla?

Sandro Perri: I’m touched to hear this, and I’m glad it found a place in your life. We have something in common – it’s the record I’ve worked on more than any other in my life. I have no thoughts about Proust, but I’m interested in why you ask. J Dilla – what can I possibly say that hasn’t already been said. I don’t know much about him. I liked Stakes Is High. I liked Donuts. “Whip You With A Strap” is my all-time favorite Ghostface. I am probably a J Dilla fan.

AD: My first follow-up was about the second question, where you talk about… I’m trying to make comparisons that you think are maybe too grandiose? With Russell and O’Rourke, these experimental composers. And you talk about a tolerance to failure, which I think is a very good proposition for art. I’m very interested in this notion of a praise of failure. In the sense of success being something that is not very interesting actually, and that is very constraining of human beings. Can you speak to that? What do you mean by that term?

Sandro Perri: It’s a good question. I think of it a lot, and I think of it in a broad sense. There can be failure in the individual steps that you’re taking towards some sort of idea of a goal that starts to develop as you’re working, as you’re in the process. You start to see some foggy image, and you start to associate that with a goal in what you’re doing. And of course, you can feel as if… you can get to a point where you realize that you’re never actually achieving that thing, that foggy image. You’re just sort of moving in a direction that is constantly unveiling new foggy images as you move towards it. And so the feeling of not quite achieving what you may be looking for is constantly there in the process. You’re constantly with it. And that’s a feeling that I think can be very… what’s the word? Uncomfortable. And can cause a lot of… People quit, people stop doing something because of that feeling, you know?

AD: Yeah, when you’re describing it, it seems like you’re describing being lost. Like, oh, there’s a fog, and I’m going through it. And it’s more fog.

Sandro Perri: Yeah, yeah, and it is really… In a way, for me, that’s kind of the essence of the process. I never quite feel like I’m achieving the thing that I initially thought I was going to achieve. And so that feeling of… It’s not quite disappointment, it’s more being comfortable with not actually going in the direction that you maybe thought you wanted to go or you were going to go. There’s that within the process. And then there’s the feeling of finishing something. By the time you’re finished, it’s more like you’re sick of it as opposed to, ah, I’ve achieved it. It’s rarely that feeling for me. It’s usually just, okay, I think I’ve gone as far as I possibly can here.

AD: There’s a Borges poem that I always mention, which is about this guy that’s trying to cipher the world. He wants to take everything that’s in the world and put it in a book. And he does, he puts everything there, and then he says, I’m done. It’s done, it’s great. And then he looks up and he forgot the moon.

Sandro Perri: That’s it, that’s it. Every time you think that you’re finished… I guess for some people, there is that sense of satisfaction and achievement and whatnot. But the nature of my relationship to doing creative things is that of a constant feeling of not quite getting there. And that’s a part of what fuels the process as well. So it’s a necessary relationship to failure, I think of it that way. A high tolerance. For me, I can constantly be in that state of, oh, I didn’t quite achieve what I was looking for. Maybe it’s not the same as a work ethic, but I think of it as somehow connected to a willingness to be in that state of not quite achieving.

AD: When I asked about governmental grants, ways to fund weird music, or as I wrote it “to generate Sandro Perris on a large scale,” you were kind of skeptical about it, right? Because I was thinking, also, of your National Park Project, and there’s a way that I think that incentivizing music is like incentivizing a National Park in a way. There is a governmental way to create these parks, these places that are nice and beautiful and that we want to be in them, right? We want them to exist. And I think there might be something about that in music too. Not that governments can plan out, like, an indie scene, but there is, maybe, a way of thinking of music as a park. Could you clarify your relationship to these kinds of material necessities of being a musician and how you go about those funding processes?

Sandro Perri: Yeah. Well, I think your idea is quite beautiful actually. But I don’t think that exists in Canada. I don’t think that way of thinking exists in the grant world, in the way that grants are organized. It’s much more about fostering the economic growth of the arts in Canada as opposed to creating a sort of a space that is connected to, let’s say, the idea of increasing an appetite for the arts. I don’t really think that that’s what they’re thinking about. I don’t think that that’s how it’s approached, whereas what you said kind of makes me think of that, something that’s more about providing quality of life for people as opposed to helping people’s careers. A lot of people who get grants in Canada, and this is not to be disparaging towards the system, but a lot of people who get the grants are already quite well established in their careers, and they’re getting more money to increase that success and to take it further out into the world. I find it really difficult to exist within that sensibility of trying to apply for government funding. As I said, you have to sort of know how to put a frame around what you’re doing before you do it. Some people can think that way, and some people can’t. I have a really hard time with that. And then you also have to essentially sell what you’re doing. It really is a sales pitch.

AD: Yeah, and at some point that kind of contaminates your work in a way, doesn’t it? I’ve talked to a lot of artists who need to reduce their art or describe it in certain ways to get a grant, and then that ends up getting it back into the way they’re thinking of it and doing it.

Sandro Perri: Yeah, that definitely happens. People start to make projects that are meant to get grants. And maybe that’s completely fine for them. Maybe it fits their way of working. But I’ve been hesitant to kind of embrace that style of work. I’ve also been very lucky, I should say, that the label that I work with gets some government support, and so I indirectly benefit from that.

AD: Another thing I asked about was your vocals. You were kind of unsure if there is a technique that you use. You were just, like, oh, I try to stay in tune. But I do feel like there’s something very particular about your voice. I think of the voice as this thing that’s very distinctive of a person. We even have this internal sense of a self as being a voice. But you claim that there’s this casual way you sing, that you’re just trying to do it as best as you can. And I feel like there’s more. For sure there’s, like… Have you done classes? Or how do you think of the voices in terms of it as an instrument? Of course, you wouldn’t say you just try to play the notes on the guitar and see how it sounds, for example. There’s a way of going about it, I guess. My question is more, like, maybe if you could expand on your way of singing? Because I feel like it’s not spontaneous at all. I might be wrong about that. It might just be the way you sing when you’re in the shower or something. But I feel like there is a technique to it.

Sandro Perri: Can I ask you, are you a singer or do you do any singing?

AD: No, I’m not a singer, professionally. I do know some music, play piano, and do some singing like any other person does, but I wouldn’t say I’m a singer at all.

Sandro Perri: And do you feel comfortable when you sing? Like, are you okay with your voice just coming out naturally as it is? Or do you sometimes feel like, oh, I need to learn how to sing before I can do this?

AD: I feel like there’s a range that I can do very comfortably. There’s some singers that I can imitate very comfortably. And I feel like the singers who are very, very good are actually the ones that can do uncomfortable ranges. So David Bowie would be good because he does something that doesn’t seem like it’s going to be good, I guess. I think it’s essentially wrong singing that still works somehow. So his personal voice comes out of that. I do feel like there’s a range that I can do, but that’s the boring range. I wish I could do different ranges.

Sandro Perri: Yeah, that’s interesting. Right there is where I think the interesting piece happens for most singers. It’s that your own voice is always going to sound less interesting to you than anybody else’s voice, in a way. That’s one of the challenges of singing, I think, even though I know very little about singing. That’s one of the challenges for me, just navigating that relationship between acceptance and dismissal, where, as you sing, as I sing, I start to learn the limitations of my voices. I can write a song and then realize after I finish writing it that I can’t sing it. Then the question becomes, okay, do I change the key of the song? Or do I change the melody so that I can sing it? Or do I abandon this entirely because I’ve overstepped my brain? Maybe I’ve written something that’s too big for me. So those are just some of the challenges that come up. And then, you know, words, writing words, of course, there’s the risk that as you write them, you feel like you’re revealing something about your identity or your character that may make you uncomfortable, or may give the wrong impression, or may be misconstrued as some sort of diaristic, confessional kind of thing… Which, you know, lyrics may not necessarily be coming from that place. That folds into the singing challenges. Do I want to sing these words? Should I change these words in order to suit the song more? There’s all these different challenges that are constantly on the table. Ultimately, I sing not because I think I’m a good singer or not because singing is the most important thing to me. It really is just another element amongst many elements in songwriting. I have great respect for people who really take the job of singing seriously, because as soon as there’s a human voice, the ear goes there first, the ear prioritizes the voice. A really good singer is respecting that fact. I come from a place of… I sing, I know that it’s limited, and I’ve taken a few lessons but it was so complicated for me that I wasn’t really sure I was improving. I learned certain things, but I’m not sure that I was able to really apply them and feel like, oh, I’m becoming a better singer. I’ve never really had that relationship to singing. It’s always been, okay, I’m going to try this, see how it goes, and if it doesn’t make me cringe, then I come to a place of acceptance with it. I grew up hearing singers that were not traditionally amazing singers, but had an interesting quality to their voice, and I’ve always been more attracted to that concept, and I’ve been probably emboldened by that concept in my own pursuit of singing. Like, I hear singers that are clearly not singing perfectly in tune and are not great, but there’s something about their voice that I like, and so it puts me at ease enough to sing.

AD: At some point in one of the answers, I think the next answer, you characterize your composition as a type of research. I was interested in that. In what sense is it a research? Because there’s a sense of a survey, or do you think of it as a form of thinking about the world? And also, that relation between restraint and freedom in the composition, how does that work? I often think of that Robert Frost quote that “writing free verse is like playing tennis with the net down.” I think about that, about the sense of restraint, or something like Brian Eno’s Oblique Strategies, sometimes. There’s a sense in which restraint is generative, right? Could speak about that, the sense of research you mentioned and this kind of relation between openness and limitation that you also addressed in that answer.

Sandro Perri: Yeah. Yeah, so the first part, the research part… I use that word because when I write, I find that the more time I spend writing, the more the music becomes the dominant voice in the relationship, and I’m becoming… I’m in service of that voice, that idea. And so it’s actually more like just listening. I’m listening to the song, and I’m waiting for it to tell me, to reveal to me what other elements fit in its worlds. Now, of course anybody could say, well, that’s just your imagination. That’s just the way that you characterize your imagination. But it really does feel to me that a song idea reveals itself, and I just have to be there, ready to hear it and to parse it out, to go, oh, yeah, I can hear this melody in here, that works. And that could take a while. And that’s a form of research. That is kind of like investigating what is going on with the song before it’s finished, in the writing process. And that can involve many stages, many layers of doing the song, many different demos, changing the key, trying different instrumentation. Just sort of seeing what resonates in the song world. What has a vibration that kind of feels true, that is memorable, that sticks, that seems to feel right. I think that is different from what I want to do to the song, if that makes sense.

AD: It’s emergent, then.

Sandro Perri: It’s very much about what emerges from the song, yeah. And when I said I’m researching, I’m putting those materials together and then combining them, and it becomes almost like a mosaic of the song experience. There can be three, four, five, six different versions of a song in the final recording that I discovered through that process of working on the song. Then the piece about restraint is tied to that, in that it’s trying to be aware of when an idea is coming from the song and when an idea is coming from me, some sort of personal need that I have, which could be like, oh, I need to have a fast song. I should make this song fast, which is very much like a… selfish is not a good word. But it’s coming from me as opposed to coming from the song.

AD: More imposing?

Sandro Perri: Imposing, yeah. Or I need a song where I got to have a guitar solo because I’ve been working on this guitar stuff and I sound really good and I want to put a guitar solo in, which has really nothing to do with what the song is asking. Maybe it does. Maybe it is asking for that. But I try to be aware of the subtle variations between what I need and what the song is asking for.

AD: I’m wondering, though, when you think of, oh, I need to put a solo in it, aren’t you also being suggested by, like, the world? Different suggestions are just passing through you in different ways?

Sandro Perri: Yeah, yeah, absolutely. And that’s the thing. It’s having some sense of… Maybe you can’t 100% know for sure anything, but it helps me to use that as a guide so that I can make a distinction between when I’m making a decision that feels, again, about me versus something that I’m doing in service of the song or the piece of music. And it’s the same thing with making a tracklist or a sequence for a record. Sequencing, for me, is super important because it’s about listening to all of the elements and seeing what story they want to tell, for lack of a better term. And that story or that arc, that flow, is much more important than what I think should be the first song because it’s the strongest song or I think this song should go last because I’m not so confident about it. I try to understand that there’s a distinction between, again, my needs and what the music seems to want. It’s a service. I’m in the service industry. (laughs)

AD: The other follow-up I had was about you trying to rebuke this ‘infinite songwriting’ description that you used at some point. You said, oh, I just made that up when I was pressed for an explanation. But I actually really like it. I really love that description. I don’t think it implies that the song is infinite. It implies that there’s an infinity within this small song form. It’s more a qualitative than a quantitative infinity. Small infinities that are happening because the songs are very circular and so on. And I was also thinking about this because I also write, right? Words, in this case.
