## Body

This week, music journalist Vish Khanna published the 1,000th episode of his long running Kreative Kontrol podcast, a fascinating conversation with the ever-prolific Ty Segall. And while lesser broadcasters might take a few weeks off for a leisurely victory lap, Khanna instead just got to work putting the finishing touches on episode 1,001 (also published this week). 

Whether coming from the standpoint of a musician himself, digging for a good scoop, or cracking wise, Khanna brings an enthusiasm to the show that’s infectious, inspiring past guests like Will Oldham to say things like “…it’s rewarding, relaxing, fulfilling to engage with Vish, as the exchanges have always been just rife with that rarest rarity: communication.” 

When I approached Justin Gage about re-launching Transmissions as an interview podcast in 2016, Khanna’s erudite, well-researched and often very funny interview style was more than an inspiration—it was a roadmap for how this sort of show could be done, proof of concept. On the occasion of his 1,000th episode, I asked Khanna to reflect on the milestone. Looking for more Khanna Kontent? Check out his interview on Transmissions, and if you’re looking for even more, give my appearance on Kreative Kontrol a spin. | j woodbury 

Aquarium Drunkard: Vish, congrats on your 1,000th episode. As someone who’s been in it for a minute, what excites you most about podcasting these days?

Vish Khanna: The creative motivation remains the same as it was when I first began to record interviews and share them publicly on the Mich Vish Interracial Morning Show!, which was a weekly college and community radio show my now-wife, Michelle, and I ran in Guelph, Ontario for 300 episodes between 2005 and 2011. I began my journalistic pursuits as a freelance writer, which I enjoyed, but also often wished I could share more of the interviews than was fit to print, what with word count and layout limitations.

I suppose I’m curious and have always appreciated insights about creative people and works that you can gain via a good interview, and so I was drawn to contribute to that kind of information transmission. And then as a fan of a comedy late night talk shows, and someone who enjoys performing for live audiences—whether as a musician, a moderator, emcee, or hosting my own variety shows—radio and podcasting still gives me a chance to listen and respond to guests in, I hope, thoughtful, sincere, and, when the moment calls for it, humourous ways.

The other aspect of my work that is significant to me, is some form of community-building—connecting artists and people I find fascinating, with an audience I’ve cultivated who trusts me, and then also meeting or encountering fans and followers of my guests who discover Kreative Kontrol and dig into the episode archives. Despite it often being made for public consumption, any kind of individual expression can be surprisingly isolating work, so to receive lovely, encouraging messages from strangers and regular listeners can be a huge lift.

AD: Absolutely. Making shows like this is oddly isolating, but comments, thoughts, and personal stories from listeners really makes a difference. It feels less like a consumable “content consumption” based relationship and more like a real back and forth.

Vish Khanna: [That] kind of rewarding community-building has enabled me to cultivate Patreon support too, which has been life-changing. For the first decade of this show’s existence, I’ve managed it while also having day jobs, and so it was either a hobby or part of my freelance side hustle that I’ve tended to for 20 odd years. At some point, to earn some kind of pay for the hours of work I do to make the weekly show, I started a Patreon, but didn’t emphasize it very much in a public-facing way, beyond a brief mention. It was always very modest and I was cool with that.

But when I left one job to take another and then lost that second one in February 2024, I tried going all in on asking people to subscribe to the show on Patreon and, to my astonishment, they’ve really responded. Thanks to their support, I’m able to make a living of some kind, primarily by doing the work I most want to do and contributing to cultural communities that mean a lot to me. Does my wife working make this possible for me and for us to support our family? Yes, yes it does.

So, the notion that niche podcasters can actually earn incomes from supportive communities has been immensely uplifting and gratifying for me, and while crowd funding has literal ups and downs, I’m riding the waves and will do so for as long as possible.   

AD: What podcasts got you interested in creating your own and why?

Vish Khanna: Interesting, I’m not sure how to answer this exactly because of my own timing in starting to do radio interviews and run a podcast in sort of the early days of podcasting. I mentioned the radio show where the interviews I did started as conventional 10-to-20-minute segments, but then got longer and longer. Maybe that was reflective of trends that podcasting normalized.

I began working for CBC Radio 3 in 2008 and was assigned to do a three-hour web streaming morning radio show I called The Wrath of Khanna. I divided the week up so that each weekday, people could expect specific segments and the one on Thursdays was called The Breakfast Club (preceding the more famous radio show with that name), where I went out for breakfast with Canadian musicians across the country, recorded the entire experience but planned it all out as radio “host breaks,” where I interviewed them in chunks between coming out of and throwing to songs.

Anyway, that turned into my first weekly podcast and guests and people seemed to enjoy it. I have some sense recall of what Marc Maron and Bill Simmons were doing with the format at the time, but I think The Breakfast Club was less about my takes and more about interviewing musicians about their latest records and creative trajectories in a prepared but informal manner—basically what I do now, but you seldom hear my guests and I ordering or making and then eating food together.    

AD: Looking back over your massive archive, what are a few episodes that stand out in particular?

Vish Khanna: Extremely difficult to answer for me, one, because I have trouble picking favourites of any kind, and two, because after mostly releasing two episodes a week for 12 years, it’s become a bit of a blur for me. But let’s see. Steve Albini was a guest on Kreative Kontrol every year of its existence until he died on May 7, 2024. We recorded our final interview on April 23, and I posted it along with a lengthy tribute on May 15, the week his band Shellac released To All Trains, their final album. When my wife and I signed off from our community radio show because we were about to welcome our son onto Earth, I asked Steve to be on that 300th episode, and he happily accepted. If he were still here, there is no doubt in my mind that I’d invite him to be on the 1000th episode of Kreative Kontrol. I bet he would’ve said yes.

I also gathered Steve and Ian MacKaye together for a rare, two-part joint discussion about their punk paths and where they intersected, which remains one of the most popular things I’ve done. Speaking of Ian, all four members of Fugazi convened with filmmaker Jem Cohen for my 900th episode. It was likely the first time Fugazi took part in a group interview for a public-facing audio or print piece in at least 30 years.

Speaking to Maria Bamford was a huge thrill. Just one of the most startling comedic performers and minds working today and she was so friendly and frank about some “comedy industry” topics. I was excited to have this on my calendar and it didn’t disappoint. David Berman from Silver Jews and Purple Mountains briefly returned to public life in 2019 and granted me his only audio interview, before he took his own life in August of that year. This is the saddest and most difficult part of my show’s trajectory. I was so happy to correspond with David in the last years of his life and to have him entrust me with a copy of Purple Mountains almost a year before it came out…and then for him to agree to having our conversation appear as a podcast…and then for him to leave us…it remains overwhelming for me. I get Purple Mountains songs stuck in my head all of the time, but I haven’t been able to listen to the album once through since David died. As of this moment, I dunno if I ever will be able to.

One of my comedy heroes, Robert Smigel agreed to be on the show because he loved a review I wrote about his film, Leo, and then accepted my DM invitation on Instagram to have a chat, which he didn’t really do much of on podcasts at the time (besides Conan O’Brien’s).

Through a friend of friends, Iggy Pop and I had a good talk in 2019. A similar and unlikely connection came about with Adam “Adrock” Horovitz from Beastie Boys in 2023. Though it was a technological and geographical challenge, Mdou Moctar made their first podcast appearance ever on my show in 2021.

A 2017 interview with Warren Ellis in Toronto was just getting started when Nick Cave did a walk-on and then stayed for the entire thing. Then Nick invited me to their show that night and we chatted backstage afterwards. I have a way with hardened, artistic men for some reason.  

I could go on about guests who mean a lot to me. In short, I will say the show often breaks and makes news when guests like Pavement, Godspeed You! Black Emperor, Jon Spencer, and Silkworm have made revelations about their work that are picked up by media outlets around the world. These artists are among a slew of recurring guests like Andy Shauf, Circuit des Yeux, Jeff Tweedy, Nina Nastasia, Destroyer, Land of Talk, The Kids in the Hall, Heather O’Neill, Will Oldham, Nick Thorburn, Allison Russell, The Jesus Lizard, Julianna Riolino, Jim O’Rourke, Cadence Weapon, Bill Callahan, Fucked Up, Roy Wood Jr., Tim Heidecker, Daniel Romano, Sonic Youth, and Tom Scharpling, who make a point of not only appearing on the show, but returning again and again, which I just feel very fortunate about. 

“It’s a good life if you don’t weaken.”
