## Body

Haley Fohr has been making intense, otherworldly music since she was a teenager, weaving her haunting, velvet-toned contralto into all sorts of extreme and challenging musics and collaborating freely within and outside of Chicago’s thriving experimental scene. Her latest album, Halo On The Inside—the first since she signed with Matador—is a riveting mix of thumping dance beats and spiritual transport.

Aquarium Drunkard reviewed the album in March, observing, “Physical transcendence comes from obliterating, jack-hammer beats, strobe-lit dance floor synths and surrender to overwhelming sensation. Yet she also taps into an otherworldly undercurrent, a trance-state union with non-human forces; her deep-toned throaty voice carries messages from other realities, other galaxies.”

We caught up with Fohr in a late spring break between tours to talk about her album, her collaboration with the producer Andrew Broder (aka Fog), her journey in developing her voice and her collaborations with artists including Bitchin Bajas and Bill Nace.

Fohr sees her work on Halo as among her most accessible, but it remains an extraordinary document of artistic fearlessness. And that courage and willingness to experiment is at the heart of what she looks for and strives for in music. When asked what separated great work from the run-of-the-mill, she explained, “Risk is a very important ingredient. I also think vulnerability, but maybe that’s the same as risk. I don’t know that everyone can perceive the intention behind music, but I feel like I can. Sometimes you can’t put your finger on it, but you just believe it, and it feels like a gift, like someone’s really showing themselves.” | j kelly

Aquarium Drunkard: I was wondering if we could start with the really striking image on the album’s cover. You’re wearing horns, and I gather it’s meant to evoke the god Pan. I was wondering if you could talk a little bit about how you chose that image and what it means to you.

Haley Fohr: The precipice for that was being in adulthood long enough and going through the loops of self-care and trying to like better myself through things like therapy and consumerism and… maybe even like being a woman in the music industry. I just felt like I was getting a lot of messages that maybe I wasn’t enough, and I started to believe and inherit that from the world.

For two years I took a break and started writing this album very slowly. I divested from the industry, played less shows than I have in the last decade. I got a day job and tried to work on loving myself and finding that sort of internal light.

I don’t know. There’s something really powerful about the image of a she-devil. It allowed me to expunge that from inside of me and it made room for me to find some light within myself.

AD: There’s a real Dionysian energy to this album. It’s very physical and dance-y, but also it’s got a spiritual transcendence to it. And I was wondering if you feel like these two things are opposed, or are they the same energy expressed in different ways, or how do they relate to each other?

Haley Fohr: Dionysius was certainly on my brain when I was making this album. I think they’re the same. I think it’s the same two different sides of the same coin, you know? It’s very human of us to want to find joy and levity, and we all have desires. There shouldn’t be so much shame in utilizing and exercising those functions, you know?

So, yeah, the album’s cathartic, It’s a little debaucherous. There’s a lot of beats. I used a lot of electronics, and that was an intentional way to sort of draw something out of me that felt a little dark and maybe even conceited or something.

AD: The sound is so massive on this album, like on “Megaloner,” that hard walloping beat and the synths and your voice, and it just fills up the space, maybe even overfills it a little bit. I was wondering if you could tell me about what your vision was when you started making these songs, if you had any touchstones in terms of albums or artists or anything else in the world?

Haley Fohr: Throbbing Gristle has been a touchstone for me since I was a teenager. I knew I wanted to make something industrial and grimy.

I’ve lived in Chicago for almost over a decade now, and it’s cacophonous and claustrophobic and I wanted to give truth to that. It also feels grounded. There’s a lot of bass and sub-woofers, and that’s new to me. I’m grappling sonically with the inability to really untether yourself from life.

AD: I like “Canopy of Eden” the best, and I have to admit, the first few times I heard the line, “I can make the radio break,” I was hearing that as “I can make the radio pray.” And I remember thinking, wow, yeah, she probably could do that. Could you talk a little bit about how you made that song and what it means to you.

Haley Fohr: For that song originally, you know, I was inspired by Amon Düül II. That was a commune in Germany. And then, the commune breaks apart, and a woman starts singing. And there’s something really powerful about the album Wolf City that I really like.

That song comes from a place of like feeling force fed and how the human voice and the embodiment of like some internal truth can really break everything away.

I was stuck, honestly. It wasn’t really developing very well. And my producer, Andrew Broder, brought in Melvin Gibbs, who is a highly regarded bass player, if anyone’s unfamiliar with him, check them out. His credits are amazing. He’s worked with Arto Lindsay, with The Roots and everyone in between. And he provided the bass line that gives that whole entire song meaning. I didn’t write the chorus until after the bass line was there. He brought the melody in.

AD: You had never worked with him before?

Haley Fohr: No never, and we did work remotely as well, so it’s not like I even really got the chance to sit and pick his brain. It was just trusting his musicality.

AD: That must be exciting but a little scary to bring people in like that that you don’t know and don’t even get to meet and there they are putting things in your songs.

Haley Fohr: There’s a lot of trust there, but also I’ve had to learn to be a little impersonal in my editing. There’s a lot in this album, but 80% of it was deleted. That’s just how this album went.

AD: What does that mean? Does that mean you had whole songs that you got rid of or specific textures within songs? What are you talking about when you say 80% was deleted?

Haley Fohr: Well, I’ve never really worked for the producer. I’ve been DIY or die. And because of it, I’m a bit of a polymath. I can play many instruments. I can record, produce, you know, do it all. But I wanted to give myself a bit of a break and use some different tools. So, there are almost no Chicago names on this record, which is different for me. It’s all Minneapolis-based or New York.

I write demos, and they have very distilled inherent meanings. It’s very personal. So, you know, communication is a big part of it. Getting on the phone and talking about the meaning of something is more important to me than trying to describe like a type of bass line I want. I let people fly, and then I have to rein it.

For instance, “Organ Bed” is a song about feeling really lonely and envisioning my cat. And being able to hold me and embrace me like a mother or something. And, you know, at one point that track turned into this hard techno song. And it made me upset. You know, I had to be like, I know everyone’s really excited about what they’ve accomplished musically, but it doesn’t serve the purpose. And so, when I say 80% of things were deleted, I mean a lot. There’s a lot of versions of songs that are entirely different.

AD: Save them for the box set.

Haley Fohr: It would be a nightmare for me to figure that out, but maybe one day.

AD: I also really like “Truth,” which has that repeated almost percussive vocal sound. How did you decide to do that?

Haley Fohr: You know, I really love extended vocal techniques. I guess I leaned more into the jazz, Annette Peacock world in the past.

I wanted to make people dance. I like how flat the ethos of that song is. It’s like a geode that’s constantly rotating. But there’s no big reveal. I’ve got all these pedals and weird little machines at home and I just experimented until I found something that felt repetitive enough…like a blanket. My voice is always the easiest way to do that, frankly.

AD: It’s got that line. “Truth is just an imagination of the mind,” which seems very apt for the world that we’re living in at the moment. What did that mean to you?

Haley Fohr: Its meaning depends on the day, you know. But I think, I think the bottom line is it gives me levity.

I’m a very cerebral person. The state of the world right now makes me very depressed. And so, a statement like that gives me not even power. It’s more just emotional ease about the way your perception can create your own story.

AD: I did want to ask you about your vocal technique, which is very unusual and striking, and I believe you’re mostly self-taught, aren’t you?

Haley Fohr: I would say half and half. I haven’t taken any lessons since I was 18, and I’m 36 now.

AD: How have you developed that really unusual style? Can you tell me about your trajectory as a singer?

Haley Fohr: Sure. As a kid, I was very shy, but I loved to sing. I took free programs like general music and was in all the community choirs. I’ve always had a very resonant voice, and I had a teacher pull me out and asked me to do a solo and it went really well. Then, I enrolled in some free voice lessons. That’s when I learned that I have a contralto voice.

AD: It’s very low for a woman.

Haley Fohr: Yeah, it’s very low. I sang lower than most boys. And it set me up for a being different from the get-go. And I’m not saying it was easy, but it was something that I ran with.

So, I was singing formal music, like Italian arias, and jazz standards, like Billy Holiday and Etta James, because my voice was in that range.

Then when I was 23, I moved to Chicago, and I gave myself a challenge to try to collaborate with as many people as possible. Because I was from a small town in Indiana and suddenly I’m dropped into this world where people can be artists and musicians for a living. And, you know, there’s just so many amazing musicians. And I really did that. And I think you can see that you look back in my discography from 23 until about 30. Very prolific. I did a lot of improvising at the Hungry Brain and Constellation, and I fell into this experimental jazz scene.

There’s a woman named Carol Genetti who is an amazing vocalist. We would have talks about how to sound like a swarm of bees or, you know, these really unconventional goals. It was really fun.

Since signing with Matador, I’ve been releasing songs that to me are a little more straightforward in terms of vocal delivery. There are verses and choruses and bridges, but you can still hear all that information in my compositions.

AD: Do you have any favorite sounds or lines or moments on this album? Things like, I’m not saying what’s your favorite song, but like, is there anything that just really pops for you when you hear it?

Haley Fohr: That’s a great question. I really love the way that the album starts. I love the beat in “Megaloner.” Like you said, it’s made out of static, and it’s this overwhelming feeling. It really excites me the way that it turns the speakers into a sculpture, you know? Like you feel like it almost should be breaking the system. And yeah, I think that’s maybe the most exciting sound for me.

AD: Okay, cool. Can you talk a little bit about the people you worked with on this album, Andrew, the producer, and any significant collaborators and what they brought to you for this album?

Haley Fohr: Certainly. I mean, Andrew, the way that I became interested and found out about him was through a record he did with an indigenous singer named Joe Rainey. He did the arrangements and production. It’s cassettes of pow-wows that are cut up into samples with a bunch of string arrangements over it. There’s something very Alice Coltrane about it, but also a heavy, earthy, vocal vibe. That’s how I found his name.

Andrew is the type of guy who generates a lot of ideas. He’s a guy that works with like 200 tracks, and I’m used to working on an eight track tape machine. So, there was a marriage there or compromise. But, you know, his spirit never sank, and he really held the torch in that way, like, keeping on when maybe I needed to rest.

I had the ability to go and stay with him in Minneapolis to do some sessions, and that was really transformative for me. I was alone, post-breakup making this album, and I got to live with him and his family and, like, make dinner, and I think that really allowed me to be really vulnerable.

It’s awkward. It’s like reading your diaries out loud to each other, making music. And, you know, so not only did he have the technicality to create whatever world I wanted, but there’s an unspoken, just beautiful, leading by example. I could just tell his life is so full. And I think that really resonated with me.

Nat Harvey is also a new budding singer-songwriter engineer that I’d like to push. They live in Minneapolis as well, and they recorded most of the record. We did it in a small studio, and I felt some kinship and growth there.

Alan Sparhawk is on only one song, but meeting him through that process has, like I said, also evoked a sort of mutual collaboration between us. Like, we’re very close now, and I just love what he’s doing right now, and I feel a kinship.

AD: Which song is he on?

Haley Fohr: He’s on “Skeleton Key.” At the very end, there are a couple things going on with the guitars. There’s this really masculine metal guitar solo, but then there’s this swampy atmosphere underneath, and he’s the swamp.

There are so many people on this record, but I guess those three are the ones that stand out.

AD: I caught your set with Bill Nace last year, and I really loved it. It was really enveloping and overwhelming. How did you start collaborating with him.

Haley Fohr: We have a lot of mutual friends. I opened for some Body Head shows maybe five years back. That led to us hanging out more. His approach to guitar is like he’s so fluent. He also has a childlike mind when it comes to trying things which I think is rare. We just committed to each other in this duo. We do it when we can. We’re working on a record right now, and it’s lovely. It’s really exciting. We’ve only done a couple tours but I’m really happy you got to see it.

We’re so similar. You know, we’re good at pushing and pulling. It’s not like he’s doing something and I’m supporting. It’s really a conversation when we’re on stage.

AD: What I noticed was that you were hardly singing at all, at least in the show that I saw. There was far less singing than in your solo stuff.

Haley Fohr: Well, yeah, I mean, silence and space are things that I’m maybe fucking around with right now in composition.

AD: Are there any other collaborations or side projects that you want to talk about in addition to the current album?

Haley Fohr: I have a back catalog of some original soundtracks that I’m putting together, and that feels really good. I’m excited about that. These large compositions, working with choirs and symphonic ensembles. It’s a privilege that I’m not sure comes by often, but it’s something I really want to pursue. It’s also a little side step away from what I’m doing with Matador and Circuit des Yeux right now.

AD: When you say soundtrack, is there a film involved?

Haley Fohr: Yes, the main one I’m working on right now is a silent film called Salome from 1923. I’m mixing it right now. There are a couple other silent films that I’ve worked with as well. I’m trying to figure out how to package it all together and present it in a way that’s not chaotic, if that makes sense.

AD: We have a venue near here in Vermont that does quite a lot of artists playing with film. The movie is usually a classic silent film. You have an artist who’s either improvising or has composed music for it. I think most people who like music also like film, so it’s really an interesting thing to watch.

Haley Fohr: It’s so generative to have such an emotional visual to play off of, you know?

AD: Is there anything going on with Jackie Lynn now?

Haley Fohr: Jackie Lynn is slumbering. Bitchin Bajas, which is the band that I work with in Jackie Lynn, is working a lot on some new upcoming material of their own. But I wouldn’t be surprised if we made another record in some years to come. I like to keep it low-key.

AD: What do you think separates really great music from stuff that’s just okay?

Haley Fohr: A couple things. Risk is a very important ingredient. I also think vulnerability, but maybe that’s the same as risk. I don’t know that everyone can perceive the intention behind music, but I feel like I can. Sometimes you can’t put your finger on it, but you just believe it, and it feels like a gift, like someone’s really showing themselves. And the older I get, the harder it is to do that, not only in music, but just anywhere in life, you know?
