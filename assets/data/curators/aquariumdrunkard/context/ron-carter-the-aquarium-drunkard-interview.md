# Ron Carter :: The Aquarium Drunkard Interview

## Body

Ron Carter has famously played bass on a lot of albums — enough to net him recognition from Guinness World Records. And the number of records gracing a Carter credit is indeed staggering: if you were to listen to two platters on which Carter appeared a day, it would still take more than three years to get through them all. Anyone who put in the time would surely emerge with a pretty good picture of jazz in the second half of the 20th century and the first quarter of the 21st, with most of the major styles, innovations and major players present and accounted for. If you were to play Seven Degrees of Kevin Bacon with Ron Carter instead of the movie star, you could probably decrease the number of degrees by a least two. The Ron Carter Universe website provides an interactive discography that allows users to filter through Carter’s work with more than 2,000 collaborators — and it covers only about half his discography.

But Carter’s longevity and dedication as a sideman, along with his stint in Miles Davis’ Second Great Quintet, tend to obscure his many other major accomplishments. His leader albums, on labels as diverse as CTI, Milestone, Blue Note and EmMarcy, capture an incredibly diverse range of approaches and sounds, from intricate string arrangements to duos with pianists, guitarists, saxophonists and even accordionists, from sleek, soulful fusion to Brazilian celebrations to a 1980 homage to John Williams’ music for Star Wars.Despite Carter’s august status and wide name recognition, many of these albums have gone out of print. The work of comprehending Carter’s achievements as a bandleader, composer and arranger has only just begun.

Now pushing 90, Carter hasn’t stopped adding to his discography, finding new ways to connect himself and others music. His latest album, Sweet, Sweet Spirit, a collaboration with gospel artist Ricky Dillard and a large choir, blends music of the Black church and jazz in bold and striking ways. But even this innovation has some precursor in Carter’s deep history. He played on an album of “liturgical jazz” by Ed Summerlin in 1968, Ring Out Joy, and that same year (working with Herbie Hancock, Thad Jones and Grady Tate, among others), even taped a jazz-inflected set of tunes modeled around a Jewish prayer service.

Though he’s rightly achieved legendary status, he has an approachable manner that’s presumably the product of intense curiosity and engagement — he wants to learn as much as he can from any conversation, even as he has so much to teach. Over a 35-minute Zoom session, Carter talked about the inspiration behind his new project, his hardscrabble and illustrious past, his philosophical outlook and his practical methods. Even in such a short conversation, he gave a detailed breakdown of music as an art, a profession and a discipline. And, most of all, Carter showed that a life spent keeping time has not prevented him from existing in the present moment. | r jackson

Aquarium Drunkard: So, this new album started as a way to pay tribute to your mother, correct?

Ron Carter: That’s right. My mother, toward the end of her life, lived in a nice senior citizens’ home. And my sisters and the people there wanted her to have some memories. And so they suggested to me that I put together … a disc or tape or whatever we could use that she could have access to play music when she wanted to hear it of her favorite songs. So … I went out and bought a computer and a music program and put together a list and the recordings of 10 of her favorite songs. … I figured how to make the string sections sound, the usual package of sounds that comes with the unit, and I was able to figure out how to put it on the disk. They brought her a little boombox machine back in the day, and when she would lay her head down to rest, she would plug it in and listen to some of her favorite songs played by her son’s invention. This lasted long enough for her to “go upstairs” with nice music in her head.

AD: Wow.

Ron Carter: And in the meantime, I was always asked what’s my next project, and I kept saying ‘I don’t have one,’ and I didn’t really have one. So, I sat down one day and finally said, “OK, my next project is going to be some kind of way of recording this stuff again with some with some string players. And it’d be a tribute to my mom’s memory of the songs we sang in church when I was 8, 9, 10, 11 and 12, early in those years.

AD: And how did that all come together?

Ron Carter: I didn’t have any idea how to arrange this stuff, who to talk to, no budget, no personnel. And the last person who asked me was Don Was, who was at Blue Note, who is at Blue Note. And I told him just what I had in mind, that I was going to hire a singer who passed away, Gene McDaniels, who was a really good friend of mine, if you know his name from the records of the ’70s. And he was a really good singer, man. We worked on the idea of him doing it, but he passed away much before it was his time and our project could come to fruition. So, Don had an idea that he could call someone in the religious gospel music scene, that I had no idea those people who were. I know the music from radio but never had any contact with those performers. He knew this person who he called the “maestro of the gospel choirs.” I said, “Wow, I need to meet this guy, man!” So, we hooked up a meeting, and we had had a long talk, and we felt that were on the same page. … This is the results of my 10- or 15-year- —whatever it was — quest to have my mom completely embedded in my memory forever, and anyone else who wants to have a musical bible, I call it, plug this plug this into your CD or your vinyl player, and you will hear my thoughts on my mom’s presence and my family’s life, and you may find some solace in these kinds of songs that you may know those melodies but not know what they refer to.

AD: Right.

Ron Carter: I did find out, that the longer I was in this project, I had to listen to the words because I had to make the bass line work around the lyrics and the choir arrangements. I hadn’t understood what the words were back in the day, we were just going “ruh ruh ruh” because you had to do that stuff in church, you know, don’t know the words and they aren’t important to you. … As I listened to this stuff for over a year with some real serious intention, because I’m trying to make the bass be responsive to those vowels and “ohs” and “ahs” that the choir did so well. Those lyrics were actually pleading to [God} to help [the singers] get out of this terrible situation. They wanted to be free. They wanted to be able to do what they wished their hearts could do, and the situation at those times was not allowing them to be anything but someone’s helper, a slave. These were early freedom songs actually, and it took a while for me to understand what I was listening to. … So I hope that people when they hear this … [they] sit and let the music wash over them, they will hear the pleas of another three or four generations before them asking for the same things that they’re asking for now.

AD: Do these hymns sound pretty similar to how you sang them when you were a kid, or did you change them quite a bit?

Ron Carter: I did as near as the melody and the generations would allow. If they go to the books that I used to find these melodies, they’ll find they’re pretty close.

AD: To me, this album sounds so celebratory and joyous.

Ron Carter: I couldn’t see who was singing because I was a little far away or my posture wasn’t allowing me to watch, but man, those, voices, there are so many incredible solo voices that are blended into one 80-voice chorus of people singing these songs at the command of Brother Ricky. I’m telling you, if you could imagine standing in front of this choir and trying to find some right notes with this air of 80 voices beating your eardrums from the behind, it’s an amazing feeling to know that two or three great notes, man, that makes that stuff have another vibe, and I found a couple of those things.

AD: That sounds incredible. It must have been very challenging.

Ron Carter: Absolutely, and we hope to do it when we find the right environment and the right space. I want people to be in the room when we do this live just so that they could levitate, you know. I want them walking on air for as long as they can hear this melody in their ear.

AD: You’ve played it live already a few times, right?

Ron Carter: Yeah, in Chicago. And we did it in Washington, D.C. … People were kind of just stunned, because they didn’t know what to expect from this guy with this instrument in front of them, with the choir in the background and once we got past the, I hate to say shock, but the awareness that this is a real event … I could see heads nodding and the feet moving, and at some point, I could imagine the back row of ladies with their fans just quietly levitating over the sweet, sweet spirit, you know, and that’s just an amazing view, to watch people … pleasantly stunned that they know some of these words, they know some of these melodies, though they haven’t heard them in a very long time. They haven’t heard this stuff in this format, let alone this one note in the background making it do something else. Once we all got accustomed to what we had stumbled on, the reward was ten- to twelvefold.

AD: Are you going to take it out on the road more?

Ron Carter: Given the size of the operation, we need a really big place, and there are some of those megachurches around, but I don’t know whether anyone has the wherewithal and the connections to put together a 90-piece ensemble like that. I mean, orchestras do it all the time. They got 110 people with instruments and stuff like that. I’m sure it’s physically possible. It’s just a matter of managing the time and the schedules to see if those voices are still available. I’d like to do it live, though, just to get that rush again.

AD: Is it at all similar to writing for or playing with a big band, or is it very different?

Ron Carter: No, man, no, the singers gotta make notes stop somewhere.

AD: That’s how you know I’m not a musician when I ask questions like that. Do you consider making music a spiritual practice?

Ron Carter: I think of it as a connective presence, and each audience presents a different need, or a group of needs depending on their moods, the space that they’re in, their experience with coming to a live jazz concert, how the sound of the room is to them, who their favorite musician on the bandstand is, what their age group is, all these factors come in to any and every gig we do. And I’ve learned for all these 75 years of being a sideman how various leaders connect with the audience depending on where the leader feels that the specific audience is leaning toward. … They learned to kind of plan their program ultimately about how we feel the audience is responding to the leader’s choice of notes and choice of tunes and choice of speeds and length of the solos and what key they’re in — it’s tough! And I think one of the things that I learned to do for all these years is to kind of tune into real quickly what my environment is telling me is the best path to get to these people’s sixth senses about this thing called music.

AD: Here’s another very metaphorical type question — Can you ever find the same note twice?

Ron Carter: Yes. In my book Chartography, what I tried to do is show what a transcription really is supposed to do, show you how it works, how it evolves, what effect this idea has on the band. … In this book, we took “Autumn Leaves,” which was the opening song for every Miles concert I did for almost six years, and we found five different [versions]. You can hear my bass line evolving. You’ll also notice how the rhythm section knew it was coming and how they adjusted their accompaniment to this figure. All of them, Miles, George, or Wayne, Herbie and Tony, they knew it was coming because they heard it somewhere before, and they’re trying to figure out how they can help this figure evolve because they know this song is coming by again.

AD: I was looking up other religious jazz albums, and I came across one by Ed Summerlin called Ring Out Joy (1968) — and you played on it!

Ron Carter: Yes, Ed was a dear friend of mine.

AD: Do you remember much about recording this album?

Ron Carter: No, I don’t, but Ed was an excellent teacher, a good teacher of how to arrange anything. And there was a [religious television] program that came on at that time called Lamp Unto My Feet. And somehow Ed got the idea that he could write music … for this program. And the people who were his friends at the time, Charli Persip on drums and Eric Dolphy on flute and bass clarinet and Steve Kuhn on piano, I mean he had this wonderful wealth of musicians around him who were as curious as he was but just didn’t have that interest in doing that kind of [thing], but they trusted Ed’s sense of value in music. And then when he brought this music in with this background, everyone said, “Wow, listen, look at that.” And we were amazed at the fluidity of the ideas with his music … We did several services with him in and around New York City, and in the studio with him, and that was my first [time] really playing in a church service with specific [parts] to play.

AD: I wanted to ask you about the other great jazz cellist Abdul Wadud — so I looked on your website, RonCarterUniverse.com, to see if you had played with him, and you do both appear on one album, Michael Franks’ Tiger in the Rain. Were you actually in the same room with him when you recorded that?

Ron Carter: Yes. He had a strap on his neck that was screwed into the back of the cello, like you hold a saxophone only it was holding a cello. He was new to the instrument, but he had some good ideas. He was working on getting the skills to play what he could hear. You know back in the day, they were just learning how to make some real transparent jazz recordings. … If they had understood how best to record his sound, it would have been even more impressive, because he was not just playing new sounds, he was playing another arrangement of his notes on this instrument that no one really knew how to record.

AD: Especially, I imagine whoever was producing the Michael Franks record might not —it’s not really their wheelhouse.

Ron Carter: There was another cellist called Calo Scott. He was in the band with Charlie Rouse that I was part of at some point — really improvising cellos.

AD: Oh, yeah, he’s on the Two Is One record, right?

Ron Carter: You’re over my head now.

AD: Charlie Rouse’s Strata-East album that just got reissued, there’s some cello on it.

Ron Carter: They were really good players. They weren’t just playing parts, and they were playing horn parts on cello.

AD: Did you ever hear Abdul Wadud’s solo album for cello, By Myself?

Ron Carter: No.

AD: It’s really pretty, and he does some wild things on it.

Ron Carter: Send me a copy of it, if you don’t mind.

AD: I’ll forward it to your people. It was on his own label, and probably sold, like, 20 copies or whatever.

Ron Carter: I call them pocketbook labels.

AD: That’s a good term! … I wanted to ask about your strong relationship with guitarists, like Jim Hall, Metheny, Bill Frisell …

Ron Carter: Gene Bertoncini.

AD: I didn’t want to mispronounce his last name, that’s why I didn’t mention him …

Ron Carter: Sam Brown, Ted Dunbar, some great players, man.

AD: Do you feel like you have an affinity with the way guitarists play or think?

Ron Carter: I think they have an affinity for me. I understand the ranges we’re dealing with, and I want to make sure that our notes, which will often be in unison, don’t get swallowed by their partner’s sound or volume or pitch. And those guys you mentioned, they are aware of instrumental ranges, and they don’t mind stepping back if their range is getting in the way sonically of their bass player partner, and they understand what a duo is. … I’m watching these guys play, and I can’t believe I’m playing with them. They understand the instrument, and they understand that if you want the bass player next to you to help your sound, you better listen to what he’s playing, so your sound won’t swamp his sound.

AD: Do you know if there are any plans to release the recordings of concerts you and Pat Metheny did a couple years ago?

Ron Carter: I have a copy of our tentative master, and I was just kind of stunned — with no rehearsal and just calling the tunes off in a certain order, you know, the magic is all there. This gospel record’s taking precedence over a lot of things right now. And at some point, when this when this dies down in 2035 (laughter), I hope we can get it out, because Pat just played some fantastic stuff, man, and to know I was helping him reach those heights — I don’t play a horn, but if I could toot my own horn right now, I’d say, “Well, you sure played good with this guy.”

AD: I believe it. He said it was an incredible concert.

Ron Carter: He’s right.

AD: Do you play the piccolo bass at all still?

Ron Carter: Only with the nonet. And, unfortunately, two of the four cellists have “left the concert,” and I just haven’t gotten past that emotional barrier of finding two more cellists who are as musical and as all for the music as those two who are no longer with us. Actually, three of them are gone –and I haven’t gotten past … trying to find somebody to replace [those people] who are so important to that band sounding and feeling like that. We used to do a “A Song for You,” and the audience used to love that melody because it’s a great melody, and the cellos really dug into the parts I wrote, and just to hear that every night because I wanted to hear it, and they wanted to hear it and they wanted me to hear it … it’s just, wow, it’s a nice feeling to have! That’s what I want to see, if they’re going to reissue some of my records, I wish they would have the nerve or the money or the budget or the concern to reissue a couple of those nonet records because the music is spectacular, man. The personnel was just perfect for a guy who, like me, who was learning how to write for them. They play what I wrote, sometimes great, sometimes not so great, but I learned — it was just fabulous.

AD: Are there any other albums that you want reissued? Because you have so many, and I was really shocked at how many are still sort of harder to find than they should be.

Ron Carter: Well, I’m not sure how far back we’re going, but one of my first records was Charli Persip and the Jazz Statesmen, with Freddie Hubbard, Roland Alexander, Ronnie Matthews — one of the earliest records I did. And one by Howard McGhee, called Dusty Blue, that was one of my early records. … I have a record I did called All Alone, I’d like to have that reissued in vinyl because it’s just me [playing]. … Anything by Jim Hall.

AD: I keep waiting for a Jim Hall renaissance to happen.

Ron Carter: Me too.

AD: Did he tutor Gary Larson, the Far Side guy?

Ron Carter: Yeah, he took a couple lessons. I met him at the Vanguard one night way back in the day. One of my favorite cartoons — “It’s only a paper moon” … it shows a man tearing the moon like a sheet of paper. A really nice man. I don’t see him anymore.

AD: He’s kind of reclusive.

AD: We’re getting short on time, so I should ask a Miles question: The Plugged Nickel box set recently got reissued — do you ever go back and listen to that at all?

Ron Carter: Well, I didn’t know it was being recorded until it was released, and Jack DeJohnette, we were at a date at some point back in the day, and he said, “Yeah, man, that record you guys did at Plugged Nickel was really good.” I said, “Jack, what are you talking about? He said, “That’s a record out now you and Miles at the Plugged Nickel, man, with Herbie and Tony, and you guys sound great. … So I went out and found the record and ultimately took it to Columbia and took it to the union and made them pay us something for the effort, you know.

AD: That’s good!

Ron Carter: Well, it’s fair, but it wasn’t good. But that’s another story for another program. So, I haven’t really heard — I’ve heard parts of it, and I can appreciate the effort we put into this record. We were all new at this. We were all basically new at recording first of all, and we didn’t know we were recording. Yet for these four amateurs and one great guy who knows how to make a record, what would we try to do, knowing that it’s forever — we had no — we were just playing, trying to figure something out, here. … This was also the time when [after the recent birth of his son] I commuted to New York three or four of those nights. I’d do the last set, about 1 o’clock or something like that, got a plane to New York, spend the day in New York … and then take a 6 o’clock plane back to Chicago … play some more and stay there for three or four nights. We had two young kids, and I didn’t want [Carter’s wife] to be by herself, and it meant just flying to Chicago and back to play with this wonderful new band. “I’m not sure what they’re doing, but man, it sure feels good playing with these guys.” And that’s my primary memories of that time, and I try to use that as an example to the kids, my students: If you really want to play something, and you have a chance to play really good with it, go outside your boundaries and find a way to make that happen. If it means taking a lower price, if it means wearing a suit. I mean, there are all reasons to say no to playing with a certain band just because you don’t want to meet their personal standards, you know, that kind of stuff. Don’t do that. Understand that here’s one more chance for you to find the right note. And to this day, going on 89, I go to work every night. Maybe tonight if I can find that note from last night, or find that figure from last night, because I know the band is trusting me to bring something new to this pot of stew. I love that challenge, and every night they’re right.

AD: OK, final question: I think they should rename the jazz apple should be the Ron Carter apple. Would you support this movement?

Ron Carter: Only if you have it in a couple of keys.
