## Body

West of Roan is a duo of Annie Schermer and Channing Showalter, two visual and performing artists, who share a love of old folk and myth, close harmonies, shifting drone and puppets. The band started in a leap of faith as two friends decided to continue a collaboration they’d begun in college. Schermer moved cross-country from Washington State to North Carolina, where she and Showalter lived in the shadow of Roan Mountain, a historic hub for Appalachian folk and craft.

Yet though grounded in old, ancestral traditions—Celtic and Norse mythology, unadorned singing and the plangent tones of fiddle—the pair have resolutely avoided folk purism.

“We’re pretty careful about performing traditional music,” Showalter explains.“ We think it through and we think about what we want to say about the song that we’re singing that’s not ours, and if we don’t feel like we really have much to say about it, we don’t always choose to sing it.”

Schermer adds,“ There are so many people taking a conservative, preservationist approach that it opened a space for us to be like, great, well, let’s keep tradition alive in this other way, by creating new tradition.”

As a result, their latest album, Queen of Eyes, contains just one traditional tune, the Annie Briggs’ inspired “Let No One Steal Your Thyme,” a lovely tribute to the communal traditions that move them. But other tracks are looser and less song structured, conjuring smoke and magic out of intertwining voices, poetic imagery and flights of bowing. You get the sense that West of Roan is a real place, grounded by history and informed by generations of artistic endeavor…but also an imagined space created and shared by these two women, their voices, their instruments and their puppetry. | j kelly

Aquarium Drunkard: I think a sense of place is important in all kinds of folk and folk-adjacent music. What sort of place is denoted by West of Roan? Where does that come from?

Annie Scheermer: Good question. I’ll start and, Channing, you can jump in. We met here in Bellingham, Washington where I am now, but when we formed ourselves as West of Roan, we moved to Western North Carolina to focus on folk music and collaborate on music and puppetry.

AD: There is a Roan Mountain in North Carolina.

Annie Scheermer: Yes, and we were living literally west of Roan Mountain, so that’s part of it. We were searching for something to call ourselves that would symbolize what we wanted to do together and what it would mean to us, just kind of as a starting point. So there was that kind of yearning for the West where we came from and also connecting with our ancestors who were from Northern and Western Europe and saw the west, especially in Ireland, as like a mystical other land.Long ago. And then the migration of our ancestors west to this continent. So we were just connecting to that motion and direction and the resonance of that.

AD: Is the place in North Carolina where you first started making music and puppetry together a place that’s got a lot of folk tradition?

Channing Showalter: It does. We lived in Madison Country and there’s a long tradition of ballad singers, particularly unaccompanied ballad singing, as well as fiddle playing. We moved there partly out of an interest in that, in the landscape and the history. But then again, we were very immersed in our work at that time, so we did meet a lot of musicians, but it wasn’t such an outward time. We were really making a lot of puppet shows and just holed up working on our stuff. But it was special being there.

AD: So having encountered you solely through the recorded music, maybe you could tell me a little bit about the puppetry and how that fits into your art form.

Annie Scheermer: Well the name, “Queen of Eyes,” it’s not a puppet yet, but we’ve dreamed of it as a puppet show.

Our work is really story based, a lot of it, and when we started working together in North Carolina we made our first puppet show based on the ballad “Tam Lin.” That was a marionette piece that we performed in Ashville. It kind of launched us into this mythological conversation. The puppetry really brought music and craft together for us. There’s also a really strong crafts tradition in Western North Carolina and Appalachia. Channing attended a couple of different folk schools in that region. We were integrating the act of making the puppets and also the stories that we were creating, that all tied together. That’s still there in our music. The Queen of Eyes is this character that keeps coming up. And “The Baron” is also one of the tracks on the album, and that’s also a puppet show that we created around these characters.

AD: Queen of Eyes has a very Robyn Hitchcock flavor to me, though I know that’s not what you intended. Tell me about making the puppets.How do you make them? What are they made of?

Channing Showalter: We have a few different ways.We do a lot of shadow puppetry, so lots of paper cutting with an X-Acto knife and using different techniques of light and reflective surfaces and experimenting with shadow. The hand-held puppets we work with often start with a wire architecture and then papier mache over it. We both do a lot of textile art, so often we will incorporate fiber in some way, for example, stitched pieces.

[Annie brings out a couple of puppets.]

Annie Scheermer: I just happen to have a couple in this room. Here’s one shadow puppet from “The Baron.”

AD: We have a lot of puppetry where I live in Western New Hampshire and Vermont. There’s Bread and Puppets and Ric Bass has a studio here.

Channing Showalter: Yeah, that’s great.

AD: I did want to ask you about the sense of place. But I also feel that it’s not entirely a real place, that there’s a layer of magic and myth in it. How does myth play into your work?

Annie Scheermer: That’s a great question. That’s definitely underlaying everything for me and for both of us, this sense of mythic resonance. We’re both poets. It comes out through the songwriting. We’re looking for this resonance in stories and in archetypes. It’s a part of how we live our lives and how a lot of people live their lives. I don’t know the exact words to describe it, but I feel a connection to the other world, whatever that is, and maybe that’s something that we’re finding through our work, defining it. Early on, that’s been a through line. What is this other place of ancestors and stories and magic, and how is that place here and present with us in our lives? Also how do we connect to it as a source of inspiration? And how do we connect the places that we’re in now to that?. Does that make sense?

AD: Is it a specific kind of myth? Is it Celtic myth? Are you drawing on any particular tradition or making it up as you go?

Annie Scheermer: I think it’s a bit of all of those things. We both really feel, as European-descended people growing up in the Pacific Northwest, that we are reconciling place with a sense of displacement culturally. Our ancestors really left a lot of their culture behind, and some of it remains but some of it has been lost. So I think for us, by connecting to Appalachian music, we’re learning how to have a culture. We’re creating it. We’re drawing Celtic mythology and Norse mythology and we’re also trying to do that in a way that is not misappropriating. We’re trying to find what it is for us.

AD: You have a lot of very close harmonies, almost dissonant and sometimes they turn into this buzzy kind of dissonance. What is it about two notes so close to you appeals to you and stirs you? What draws you to use this sound so much?

Channing Showalter: We have sung together for a long time. Since we were about 20 and we’re in our mid-30s now. It’s always been a really special way of connecting and having friendship.

When we started working more closely together, we also started experimenting more with singing. Having the time and space, giving the time and space to playful experimenting—it’s sometimes really hard to find with people. I think we just got really comfortable using our voices in that way, and often that meant experimenting with dissonance. We don’t map out our harmonies at all. We just make them as we go, using what’s feeling most intuitive.

A lot of the music we’ve listened to has influenced us so much—and that influence is coming through us whether or not whether we’re always aware of it in the moment—emphasizes harmony. We’ve had an interest in that for a long time and have taught singing workshops. We’re always thinking about the melodies crossing and going into unison and becoming dissonant. We’re trying to kind of throw out the rule book that maybe we once thought there was about harmony singing and just do what we want. Do what our hearts most feel right.

AD: Both with the voices and with the fiddles.

Channing Showalter: With both. They’re kind of similar in that way. The fiddle can be a real voice, I think.

AD: Do the songs change from performance to performance? It sounds like they must.

Channing Showalter: Yeah, a little bit, and especially some of them. Some of them are a little bit more set and it’s kind of nice to just play them the same way. In other ones, we have a little more space.

Queen of Eyes by West of Roan

AD: What’s a good example of one that’s set and one that’s more fluid?

Channing Showalter: One that is really a little bit more fluid is “Gentian”. Partly because when we recorded it, it was still pretty new for us, and the recording was charged in a really great way. We weren’t trying to get it a certain way. We were trying to feel it in the moment and were really not sure what would come out. I feel like we ended up being happy with what happened, and that just doesn’t happen on every song. So now when we play it, it still feels open. Maybe because I’m not the one singing the melody. I’m just kind of like – do I even remember all the words? I’m playing it a little bit different every time, because I bowed a banjo in that track when we were recording, and I don’t usually play that live. I choose a different instrument.

AD: And which ones are more structured and set?

Channing Showalter: More structured and set, I guess, would be “Bright” or “All the Water.” Annie made fiddle parts that we really liked and they were easy to play in front of people in a performance setting. They’re pretty comfortable songs for us that we just play.

AD: I’m wondering if you could talk to me a little bit about influences, and I assume you draw inspiration from older folk music and I’d like to hear about that, but also if there’s any other kind of music that really feeds into your art that people might not expect.

Annie Scheermer: Yeah, we listen to a lot of different things. One thing I was thinking about with harmonies was that when we lived in North Carolina, we got to sing some shape note music and go to some sacred harp sings in the Asheville and northeast Tennessee. So those intervals are in there for sure. A lot of fifths and not necessarily dissonance, but a lot sounds that make western ears think, “That’s kind of weird.”

We also have gotten to sing and listen to some Eastern European choir music. Both of us were listening to the Bulgarian Women’s Choir before we even started singing. That was in there, like, “What is this What are they doing?”

AD: What was it about that that really caught you?

Annie Scheermer: For me, the power of singing in the full voice. An unconstrained chest, body voice. And also the power that can come from what we hear as dissonance. It catches you and carries a lot and opens things up, for me.

Channing Showalter: One of the groups that we cite a lot for harmonies is this duo Kathy and Carol. They recorded in the 1960s. They’re really great. They were really young when they made an album together. I started listening to that right early on in learning to sing and just felt so inspired by their harmonies. And their creative play with that.

And then we also, when we lived in North Carolina, we helped host the Georgian Men’s Choir, and they taught some workshops and did a concert. It was really inspiring hearing their music. I hadn’t really been exposed to it before that and it was just really powerful polyphonic singing.

AD: The other thing is that you can hear all these older music traditions percolating in your record, but it sounds very fresh and direct and not museum-y at all. How do you keep it from getting too studious?

Annie Scheermer: I want to say our personalities. In the folk music world where we met and started to play music, there felt like there was this strong …at one point I thought it was a divide and now I don’t think of it that way anymore. Still there was the museum orientation where people would say, this I how the song was and is and shall be. There is a place for that. But personally, I’m more interested in how to respect tradition and also create it. And if there weren’t people like with that conservation orientation, I might feel more of a responsibility to preserve. But there are.

AD: And they’re doing that, so you don’t have to.

Annie Scheermer: There are so many people taking a conservative, preservationist approach that it opened a space for us to be like, great, well, let’s keep tradition alive in this other way, by creating new tradition.

AD: Is there a divide? Do you go places where people object to what you’re doing because it’s not exactly the way the folk music was in the 1800s or whatever?

Channing Showalter: I wouldn’t say that. We both play fiddle. This is kind of a different thing than what we’re recording, but we play old-time tunes and Scandinavian tunes and some Celtic music. In those settings, maybe, that’s where we’ve encountered a lot of tradition bearers who are trying to play it like the source recording went.

But also I would say that, I think that we’re pretty careful about performing traditional music. We think it through and we think about what we want to say about the song that we’re singing that’s not ours, and if we don’t feel like we really have much to say about it, we don’t always choose to sing it. Unless it really feels called. And that’s partly why it feels really comfortable to play our own music that’s so inspired by the old songs. But it’s our music.

AD: So this album, for instance, is all originals, but you have one song that was inspired by Annie Briggs?

Annie Scheermer: Yeah, that one is “Let No One Steal Your Thyme” and that one is the only traditional track on the album. We wanted to include a traditional track that we love, partly to acknowledge those roots.

What Channing said is so true about how we’re very careful about how we perform traditional music. But we also don’t want to be like, “We have nothing to do with traditional music. This is purely ours.” We want to really acknowledge the connection and the roots, so including this track felt like that. We learned it from a recording of Anne Briggs that we had with us in North Carolina. I remember listening to it in that cabin and all the autumn leaves. A lot of people have recorded it, too.

We sometimes struggle to feel that we have the right to perform traditional music. Just because the sounds have been carried by people and there’s a complex history of who owns traditional music once it’s recorded. There’s been misappropriation and that history. But this song is in the public domain in a way that felt good to record.

AD: It’s a really pretty song, too.

Annie Scheermer: Yeah, we love it, and we made this harmony to hold the energy of it. We have some good friends that we sing a capella with up here in Bellingham, and we’ve arranged it with them, too, and sung it as a group. It feels good to sing.

AD: I understand you made this album really simply, with minimal tools and equipment. What was the process like?

Channing Showalter: We got a recording program on my computer…first on Annie’s computer, actually. There were two recording sessions a few years apart. We just learned how to use the software as we went. We had our microphone plugged in and the simplest set up. It felt like it gave us the spaciousness to do whatever we wanted and not be working in a time constraint as you would in a studio. The comfort level of having just the two of us in the room felt really nice for this record.

AD: It sounds like you’re right there, when you listen to it.

Annie Scheermer: That’s really what we wanted to share.

Channing Showalter: Yeah, that’s what we were hoping.

AD: Do you have any favorite moments or sounds or lines in this record?

Annie Scheermer: I really felt like “The Bell” was one of those tracks that wasn’t planned at all. Each of us brought songs that we’d worked on. We’d performed some of the songs before and toured with them and were totally ready to record. But some of them were like Channing’s in there messing around on this harmonium that we borrowed from someone who teaches yoga on the island. We were just messing around with things and seeing what comes out. And seeing what comes out of the conversations we’re having in the sessions. “The Bell” really felt like, oh, where did that come from? And it felt like it was coming from a really deep place. Partly because it wasn’t so planned or thought out. It was emerging. That was a really nice surprise and I really love that track.

Queen of Eyes by West of Roan

AD: How about you, Channing?

Channing Showalter: For me, also “The Bell” and also “Gentian.” Those two tracks in the moment, not planned out, and because of that they were able to have a kind of emotion that is a little bit harder to come by when you’ve played a song a bunch of times. So I really felt like they had that rich level to them that felt more special.

AD: Your songs have this mythic quality that make them feel maybe not as personal as some music.Do they reflect things that are going on in your lives and your emotions?

Annie Scheermer: For sure.

Channing Showalter: Yeah, they do.

AD: In metaphor?

Channing Showalter: Yeah, in metaphor, but like….they’re very personal, I would say. They feel in some ways to me—and I’ll just speak for the songs that I wrote—they’re literal, even though they’re metaphorical. Most of the time when they’re being written it’s a particular feeling that’s coming through at that time. Definitely. What about for you, Annie?

Annie Scheermer: Yeah, I agree. Maybe there’s a little bit of hiding? Sometimes I feel like I’m hiding the personal stories in layers. Like I’m going to tell this other story about this other person. I write fiction, and I’ll say, this is not me, but it is, too. You can’t write it unless it’s inside you somewhere. So for me, the work is figuring out is this true? In a way that you can’t even define. Like does it seem like a true thing to be sharing and saying? That’s the bar.

AD: Could you give me a recap of how West of Roan came together? How you met and how you decided to do what you’re doing now?

Channing Showalter: We met in a class, a college class. We took a print-making class and a songwriting class. And in the songwriting class, we already had a little bit of a friendship, so we sought each other out. There was a collaborative element of writing a song together. We did that that year, and it felt alive and fun, and we kept doing it. Each year, for three years, we wrote a song together. But otherwise we didn’t really…We would play some music but it wasn’t really a big part of our friendship.

AD: Which college was this?

Channing Showalter: It was Fairhaven College, which is part of Western Washington University.

Then we just had a moment later. We hadn’t even been in that close touch, but I was living in Tennessee, and Annie was living in Washington, and I remember writing a letter or giving you a phone call, maybe? Like, let’s do something together. I’ve always felt so pulled in by the art that you make. What should we do? It was kind of a leap of faith. And at the end of that Annie moved across the country. It was a really big jump for both of us. And we knew that we wanted to do some kind of visual art aspect of craft in what we were doing, as well as the music. That was already there for us.

AD: Are you both still making visual art, outside of the puppets?

Annie Scheermer: Yes. I teach art at an art school in Bellingham. We both still do printmaking and Channing does a lot of textiles. I wish I had more of them here to show you. Really intricate. I wanted to say, too, that’s something that Channing always brought to the puppetry. Detail and intricacy. Love of craft. That really comes through, and it’s beautiful.

AD: Are you working on anything else that you want to talk about?

Annie Scheermer: Doran has another album coming, too. There’s been a bit of back and forth. We recorded the Doran album while we were finishing this one, and it’s kind of a simultaneous thing. We don’t have a release date for that yet, but it’s recorded.

Channing Showalter: I have a toddler, a girl, so that’s got a lot to do with how I spend my time now. She’s one and a half. So that’s been a part of this whole process. We recorded some of the songs when I was pregnant, and then working on the album felt like a really special thing to do while also parenting.

AD: My son is almost 30. It goes very slow and then it goes really fast. If you could tell people who are not familiar with you one thing, if you could get one thing across, what would it be?

Channing Showalter: I would say, we’ve always wanted to help people feel their emotions. That’s partly what we’re doing with our music, helping ourselves feel different emotions, including grief and sorrow, and consistently reminding ourselves that that’s okay. Not only is it okay but it’s deeply important to process those emotions. So I think that’s partly what we’re hoping to do with our music.
