## Body

Outside. South London, early 1983. The tightly wound scaffolding of This Heat begins to loosen. Not through reinvention, but absorption. On For a Reason, the sole Lifetones full-length from Charles Bullen and Julius Cornelius Samuel, Brixton seeps into the recordings. Reggae drifts through walls. Basslines circulate. Rhythms move patiently.

The transformation announces itself gradually. Where This Heat often coiled inward, For a Reason opens outward. Julius Cornelius Samuel’s bass grounds the record, slowing its pulse and letting repetition unfold at its own pace. Rarely pushing the material forward in any conventional sense, it instead thickens the space beneath the arrangements as dub echo hovers and percussion lingers a fraction longer than expected.

Throughout its six tracks, traces of This Heat’s angularity still surface in the asymmetrical guitar figures of “Distance No Object” and “Good Side,” but the mood has changed completely. The tension remains, but the pressure has eased. Even the sharpest edges no longer press inward.

Released independently in a small pressing in 1983, For a Reason arrived as much of Britain’s experimental music seemed increasingly drawn toward abrasion, fragmentation, and anxious tension. Lifetones moved differently. Forty years later, the record still sounds uncannily present moving through Los Angeles after dark. No matter the geography, psychic or otherwise, that sense of openness remains difficult to separate from the music itself. Even now, its thirty-three minutes continue beyond the walls of Cold Storage.
