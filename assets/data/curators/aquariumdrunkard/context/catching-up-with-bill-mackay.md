## Body

Bill MacKay is one of the unsung heroes of contemporary guitar music, working across folk, bluegrass, jazz, improvisation, blues, rock and post-rock genres with a broad array of collaborators. Last year he released albums with Black Duck—a trio with Doug McCombs and Charles Rumback—and BCMC with Cooper Crain, toured with Nathan Bowles and accompanied any number of independent musicians living in or passing through Chicago. 

This year, he’s got a gorgeous new solo album called Locust Land, his first since 2019. It’s a mix of folky vocal melodies and transcendent instrumental reveries, intricate in execution but sublimely easy to listen to. We caught up in early March to talk about MacKay’s life in guitar; the way that structured, sung songs and open-ended improvisations can say the same things in different ways, and how he hopes his music will land with people. 

“A friend of mine once told me that what he got out of concerts was that the next day, people’s lives are a little bit different and they may go on to do something different as a result,” he said. “I love the idea that people would go out and be like, hey I’m going to put more into that or I’m going to do that thing that I’ve been thinking about forever. I would love for my music to inspire people to be creative and to want to evolve into something.” | j kelly

Bill MacKay: It had various meanings for me. One was a comment on the perilous times we live in, almost in a T.S. Eliot way. It did have a biblical resonance in that sense. I was also interested in how many different meanings that animal has in different cultures.

AD: Really?

Bill MacKay: Yeah, it surprised me. Because I didn’t want to be “too doom.” Not all doom. 

AD: Just moderately doom.

Bill MacKay: Right. Locusts represent a lot different things, including fertility and abundance and creativity. All kinds of things.

AD: Because they have a cycle of death and rebirth and you can’t have one without the other?

Bill MacKay: Yeah, that’s right. It may be as simple as that. Certain people look at one aspect and emphasize it more than the other. 

AD: So my sense is that you’ve been playing guitar for your whole life now. Tell me a little about how you got started and where it led you, musically, up to now?

Bill MacKay: My dad played trumpet. My mom dabbled in music. They were both big fans. That was part of it. But essentially I remember, like a lot of kids, getting into various kinds of rock and roll.

AD: Was this in Chicago or did you come from somewhere else?

Bill MacKay: This was in Rochester, New York. I have two hometowns, Rochester and Pittsburgh. I left Rochester when I was 13. 

I started playing…yeah, you’re right, it’s been most of my life. I can barely remember not playing. I think I was nine when I started. It was really just having the music around and kids in the neighborhood playing. That was part of it, seeing these older kids play with real electric guitars in their basements. Playing along to the Grateful Dead and things like that. But there was lots of different music around, and an older brother helped. 

I got a guitar when I was nine and I ended up taking lessons from the woman who sold to me from Sears or something. Her husband gave lessons. She gave us his card. It all started there. 

I had a few influential teachers in Pittsburgh and in Rochester. One was a guy named Kevin Morse who played classical guitar, a fabulous player and then there were two more jazz-oriented guys in Pittburgh, Eric Susoeff and Joe Negri. None of the lessons were for a super long time, but they were just really influential and I absorbed things really quickly. There’s still things from all those people that I use, that I was able to absorb and extrapolate and extend into a lot of other dimensions. 

AD: But it’s a lifelong project, right, getting better at the guitar? Do you still feel like you’re learning and developing?

Bill MacKay: Yeah, I do. I mean, there’s the guitar or whatever instrument you use, the technique, whatever machinery that you’re grappling with that helps it produce sounds. But beyond the guitar, I think of everything else that makes statements, whether it’s melody or a riff. Compositionally, I guess. What are you saying? What’s the composition? What’s the statement? In that ongoing way. But you’re right, it gets deeper and deeper.

AD: Is there anything in particular that you’re working on now, in terms of the guitar or the music?

Bill MacKay: I’m always working on the follow-up album. They overlap, which is nice. I’ll always be working on that and have some things recorded. In my mind, the way the songs come out, it’s always intuitive. But in my mind, I’ll think of directions for it. It’d be nice to do something more baroque. It would be good to have something more abstract here. Or more like David Bowie here. But whatever, because it just comes out to be you. 

I’m working on that stuff. I’m also trying to find ways of keeping it fresh to explore new pathways melodically, and ways to play solo, ways to play rhythm. So all of that. 

AD: Did these songs come out of a specific period in your life? Do they reflect anything special going on in the background?

Bill MacKay: They mostly came from this last era of the pandemic and the shut-in years. A time when we all felt collectively isolated. So I think part of it was that and trying to get something energetic out of myself, to break through the torpor, break through the malaise. That’s probably why this one has more aggressive guitars than some of them and more distortion. I was finding distortion to be soothing. 

AD: Really?

Bill MacKay: It was an interesting development. It was more soothing than usual given all the anxiety. But the songs have a range, a variety, so I think it was that urge to find some kind of freedom. 

AD: There’s a lot of really beautiful guitar playing on this album, as one would expect, but I understand you were also playing a lot of keyboards. How did that come about?

Bill MacKay: The keyboards, that was interesting. I’ve been fascinated with getting better at keyboards over a long time but not spending a lot of time on it. But I would hear it in songs, and say, oh right. It just kept being a good idea. To have an organ here. And different kinds of organs. There’s almost a cathedral organ in that first song and other organs are almost surf-oriented or R&B. It just kept fitting. I wanted to layer this record in a different way and have layers of guitar all the time. There’s a little bit of that, too. But it got to be really fun because you’ve got so many choices of tonal color.

AD: Did that affect how you wrote the songs or how they came out or how they sound?

Bill MacKay: It affected how they sound and how I think about playing them, although I’m going to be playing them mostly without keyboards. But the ideas that happened from the keyboards are informing how I’m going to perform them. I’d like to find a way to work in some of the keyboard parts, too, but they weren’t written that way, so they don’t really have to have them. They’re not dependent on it.

AD: Isn’t it possible to write a song on keyboard that’s almost impossible to play on guitar?

Bill MacKay: Yeah, I think so. I think that’s maybe true. You can always transpose, but I think it’s going to lose a lot of the flavor. 

AD: Do you have all these keyboards at home? You recorded at home, right, or did you go somewhere?

Bill MacKay: No, for this one, there were only a couple of tracks I recorded at home. For those ones I did. I used my Moog synthesizer. I have an old Ballistic Moog Synthesizer. One of the ones they used to sell at Radio Shack. It was a Moog for the everyday American. That one I have on a couple of tracks, and that’s here. But mostly I used what Cooper Crain had at the studio. He engineered it. 

AD: And he’s got a ton of keyboards and electronics. 

Bill MacKay: We used some of them. We used a piano that was there. We used another organ that was there. We may have used another of his. It was nice. I like doing that, moving from instrument to instrument in the studio, because I think you end up getting a wider variety of sounds. At the same time, I’m happy because I think the songs hang together well. It feels like it’s coherent. 

AD: They do but there’s a lot of difference between them. Some of them are these sort of gorgeous, oblique instrumental pieces, like the opener “Phantasmic Fairy,” and others are more like songs with vocals and verses and choruses. Do you feel that those are two different aspects of what you do, or are they linked somehow?

Bill MacKay: Yeah, I think it’s both, but they’re really linked. I resisted that idea for a long time when I started to sing again and then record vocal songs on the last three albums. This will be the third one. 

I understand why it’s this way, but I’ve always thought it was funny that there was such an emphasis on the voice. There are people who relate to the voice the most out of any instrument. I was never that way. If it moved me, I didn’t care if it was voice or violin. It was all in the same arena. 

But to answer you more directly, they’re linked by that same sensibility. By the composition or the melody. That’s what it all comes back to. So they feel the same and maybe they feel the same because when you’re doing it, when you’re playing keyboards, you’re kind of singing through your fingers. When you play guitar, you can play guitar so vocally that it’s like a saxophone. Singing can be kind of blunt like power chords on a guitar, too, depending on how it’s done. 

AD: Is that a slide that you’re playing on “Phantasmic Fairy.” 

Bill MacKay: I don’t think I played slide on that. I think there’s just some very echoey flurries of notes.

AD: It’s got an eerie sound to it.

Bill MacKay: Yeah, I thought it did, too. And then there’s synthesizer and organ and guitar. I think it was really just the guitar. But there’s something about it that’s just very glissando. The slide on this one is mainly on “Oh Pearl.”

AD: How did you get started playing the slide?

Bill MacKay: Gosh, you know, very slowly and awkwardly. I can’t remember first being aware of it. Maybe from blues records or Jimmy Page. You always wanted to try all these weird things you’d see these guys do. Like the violin bow. Someone would take one from school and see if we could use it on the guitar. 

The slide was interesting because it took so long to find out why it was always sounding wrong, you know. I always had these metal slides, and it very rarely worked. But you kind of kept up with it for years and it would never go anywhere. 

I’d hear it in rock and roll and the blues, and think that’s an interesting sound. It was the water-i-ness and vocal quality that got to me. And then slowly, over a long period of time, I got into the glass slide. I like the sound better. It’s warmer, but it can still cut. And then I actually figured out where it was supposed to go on the fretboard to sing. You want it to sing. To have it resonating.

AD: Just by fooling around and doing it wrong?

Bill MacKay: Pretty much, and then investigating whenever I got it right and why that was happening. It was placement mostly. Once you got the placement down, it became very easy to move around quickly or however you wanted to. It’s still easy to hit wrong notes with it, and again, that’s part of the charm.

AD: You mentioned Cooper Crain. Can you tell me about the people you worked with on this album?

Bill MacKay: Absolutely. Sam Wagster played bass on three tracks. He’s a long-time collaborator of mine. 

AD: Is he a jazz guy? What’s his background?

Bill MacKay: No, I think he’s more rock and roll, punk, country, He played in the Fruit Bats for a while. Jim Dorling’s band Pillowhammer. And then he has his own project called the Father Costume. He’s in a lot of bands. And he’s a multi-instrumentalist. He’s really good on guitar and he plays keys too, but he plays bass on this record. We just always had a great chemistry and he’s a great friend and he has a beautifully solid feel on the bass. He just holds it down and he’s creative, so I was really happy to have him. 

Then, we actually sent the two of the tracks that we did to Michael Patrick Avery who, I think you’re familiar with. He played percussion on a couple of tracks. He’s been a friend for a long time, too, and such a creative person. When we got the tracks back, I couldn’t believe it. He laid down his tracks in Philly, but it sounded like he was right there in the room with us. I don’t know how people do that, but it was great. And then Janet Bean sang on one track.

AD: From Freakwater

Bill MacKay: Yeah, Freakwater and Eleventh Dream Day. She was in Spain at the time. I sent her the tracks and she sent her parts back. She had two vocal parts and she was like, “I don’t know if I gave you anything good.” I put them together, and it was like angels, you know? I’m really excited about her contributions. And that’s it for the musicians. I played all the rest, guitars, vocals, keys. And then Cooper did the majority of the engineering.

AD: Do you have any lines or bits or moments on this album that really stand out to you?

Bill MacKay: Oh, I think there are. There are a couple of things. The mood of “Locust Land,” the way the guitars overlap, there’s something Eastern about it but also celebratory. The emotional feel of that, it feels burnished, it feels golden. That seemed like, oh great, we hit the mark of what I was feeling. 

I also think “Glowdrift” was like that. It was very much like the demo, a surfy instrumental, and it just had that feeling, you know, where you’re riding to the see someone. That whole vibe was so uplifting. 

Maybe the last one that I would mention was “Keeping in Time” because that was one of those that just pours out, the melody and everything came really quickly. So it seemed like the demo, I was trying to capture a better version of that. And that’s what happened. It’s a simpler instrumentation on the record, but it captured the feeling. But I will say, on that one, I must have written 30 or 40 versions of that brief chorus. I’d never done that before, but it wasn’t quite right. Just keep sculpting, you know?

AD: What can you tell me about the art?

Bill MacKay: Well, I was struggling to find some art that was right. There are certainly many people who are more accomplished visual artists than me. But it was all about finding art that matched the feeling, and I was having trouble doing that. So I started to do a lot of drawing. I had sort of a deadline, too. There wasn’t much time left when we chose it, and I showed it to a few people also because I was feeling like it might work. And they liked it. They said, it has your feeling. It has your vibe. And the colors. And also I wanted it to be different from the previous records, to have a different color-scape. 

AD: It’s very bright.

Bill MacKay: Yeah, and it’s got a different background, so it stands out from the others. I also wanted to have a version of what Locust Land could be. Although in my mind that was varied. It could be the name of a theme park or a bar, you know, or lots of things. I like the openness of poetry and language in that way. It allows your imagination to work. Things can go lots of ways. But I wanted to have a pictorial of that. It appears to me like it’s possibly a little city. Or a region. 

AD: It’s been a while since you made a solo album, but I know you’ve been pretty busy in the interim, so I wanted to just check in on some of your other projects. I just saw that Black Duck is doing Pitchfork this year.

Bill MacKay: That’s right! 

AD: Was the record from last year a one-off or are you going to do more with them?

Bill MacKay: I think we’re going to do more. We actually been playing together for a while. I want to say it’s been happening for about six years with these sort of sporadic performances every year. And then last year we made the debut LP. We’re going to do more definitely. We just found out last week that we were going to be in Pitchfork. That’s a big one. We’re really psyched. 

AD: Have you played Pitchfork before?

Bill MacKay: I haven’t played it. I’ve been an attendee and I’ve worked at the festival, so it’ll be exciting. After going to it since Mike Reed started the Intonation Festival, it’s really exciting to play it.

AD: Good, well, I hope you get good weather.

Bill MacKay: I hope so, too. It’s one of those where it if rains…

AD: I’ve seen some really dreadful pictures of when it rains.

Bill MacKay: And there’s no make-up day. You hope for the best.

AD: You also had that really nice BCMC album with Cooper last year. How did that project start?

Bill MacKay: That one came about really casually. It must be three or four years now. 

I live close to Cooper Crain. We live in Pilsen about three or four blocks apart. We had talked about playing. I don’t even know what the impetus was. Just friends who hadn’t played together much. So we went over to his place, and he just started to record stuff. And, you know, Cooper will just have stuff in his living room that sounds really great. He’ll set it up. He’s got things that record very well. It just started like that. We were adding tracks, and then we did go into the studio. But he was reminding me the other day that we actually kept most of the stuff we did at his apartment. Three of the four tracks were from those sessions. 

So it was a little bit incremental and slow developing. We just did this tour, I don’t know if you saw, in February. And I’m really excited about that, too. We’ve already got plans to work on a second record. I thought the playing was really good. We were just firing on all cylinders and really playing well together. 

AD: Do you have any other side projects or collaborations that you want to talk about?

Bill MacKay: I’ve been playing with Michael Zerang, the percussionist. We’ve played a handful of shows. I really like it because it’s in such a different zone. 

AD: Electric or acoustic?

Bill MacKay: Acoustic, and it’s not like the folk or bluegrass acoustic stuff I was doing with Nathan Bowles on acoustic. It’s a lot more in this Iberian Peninsula, Spanish, Morocco kind of zone. Some very eastern sounds and drawing music from that area. It sounded really good, and we’re excited to go forward, but spring and summer got very dense very quick, so it’s all about finding the space for everything. 

AD: It seems like Chicago is the kind of place where you have all kinds of opportunities like that, to play with various people. What can you tell me about living there, as a musician?

Bill MacKay: It is like that. That’s what’s continually interesting to me. It is a place where people mix in fairly easy ways and have the inclination to do that. I’m sure it’s aided by things like for such an epic city, it’s still fairly easy to traverse it. And also rents, compared to New York or something…

AD: Yeah, I think the affordability is important. People have to work so hard to make art in New York. 

Bill MacKay: Yeah, right, but for whatever the reasons are, there is that spirit and I really appreciate it. People cross-pollinate a lot. They’re not so hemmed in by their genre, whatever it is. That’s been just great. When I got here I was playing a lot of jazz and improvised music. I was meeting those kinds of people and I could improvise, so I was doing that and forming groups out of that. And they’d be playing tunes that could have been written for a rock group. It was just with different instrumentation. Still the people that I’m meeting now, there is an open spirit. People are trying things. Chicago always seems like it’s simmering to me. 

AD: I thought I saw on Instagram that you were doing some work with theaters, maybe a puppet theater?

Bill MacKay: Oh yeah, that’s right. That was a show with Theater Y called “Little Carl.” Theatre Y has been around for a long time. A woman, Melissa Loren, leads it and is the director. It was a really beautiful, really positive show about gun violence. Allegorical, full of symbology. There were a couple of adults in the show. Marvin Tate was in the show. But also a lot of kids. 

AD: He’s great.

Bill MacKay: He’s awesome. He was great in the show, and the other adults were great, and the kids were great and none of them were coming from a trained actor place. They would give very emotional, stirring performances. The pieces were lovely and powerful. There’s a guy named Fred Jackson in town, a great saxophonist and improvisor and he plays keys too. Between the two of us, we were the orchestra, and we were playing the different dances and the interstitial music.

AD: Is that something you’d like to do more of?

Bill MacKay: I would. You working on a play? 

AD: I am not.

Bill MacKay: I would like to do more theater. That has been an ongoing thing over time. So if anybody’s out there, I’m interested. And film, too. I haven’t done a ton of music for film, but I’ve done some short pieces. 

AD: If you could get one thing across about your work and your new album, what would that be? What do you think is the most important thing?

Bill MacKay: The most important thing to me is that …I do think that I would like the record to be something that encourages, that offers some kind of lift soul-wise, spirit-wise, energy-wise. I’d like it to break whatever kind of ice people might be feeling because of the times that they’re in. Because these are stressful times in a lot of ways. 

A friend of mine once told me that what he got out of concerts was that the next day, people’s lives are a little bit different and they may go on to do something different as a result. I love the idea that people would go out and be like, hey I’m going to put more into that or I’m going to do that thing that I’ve been thinking about forever. I would love for my music to inspire people to be creative and to evolve into something.
