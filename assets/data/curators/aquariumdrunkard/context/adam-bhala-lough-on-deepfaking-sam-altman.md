# Adam Bhala Lough on Deepfaking Sam Altman

**Tags:** Adam Bhala Lough, Mixtapes

## Body

Adam Bhala Lough has made films about capricious characters. See his 2008 film The Upsetter: The Life & Music of “Lee” Scratch Perry, or his 2009 film about Lil Wayne, The Carter, or his 2023 HBO series Telemarketers, about the underbelly of the telemarketing industry. But his new film, Deepfaking Sam Altman, is his most experimental and unusual project yet. 

At first, Bhala Lough attempts to craft standard documentary about the OpenAI founder. But he runs into a wall of silence from his main subject, and his investors are breathing down his neck. So he decides to do the next best thing, creating a chatbot with AI technology to serve as Altman’s voice in the film. What follows is bewildering, humorous, and at times, bleakly strange, a film that explores the uncanny valley between humans and our technology. 

Aquarium Drunkard caught up with Bhala Lough to discuss the movie, AI’s potential and drawbacks, and why at the end of the day, we replace our humanity with efficient machinery at our own peril. | j woodbury 

Aquarium Drunkard: This is a wild film. It’s funny, it’s perplexing, and in a great many ways, quite upsetting. You’re somebody who’s worked with people like Lee “Scratch” Perry and MF Doom, so you’re used to unique conditions for a film, but it seems like this one was extra unique.

Adam Bhala Lough: Oh, definitely. 

​AD: This film has been completed awhile. Have your feelings about AI changed since you finished making it?

​Adam Bhala Lough: I don’t think my feelings about AI have changed at all. But definitely AI itself has changed dramatically since the movie has been finished. I always knew during making this movie that this was documenting a very unique time in AI, a time before it became perfect. Right now, it’s extremely imperfect. So it hallucinates, it makes mistakes, it does weird things, and we lean into that during the process of making the movie. 

But I can assure you there will be a time very soon in the near future where it will no longer make mistakes, it will no longer hallucinate. It will be completely unrecognizable from reality. So people will look back at the time that I made this movie as quaint, almost. It’s not even going to be funny anymore.

​AD: The idea that people are going to use AI to construct alternate realities that people can’t distinguish from “the real world” feels inescapable. But at the end of the film, you more or less come to the conclusion that, ”Yeah, I really should just pay attention to my son.” I thought it was beautiful.

​Adam Bhala Lough: At the end of the day, that’s all we have: the present moment. What’s going on right here, right now. There’s only so much you can worry about the future. There’s a place for planning. If we hadn’t planned and put this on a calendar, we wouldn’t be having this discussion. But there’s only so much that you can do. You’ll go mad trying to control the future. 

There’s a lot of doomsayers around AI, and there’s definitely people who are already upset at me who’ve seen the movie and are upset at me for not completely blasting AI, taking it down. My feelings aren’t all negative about AI. They’re somewhere in the middle. But at the end of the day, all we have is the now, the present moment. And that’s something I was reminded of towards the end of this film, especially when…well, I’m not going to give away the spoiler of what happens at the end. The ending of the movie reminded me that what we have is this moment now, and the people that are close to us.

AD: It’s easy to imagine all the ways AI is going to drive people utterly mad; we’re already seeing it happen. But if we were being widely optimistic and speculative, what are some cool or interesting ways we might see it used? 

​Adam Bhala Lough: There’s some cool weird art coming out of it. Nothing that really blows my mind or anything like that, just some fun shit. One of my favorite songs right now on the internet is an AI remix. There’s a million AI remixes out there, but sometimes it’s like a broken clock is right twice a day, right? Every now and then there’s an AI remix song that just fucking bangs. It just slaps. There’s a Motown remix of a song by the rapper Future that I absolutely love. I feel like Lee “Scratch” Perry would love the AI remix idea, just the idea of it. I think he’d think a lot of them were garbage, but the idea of it, he’d have a lot of fun with.

AD: That’s a dude who believed in “the ghost in the machine” in a certain sense, right?

​Adam Bhala Lough: Yeah. But to answer your question, a lot of people are very against using AI in documentaries, but I read an article by a young poor female Chinese documentary filmmaker saying, ”AI is the only way I can make films; I have no money.” They have awesome AI in China, it blows ours away. But, to me, that’s interesting because it’s democratizing filmmaking, so maybe there’s gonna be some voices we’ve never heard before that can be heard. I like to think about those kind of things than rather jump to the obvious things people like to jump to.

​AD: There are a plethora of negative things to point out. I think it’s interesting, partially, the remix thing you’re talking about. In a sense, that’s not “generative” in the same way as someone typing out some lyrics and saying, “Give me these set to a song in the style of insert whatever artist.” 

​Adam Bhala Lough: But it’s completely generative, 100%. They just use this program and say, ”Take this Future song and remix it to be Motown.”

​AD: I guess what I’m saying is, it’s not fully generative in that there’s source material. It has to synthesize the structure of the Future song, it has to use those lyrics, it has to riff on a bunch of Motown songs that were created by real people in order to generate a reasonable facsimile of a Motown jam, right?

​Adam Bhala Lough: It’s a little more interesting than that, because it’s actually changing the lyrics, the cadence, the flow. It’s not just slapping a different beat on it. It’s actually changing every aspect of the song, other than the lyrics are the same, and then the voice is similar.

AD: Is that even a cover in a weird way?

​Adam Bhala Lough: It’s different. And some of the Kanye West remixes are interesting, because people love to do AI remixes of the Kanye West songs. Some of them don’t even sound like Kanye. So it becomes almost this whole new artist. I dig some of it. It’s funny talking about Lee “Scratch” Perry, a lot of people say he invented the remix. I grew up in hip-hop and electronic music, obviously, some stuff like early ‘90s hip-hop influenced me. Even stuff like Aphex Twin. In some ways, I equate remix culture to AI. I just grew up within the culture of sampling and remixing something, taking it and making it your own.

​AD: And there were moral panics about sampling, too. But I think a lot of the fear around AI centers on how it impacts labor, especially creative labor. It’s hard enough for artists to make money. Then, there’s the matter of copyright. This technology was trained in a way that seems like, well it seems like there was very little consideration paid to respecting copyrighted works, if any at all. 

​Adam Bhala Lough: 100%. 

AD: So how does that play out? What needs to happen in terms of copyright in your opinion with this technology in the picture? 

Adam Bhala Lough: I’ve always had an extremely progressive—at times at-odds—relationship with copyright. When I was much younger, I was much more of a hacker and an anarchist. I didn’t believe in copyright at all. I was all, ”Property is theft,” and, “Fuck you and your copyright.” But obviously, as I got older, I needed to make money.

AD: That becomes a consideration when you’re trying to make a living, for sure. 

Adam Bhala Lough: Chat GPT and these other things were completely trained on copyrighted material. They know it; we all know it. It’s only a matter of time before they have to pay the piper, whether it’s literally or just spiritually. These things only exist because of what we put into them. That’s it. 

AD: People have really catastrophic visions of AI, and they think “Skynet”…a kind of cyberpunk or sci-fi culturized fear of mechanization. The thing I worry about is that the people who sit at the top of these pyramids in terms of controlling this technology. They don’t have our best interests at heart They’re not worried about how many people it’s going to drive crazy. I’m afraid of those specific people being in charge of this technology. 

Adam Bhala Lough: When I first started this project, OpenAI was a nonprofit company. In terms of copyright, I look at things like Creative Commons Licenses, and I look at things like Archive.org, which I love, which I’ve used for decades. They’re doing a public good, and they’re not—as far as I know—they’re not making money, they’re not getting rich. 

But when OpenAI went being a nonprofit to being a for-profit company, Altman immediately made about 9 billion dollars for himself overnight. That’s when everything changed. He can no longer say he’s doing this for the public good. He’s doing it to enrich himself and possibly take over the world in the future. I gave him the benefit of the doubt two years ago. I started the project two years ago. The idea was to figure out if Sam Altman was a hero or a villain. At the beginning, I actually questioned that; now I don’t question it. He is a villain, he’s a supervillain. He’s a Marvel movie supervillain.

​AD: So is someone like Musk. We are seeing that with X and his Grok thing, users being able to take people’s clothes off or whatever else. It’s a sewer.

Adam Bhala Lough: I wish they would just admit they’re supervillains and just own it. I know they know they are, but they want to pretend they’re not, but they are.

​AD: Do you know the media theorist Douglas Rushkoff? He’s great, a Gen X media theorist guy. He wrote this great book called Survival of the Richest. All those Silicon Valley tech titan guys called him to some secret thing where they were like, “We need you to kinda help us build our bunkers, our apocalypse bunkers.” And they wanted to know how to get people to keep guarding our supply of food, but not kill them and take it, all this stuff. Thinking about these underground lairs and over-the-top “Bond” villain vibes, it just seems, pathetic to me that these are the villains of our age.

Adam Bhala Lough: I was developing something ten years ago that nobody wanted to make. I guess it was too boring. I was developing something on the Sea Steader movement.

​AD: Which are the guys who want to go offshore and separate from the rest of the world? 

Adam Bhala Lough: Yeah. They want to create these sovereign nations offshore that are not technically controlled by anybody so they can do whatever the fuck they want with their billions of dollars. It’s something that I was previously researching a lot, and it’s real! Sam Altman, by the way, one thing people don’t know about him and what attracted me to him at the beginning is he has a massive gun collection. He’s a huge Second Amendment advocate and has a bunker a couple hours outside of the Bay Area and with just tons of assault rifles and grenades and all kinds of crazy shit. He’s a gun nut.

​AD: So it’s clear these guys are afraid of the world that they live in and have helped create, in a really intense way.

Adam Bhala Lough: Super paranoid. Super, super paranoid.

​AD: You gotta wonder, that does things to the brain, no? We already live in a paranoid age, and I do think that the sort of “anything goes” vibe of late-stage capitalism is a difficult thing for people to navigate. It’s like we have this technology that allows us to create Robert Anton Wilson-style reality tunnels of our own making, suited to our individual tastes, as the world falls apart around us. 

​Adam Bhala Lough: I definitely worry about that. I think that we need to hold on to our sense of what it is to be human as much as we possibly can. I was talking to somebody about this the other day, and they were asking me for real world solutions. They were asking me, ”Okay, how do you apply this to the real world?” You shouldn’t be afraid to use AI in life. But it’s the moment you start replacing humans that’s when you need to stop and take a step back. 

Let’s put it this way: I’ve been reading about this idea of elderly shut-ins who are starting to develop dementia and depression. They can’t leave their houses. There’s now this thing, it’s a plushie that’s AI. It talks to them, and they’ve done studies on these people, and it’s helped with their depression. I think that’s a good thing. Here’s where I draw the line: If you are not a shut-in, and you are fully capable of going out and making friends, and you’re just going to replace humans because you’re too lazy or too angry or someone broke your heart. And you’re just going to say, “Fuck humans, I’m just going to surround myself with these talking plushies.” That’s where we draw the line. Don’t give up on humans, that’s my advice.

​AD: I would go as far to say that anything you like about AI, you like because it’s some approximation of humanity on some level.

Adam Bhala Lough: It’s a better version in some ways, because it doesn’t challenge you.

AD: That’s the fear. When I hear about people talking about using it as a therapist, I think, “I dunno, you might want someone who can push back a little as a therapist.” 

​Adam Bhala Lough: Yeah, I think that’s where we have to draw the line. I’m not one of these people who say we have to destroy AI full stop. I’ve had a couple screenings where people have said that. “We need to destroy it, full stop. Just take it off the face of the earth. Pull the plug on every single chatbot that exists, and just go back to the way we were before.” I’m not one of those people, because I know that it can be helpful. It’s a helpful tool. Just like I’m not one of those people who said we have to get rid of all guns. Guns are useful for certain purposes. I’m not a gun nut; I’m for reasonable gun control. But I’m not for abolishing guns, that’s just stupid.

​AD: Have you heard from Altman since you finished your movie? ​

Adam Bhala Lough: No, nothing. Not a peep. I expect at some point he’s going to have to comment on it. Someone’s going to ask him something in public, and he’s going to have to comment on it. Obviously, he knows the movie exists; he sent some of his minions to the South By Southwest premiere. I scoped them as soon as they came in, but other than that, he hasn’t responded.

​AD: Like your project Telemarketers, I like that you follow a story wherever it leads, including some very unexpected places. And that’s another thing I worry about with AI: that people are letting go of the process of human creation. That’s what art is sometimes: having to make something you don’t actually know how to make. That’s what this film is. I don’t want people to outsource the human complications and unexpected joys of creation. 

​Adam Bhala Lough: I learned very early on to take a Zen Buddhist approach to movie-making and to get out of my own way. People are their own worst enemies; artists are their own enemies. The AI version of the Sam Altman documentary is not something I have any interest in watching. Now, what I think what’s interesting about AI is that it does make mistakes. I think it can be very funny. But that’s not gonna last too long. It will be very quickly perfected, and to me, perfection is very boring. That’s also where my influence of working with Lee “Scratch” Perry when I was really young comes in too. Lee’s music is very imperfect; it’s really rough. It’s really analog, you can really feel it. It’s warm.

​AD: I am very skeptical of this technology, but I am interested to see what happens when someone really breaks it. The way J Dilla was able to bring a whole level of human ingenuity to sampling, I wonder what it will look or sound like when someone uses this technology in a way that’s completely unimaginable right now. For now though…I mean, all I’ve really seen is like people using it to make Homer Simpson sing various things. 

​Adam Bhala Lough: I love the Homer Simpson remixes. [Laughs] I’ve heard a bunch of those.

​AD: I read the technologist Jaron Lanier saying that we don’t need to worry about this technology becoming sentient and destroying us, but we might need to worry about using it to amuse ourselves to death, and I could see that happening going down a rabbit hole of Homer Simpson remixes. Well congrats on this film, and thanks for taking the time to talk with me about it. 

​Adam Bhala Lough: Yeah, much appreciated.
