## Body

Avant-pop pioneer Keigo Oyamada (Cornelius) has been on a creative stride in recent years. Following a recent six year hiatus in studio albums, last year saw a return to form with the lush, melodic beauty of 夢中夢 (Dream in Dream), while upcoming record Ethereal Essence is an ambitious reworking of twelve previously released ambient tracks, the (mostly instrumental) songs all possessing a self-proclaimed “ethereal” quality. Channeling his own influential ambient touchstones like Steve Hiett, Hiroshi Yoshimura and Raymond Scott, it’s a collection of richly woven soundscapes, an early precursor perhaps being the lush, abstract textures of 2001’s Point. 

With a prolific career now spanning three decades, Cornelius has been a trailblazer in a variety of genre-defying avenues. From his early days in pop outfit Flipper’s Guitar to a solo auteur in the canonical Japanese movement dubbed Shibuya-kei (seminal 1997 record Fantasma being released on Matador). Often portraying a keen syncopation of sound and visuals, Oyamada has also worked as a restless composer of many cinematic projects for screens both big and small. In recent years, the musician took part in ambitious projects like Ambient Kyoto, a collaboration with Ryuichi Sakamoto (as a tribute, the Cornelius remodeling of the late composer’s “Thatness and Thereness” is included on Ethereal Essence). 

From his home in Tokyo, Oyamada joined us for a conversation via Zoom. We discuss the ethereal qualities that make up his current material, his longtime admiration for The Durutti Column, looking back on breakthrough album Fantasma, the Kraftwerk-inspired visual components that augment his live performances, and much more. Special thanks to Yosuke Kitazawa for being the interpreter for this discussion. | m neeley

Cornelius: So this new album wasn’t really made as an album. I had a whole bunch of tracks that I had recorded, starting from maybe around the year 2000 or so, until now. So I chose some tracks from that archive, these archival recordings and then chose ones that were kind of ambient-like and I reworked some of the tracks. And that’s how it turned into the shape of this new album.

AD: Right – you said that all of the tracks you chose have an “ethereal” quality to them. In your own words, what makes a song ethereal?

Cornelius: Ethereal … meaning it doesn’t have much of a structure, but there’s a space and there’s an airiness to it. And for this album Ethereal Essence, the title was chosen in part by asking AI what the title should be.

AD: Was it difficult to narrow it down to just twelve songs?

Cornelius: I had way more songs to choose from, but then I wanted to turn it into an album so I wanted it to be an album length.

AD: One song, “Sketch For Spring”, is a beautiful track and homage to guitarist Vini Reilly. How long have you listened to The Durutti Column?

Cornelius: I’ve been a fan since I was in high school … back in ’83-’84. A long time.

AD: Another influence of yours is electronic pioneer Raymond Scott. I’ve read how early on, you were inspired by television and animation soundtracks – do you remember hearing Scott’s music in those?

Cornelius: I don’t remember hearing Raymond Scott specifically, but I was influenced hugely by watching television, especially with the music they used in children’s programs back then. They had a lot of musicians doing experimental stuff on the kid’s shows. And myself, I produced a TV show for children called Design Ah! And that was influenced by those programs back then. Music from anime was a big influence, and music from like special effects and monster and superhero type shows. A lot of those types of programs would use synthesizers in a really interesting way, so that left a big impression on me. 

AD: Speaking of which, I love the Cornelius performance on Yo Gabba Gabba.

Cornelius: [Laughs] I’m really happy to hear that. That was also a really interesting kid’s program, with people like Mark Mothersbaugh and Money Mark providing music.

AD: The visual components to your concerts have always interested me. They seem uniquely synonymous with the performance itself. Where did that come from?

Cornelius: Sixties liquid light shows are a part of the influence for my visuals, but the biggest inspiration is actually Kraftwerk. My current band has four musicians lined up on the stage with the visuals behind them, and that took a lot of inspiration from Kraftwerk. 

AD: Similarly, one of the most unique record inserts I’ve seen was with the Mellow Waves album, a phenakistoscope disc that displays animation when you spin it on a turntable. 

Cornelius: The guy who made it, Drew Tetz, lives in America. He brought up the idea to me and I liked it so we included it with that album, and something like that will be included with this new album, too. This guy specializes in this sort of thing. He’s done one where it almost imitates 3D animation, where you put the disc in the middle of the turntable and you see it being animated almost in a 3D way.

AD: You’re touring in America and the UK in the fall … I wanted to ask about Fantasma. Do you feel like you created a timeless record that still really resonates with people?

Cornelius: We’re going to play a few songs from Fantasma. That album was the first one that introduced me to an audience outside of Japan. It played a big part in that and is still a huge thing in my discography. 

AD: On the Fantasma remixes disc, there’s some really great artists. Did you meet artists like The Pastels and Sean O’Hagan back during Flipper’s Guitar, or was it through that album specifically?

Cornelius: With Sean O’Hagan, I was a big fan of his band before the High Llamas, called Microdisney. I have been a fan of theirs since before I was in Flipper’s Guitar. So I asked Sean O’Hagan if he would be a part of the remixes album. And with the Pastels, they actually asked me if I could remix one of their songs, so it was like an exchange where they would be a part of my remix album.

AD: Is that where “The Micro Disneycal World Tour” came from?

Cornelius: Yes.

AD: It took me way too long to put that reference together.

Cornelius: [Laughs] Only otakus would understand that.

AD: You also did a song for the recently announced covers compilation of Haruomi Hosono’s Hosono House. What are your earliest memories of that record?

Cornelius: I actually didn’t hear Hosono House until later into my adulthood. I was about in my mid-twenties, maybe 24 or 25, so that would be around 1995 or so.
