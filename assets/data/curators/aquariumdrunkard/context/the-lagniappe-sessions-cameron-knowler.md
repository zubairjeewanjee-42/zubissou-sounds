## Body

Lagniappe (la ·gniappe) noun ‘lan-ˌyap,’ — 1. An extra or unexpected gift or benefit. 2. Something given or obtained as a gratuity or bonus.

Cameron Knowler is one of the latest young guns to distinguish himself in the ever evolving world of guitar soli, most readily apparent via his 2025 long-player, CRK, released earlier this year by the eveready Worried Songs. As we noted in our review, Knowler is indebted to his instrument’s history; his playing steadfast, concise, and open to the possibility of the unexpected. For this installment of the Lagniappe Sessions Knowler pays tribute to genre godhead John Fahey, Norman Blake, David Nape and Elizabeth Cotton. Knowler on his selections, below.

Cameron Knowler :: Sunflower River Blues (John Fahey)

John Fahey’s “Sunflower River Blues” is one of the most influential and immovable chestnuts in the tradition of American solo acoustic guitar playing. Because of this, I resisted playing the song for many years until I finally caved. I’d secretly play it on the couch, then on a gig, maybe for a friend. A couple years go by, and I get offered to do a Lagniappe Session. Perhaps masochistically, my first thought was “Sunflower.” To steer it in a new direction, I asked my friend Matt Combs to do the string arrangement, and he kindly agreed. He calls the end result “musical Valium” which is a description I quite like.

Cameron Knowler :: Shallegra / Steamboat Around the Bend (Norman Blake)

Being a longtime (and now recovering) Norman Blake devotee, I’ve listened to all his records and tunes ardently. However, there was always one cut, “Shallegra,” that I never clicked with. Then one day my friend “Wrangler” Joe said that it was his favorite Norman original tune. As a sort of practical joke with myself, I learned it—and lo and behold, it’s one of my favorites now. “Steamboat Around the Bend,” the second tune in the medley, comes from the playing of James Bryan, one of Norman’s longest standing collaborators (from his record Lookout Blues). I asked my friends Brittany Haas, Jordan Tice, as well Matt and Rachel Combs (James’ daughter) to sit down and play it with me live in front of some mics in their home studio, and this is the result of that collaboration.

Cameron Knowler :: The Gardenia Flower (David Nape)

Originally composed by Hawaiian composer, David Nape, “Pua Sadinia” is a vocal song from the 78 RPM era, and was first recorded in October 1923 by Prince Lei Lani. I first became aware of the song from a recording by Raymond Kane on his This Is Slack Key record, who plays it instrumentally. It is widely known that so much of pedal steel and flattop acoustic guitar playing was borrowed—and oftentimes lifted—by the early Hawaiian recording stars… with that said, I rarely see folks acknowledge this while putting the material in conversation with other traditions that borrow from it. That’s what I’ve attempted here.

Cameron Knowler :: Wilson Rag (Elizabeth Cotten)

As far as anyone knows, Elizabeth Cotton composed “Wilson Rag” and recorded it a few times on various projects. Though her recordings include a third part which changes slightly from performance to performance, I decided to focus on the first two parts, adding a bit of reharmonization to make the tune sing with my buddy Will Ellis’ pedal steel playing. I’m the one playing the ratty Lyon & Healy kick drum from the nineteen teens or twenties, and yes, it was played live with an early-1900s Antonio Grauso acoustic guitar. This tune sure feels great under the fingers.
