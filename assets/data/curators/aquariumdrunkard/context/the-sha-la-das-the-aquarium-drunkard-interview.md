## Body

Sometimes a photograph can transport you back in time. On the cover of Your Picture, the second album from Staten Island soul combo The Sha La Das, appears a picture of the family matriarch, Linda. It was the same case with 2018’s Love in the Wind. The photos were taken by the 79-year-old Bill Schalda, who fronts The Sha La Das with his sons, Paul, Will and Carmine. Though aged by time, these images present a different world, but one that Schalda says feels as real and immediate to him as the present.

“You can see a picture and immediately it’ll take you back sixty years,” Schalda says. “All of a sudden, these ideas flow into your head of times gone by or sounds gone by. That certainly can stimulate the creative juices.” 

Produced by Tommy “TNT” Brenneck, the sounds of The Sha La Das are similarly transportive. Though rooted in doo wop, Your Picture finds the group expanding into a psychedelicized soul swirl. Still, it’s aching, heart-on-the-sleeve soul that lies at the core of the group’s style—the kind of singing Bill has been doing his whole life. In fact, it’s the kind of singing that brought Linda and Bill together, as he sang “Try a Little Tenderness” to her in a little club five decades back:

“We saw each other before that in the venue, but she didn’t know I was one of the performers…I just was some weird guy in a dracula cape, but that was alright with her. It worked! She still here, man, over fifty years later.”

Schalda joins us to discuss photography, his work with the late Charles Bradley, the sounds of family and more. | j woodbury 

Aquarium Drunkard: Bill, I really have enjoyed spending time with this album.

Bill Schalda: Did you? I’m glad to hear it. It’s really nice to hear that. Because to some extent, you kinda do this stuff for yourself and you just hope everyone else likes it.

AD: Especially when you’ve been doing it as long as you have. I imagine that you’ve had music come out and connect over the years, and stuff that people haven’t heard as much. But to some degree, you music because you simply have to.

Bill Schalda: It’s like that old John Lee Hooker line, ”You got to let that boy boogie-woogie, cause it’s in him and it’s got to come out.” Once music is in you, it has to come out. I was sixteen years old when my first record was released. It started to get some traction, but it was in the middle of the British Invasion. So the sound was completely changing. So you stay with it, you do what you can, but ultimately, you start to say,” Well, I better do something else for a while to support my kids,” and then come back to it later on when you can. But it does not go away; it’s in there and it doesn’t go away. 

AD: Your Picture is, despite its doo-wop roots, a pretty psychedelic record. Producer Thomas Brenneck brought some of that to “Magic Mary” and “Stop Using My Love.” “Gonna Catch You” is far out. How does it feel to have them integrated into your own musical project?

Bill Schalda: Well, it’s great. As a group, a lot of people that write about music or comment about music feel as though they’re required to assign a genre to it. I was out there in this era where we were all singing on street corners in three part or four part or five part harmony. But it wasn’t until maybe 1962 or so that people started calling that doo-wop. Because people had to say, “Oh, here’s the box that this is in.” 

But what we’ve been doing ourselves as individuals and collectively as musicians or anything else is you draw from the gene pool of all the music that’s been out there. I was exposed to The Ink Spots in the ’40s and ’50s. Then ’60s psychedelia, the blues revival, all these guys with soul. It’s kind of like when you’re sitting at a family gathering. You look around and say, ”Hey? Where’d this guy get that nose from?” And someone will say, ”Oh, grandma’s brother had a nose like that.” Psychedelia pops into this simply because it’s part of our musical genealogy as a family.

AD: What helps to bring it all into cohesion is the fact that this is a family project. When people who share DNA sing together, there tends to be a pretty special thing that happens. 

Bill Schalda: Absolutely. I wonder how much of it has to do with the similarity of the sound, or if it’s simply just as a family you learn to work together. I got my three sons; I don’t know how my wife happened to produce these guys with these voices. They’re three completely different sounding voices, but when they come together they really produce something special. 

AD: Did you play music for the boys growing up? 

Bill Schalda: Yeah! Linda and I just had a collection of the records. They certainly heard those. I had one guitar in the house and I sang them to sleep every night. They just grew up always hearing music, and always going in their own direction with it. Will is into this lo-fi stuff. Paul is into organic-sounding, folk and country sounds, among a lot of other things. Carmine is very much into hip-hop and rap. You bring those three totally different things together in a three-part harmony and it just creates this whole other sound.  

AD: And then there’s a cross generational thing, where they brought you into their music.

Bill Schalda: The greatest example of them bringing me into [their] music was when Will and Paul were part of the traveling ensemble of The Extraordinaires with Charles Bradley. At one point—and this is how we really started singing as The Sha La Das—they said, ”Hey, why don’t you guys come up to the studio, because Charles wants some backing vocals on ‘Victim of Love.'” I was 60 or 65 when it happened, and it was very interesting. It was just, ”Hey, we’re doing this, why don’t you come on along, dad?”

AD: Were you nervous to go into the studio?

Bill Schalda: No, it’s something I’ve done all my life. And Tommy’s the greatest producer in the world, he’s a remarkably great guy to work with. 

AD: I met Charles Bradley once at a record store. Something special radiated off him. But he was a very, very quiet guy. I got the sense he was very shy. 

Bill Schalda: Very much so, but a beautiful person. He had a heart of gold. Just the most wonderful, pleasant guy you’d ever want to be with. Very encouraging, very generous to allow us to be on the record in the first place. After that, we got very close. He’d come over for holidays now and then.

AD: When I think of the Daptone label, I think of Charles and Sharon Jones, both very sad losses for the world of music. But they were people for whom music was part of their life for their whole life. And like you, they had to make livings in other ways, but because of the Daptone crew, they were able to return to music later in life and experience a new audience finding their music. And that’s the case for you too. 

Bill Schalda: Exactly, and even though it’s now spun off and now it’s Diamond West now, it’s still Tommy, who was with Daptone at the time. We’ve known him since he was a teenager, along with the whole Budos Band, all those guys. Tommy’s really found his niche.

AD: It’s clear listening to this record he knows what he’s doing! 

Bill Schalda: The Beatles had George Martin, we got Tommy Brenneck.

AD: Something interesting about this record, and I I think it carries over from that first one, is the visual palette of these vintage photos is really something. The photo of Linda brings you into the world of this record. 

Bill Schalda: It is definitely a different world, from a different time. I think I took the pictures with a “brownie,” one of those little Kodak cameras. I see those photos, and I hear Sly in the Family Stone right back, it’s 1969, and Linda and I are in the Apollo Theatre listening to Sly in the Family Stone. The opening act was Red Fox, before he even had a television show. He was still just a standup comedian. It just immediately takes you back to that moment. If I hear Sly, I’m right there again, every time.
