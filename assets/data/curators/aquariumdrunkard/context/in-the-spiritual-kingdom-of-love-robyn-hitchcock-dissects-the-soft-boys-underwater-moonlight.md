## Body

Forty-five years after it was first released, the Soft Boys’ Underwater Moonlight sounds better than ever. The glorious chime of Robyn Hitchcock and Kimberley Rew’s guitars, the buoyant rhythm section of drummer Morris Windsor and bassist Matthew Seligman, the interlocking vocal harmonies, Hitchcock’s surreal and bewitching lyrics … it all adds up to a bona fide masterpiece.

Underwater Moonlight has been reissued and repackaged in various forms over the decades, but it’s getting a notable sonic upgrade in 2025. The 45th anniversary edition is the first reissue to be remastered and cut from the original tapes (including the original “Old Pervert”!) and it’s being pressed on what Hitchcock calls “billion-dollar vinyl” at Paramount Pressing in Denver, CO, in addition to CDs and digital Whichever way you choose to commemorate Underwater Moonlight at 45, rest assured that the album’s singular beauty remains a thing to behold. “A celebration of transformation,” Robyn says.

To dive deep into the stories behind the songs, we went straight to the source. Below, Robyn Hitchcock walks us through the album’s 10 tracks. | t wilcox

I Wanna Destroy You

Aquarium Drunkard: Not to start on a depressing note, but has there ever been a time since you wrote this song that it didn’t feel relevant?

Robyn Hitchcock: There have been points where it’s been more relevant than others. Sadly, it’s very relevant at the moment. It’s basically a protest song against human nature. It’s not simply “We hate Trump” or “We hate Reagan or Thatcher or whoever.” Back then, the Cold War was warming up and the chickenhawks and saber-rattlers were coming out to flex their phantom muscles. But unfortunately, it seems to be a generally true song. There’s just a design flaw in our species that allows a certain kind of alpha person to come in and have too much power. You have the effect of fascism amplified through media or technology and you get that fantastic combination of money and ignorance. Which is what’s happened at the moment.

I have to watch it, though, because this is me rattling on about right now and then there’s what the song is about. The song is basically about the destructive impulse in humans. And I suppose I’m singing that I would like to destroy that! You know, hatred is contagious. That’s the real thing. Malice breeds malice. Liberals don’t carry guns — but how long will that go on for? It’s very hard to develop a Gandhi-like attitude. I sort of see the human race as dying with its fingers round each other’s throats. There’s a happy prognosis. I thought that back then and nothing has changed [laughs]! Unfortunately, I’m in complete alignment with the 25-year-old me.   

AD: Do you remember the specific thing that sparked the song?

Robyn Hitchcock: No, not really. I’m basically very solipsistic. It was probably me thinking about myself on some level. I wasn’t like, “Oh god, these people have gone too far!” I’m no Billy Bragg, I can’t sing songs about the common good — or at least very seldomly. If I write something that’s apparently political, it’s probably because I personally fear for myself or for my immediate loved ones. And my cats.

AD: Well, the thing that makes “I Wanna Destroy You” a not-depressing song is just the power of the music — especially that high harmony that Morris Windsor hits on the chorus. Do you remember how that came to be?

Robyn Hitchcock: I was just singing it round and round. I had just made it up. The other day, I found a series of completely different lyrics that I’d written for it. I don’t think they were any better, but it was before I’d fine-tuned it. What I think happened was Kim hit one harmony and then Morris just came in after him. It’s a very long note. Kim came in a bit later than me, and Morris came in later still, so you’ve got that build, rather than coming in all at once, like the Byrds or something. That’s probably what makes it. Or one of the things, anyway.

AD: A very specific question — what’s that screeching sound at the end as the song kind of breaks down?

Robyn Hitchcock: I think it’s Kim.

AD: I wasn’t sure if you had brought in an eagle or something.

Robyn Hitchcock: [Laughs] I don’t think a bird would have survived in Alaska Studios! There were no other lifeforms.

AD: “I Wanna Destroy You” has become kind of an anthem over the years — I’ve heard Uncle Tupelo do it, the Circle Jerks do it … and the Replacements! They might have been the earliest.

Robyn Hitchcock: I don’t think I heard it back then. I’ve never had my ear to the pulse. Maybe I did back in 1967 and then I let it all go. By the early eighties, I was stranded in the future, really. I was surrounded by things that baffled me. I was like a trainee old man. The old man I now am, legitimately. No, I found out about the Replacements later. They sent some albums to the little indie record label that we were on. They wanted to know if I’d produce them. And I didn’t have a record player even! Or I had several, but they didn’t work, Me and hi-fi have always been at opposite ends of the field. If I listen to music these days, it’s either on my phone or on an old beatbox. I’m the antithesis of the record collector-type.

So I sent these Replacements albums to Morris and said, “What do you think?” He said, “I don’t think it really sounds like your kind of thing.” My partner at the time said, “I don’t think you should produce these people — you’d drink too much!” [Laughs] When I started touring over here, I started to hear tales of the Replacements which put me practically in the sobriety category. I’ve never drunk in the studio or really onstage. But in the Replacements, you’re drinking and taking drugs and doing the whole thing in a party mode. You can work out how to do it. I couldn’t do it. I would just become incapable. I had to choose between partying and playing music. I’m a simple fella, I couldn’t combine the two.

Kingdom of Love

AD: This song would be my answer if someone asked me “What do the Soft Boys sound like?” It’s got everything, from lyrics to guitars to harmonies. It’s perfect.  How did the Soft Boys function as a unit at this time? Were you bringing arrangements in fully formed or did you give your bandmates a lot of leeway there?

Robyn Hitchcock: More of the latter. Matthew put that fantastic bassline in, which many subsequent bass players have learned. It’s one of our most popular songs in terms of other people playing it. And that’s courtesy of Matthew. He also did some clever thing — that part: “Now they’re hatching out under my chin!” Originally it was a bit more pedestrian. He said, “Why don’t you go straight up to it? You’re in E and you’re going to A. Why don’t you just go up here?” He put a shortcut in there that was really effective. But I’m not a Paul McCartney figure. I can’t come in and say, “Here’s my demo, just follow it!” It’s more like, “OK, folks, here’s the song.” And we’d go from there.

AD: You wrote about this on your Substack recently but tell me about “Harold” and what part he played in this song.

Robyn Hitchcock: Harold was a mendicant, a kind of holy man. A primitive artist, in fact. I don’t know if he sustained some damage in World War II, but he lived by himself in a sort of polythene and plywood shack in some freezing field on the edge of Cambridge. He was very self-sufficient. I suppose he was in his late 50s, and he would just lurch around. He had a slight limp, and he was very strong. And he just walked down the back streets of Cambridge, and he caught my eye. He had these catch phrases and one of them was “Bless you, father, in the sacred light of life, in the Spiritual Kingdom of Love.” And being someone who is fed by repetition, after I’d heard Harold going on about “the Spiritual Kingdom of Love” for a couple of years, that took root in my mind. Initially, I imagined it as a kind of Jim Morrison voice over a grungy background.

Fortunately, that transmuted into the version that we did in the Soft Boys, which, like you say, shows the band at its best. Matthew’s buoyant bassline, Morris skipping along on the drums. You don’t realize how architectural Morris’ drum parts are until somebody else plays them and you think, “Well, that’s good” — but they’re just copying Morris. He’s so Buddha-like and self-effacing. He never claims any credit. If you compliment him on something, he’ll go, “Ohhhhh, well, I didn’t manage to do what I wanted to do” or “It’s not very good.” He’s so British. Kim as well. He can throw his sonic weight around, but he doesn’t big himself up. They’re all very humble people.

AD: That “You’ve been laying eggs under my skin” line — an amazing image.

Robyn Hitchcock: Lines like that, they’re just what come into my head. Whatever feeds things into my mind, I’ll hear something like that — “You’ve been laying eggs under my skin” — and I just think, “That’s it. That’s the right line.” “Kingdom of Love,” when I look at it now, is about how intense and ugly human love is. Our obsessiveness. Whatever it is in the song, it’s not a healthy relationship. But it’s a strong one. Clearly the singer is in way too deep. They’re not in a particularly pleasant place. I think I nailed that one. But I didn’t say to myself: “I’ve got to write a song about someone who’s in way too deep!” I’ve never managed it. You let the song come through first, and then you decide what it’s about.  

Positive Vibrations

AD: On the face of it, this seems like an answer or counterargument to “I Wanna Destroy You” — but were you feeling at all optimistic at the dawn of the 1980s?

Robyn Hitchcock: It was a bit ironic [laughs]. When I wrote that, the Russians had just invaded Afghanistan and my partner had taken our old dog to be put down. It was January 3rd or 4th, and Cambridge was just bleak. I probably had a hangover. So, I thought I’d just flip it ‘round. Positive vibrations! I imagined something almost Pete Townshend-y about it. Kimberley liked it — I think it reminded him of something he might’ve written. And then we put the sitar on it as a kind of nod to the psychedelic past.

AD: Yeah, who is that playing the sitar?

Robyn Hitchcock: That was Andy King. He was a college roommate of Jim Melton, who was half-in the Soft Boys at one point. A roadie-slash-harp-player, a glue man. But Andy happened to play the sitar, and he came along one day to a folk club where I was going to play. I had decided to do a version of “Sister Ray” acoustically with a sitar and maybe Morris on bongos [laughs]. We were going to reverse how “Sister Ray” is normally done. Same lyrics: “Too busy suckin’ on a ding-dong,” and all the rest of it.

AD: So it’d be like the Incredible String Band playing “Sister Ray”?

Robyn Hitchcock: Yeah, it would have been like the String Band — Robin Williamson and Mike Heron playing “Sister Ray”! That’s probably exactly what I was thinking.

AD: Wow, you’ve got to find a recording of this.

Robyn Hitchcock: I think you’ve just got to put your String Band records and your Velvet Underground records on one after another and let them merge in your mind. But yeah, Andy played with me on that, and he was still around when we were recording Underwater Moonlight. He ended up running a kite shop in Covent Garden. I don’t know where he learned to play the sitar, but he was certainly good enough to play it with the Soft Boys [laughs]. That was a lucky thing, though. It put the icing on the song.

And the group was very good at playing that sort of music. We weren’t too light; we weren’t too heavy. At that point we recorded better than we played live. Live, the harmonies were all out of tune because the monitors weren’t very good, and we were too loud. It didn’t really feel like fun onstage. Maybe because the songs weren’t fun. Of course, it sounds absolutely brilliant now.

AD: You mentioned that the sitar is kind of a reference to the psychedelic era — and in old advertisements for the band you played that aspect up a bit. One of them says: “Psychedelic without the flowers.” I wanted to ask broadly what “psychedelic” means to you, whether referring to music or life or drugs?

Robyn Hitchcock: Musically, it means a synthesis of the sounds people made in and around 1967. Starting around ‘66 with the Byrds until ’68 when it was largely abandoned. It involved things like high harmonies, phasing, sitars, drones, tambouras, backwards guitar. George Harrison’s guitar solo in “I’m Only Sleeping” or Hendrix’s guitar in “Are You Experienced?”

You could identify it by when it left, really. You could see the remains of it in something like “Street Fighting Man” where there’s still a drone going on. You can hear it in “Mr. Fantasy” where the last verse, I think they’re singing through a Leslie speaker. You can’t hear it in anything Bob Dylan did and yet he was the guy who triggered it. It all happened while he stepped aside. He came back with his beige album and cut it right off.

But those are some of the musical totems of psychedelia, which I’ve used in my recordings ever since. I deploy them from time to time because I love them and grew up with them. But you can’t really put them on every record. Or you can, I suppose, but it might not be the best idea.

AD: There’s something like Andy Partridge’s Dukes of Stratosphear records, but those are very much in the side-project category.

Robyn Hitchcock: Yeah — Andy is the same age as me. When we actually did some recording together, I don’t think we really did any of that. It was more implied. We’re both children of ’67. Andy, interestingly, never took any drugs at all. He’s quite delicate; it was self-preservation, really. He never smoked pot or did acid. He was like the reverse of the Happy Mondays, who could take anything and come out the other side like, “Yeah, so what?” Andy, wisely, unlike Brian Wilson or Syd Barrett stayed away from all that.

I was very cagey about LSD. I took it a few times but didn’t want to wade out into the abyss. I was much more influenced by the music of the people who had taken it, not the drug itself. I absorbed it by proxy. But my memory of taking acid is that whatever you looked at closely started to change. And I suppose you could say that truly psychedelic music does that as well. If you focus on it, it actually becomes not what you think it is. I don’t know if I’ve done anything like that. Maybe lyrically, but not musically.

I Got The Hots

Robyn Hitchcock: “I Got The Hots” is a very summery song. Kind of dreamy sunlight with various characters moving around. There’s a lot of desire in there. Desire in slow-motion in the sunlight. It does have its creepy side; you can see all the shadows. And maybe what’s going on in the shadows, you just about see and it’s rather different from what’s happening in the sunlight. It’s almost as if there’s two different movies. One is the sun shining on the landscape and the other is all those creatures — what they’re doing in the dark. You could do it very easily with film. It’s got a bit of The Crystal World by J.G. Ballard in it, I think. I was re-reading a lot of his short stories when I wrote those songs.

But there’s a lot of joy in it, too. Matthew had only been with us for about three days when we recorded it. I think we may have rehearsed it with the old lineup of the band, but it made total sense with Matthew. It’s a rough mix. We didn’t have much money, and I just thought that I liked the rough mix. I never re-did the vocal and there’s some appalling singing by me. It’s like old George Harrison — a quarter-tone flat! But it’s part of the song now. It’s too late! And I overdubbed that propellor guitar at the end. I was very pleased with that, one of my best recorded pieces of electric guitar. I’m sure I couldn’t have done it again if I had to [laughs]. I got it absolutely right once. Kim and I are very in sync. My playing’s a bit spidery and his is more on the beat. The pair of us doubling up on a riff like that, it really sounds good.

Insanely Jealous

AD: The band’s performance on this one always knocks me out — such a sustained intensity. I was especially struck by Kimberley’s wild solo towards the middle — was that the sort of thing you could just kind of point to him, and he’d know what to do?

Robyn Hitchcock: I didn’t have to point at him, he just did it! Kimberley was a fountain of electricity at that point. I think the frustration of not doing his own material meant that he played more intensely on mine. I just played a little green bass, which didn’t have much bass to it. You hear it at the beginning, going dun-dun-dun-dun. And then Matthew comes in with a proper bass. The way the three of them come in — it’s probably the way they came in when I first played them the song. It was lucky. Or it wasn’t just luck — they got it right. Morris decided to stick to the bass drum and high-hat for the whole song. He never moved on to the rest of the kit. Kim does a harmony on one word, which is “love.”  That’s the only thing he sings on the song. Halfway through Kim’s solo, Matthew comes in with that incredible circular bass. Which is interesting; Matthew didn’t think of himself as a melodist. He didn’t really sing. He never did harmonies. But he would come up with these very tuneful bass parts. That’s another one of his classics, really.

AD: Where’d this song come from? Was it something you felt personally or was this kind of jagged paranoia in the air at the time?

Robyn Hitchcock: These songs are all personal. They’re all me singing about my world, what was going on in my life. My emotional landscape, if you like. They were all written around the same time, the summer of 1979 going up to early 1980. It had probably taken a couple of years to build up, but I just put myself in a situation that made me write those songs. I don’t know if I was unhappy, but I was certainly uncomfortable [laughs]. That probably sums me up, anyway.

Tonight

AD: Is the narrator of “Tonight” the same as “Insanely Jealous”?

Robyn Hitchcock: Oh yeah, they’re all the same narrator. It’s me! Some personalities are very different from one song to another. “Tonight” is less violent than “Insanely Jealous.” It’s more of a stalker/creep song. I was homing in on someone that I couldn’t home in on.

AD: Jealousy, obsession — these are kind of ugly emotional states … were these songs a way of exorcising those feelings for you?

Robyn Hitchcock: Well, the thing about those emotions is that they fueled the songs. I wanted to be a songwriter. I’d been writing songs for nearly 10 years then. This was the first time I was writing about something real, rather than an abstraction in my head. I mean, arguably, I’d written songs before that were fun and some were quite good in a way. But this was the first time I was able to harness what I was feeling and use it to fuel songs. That became how I did things from then on in. I didn’t continue writing songs about that particular fixation or that particular person. I figured out how to do it. So, I’m grateful, I suppose looking back, for all of those ugly feelings — because I got some good songs out of them. Which is what I wanted to do with my life! I didn’t want to be happy or rich or famous or anything. I just wanted to write good songs. Underwater Moonlight was where I proved to myself that I could.

AD: Adding to the paranoid vibes, there’s a murmur of conversation underneath the mix of “Tonight.”

Robyn Hitchcock: A couple of our friends from the Hope & Anchor came down to the studio when we were recording. And either Pat Collyer or Matthew said, “Why don’t we have some voices here?” So we did. There was a rough mix that might have gotten the voices a bit better. Pat also loved the totems of psychedelia, so he put the phasing on the drums at the end, which was another nod to that era.

AD: Going back to Lou Reed, those voices make me think of “Kicks” from a few years earlier.

Robyn Hitchcock: Oh, you’re right!It is “Kicks.” There’s also some of that on “All Through The Night” on The Bells. Lou did that a couple of times. Yes, it is like that, but I don’t know if it was a conscious thing.

You’ll Have To Go Sideways

AD: The album’s lone instrumental — how’d this one develop? As a jam? What time signature are we in here?

Robyn Hitchcock: I think [original Soft Boys bassist] Andy Metcalfe had left, and Matthew was due to arrive. I remember recording it with Morris and Kim. There was no bass player. I just had this riff that was in some funny time signature. “Let’s do this!” Kim doubled what I was playing so it sounded stronger, and Morris found some way to just play 4/4 underneath. It’s probably technically in 19/4 or something [laughs]. In a flash of ingenuity after everyone had gone, Kimberley played a bass part and then a synthesizer part. He’d never played either before. It was just a fantastic accident. I’d put that thing down and thought no more of it, but he finished it off. It’s such a great palate cleanser on the record. A great sense of tension and release. I’ve always thought it was quite good to have an instrumental on the record. Lose the words for a bit.

AD: I’ve read that you and Kim had kind of a synesthesia thing happening when it came to chords and keys and so forth.

Robyn Hitchcock: We’re both pretty much what you’d call “on the spectrum.” I think most musicians are. I remember talking about B as “gun metal gray.” I think of E as sort of golden. Triumphant. Though then again, “Old Pervert” is in E. That’s not a golden song [laughs].

Old Pervert

AD: Who is the Old Pervert? A real or imagined character?

Robyn Hitchcock: The Old Pervert is a bit of a synthesis. I think it’s me looking at myself as what I essentially was and might become. It has a little bit of Harold in it, too, though Harold was no pervert. He did used to gargle with disinfectant, though. He would clean his mouth out with disinfectant. So, the Pervert is something somewhere between me and Harold.

I mean, the Old Pervert is not really doing damage, he’s just realizing what a disgusting figure he is, really. The whole thing is an exercise in misanthropy. I am not proud to be the species I am, to be the creature I am. Take away God, take away Jesus, take away all these other figures and what are you? You’re a rather disgusting figure with an electric guitar and 75 years or so to kill. So, let’s rock!

AD: Listening to the record this past week, I thought the thread running through Underwater Moonlight was “toxic masculinity” to use today’s term. But maybe it’s just “toxic humanity.”

Robyn Hitchcock: We’ve evolved too fast. We’re still savages. We’re like the 2001 apes. These savage, feral apes who have developed this technology. And everything that we’ve developed, we’ve used to harm ourselves as much as to benefit ourselves. We don’t seem to be able to tell the difference. We go to war with the best intentions, and we go to hell with a song in our hearts. We’re the species that triggered the Great War and the Holocaust and God knows what’s going on right now as we speak.

Some people are driven to religion by these thoughts. And I see their point. If you don’t take refuge in a higher power, you have to look unflinchingly at what you are. And it’s not great. I admire people who do things for others. The healers, the helpers, the people who are flooded with kindness. They do exist. Unfortunately, I don’t have that. My gifts are entirely artistic. So, I feel bleak about myself and the entire species. Absolutely nothing has changed my view of the world since back then. What a bummer [laughs]!

The Queen of Eyes

AD: Well, yeah. But somehow, Underwater Moonlight doesn’t make me feel disgusted with humanity. The opposite, in fact! Maybe there’s something about music that redeems us, to some extent? Which takes us into the next song — “The Queen of Eyes,” which is still one of the most beautiful songs I’ve ever heard.

Robyn Hitchcock: Art is the highest part of all of this. I mean, there are those who are capable of self-sacrifice and those who can put others first. If you’re not capable of that, then art is a good substitute, maybe. Music is a form of alchemy. It turns the lead of humanity into the gold of the angels. We suddenly become these extraordinary portals to … the Spiritual Kingdom of Love! A place where there’s a transcendent understanding of our condition. It’s where compassion exists, but you don’t need any God to do it. I mean, you might end up embracing Bob Dylan or Arthur Lee or something, but you do so at your peril [laughs].

But basically, yeah. To me, music is salvation. That’s true for most musicians. It’s a raft to which we passionately cling. It can be stronger than addiction. Not always. It’s an addiction in itself. It helps you communicate with people that you can’t necessarily otherwise communicate with. With a lot of players, the corridor opens up between you in a way that you can’t do with words. People like me — I’m an artist, but I needed to be a musician in order to function emotionally.

I draw and I write a lot these days. There may come a time when I can’t go out and do gigs. I may have to make money from writing and drawing and other artistic pursuits. But music is still the core. That’s the fire ‘round which we warm ourselves. I think that’s how we justify our existence. If I was being judged by the court of eternity, I’d have to say, “Yeah, OK, I sucked. The species sucked. But, your honor, I’d like you to take into consideration Underwater Moonlight or I Often Dream Of Trains or some fantastic cosmic jams I’ve had with people around the world.” Music is redemption, and that’s what a lot of us are looking for.

AD: I absolutely agree. To get more technical, the guitars on “Queen of Eyes” — and the whole record really — sound so great. Tell me about Pat Collyer, who passed away recently, and his work on getting that sound.

Robyn Hitchcock: He definitely got what the Soft Boys were about. And he used his limitations as strengths. He knew how to bounce down two guitars, bass and drums onto two tracks and then put three double-tracked harmonies on top. “I Wanna Destroy You” is just that — four tracks, like the Beatles did. Pat understood about where to put the sonic architecture. Particularly the guitars. The guitars aren’t even overdubbed — it’s just a Telecaster and a Strat. People wrongly thought we were using twelve-strings, but we weren’t. Kim would double certain things I was doing and then put a solo over the top. Pat recorded it in a way that we didn’t have to massively overdub anything to make it work.

AD: I’ll ask the same thing I did about “Old Pervert” — who is the Queen of Eyes to you?

Robyn Hitchcock: She’s another element of the same personality that I’m singing to in other songs. They’re all kind of addressed to the same person. They’re all kind of from me to them. But each song varies a little. They’re not exactly like, say, Steve singing to Barbara, right? It’s actually Steve plus one, or Barbara minus two, or Alan singing to Veronica. As with stories and books, there’s a lot of hybrids going on in the recipe. That’s one of the reasons why we tell stories, I suppose. It’s truthful but it’s not factual. There are points when things have to become fictionalized. There might be points that are literally true, but the great thing is to make the joints seamless, so people don’t know. Or maybe they do, I don’t know. But the Queen of Eyes is certainly the same thing, but it’s a more positive slant on them.

AD: It’s always kind of amazing to me that “Queen of Eyes” comes second to last in the running order — which might be where other bands would put some filler. Do you spend a lot of time sequencing your records?

Robyn Hitchcock: I do spend time on that — though the more records I make the less they matter, because there’s so many of them. To the point where I can’t remember the order — or even the songs — on some records from the last 20 or 30 years. Whereas Underwater Moonlight and Black Snake Diamond Röle are kind of vividly emblazoned on my mind because they got there early. They really mattered to me because they were firsts.

But I don’t think there is any filler on Moonlight. All the tracks have their place. “You’ll Have to Go Sideways” isn’t filler — it’s what’s needed there. “Old Pervert” isn’t a great song, but it shows the band as a sort of art rock vehicle, how we used to be on the Beefheart-y side of things. It’s also an exercise in rampant ugliness. There’s no attempt to be pretty in it, and it helps the other songs to be prettier, because you go from “Pervert” to “Queen of Eyes.” We could’ve used “He’s A Reptile” there instead, and it’s a much nicer song, but it would have made the album a bit more one dimensional. All what Kimberley calls “power pop gems.” “Pervert” is so at odds with everything else that it helps glue everything else together. Like “The Murder Mystery” on the third Velvet Underground album. It’s this thing that’s put there to throw the other songs into relief.

AD: I think of the White Album by the Beatles, which becomes more than the sum of its part through the sequencing. Those might not be their greatest songs, necessarily, but the way they bounce off of each other gives it some kind of interesting friction or frisson or something.

Robyn Hitchcock: Yeah, I don’t know if there’s anything quite like the White Album. But as time goes by, it becomes the White Album. You couldn’t change anything about it. On a smaller scale, it’s like the out of tune vocal on “I Got The Hots” — it’s just what it does. You’ve got to have “Wild Honey Pie” [laughs]. Of course, the Beatles … everything they did was classic. It’s like, “What do you mean, cut down the White Album to one record … it’s the White Album for fuck’s sake!” What are you going to do with it? Whatever you thought of “Revolution No. 9” back then, now it’s just part of the mythology. OK, there it is. It makes me think of “Sad Eyed Lady of the Lowlands” taking up one side of Blonde on Blonde. You do need something that’s kind of at odds with everything else, just to make it not all be the same. There’s got to be the odd song out. I’ll remind myself of that the next time I’m putting a record together [laughs].

Underwater Moonlight

AD: Was Underwater Moonlight always going to be the name of the LP?

Robyn Hitchcock: Yeah, it was. I walked into the café where we all used to go to and some of the guys were there, and I just said: “Our next record’s going to be called Underwater Moonlight.” And you know, they nodded and said, “OK. Are you having a roll or just coffee?” I’m not sure I’d even written the song. But that was it. It was a given. It all fell into place.

AD: The cover art of the record — I think I’ve always assumed that the mannequins on the rocks there are meant to be the couple in “Underwater Moonlight,” but is that right?

Robyn Hitchcock: Yeah, that’s right to some extent. My sister Lal made them. They probably look like her parents-in-law a bit. Who knows, you know? I don’t even know how we came to do that! I definitely asked her to make the mannequins and George, her partner at the time who was a great photographer, took the pictures. They definitely relate to the song. They were in my attic for a long time. And then they sort of disintegrated.

AD: As I was singing along to this song earlier this week, I realized I didn’t really know what the term “Underwater Moonlight” means, beyond just being a very evocative image. There’s this couple being drowned by a giant squid and then they’re replaced by statues.

Robyn Hitchcock: Well, it’s redemption through death. And the substitution of the statue — that’s the eternal you, the immortal you. You’re replaced by your avatar. You achieve immortality by ceasing to exist.

AD: Oh, man.

Robyn Hitchcock: Matthew said, “Underwater Moonlight — do you realize that’s the weakest, most diffused light you can have?” It’s the moon shining through the water. I’ve never actually seen it [laughs]. There’s a full moon right now, maybe I could try it. I don’t know. The moon is the great motivator. The sun feeds us and scorches and maketh the bird to fly and the worm to burrow. But the moon is what motivates crabs and activates fishes and triggers menstrual cycles in some way. The tides, of course. The moon has become a symbol of our emotional undercurrents. And I suppose if it’s underwater, it’s affecting the tide. You’re under the water that’s been summoned up by the moon … I’ve never really thought it through, Tyler [laughs]. It’s something to do with intuition and compulsion and death — but also renewal. It’s not to do with extermination, it’s to do with transformation. That’s the answer. The song is a celebration of transformation, you know? The humans become the statues. The statues become humans. They’ve swapped places. It’s not a negative song. It’s transformational. So, the best place is to put it at the end of the record.
