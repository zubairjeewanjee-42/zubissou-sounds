## Body

Way back in the 1990s, Neil Young started mentioning Early Daze, a collection of previously unreleased studio recordings he made with Crazy Horse in the late 1960s. As with most everything in Shakeyland, it was a long time coming … but this summer Early Daze finally emerged. Worth the wait? Oh yeah. We’ve heard a few of these performances via various Archives releases over the years, but taken as a whole, it’s a necessary chapter in the Horse saga, filling out our vision of the band in its formative stages, when guitarist/vocalist/songwriter Danny Whitten was at the helm. With Danny taking the lead on several tunes (including a wonderful version of his classic “Look At All The Things”), Early Daze presents Crazy Horse not as Neil Young’s backing band, but as a band. For further insight, we went to one of the guys who was there for it all — bassist Billy Talbot. | t wilcox

Billy Talbot: It’s a little early in the morning here.

Aquarium Drunkard: Well, we’re talking about Early Daze, we gotta do it early in the morning, right?

Billy Talbot: Yeah, but not too early [laughs].

AD: So what do you think of Early Daze? Do you enjoy going back and hearing these recordings more than 50 years later?

Billy Talbot: You know, I like it! I really liked hearing Danny again. And I really liked hearing Neil Young … young! I had completely forgotten about those recordings, as far as what they were about or when we did it. It was all part of the beginning of Neil Young and Crazy Horse. it was cool to hear that.

AD: Do you have any favorite moments?

Billy Talbot: I like them all but let me think. I like “Everybody’s Alone,” that one is great. And “Look At All The Things. I like hearing Danny singing that. “Come On Baby, Let’s Go Downtown” — I like that version of it.

AD: A song like “Everybody’s Alone” — that one didn’t come out officially until decades later. Neil’s kind of famous for that. Did you ever think over the years, “Hey, why aren’t we doing this one? Why hasn’t this one come out?”

Billy Talbot: No, I don’t think that way. It seems evident to me when things do come out, why they came out right at that time. When I heard that song recently, I thought, “Well, this is a good song, we should do that more.” But I’m just a little part of this whole thing. Well, not a little part, but I’m just a part of it, you know?  

AD: Let’s talk about the earliest thing on Early Daze — this alternate version of “Down By The River.” There’s a lot that I love about that song, but one of them is the groove that you and Ralph play. Can you remember where the inspiration for that came from?

Billy Talbot: I can remember, actually. That bass and drum part came from rhythm & blues. Plenty of songs have that boom-boom-BAP-boom-boom-BAP beat. And that’s what we did — we slowed it down. We had been playing it up there at Neil’s house and it was kind of like … jazzy and stuff. And I was thinking, you know, this isn’t really the way it is. I said, “Hey, Ralph, maybe we could do this beat!” I think it’s from a Sam & Dave record, maybe. A real typical beat. “If we take that beat and slow it down, it might work really good for ‘Down By The River.’” And it did! I’m glad we did that. It was just a little thought, right?

AD: I’ve heard plenty of people rip it off since then.

Billy Talbot: Oh yeah, it’s definitely the kind of thing that has been on a few records I noticed through the years [laughs]. But I never thought about it much except that it worked.

AD: It still sounds so good whenever you play it. Speaking of which, you played “Down By The River” and the rest of Everybody Knows This Is Nowhere at the Roxy last year. What was that experience like?

Billy Talbot: A lot of fun. And there’s a certain genuine feeling of appreciation for the fact that we had never done that before and it felt so good, right? Like, well, this is what happens when you don’t overdo things. It still felt alive. And plus, we had played at the Roxy back in the day. We opened it and we did a lot of the same songs there again. It was fun revisiting all of that. And we had Nils [Lofgren] with us, as well as Micah [Nelson].

AD: There are a few songs from Everybody that you’d never — or rarely — played live before. Like “Running Dry,” had you ever done that one onstage?

Billy Talbot: No! That was really cool to do because Bobby Notkoff, who played the violin part on the record, is no longer with us. And it was such a great violin part that we had shied away from doing it after that. But when it came to the Roxy show and playing the whole album, we wanted to do it, and Micah had some cool ideas to make up for the missing violin. You know, he likes being a violin player without being a violin player.

AD: The late David Briggs, who produced most of the Crazy Horse albums with Neil, is obviously a big part of Early Daze. What did he bring to the whole experience?

Billy Talbot: Stability. He was a steady force, right? He kept his head about him; he didn’t go crazy with excitement or crazy with disappointment. He kept his head about him and helped us keep ours, I would say. He was cool. It was a little bit of a roller coaster back then, but mostly it was just a steady flowing of a train. You just kept going. Sometimes we rode up a hill and sometimes we came down the hill, but still we just kept riding. And David was a steady force — as steady as any of us could be in those days, right? The early part of our lives, when we were more wild.

AD: It does seem like things were moving fast back then, with Neil working with both you and Crosby, Stills & Nash at the same time. Did it feel chaotic?

Billy Talbot: It was always moving along as it should. When Neil started playing with CSN, it seemed like a natural thing for him to do. And we had been doing our thing; we made a couple of good records, and I was happy with that. We were doing the first Crazy Horse album at about the same time. I think I like the version of “Look At All The Things” we did for that album better than the one [on Early Daze]. We had a good feel going. And I love Danny’s voice.

AD: It’s really a gift to get more of Danny on Early Daze, since there’s so little of his work out there. 

Billy Talbot: I’m so happy that he’s getting heard more. and that [more people] understand his importance in the whole thing. He was great. And it’s good for him to get that recognition, even though he’s gone. He was a really good friend of mine. We were like brothers. I thought he was a great artist. I knew it all along. It was really sad for me that he got into drugs the way he did. I just knew how it would end. Really, we knew lots of people who did those kinds of things and they all basically either got clean or they ended up the same way. You know, it’s a miracle if you make it through youth to become a full-grown man. “Miracle” might be too heavy to word, I don’t know. When you’re with your mom and dad, they raise you up. And then when you leave the nest, one of the things you have to do is survive.

AD: Totally. It does feel like there should be a whole catalog of Danny Whitten music for us to listen to.

Billy Talbot: There should have been. There should have been, but it wasn’t meant to be. I know that it would have been really good for the rest of us if he had lived.

AD: One thing I’ve read over the years is that there was a plan back in the day for an album that would feature both Danny’s and Neil’s songs. Was that ever part of the plan?

Billy Talbot: We didn’t really know. We didn’t have a plan. We were just kicking the ya-yas out, so to speak. Just getting it out of us onto vinyl. We didn’t really have a plan otherwise. We were exploring what it meant to be together and that was always a lot of fun. You can tell that by listening to Early Daze. We weren’t too dead serious. We were alive, trying to grab ahold of that tail before it got away.

AD: We mentioned CSN, but there’s a very early Crazy Horse version of “Helpless” included on Early Daze, which is great to hear. That song has obviously become a classic over the years — but not as a Crazy Horse song, really.

Billy Talbot: I would’ve liked to hear the harmonies we did back then. I know we did sing some harmonies for it. But they’re not there. I love that version, though. It’s just simply the song. It’s not blown out of proportion. The feeling of it really comes across. Helplessness!

AD: You’ve played “Helpless” a few times with Crazy Horse, but did you ever think, “Hey, that should be our song!”

Billy Talbot: No, I didn’t [laughs]. I mean, when Crosby, Stills, Nash & Young did a song, it sounded really cool! But as far as “Helpless” is concerned … I don’t seem to do that, for some reason. Looking back on things, it’s like, “Why not?” But I didn’t think, “Oh my god, they’re doing that song and it’s not us!” It was fine, let ‘em do it. It wasn’t a competition.

AD: Well, speaking of backing vocals, that’s another thing that’s great about Early Daze — hearing the vocal blend you guys were getting with Neil at this time. Where did that come from?

Billy Talbot: Neil, Ralph and Danny together on any record is gonna sound great. Danny and Neil blend so beautifully together — these two great, distinctive voices. And then Ralph puts this high part on it … it’s just wonderful. It was very organic and natural. That’s gotta be appreciated.

AD: In late 1969, Jack Nitzsche joined the band, and he became part of Crazy Horse for a while there. What was Jack like? What did he bring to the band?

Billy Talbot: He brought Jack Nitzsche [laughs]! He had already done a bunch of stuff, of course. We had already recorded with Jack, actually — Ralph, Danny and me. We had done a song of Dylan’s, and Jack did the whole orchestration. I’m trying to think of the name of it … I know the song, the title’s another thing! [Sings] “My love, she speaks like silence …”

AD: “Love Minus Zero.”

Billy Talbot: Yes, that’s it! Ralph sang lead on that. And then Jack had worked with Neil on his first solo album. Neil brought Jack in, and Jack worked on a few of those songs that are on Early Daze.

AD: He’s on “Winterlong” and I’ve always wondered about that song’s little “Be My Baby” breakdown in the middle — did Jack add that reference?

Billy Talbot: [Sings] “Boom-boom-boom — be my, be my baby!” [Laughs] No, I don’t think Jack brought that. I think that was Neil, or even me and Ralph … That’s something that Ralph and I would’ve done. It just seemed like a good idea to have a break like that. It sounds good!

AD: A couple months after these sessions, you went on tour across the country with Neil — which we can hear on the Fillmore East live LP, which came out a little while back. That’s one of the best live albums ever, I think. What do you remember about that tour?

Billy Talbot: We played the Fillmore East with Miles Davis. His band was playing there — and we followed them! It was just such an honor. And Steve Miller went first. Steve Miller, then Miles Davis, then us. Pretty good bill! We were so alive at that point. Really alive. We just attacked all the songs in such a cool way. I’m glad we have that record now. Neil is so cool; he keeps track of everything. Not in a fanatical way, but in a cool way. He has everything. He’s a collector. He remembers.

AD: The Fillmore East record has those amazing versions of “Down By The River” and “Cowgirl In The Sand” — these awesome Crazy Horse epics. What’s it like playing that stuff?

Billy Talbot: When we’re in there, it goes to another place. When it’s really happening, it gets really magical. We’re lucky to have that happen. We cherish it. Now, looking back on everything, we realize how much we cherish it, because it just doesn’t happen so easily, where songs can take on a new life by the fact that we’re playing them that way. Like in “Cowgirl In The Sand” or “Down By The River,” when you get into these long solos, it just becomes surreal. It’s not even the song anymore. It’s something else. I’m happy about that.

AD: I’m always happy about it, too. Do you have any thoughts on why Crazy Horse has been this lasting thing, from the Early Daze to now?

Billy Talbot: It’s fate. And I don’t even really believe in fate. We probably make our own fate, as we move along in this life. There seems to be some kind of other plan for us that we latched onto. But I’m not sure about that. All I know is that it happened. And I’m grateful. Grateful to have been a part of it. And still am, I hope!
