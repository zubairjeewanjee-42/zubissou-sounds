## Body

Sam Beam thought he had writer’s block as he entered the studio post-pandemic. But it turns out that wasn’t the case at all: the sessions produced so much music Beam had to split it into two different albums, 2024’s Fiona Apple-featuring Light Verse and his eighth album, Hen’s Teeth, released by Sub Pop in late February. 

Whereas Light Verse was delicate and ornamental, Hen’s Teeth is more rough and ready, with rustic shuffles and tropicalia touches threaded throughout. Recorded at Waystation studio in Laurel Canyon, the album features Beam joined by a cast including guest vocalists I’m With Her and his daughter, Arden Beam, who sings on four tracks. It makes for a family affair and a full circle moment for the elder Beam, who released his debut record, 2002’s The Creek Drank the Cradle, just months after she was born. 

Beam joined us by Zoom to discuss how the new songs fell into place, why he doesn’t usually write from a political standpoint, and touch on the influence of poetry in his music. | j woodbury 

Aquarium Drunkard: This new record, Hen’s Teeth, I really enjoyed spending time with it. You recorded this material right around the same time as 2024’s Light Verse correct?

Sam Beam: I went in thinking I was suffering from writer’s block but turns out I was suffering from the inability to finish stuff. So when we started the session, we recorded for about a year, off and on. I think it was maybe four sessions. The first one was pretty long and we got a lot of songs. Once I knew who the band was, I went back and finished a bunch of more stuff with them in mind. Things I wanted to try, or things I thought they might bring something interesting to. 

I ended up with a lot of material, something I thought wasn’t going to happen when we went into the studio for the first time. I had about two records’ worth of stuff, and thought about putting them all out together. But I am a merciful person. It’s a lot to ask these days. It’s a lot for someone to finish a whole song, let alone [a double record] worth of stuff. [Laughs] 

AD: How did you know which songs would land in the Light Verse pile, versus Hens’ Teeth? 

Sam Beam: The process of deciding what went on which record was kind of intuitive. We had done some stuff with an orchestra, so a lot of those pieces ended up being on the first album and gave them a cohesive feel. Also, those songs tend to be a bit more fragile, ornate feeling. Some of the structures of the tunes are a bit more adventurous, whereas this record’s experimentation is a bit more hairy, the song structures are a bit more straightforward, they’re just a little more dry. There’s a lot more Brazilian, tropicalia kind of stuff that’s going on, stuff that seemed to make sense together.

AD: I really like that side of the record, the sort of tropicalia side of things. But then you’ve got something like “In Your Ocean,” and it almost reminds me of The Faces. 

Sam Beam: I love those records.

AD: They’re great songwriters obviously, masterful songwriters. But they had such a feel as a band. But it’s interesting to think about the two records in relation to each other, even down to the album covers: Light Verse is blue, Hen’s Teeth is red. Are you usually somebody who goes in with the whole record structure more or less mapped out? Has that happened with other records? 

Sam Beam: The only one I ever did that on was The Shepard’s Dog. I was into these records that become one long musical piece, so we kind of sequenced it out so we could connect things. Usually, I end up with a handful of extra tunes. Deciding on which ones go on the record is just an intuitive thing. There’s no right or wrong answers, you just try to find ones that flow into the next, keep you on your toes. You don’t want too many of the same thing, it also depends on what you got. [Laughs] 

Hen's Teeth by Iron & Wine

AD: This one, in addition to maybe being a little more direct then Light Verse, I was tempted to think of it in terms of darker territory. But at the same time, there’s a lot of humor and absurdity in this record, like on “Robin’s Egg,” where you’re using these nonsensical terms.

Sam Beam: Silly words, just in terms of how they sound. Fun to hear, fun to say.  

AD: That’s a great song; it doesn’t do what you expect an Iron and Wine song to do. But I’m glad you brought up The Shepherd’s Dog. I mentioned that I’ve listened to your records from the beginning, but that one was really important for a couple reasons. I love that it didn’t take you long as an artist to wiggle out of any box that people might have put you in. Shepherd’s Dog hit me right as I was getting into Califone and Red Red Meat, and so Brian Deck is a direct throughline. I also loved your work with Calexico, how you were bringing in those Southwestern elements. Anyway, I’m sure there are people who still think of you as the lo-fi, hushed folkie guy. But The Shepherd’s Dog was a record were immediately, you were getting out of the box people might have put you in. 

Sam Beam: For better or worse. I couldn’t be doing that forever. Nothing stays the same, so you might as well just embrace it.

AD: I read a quote from you from the time talking about the political confusion of that moment. When I think about that to now, to write about what’s happening now is such a daunting task. That’s what I found myself thinking about with “Robin’s Egg”. You have to embrace the absurdity of language to even approach talking about what everybody is feeling now. I don’t necessarily want to call this a political record, but if it definitely seems like one where you’re trying to approach how upside down the world feels. I don’t know if it feels that way to you.

Sam Beam: I think what you’re touching on is: it’s not necessarily writing about politics, it’s an embrace of the grand contradictions. It’s a role that poetry plays for me. What I go to poetry for is embracing the beauty but also the tragedy. It sounds lofty and pretentious, but it’s the human experience. There’s no other way to say it. I try to make the language as simple as possible, I don’t like big words in songs. It’s just about common thing, common sayings. There’s always both sides of the coin. You’re trying to talk about how beautiful things are, but at the same time widen out and show that it’s in a strange, menacing atmosphere. 

You can politicize anything you want to talk about, we’ve proven that. There’s some really powerful political songs; I’m always obsessed with what’s happening with politics, but that doesn’t make me want to write songs. I feel like the atmosphere is trying to make me stop writing songs. My writing songs of this type is my own type of rebellion. 

AD: I think about resistance, people use words like that. I’ve been thinking a lot over the last couple years—and this is an insidious thing—where we begin to almost feel all the things that make us human are something we should feel a little embarrassed of. I was talking with a friend. He was talking to me about, however bad things have been, these good things happened in his life. You almost feel guilty for that, that’s what this is doing to us. It’s destroying our ability to live. To not be consumed by grief or fear or anxiety. All good art shares it makes space for paradoxical things. It’s not an either/or. That’s what I find so very moving about a lot of what you’re doing on this record. You’re just making space for the human contradictions that we all share.

Sam Beam: I agree, thank you. That’s what I’m trying for. Backing up and talking about our obsessions, our inspirations, or our fears in a way that’s not so specific of what’s on the news that day because it always changes. The problems are familiar but the specifics change. I just find it means your song is outdated the minute it comes out.

AD: Or even worse, didactic. Nobody wants a song that can just mean one thing. Like a lot of people, I think I heard the “Such Great Heights” cover really early on. It was on that Postal Service single. I remember being in my first car, sixteen, or seventeen. I heard it and I started crying. But I didn’t actually know why I was crying. My girlfriend hadn’t dumped me—yet. I just remember feeling a lot of feelings listening to it, and realizing that’s what music can do for us. Is give us a space where we don’t have to say, ”This is what it means and only what it means.” So much about the way we talk politics wants us to do that. Say, ”This is it, and you don’t diverge from that.” That’s not necessarily a real human way of communicating.

Sam Beam: No, it’s a manipulation. It’s a political propaganda manipulation. It’s what it feels like. I don’t feel like it’s irresponsible to claim your own life, to claim your own time. But that’s not the voice you hear most of the time. If you’re not engaged, then you are somehow letting a side down. I just find that manipulative. But I’m also one who’s not going to vote for somebody just because, they gotta earn it. It’s a very particular, toxic, partisan time. The way information works on top of that is pretty toxic. But, it’s a great time to be alive. There’s been a lot worse times in human history, that’s for fuckin’ sure.

AD: We have to remind ourselves that, because it’s really true. Things that we can go to the pharmacy for would have killed someone in the past. 

Sam Beam: Totally. It’s a perspective that’s not appreciated enough, because there is a lot of bad stuff happening. The thing is you hear about all of it, all the time.

Hen's Teeth by Iron & Wine

AD: That’s true, that’s true. And that incessant noise actually doesn’t serve to help us understand things better, it kind of does the opposite. I was thinking about on this record, songs like “Paper and Stone.” There are times where you’re framing it in sort of relational or romantic terms on the record. Where two individuals blurring back and forth into the same person. I think that you can zoom out on that and recognize it’s sort of about how we live in a time where our perceived individualism is so focused on, that we sort of forget that we are always in relation with each other. 

Sam Beam: Totally. That’s the thing, the minute you find a metaphor you’re trying to wiggle out of it. Because then it just makes the song boring. You try to include enough things, associate freely with others, not necessarily personal relationships, but as far as objects that have the same relationship or ideologies. It’s the best job. Playing with these things all day. Sometimes I feel like my stuff gets overboiled, there’s no way to absorb all those things on a passive listen. At the same time: fuck it I don’t know. I definitely feel like I could simplify things, sometimes. But as artists we always go back and forth, you’re supposed to be your own worst critic. 

AD: I think what you are, and that’s helpful for the process to continue. If you felt like you nailed it, you might not keep doing it. I always think of Willis Allen Ramsey. For years, people would always ask him ”When are you going to make a second record?” His reported response was,”Well what was wrong with the first one?” I always loved that.

Sam Beam: Did you see that Leon Russell movie that came out? A Poem is a Naked Person?

AD: The Les Blank film? Yeah.

Sam Beam: Did you see the footage of Ramsey in that movie? It was so cool to watch. I think Leon took him to the studio and helped him make that first record.

AD: I think that record’s on Shelter, yeah. 

Sam Beam: But then they were talking about the extra stuff. They have all the interviews with Leon talking, not long before he died. He was talking about Ramsey, how he was so in his head about the way it was supposed to sound. It didn’t sound right when he got in the studio. It sounded different when he listened to it in the room, and he never could adjust. He stopped doing it out of fear, which is intense, man, because that record was so good. But you can get in your own way, that’s for sure. 

AD: It’s so tempting I feel for artists to indulge in that voice because it feels so authoritative.

Sam Beam: There’s a fear attached to what you end up with as opposed to being obsessed with the creative process, the making of it. When you get so results-oriented it can get really debilitating.

AD: That’s a tough thing. By the way, man. Les Blank, one of the great film; when people ask me,”What are good films about music”, I always cite some of his. The Gospel According to Lightning Hopkins is a great one. 

Sam Beam: Leon Russell didn’t have a lot of good things to say about him.

AD: Maybe Leon Russell was upset about that scene where the snake eats the rabbit.

Sam Beam: He had a list. [Laughs] 

AD: I understand that you grew up in a Christian culture and grew up in the church. You don’t necessarily feel the same way or share those beliefs in a literal sense these days, is that how you see it?

Sam Beam: I don’t really feel the need to quality it, or moralize. It’s not used in a moralistic way, it’s just sort of part of the scenery, and the metaphors are there to use. I feel like it has a lot to do with how I grew up, it’s also a storytelling thing. As a writer, I feel it’s important where you should be really specific about where these stories take place. It’s usually the southeast, since that’s where I’m from. Then you can take off into fiction from there, but it’s a grounding place for me to have these religious ideas in the geography for me.

AD: I think that’s fascinating. I grew up in the southwest, similarly “churched.” 

Sam Beam: I thought it was a unique southeastern thing, but all the people I meet from California, Washington state, the Midwest, they’re all more churchy than me. But I just use it as part of the cultural water. The fish you’re writing about are swimming in it. Let it be as important or unimportant to the story, but it’s an element to be used. I said it before, it’s the best job. You can just suggest these things, it’s not a fucking essay about religion. You can just suggest little connections, you don’t even have to get into what belief structure you’re talking about. You suggest a name from the Bible, all of a sudden it does all this work for you. It’s the best job; I’m such a lazy bum. 

AD: I’m thinking about “Jesus The Mexican boy”, this idea where you’re making this association, and then undercut it in this way. Where it’s like, ”Maybe it’s not about what I thought it was about one second ago.” That’s the beauty of art. 

Sam Beam: I hope so. If you’re into that stuff, it’s there to be had. You’re listening to that song in the car, your emotional reaction not even understanding why. It’s also just about the sound. There’s just so many things going on at one time. I concentrate a lot on the words, and sometimes I’m like,”What the fuck are you doing? Just say this one word over and over.” Cause the sound that people are reacting to. But there’s no right or wrong answer, just make your best stab at the time.

AD: And that conveys. One of my favorites of yours is from 2013’s Ghost on Ghost, “New Mexico’s No Breeze,” that’s one of my favorites. I don’t know what the song’s about. I think there’s some scenes and images that conjure for me. But if you were to tell me,”explain to me what’s happening in this song,” I don’t know that I’d be able to. But that’s the beauty.

Sam Beam: It’s the best job on the planet. I don’t feel like that’s irresponsible either. There’s definitely a type of song that needs a very specific resolution. And then there are some about the mood. It’s art, it’s not math. 

AD: One of my favorite filmmakers is David Lynch, he would always be like, ”You don’t need the explanation, how did it make you feel?” That is the thing.

Sam Beam: Especially with him. You’ll hurt yourself trying to dive too deep. 

AD: Your sister Sarah sings on a lot of your music. On this new one, your daughter Arden is singing on the record. 

Sam Beam: That’s right, Sarah’s got some competition.

AD: First off, it must be a really fun thing to have the familial connection through your music. But it must be especially something to have this human you helped raise contribute her voice to your record. How did she come to get involved? Did you ask her to do it?

Sam Beam: I asked her. I knew she could sing. She was pretty shy, so it took some convincing. I don’t think I could get her on the stage. But I was a beaming, proud dad. It’s exactly what you imagine, I was over the moon. It was so fun to hear her voice, she was born a month or two before the first record came out. It’s a fun benchmark, full circle kind of moment. But also just to be able to share with them; I don’t have a home studio anymore so they’re not watching me dabble with things. But to be able to share that part of my life with them is so fun, so sweet.

Hen's Teeth by Iron & Wine

AD: Were you the kind of dad who was introducing her to stuff, or did you let her find her own way?

Sam Beam: It’s a bit of both, they weren’t interested. You have all these plans to create the perfect, “They’re going to appreciate everything that I love, and just understand,” but they don’t give a shit. Sometimes I’ll find them listening to something I know is not on the radio but they heard. But accessing music is so easy now, it’s different from when I was growing up. Now, I have no idea where they get music from most of the time because it’s so random because they have access to all of recorded history. It’s insane.

AD: I love the way you’ve always incorporated women singing on the records too. You had Fiona Apple on the last one. I really love the way that fits into the sonic tapestry of things. 

Sam Beam: I just love singers. Especially, because most of these songs, like you said, are based on relationships, it’s a really functional starting place. We’re all talking about human relationships, whether it ends up being a romantic story. It’s also so close to our core need for closeness. It’s an inherently dramatic place to start writing songs. Because most of the songs are like that, it’s nice to have a sort of foil, have that other voice who you’re talking to and have them talk back. 

AD: The sort of duet thing.

Sam Beam: I’m obsessed with that stuff. It opens up a song to subtext, and all these other things you don’t get when you’re just pontificating by yourself. 

AD: That’s a great point. We might not still be talking about Gram Parsons were it not for Emmylou Harris. Don’t get me long, I love Gram Parsons, no shade. [Laughs] But there’s a real special thing that happens when voices pair up that way, people like Richard and Linda Thompson. 

Sam Beam: I’m going to have to go get off this interview and listen to a record, I love those records so much.

AD: I want to ask you about the Norman Dubie. I understand when you were in Arizona once, you and the Calexico guys tracked him down. 

Sam Beam: He was teaching in Phoenix for a long time. He was a New England poet for a long time before that. He always had a couple of assistants. When I was making music with Calexico, one of their friends was his assistant. She came to one of the shows, and I couldn’t believe I was talking to someone who worked with him. He was a hero of mine. I got it from a poetry class in college. As famous as poets get I mean, they’re not really in the lexicon. Not on the cover of Time Magazine. But he was super popular in the day. Just incredibly.

AD: I read he was big influence on Carried to Dust, that Calexico record. I went down the rabbit hole and started reading a bunch of his stuff. He’s got this poem called “Homage to Philip K Dick”. So good, it absolutely blew me away. 

Sam Beam: He’s very eccentric, very loved, very gentle. I studied to be a filmmaker, so did he. But he walked away, because he was on the set of a western once, and he could tell the horses weren’t into it.

AD: That’s pretty beautiful. But scanning through his work again, so many of the poems do what you were talking about. They’re beautiful, but there’s complications.

Sam Beam: I don’t think that’s by chance. I studied his poetry books—those were some of the first books I really absorbed. It was a new sensation, I had never really read poetry like that before. I’d read stuff I was into, but it never made me stop and need to finish. I needed to dig in and figure this thing out. How does this thing work? What are the mechanics of this thing I’m reading? How do I take from those who have, which I have not? So I learned a lot about him specifically, and learned about a lot of other poets connected to him. And sort of started a journey through poetry through him. 

AD: The mechanics of it. Songwriters, guys like you and others, were how I found my way into that stuff. Eventually, I was reading reading John Ashbery early on. Feeling like…

Sam Beam: I’m too dumb for this.

AD: Oh, I definitely felt too dumb for it. But I also felt, “I bet my left leg that Jeff Tweedy has read some of this stuff.” There was that weird way he would juxtapose words and phrases that felt nonsensical, but nonetheless conjured a specific feeling. When you realize poetry can do that, I get weirded out about how music can impact music or poetry. If you’re just reading it on a screen, it’s a different feeling than a page. Maybe it’ll break the form in cool ways.

Sam Beam: There are things that will change. A lot of those things are so time-specific. Because of those little books, that’s how we absorbed them. It’s like the record store thing. There are things that are lost when record stores close, there are things that you hear that you’d never have heard if you were stuck in that system. I have a hard time reading poetry on the screen. I feel too old to do it. I’m only 50.

AD: I hear you, I feel too old sometimes. Before I let you go, Sam, there’s one other thing I wanted to ask. When you were young and studying film in Richmond, you were sort of in the middle of metal and hardcore, right?

Sam Beam: Yeah. All my roommates were in metal bands, punk bands.

AD: Were you drawn to any of that sort of music?

Sam Beam: I’m like a frustrated drummer. I’m a sucker for the rhythm section. Metal is so heavily percussive; for a teenager, early-20s, all that stuff is so fun to play. I love that music. But it was a little frustrating, it kept me from pursuing what I was doing, because it was very macho. I like playing the music, but it wasn’t the type of songs I wanted to write. But at the time that’s all there was in Richmond. Now there’s all these alt-country Americana bands, but at the time it was full of Fugazi rip-offs and metal bands. It was really fun but there’s weren’t a whole lot of other scenes to jump into or try. I feel like the only thing that was communicated was rage. It was fun to go to the shows, and get in the pit. It took me leaving there to access what I wanted to say with music.

AD: I’m sure somewhere down the line, there will be a metal tribute to Iron & Wine.

Sam Beam: [Laughs]
