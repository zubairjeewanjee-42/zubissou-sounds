## Body

Welcome to the kick off of Transmissions’ new season with your host, Jason P. Woodbury, after a wonderful summer mini-series from Tyler Wilcox,⁠ All One Song: A Neil Young Podcast. ⁠

We first encountered this week’s guest, New Zealand songwriter, actor, and composer Bret McKenzie, as one half of the indie pop/comedy duo Flight of the Conchords in the mid-2000s. But did you know that before that, he was a member of one of New Zealand’s most popular reggae party bands?

“Just for context, reggae music in New Zealand is kind of like rock music in America or maybe even country,” McKenzie says. “Outside Jamaica, New Zealand has the highest sales of Bob Marley records in the world—and it’s the music you hear playing in the background when you’re out.”

Transmissions :: Bret McKenzie (Flight of the Conchords)

Since then, he’s gone on to compose for film and TV projects like The Simpsons, The Muppets, Spongebob Squarepants, and more, and in 2022, he released Songs Without Jokes via Sub Pop Records. Inspired by vintage Los Angeles pop, the record showcased McKenzie sans obvious jokes, but not without levity and good humor. His new album,⁠ Freak Out City ⁠came out on August 15. Bolstered by vintage electric piano and groovy and psychedelic touches, it finds McKenzie expanding his Nilsson-esque palette with touches of Steely Dan, JJ Cale, and Todd Rundgren. McKenzie joins us on Transmissions to discuss the rabbit holes of modern life, cutting the record with studio legends like Leland Sklar, the Conchords’ specific brand of comedy, and shares details about his proposed Emmet Otter reboot with Ed Helms.

Aquarium Drunkard: Hello Bret. How are you?

Bret McKenzie: Great. It’s working. 

AD: This is a rare situation, where all the Zoom stuff just works right away.

Bret McKenzie: Yeah, exactly.

AD: You’re a true professional.

Bret McKenzie: We’ll see. 

AD: Do you have to do a lot of your collaborative work via Zoom? Like when you’re doing movies and TV projects?

Bret McKenzie: Yeah, a lot of video. A lot of Zoom or Zoom-y-type things. Everyone uses different ones. Apple has their own internal software for the security of their calls, but it never quite works. Everyone’s like, “What the hell?”  

AD: Well, I’m excited to speak with you about this new record, Freak Out City. It’s been a lot of fun spending time with it.

Bret McKenzie: Oh, thanks man. 

AD: I’m picking up on a lot of ’70s singer/songwriter types, Nilsson, Newman. I hear some Warren Zevon, The Beatles. Obviously, it’s not just one thing, but I’m curious just to start off, the kind of stylistic thing that you sort of tend towards, is that something you associate with your early memories of music? 

Bret McKenzie: My dad used to listen to quite a lot of Billy Joel and The Beatles were around quite a lot. I had this teacher at school, an amazing primary school teacher. He had his own bus. He was an old hippie kind of teacher. He had his own bus that he would take on school outings; we’d all sing Beatles songs while we drove around. He was a great piano player. He would sit down and we would sing Beatles songs for an hour every day, that was our school assembly. It was a very small little Wellington school and I didn’t think of it at the time. It just seemed like a normal thing to do. He just loved playing piano. So he would play Beatles songs and we’d sing along. It was really fun.

I’m not sure what it is about that ’70s stuff. There’s the songs. I love the songwriting style. It’s definitely very influential, a big influence on me. I also love the sound of a lot of those ‘70s recordings. I think if I could go back in time, I would love to be in Los Angeles in the ‘70s in those recording studios. It feels like gold records were just pouring out the doors. I’m sure there were lots of others. But yeah, it seems like a very romantic time in the music recording industry. 

AD: When you talk about some of the sonic touchstones from that era that I hear on this record, I think of the Rhodes electric piano. The ‘70s were the glory days of really beautiful Rhodes playing. But there’s some pedal steel on the record too, and that reminds me that you’d hear pedal steel in sort of singer/songwriter records, not just country records, in the ‘70s. You really tap into that spirit very nicely. You talked about being in the studio. I know some of this record was done in New Zealand, but some of it was done in L.A. as well, where you have people like Joey Warner playing on it. 

Bret McKenzie: And Leland Sklar. 

AD: Oh my god. Leland Sklar, that guy. I’ll watch his bass videos on YouTube. He’s the best. That’s somebody who’s played on so many records. What was it like to be in L.A. with those heavy hitters? 

Bret McKenzie: They were some of the players on those records in the ‘70s and the ‘80s. They played for decades on a lot of classic albums. I was so lucky to get to meet those players doing film jobs. They would hire session players to come in and because it’s a film project, well, firstly, there’s a budget, and secondly, there’s a real time pressure. So you have to have players that get it right in the first or second take. You only do two takes or three takes and then you’ve got to move on. It all moves really fast. Those players are just unbelievably accurate. You play a song and it sounds awesome, and then [session guitarist] Dean Parks will say something like, “Okay, can we just punch in bar 27?” He just knows there’s one little bit he could do better. So you just drop the guitar player in for one bar and he fixes that bar, because he’s made a note in his head. The track building of songs these days, you’ve got the multitracks and digitally you can move them around, cut and paste them, and finesse them and massage them.

You can do all sorts of things now where you can make everything a bit more in time. But honestly, you just take huge pieces of Leland’s pass bass or you just take the whole song and you can move the parts around Sometimes drummers, they go on and off the beat, they sway around. But with Joey, you take that verse and if you like the way that first verse was, you can put it on the second verse—it just all locks in. It’s pretty unreal. There aren’t many players that you can do that with.

AD: I guess it’s easy to, I don’t know, be intimidated?

Bret McKenzie: Oh, yeah.

AD: But also, they’re playing on your record and these are your songs. It must be a mind-blowing thing to sort of wrap your head around that.

Bret McKenzie: I think when I first floated the idea of doing a solo record, Leland Sklar said to me, ”Oh, I’ll do it. That sounds fun.” Just them wanting to be involved was a huge boost of confidence and obviously, they’re the most generous spirited people and lovely, really open-minded. To be a session musician, you really have to be a person that is easy to hang out with, because that’s such a big part of the job. You have to be open to ideas happening and things changing. They’re just great. But also, it makes me feel, I mean, I’ve done quite a lot of studio time recording all these things over the years, but nothing compared to these players.

It makes you feel like an apprentice in a really fun way because they might say something [that changes the process]. I remember Dean Parks, who played a lot on Steely Dan records—he did a lot of the solos on Steely Dan tracks—and he has that ability to play. He actually started off, I think as a saxophone player. But Dean has the ability to solo over really complicated chords, but still make it feel melodic. He said, “Sometimes Bret, with a Steely Dan track, we would just put an acoustic guitar track low in the mix just to glue it all together.” And obviously I said, “Well, let’s definitely do that”. And then he puts down this acoustic rhythm part and you throw it in there. You’re like, oh yeah. Suddenly the feel of the song locks together.

The little things they know from spending so much time in the studio or just their instincts…yeah, it’s a real masterclass. It’s really one of the cooler things about it. But then the other thing is, some of those things I’ve learned with them, I then take to the studio and work with my New Zealand band who are also amazing musicians. They haven’t spent decades recording. New Zealand doesn’t have a huge studio industry of session work, but it’s just cool learning things from Los Angeles studios and then bringing them to other studios and letting that knowledge connect up. Not that I’ve ever been a tradesman, but it reminds me of woodwork or something like that, where there are odd little tricks and little techniques that you just catch by just working on things. 

AD: You’ve got your New Zealand band and they are good musicians, people that you’ve got to play with on the road, and that builds camaraderie and a sort of sixth sense as a musician, where when you really spend a significant amount of time playing with people, you start to pick up on stuff, you start to lock in, these really cool things happen. But on the other hand, when you’re surrounded with people who have been doing high class recording for decades, you recognize that when those people are gone, when that generation is gone…there are always going to be great musicians, but the culture that produced those records and the industry that produced those records doesn’t exist anymore the way it did. And so I think about how that’s a skill that might not last forever, the ability to be that kind of master level. And that’s a little upsetting to think about. Yeah.

Bret McKenzie: Leland, Dean, Chris Caswell, who plays keys on it as well, they’re all in their seventies, I think. One cool thing about being a musician is you can keep doing it as you get older. It’s pretty cool. There are always amazing musicians. I think that’s always the case. There are people who just have this gift, you see them playing and you hear them playing like, “Whoa, there’s something.” But yeah, that ability, I think a lot of those players started off playing live gigs five, six nights a week.

So they spent years every night playing gigs playing with other musicians. And I think you’re right. I think now the recording process is far more “one at a time,” usually with a laptop playing. It doesn’t mean people aren’t great musicians, but that ability to play with other people is a skill that I think is less common. And it’s sad because it is really the heart of so much with what music is. And when you’re playing by yourself, people are recording by themselves. 

They’re basically pretending they’re playing with other people. Yeah, yeah. I mean, I remember one time Ian McKellen, he’s the guy who played Gandalf in the Lord of the Rings. I saw him one time, he lives in Wellington. I live in Wellington. And I said, “How’s it going?” And he said, “Oh, I’ve had a terrible day. I’ve been filming a scene.” He was in a room, just a blue tent, surrounded by a bunch of little LED lights, and there was a guy next to him who’s just in a t-shirt and a hoodie, who’s the camera assistant. And then he’s dressed up as a wizard, as Gandalf. And then next door, in another room, there are all the dwarves. And to make them small, they film them separately and then they superimpose them. But the result is that he’s in a room in a tent with just 10 little lights that come on when someone’s talking. And so the whole day he’d been in this tent acting to LED lights, and he was so sad. He was like, “It was the worst day—this is not the job,” what he was hoping the job would be. As an actor, as with music, bouncing off people so much is part of the art. 

AD: I think maybe, even unconsciously, we look to music as a way of connecting with other people and as a common language. Even when it’s just one person listening to one song, it’s kind of a two-way thing. That is an energetic exchange, but the feeling of playing music with real people is, I assume, like acting against somebody who’s really at the top of their game. Somebody like Ian, he’s as cool as it gets, but you take away the actual human connection part of it, and you realize just how tough it is. It’s not that you can’t build a great record track by track—many people do.

Bret McKenzie: No, there are lots of them. It’s not the only way. There’s lots of amazing music made by one person in the studio. It’s awesome, isn’t it? There’s no definite path to doing it. But for this record, I did want to really embrace that energy of the people in the room. I tried to write songs and allow the songs to have space for everyone to play into it and fill up those gaps. And that was really fun. And I love those songs. There’s a Nina Simone song, I can’t remember what it’s called now, but the track starts and then she’s like, “Stop, stop, stop. It doesn’t feel right. Try again.” And then it starts again. I love that kind of thing in recordings where you feel like you are really listening to these people playing at the same time. I think that’s pretty cool.

AD: Yeah, that’s one of my favorite things about Tom Petty and the Heartbreakers’ records is often you’ve got little snippets of them talking in the studio or whatever, and it feels like you’re being invited in. I think that this record really has that quality going for it. I was curious, the title of the album, Freak Out City,  and the song “Freak Out City.” I love when the word “city” is used as a slang term. Do you know the band Big Star, the power pop band? 

Bret McKenzie: No, don’t think so.

AD: Alex Chilton was this guy who was in this group called The Box Tops. They had a big R&B-inflected hit, and then by the ‘70s, he starts this pioneering jangle pop band. But their first record, they called it #1 Record, they assumed it would be—because it’s so good. It didn’t sell well. But their second record, they called Radio City. And again, it was this idea, this record is “radio city” because all these songs are going to get played on the radio. But none of them did. 

Bret McKenzie: My album’s going to get played in a lot of freak out cities.

AD: I feel like I’ve been living in freak out city for the last decade or so. Is there some of that feeling in the mix?

Bret McKenzie: It came out of the conspiracy wave that has emerged out of division through social media. There’s a lot of people coming up with crazy stories and perspectives and real far out angles on life. And in the middle of that song, there’s a sort of bridge that suddenly almost gets almost kind of Todd Rundgren-y, and it throws to this character, it jumps into the perspective of someone who is a conspiracy theorist who’s out on the town and no one wants to talk to him. No one. What’s wrong with these people? That idea came from that. Everyone’s freaking out at him, but he doesn’t realize because he’s gone down the rabbit hole.

Really, that’s that little angle on that moment because at that point in the song, it’s jumped into the perspective of that character. But I think you’re right in a bigger sense. It’s a very strange time. I’ve recently almost stopped engaging with news. It’s really weird, but I feel a lot better for it. But that is not healthy for society.

AD: It sucks because it’s really easy to understand where you’re coming from. There’s only so much stimuli or information that I could ever hope to process in any meaningful way. I feel like that’s really one of the reasons why when you just feel like you’re getting hit with it nonstop—you begin to lose the ability to tell what’s what. It just starts to become one loud buzz. 

Bret McKenzie: It’s a bit of a weight that you’re carrying around. There’s another song on the record called “Eyes on the Sun,” that touches on a similar thing. It started with a friend of mine who was talking about this idea of people having two different opinions and seeing society. I could see she was stressing out about all these big issues that we were all faced with: social change and climate change. But at the same time, she was so broken by things that were so conceptual, even though they’re real at the same time. It’s like, “That’s not helping. It’s not worth you being actually making yourself sick in a way or wearing yourself out thinking about these things that are real, but abstract.” In reality, her day is carrying on regardless. And something in there was kind of what similar theme, I guess.

AD: Yeah, I thought about that song a lot too. I thought about when Donald Trump looked at the sun, which is a real thing that he did during an eclipse…

Bret McKenzie: He didn’t wear the glasses? 

AD: He didn’t, no. 

Bret McKenzie: He didn’t need them. 

AD: If you want to delude yourself or if you want to drive yourself crazy, you can just mainline the oblivion. You can sort of just tap into this thing where everything starts to feel apocalyptic. But for most people in the West, our lives are in a historical sense [incredible]. There’s never been a better time to be a human being in so many ways. But that’s not in any way to say that that’s not a real thing. It’s like you’re saying these are all real things, but they’re also sort of conceptual. And they’re also these sort of things that sometimes the conceptual model, we start to mistake the map for the territory or that sort of thing. We lose the ability to do anything. That’s a beautiful song, and I liked the way you were able to write about it, but without being heavy-handed, it doesn’t feel like you’re sort of putting your thumb on the scale in terms of how these songs are telling you what to feel. I really liked that about the record. 

Bret McKenzie: That one has a bit of JJ Cale to it. I always love a drum machine. Someone told me the reason he’s got a drum machine on that record is he couldn’t afford a drummer.

AD: 100%. JJ Cale and Sly Stone, both are examples of guys who could so thoroughly groove with a drum machine that it’s mind-blowing. 

Bret McKenzie: Shuggie Otis! 

AD: Another one. I love the way you’re able to take all those different textures. On the last record, Songs Without Jokes, you’ve got that song “That’s L.A.” You already brought up Steely Dan via mutual collaborators. I felt like that one wears its Steely Dan influence on its sleeve a little bit. I love Steely Dan. What have your experiences with Steely Dan been like? Have you seen them live? Have you ever got to meet those guys?

Bret McKenzie: No. There was a time that was Steely Dan was playing live a lot about 10 years ago. I feel like they were doing so many tours and so many gigs, and I just thought, “Oh, I’ll catch them when I’m in the same town.” And then Walter Becker passed away, so now I can’t see them. And that’s a real lesson of definitely go see those artists that you love, because you feel like they were all going to be around forever, some of those bands, but obviously not. 

AD: Yeah, I know what you mean. I got to see ’em twice sort of in that era. I went with my dad, and then I saw them in Los Angeles at the Hollywood Bowl where they had Elvis Costello as their opener. At first it seemed like a very kind of odd combination to me, but live, I didn’t think that so much. I was like, “No, there’s a lot of overlap here. They both kind of write a lot about weird and seedy characters acting out and strange people,” so it worked really well. But Steely Dan is one of those bands that I feel like now it’s maybe much cooler to say you like Steely Dan, but it wasn’t necessarily that way. 20 or even 15 years ago…

Bret McKenzie: Liking Steely Dan was not cool at all for a while there. I’ve always liked them, but it turned people off. It was too jazzy and too proggy for a lot of people. They couldn’t handle it. But I’ve always loved it. I love the kind of wry comedy of it all. I love that Donald Fagan solo album.

AD: Oh, The Nightfly.

Bret McKenzie: The Nightfly, yeah. “I.G.Y. (What a Beautiful World).”  “What a glorious time to be free.” So it’s so good. There’s so much to love. “Who is that gaucho and that spangled leather poncho?” The lyrics are so funny, and I love the history of them being songwriters, writing for other people. And you see, I think there’s something about them, Randy Newman…Nilsson, not quite so much, but a little bit. 

It’s like they didn’t want to drive down the main road. They set their GPS to “no highways,” and that’s how they wrote their songs. And it put a lot of people off. Elvis Costello actually has a little bit of a similar thing. There are these songwriters who could write a hit, but they would get to the bridge and they would do a handbrake turn and do the craziest move, they wanted to play with the structure and the form. It was really inspiring stuff, I reckon. It’s like Todd [Rundgren] is another person who does that—he does these insane moves in the middle of a song and you’re like, “This is a hit song.” And then “What the, where’d he go?” It must’ve driven the label nuts! 

AD: I mean, Todd’s one of those guys, you hear a song, I dunno, “I Saw The Light,” or so many songs, “Bang The Drum All Day,” that huge hit that he had. He could just casually toss that off. And then I’m sure somebody at the label was like, “We’re back in the money printing business with Todd!” and then he’s like, “Next album? A seven-part symphonic rock odyssey about Madame Blavatsky and Theosophy.” You know what I mean? 

Bret McKenzie: I love that about him. I feel like people obviously feel like I’m changing lanes. Conchords is really popular and I spent a long time doing Flight of the Conchords and touring and performing live. It’s the comedy music lane. And I think for the audience, I underestimated how challenging it is for people to hear someone they know doing comedy, doing not comedy or vice versa, or just people jumping into a different lane

Making music or making things, whatever you make, you’ve got to keep making new things. And so that’s kind of what I’m doing. People like Rundgren are inspiring, because you’re like, “That is crazy, but it’s kind of awesome.” And you never know. You just keep turning corners. Particularly music, you’ve got to just keep making it. And you don’t have any idea. All the stories of people in studios, they don’t know which one was the hit. I have a hunch that a lot of the great songs, a lot of great recordings with the song the band was, they’d been in the studio all day and then they were kind of getting bored and they’re like, “Oh, let’s do something else.” And they knock out that other random thing. And I honestly think that is often where you get some magic happens.

AD: Were there any songs like that on this record for you? Where it came together late in the game? 

Bret McKenzie: We added the John Prine cover. “That’s The Way the World Goes ‘Round.” I’ve been talking to Drew Erickson, who plays keys and arranges the strings. He’s a great L.A. musician. And he’s doing a lot of producing now. He said, “You should do a cover on the record.” He’d been reading some book about some legendary ‘70s producer, and he said they’d often put a cover on there. And I was like, “Okay, I’ll do that. That’s a good idea.” You’re supposed to just pick a hit. But I just picked a really rare John Prine song, but something about it felt like it connected with my music. It’s kind of funny. It’s kind of uplifting. Wwe recorded that, I think in the last week of the sessions. I just got the band and we tried it a few different ways. At first it sounded too country, too much like the original and then reworked it with a bit of a different feel. And I think in terms of a recording, it’s a real little gem.

AD: You’ve brought up Todd Rundgren, Steely Dan, and John Prine. All of them are funny. Lyrically, they are all interesting and humorous. A lot of Steely Dan stuff is laugh out loud funny when you read it. There’s a song by Walter Becker on one of the late-era records, and it’s called “God Whacker.” I don’t know if you know this song. It’s about a group of ninja assassins who are going to kill God and literally called “God Whacker.” It is so stupid, so silly, so funny. I just crack up every time I play it. What a singular joke that he was willing to commit to. 

Bret McKenzie: I just think I’m going to definitely check that out. I think in the way that records are made now, it’s harder to get that because you don’t just do a silly song for fun. It tends, you’re not in the studio long enough. There’s time pressures. You do one song, do you know what I mean? That’s a song you do when you’re doing 15 other songs. That’s not the song you do when you’re going in just to do one song. This is seriously what we’ve come here to do, but I do think there’s something kind of magic about it. I love that. That was, even something like that was even being created is so great, so mad. It’s good.

AD: I think that the madder the better in terms of people making art.

Bret McKenzie: Sorry to jump in, but think how funny The Beatles are, how funny John is? So funny. There’s so much great video footage because they filmed so much. Their press conferences are straight up comedy routines. They’re so funny. I found touring with comedians and also touring with musicians in general, musicians that actually often a lot funnier people than comedians. It’s a strange thing. A lot of the funniest people are musicians. 

AD: I feel like Jeff Tweedy said something like he feels like every rock singer wishes he was a comedian, and every comedian wishes that they were a musician. You mentioned that, to a certain degree, this is a little bit of a lane switch for you because people are used to maybe one thing from you with the Conchords, and while your songs, you’re not making joke songs. These aren’t goofs, but sometimes they are light, sometimes they are still fun. I don’t think that it’s like a Conchords fan should feel precluded from them, obviously. But it is interesting the way people get ideas stuck in their heads.

Bret McKenzie: Well, the last album I was kind of obsessively trying to not do jokes. I wanted to try that. It was new for me. And then this one, I was more relaxed about it. I was like, if I had a funny lyric and I liked it, I used it. And if I had a serious heartfelt lyric, I used it. I wasn’t trying to overthink it. And I think you’re right. I think Conchord fans will still enjoy it, but the songs are not written as Conchords songs; We would write to perform in comedy clubs. So you’re just creating songs that have to have punchlines and these songs, I absolutely am not focusing on punchlines. That’s not where the headlights are pointed.

AD: Music has always been, it’s long been a part of your work, your creative output. I actually hadn’t really ever explored the music of The Black Seeds, this other band that you were in. I think that, if I understand right, you left that group around the time that Conchords are starting to sort of maybe break in America. Is that about how it lines up?

Bret McKenzie: Yeah, that’s exactly right. When Conchords was starting off, I was also in a band called The Black Seeds, and we were a big nine-or-10-piece kind of live reggae funk band that was doing huge outdoor festivals and touring, kind of blowing up in New Zealand and Australia. And at the same time, I’d do a Black Seeds tour, then I’d do a Conchords tour. I’d sort of do back to back, do both. And then Conchords started getting the work overseas, and I think that’s when I stepped out of The Black Seeds band. But a lot of The Black Seeds experiences were kernels of stories that we used for the Conchords TV show, because I was touring with a real band. I had real life, crazy, weird stories that you get.

One time The Black Seeds did this festival in Australia. We got back to the accommodation. It was a house that the band had rented. So the 10 of us are all strewn around the house. There was a spa pool. When we got to the house, everyone said, “Wow, this is a really nice house.” Normally we’d stay in backpackers or hostels and things, and at about 3:00 AM, this family arrived home and it was mom and dad and two kids. And somehow it was pre-Airbnb. Somehow the agent, the real estate agent, had given us the key to the wrong house and we’d gone into the wrong house. So this family arrived home having driven seven hours across country home to find 10, 20-year-old dudes just strewn around the house.

And then luckily, our manager was a really smooth talking dude, and he convinced this family to let us stay there then. So we spent the night in this family’s house. But yeah, stories like that, they’re germs for creating stuff. I was the keyboard player, wrote a few of the songs, and it was just that time where we were finding our way. That was also where I became interested in the recording studio because weirdly New Zealand…just for context, reggae music in New Zealand is kind of like rock music in America—or maybe even country. Outside Jamaica, New Zealand has the highest sales of Bob Marley records in the world. Reggae it’s the music you hear playing when you’re out, in the background. And it’s also really cool for studio stuff because it’s really studio based, all the techniques of dubbing, and it’s like taking a song, but then the studio is a big part of the sound. So we were just learning how to record, learning how to use Pro Tools, learning how to use delay machines, and it was a really fun time just beginning to explore how to work in a studio. It was cool for me.

AD: I mean, that’s awesome. Dub music is purely, the studio acts as an instrument. It’s a part of the band, right? Somebody like Lee “Scratch” Perry and the stuff he made at the Black Ark, I mean, just mind blowing stuff. I’m curious, were you guys playing shows alongside jam bands and stuff too? Like funk and jam? Was that sort of cross-pollinating?

Bret McKenzie: Yeah, we were more of a funk jam kind of band. Long guitar solos. The crowd is dancing. It’s like party music, so fun to play because the aim of the band is to get people dancing and having a great time. It was really a different thing for me and weirdly, when I was a teenager, I was really into James Brown. That was one of the first bands that I got most excited about. I remember my friend played it to us and we were playing music, and that was where I listened to the speakers and I was like, what? I was looking at the speaker going, “Huh, what is that?” I felt like I couldn’t see, I couldn’t understand how they were possibly playing these feels. And I was about 14.
