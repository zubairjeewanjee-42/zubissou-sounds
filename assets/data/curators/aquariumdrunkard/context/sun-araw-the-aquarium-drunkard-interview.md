## Body

Sun Araw’s Cameron Stallones doesn’t stick to one mode. Since 2007, he’s exercised extreme artistic freedom, creating tapestries of experimental pop, zoned-out soundscapes, MIDI-symphonies, and dub-inflected synthscapes. On his 10th album, Lifetime, he spent years manipulating his bandmates’ contributions digitally, blending improv and post-production, resulting in a florid space of clapping drums, guitar scrawls, and oozy synths, all aided by sunny, yacht rock ready melodies. 

He joined us to discuss the record, as well as his collaborations with legends like Laraaji and The Congos, and, oddly enough, the influence of Christian indie rock band Starflyer 59. The conversation has been edited for clarity and cohesion. | j woodbury 

Aquarium Drunkard: You reached out to me about Starflyer 59 after we had Jason Martin on the podcast. For me, SF59 was a gateway to a wider musical world: ‘80s post-punk, psychedelic pop, shoegaze. And Tooth & Nail Records, in general, also served as a gateway. Was it like that for you too? 

Cameron Stallones: 100%. They got you with MXPX and then they spun you out. [Laughs] My parents were evangelical. I was a little bit in that bubble. I remember reading an interview with Martin where he mentioned some bands he was into. My parents had records around the house, so I had a basic working knowledge of music, but I was like, “OK, I gotta find out who The Smiths are. I gotta find out who New Order is.” It was the beginning of a certain kind of record hounding that I still unfortunately can’t stop.

AD: SF59 works in sort of “eras” or phases. I would say that’s one thing that carries through to Sun Araw, you know, is that you sort of seem to like, you’re not. It doesn’t seem like you’re worried about shifting styles, changing, approaching things from different different angles. The sounds change but you keep in the mix as a throughline. 

Cameron Stallones: I feel like I just can’t escape whatever magnetic field has got pull over me. I don’t think my music sounds anything like [Jason Martin’s] at all, but at the same time, there’s something about that kind of continual need to burn it all down and start over every time. That definitely got deep into my veins. It’s what I look forward to in a certain kind of band. 

AD: This is the first album in a few years, and it’s really wild sounding. What did you want to do with Lifetime going into it? 

Cameron Stallones: I usually go in with a tight plan, but this one kind of surprised me the most. The previous record, Rock Sutra, was a tight record in my head. It was recorded with the live band lineup. I have a tendency to go on and on, but I really tried to keep it tight. I was like, “Single LP, let’s go, let’s get there and do it.” That you know came out right on the cusp of COVID. We had all these tour plans and like everybody just got atomized in the face of whatever going on. This one was just way more piecemeal, you know? I’ve been working on it for three years, but I haven’t had a burning desire to put it out. I just kept working on it. 

AD: You’re working with a lot of digital equipment these days. 

Cameron Stallones: I’m really into trash digital at the moment. And there’s a lot of trash digital on this one.

AD: When we had Scientist on the podcast, he couldn’t stop talking about how good digital technology is these days. 

Cameron Stallones: I have to agree. It’s been hard for me to swallow too, but people should be liberated by it. 

AD: You’ve worked lots of different ways, so I take it it’s not necessarily about just one thing or the other. 

Cameron Stallones: I made On Patron, which I get the impression sometimes is the one people like the most, on a tape deck from Goodwill. Sun Araw has always used garbage, as far as tech was concerned. And you know that that was a liberating thing to realize, you know, and and you do you have to be very thoughtful. If you just lug a VST into your Ableton and start playing on it, and then listen back, it doesn’t sound great. You have to know how to tweak, work and create, you know?

But at the end of the day, the tools are pretty neutral, I occasionally get emails like “What’s that sound?” And I’ll answer them, but also, don’t worry about it like that. That’s not the point. Like, because you’re gonna be disappointed when you figure it out. I worked with Pete Kember on a couple of records. I was dying, because Spaceman 3 was one of those bands that made me want to stop making music when I heard them. I was like, “Oh well, I guess..”

AD: …they already did it! 

Cameron Stallones: Right. So I’m working with Peter Kember, who when I’m introduced to him, I almost fell out of my chair. And he’s like “Man, I’ve been wanting to ask you forever, what’s this one sample” and it was me playing guitar into GarageBand, off the mic built into my iMac, and then pitching it down 12 steps. Like that is the sample. [Laughs] 

AD: You and M. Geddes Gengras have a label, Duppy Gun, but one of my favorite things about what you do is that it’s clearly dub music, but not trying to recreate some sound or emulate it. 

Cameron Stallones: It’s like when we got tapped to do that Congos record. I had no idea what to do. It’s still this thing where I can’t believe I said yes to that. But to go there and try to make “a reggae record” would be a disaster. It is a recipe for failure to try and emulate what some other culture does. I was glad that I was young and dumb enough to say yes to you, because even now, you know, I would just be like, that’s the worst idea I can imagine someone like me trying to make a record with someone like them. But they were so gracious, you know, I’ve talked about it a lot. But they were so gracious, meeting us musically in this interesting middle ground.

AD: I wanted to ask you about another collaborator, Laraaji. What was working with him like on 2016’s Professional Sunflow like? 

Cameron Stallones: It was really incredible. We just flew to New York, me and my buddy Alex Gray, who was my main music collaborator for about a decade. We figured we’ll have two or three days to improvise. We went there, set up our gear, and we didn’t really say anything to each other. There weren’t any pleasantries, no, “Hi, how’s it going?” We just set up, he set up some stuff, and we started making noise and then we just jammed for about eight hours without really speaking. [Laughs] And then we went on tour together the next morning. We saw him at the airport and he had two pretty sizable bright orange suitcases of gear. We were doing late nights, playing long sets at clubs,  and it was so inspiring seeing someone of that age have that energy, Improvising at that high of a level. Improvising is a huge part of my practice and a huge part of what I dig about music, period. It’s just this feeling of discovery, this feeling of exploration. Like, that’s what I want. That’s what I’m trying to get for myself. That’s what I’m trying to put on record and it’s kind of happenstance if it happens. And seeing someone be able to do that at that level night after night, with so little cynicism, so little exhaustion? 

I’m interested in spirituality, so being able to spend a lot of time with him and speak with him about where he’s coming from and how he perceives himself in relation to the cosmos [was fascinating]. A lot of New Age people come across very false—either deluded or somehow willfully ignorant to certain aspects of the self or reality. But stuff beams out of this dude’s soul with such a genuineness. His laughing meditation yoga sessions are a good example. He was teaching those sessions during the day. Alex especially was a wild man. He’s got an, I would say, decidedly anti-New Age-type persona. Anything that asks for that kind of open-heartedness can be like a big ask for him, you know? That’s not to say that he’s not a big hearted dude—he is. But that’s just not his natural affect or whatever. So we’re on tour for a few days, and on day four, we’re in the Netherlands and we walk into one of his laughing meditations. 

There are these older guys, business guys, I don’t know what that’s about. But they were trying to expand productivity through whatever. So he’s doing his thing, he’s laughing. People around him start trying to participate out of good will. And me and my buddy and other people present also start laughing at it, in a direct manner of like, “I’m laughing at this. It’s funny. I’m making fun of this.” But what’s great about it is within about 30 seconds, it doesn’t matter anymore, right? Because all of a sudden you’re caught up. You’re laughing and you’re doing the thing and he’s leading you through it. It just defeats like any ability you have to sort of try to position yourself ironically to it or with any kind of distance to it, because it leads you directly into it. And by the end of it, I was like a true believer in this practice. It’s like how you feel after you have a good cry—you release so many endorphins. I went in with utter cynicism and somehow you break through all of it.
