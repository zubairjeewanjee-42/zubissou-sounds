# Pat Metheny :: The Aquarium Drunkard Interview

**Pat Metheny — The Aquarium Drunkard Interview** · _2024-08-20_ · by Justin Gage · `interview`
**Tags:** Pat Metheny, Mixtapes

## Body

Guitarist Pat Metheny might be the only musician to win the Grammy for Best New Age, Ambient or Chant Album who’s also recorded at least one noise record. (Fact-checking cuzzes, prove me wrong!) Since joining pioneering vibraphonist Gary Burton’s band in his late teens during the 1970s, Metheny has released a truly overwhelming array of albums that explore a bounteous multitude of sounds, textures and moments. While primarily known for his eccentrically smooth yet rambunctiously buoyant work with the Pat Metheny Group in the 1980s, he’s also trafficked in dissonant squalor (1994’s Zero Tolerance for Silence), austere minimalism (1987’s Electric Counterpoint, composed by Steve Reich and later sampled by the Orb), ambient {1981’s As Falls Wichita, So Falls Wichita Falls with Lyle Mays) and collaborations with free jazz giants (1987’s Song X with Ornette Coleman and 1996’s The Sign of Four with Derek Bailey). And that’s not to mention the dizzyingly complex album he made using an array of self-playing instruments (2010’s Orchestrion), his work with singer/songwriters like Joni Mitchell and David Bowie and his recent small group dedicated to showcasing young musicians, Side-Eye.

His latest record, MoonDial, like last year’s Dream Box, is a languid solo affair. Using a baritone guitar, as he did on 2003’s One Quiet Night and 2011’s What’s It All About — both of which won the Best New Age Grammy — but now with nylon instead of steel strings, Metheny continues to innovate. At the same time, having just turned 70, his work has become more reflective, looking back at absent friends, bygone mentors and old songs, and considering how they shaped — and continue to shape — him. Entering the sixth decade of his career Metheny continues to make boundary-blind music that, though clearly in touch with a wealth of traditions both orthodox and esoteric, is unmistakably his own. | r jackson

Aquarium Drunkard: Miles Davis said something about how wanting to be a jazz musician in the late ’60s was like wanting to become part of a museum — as a kid in the ’60s who witnessed the arrival of rock and its eclipse of jazz as a popular art form, did it feel that way for you?

Pat Metheny: I am probably like most musicians in that I became a musician because I am a fan of music. My immediate response to the music I love is an almost overwhelming desire to understand why I love that particular thing so much. I think I benefited from growing up in a place [Kansas City suburb Lee’s Summit, Missouri] where there were not a lot of indicators to me of what was good or hip or cool or anything – I just took music for what it was based on what I perceived in my limited 10-year-old self as doing its thing on me or not.

AD: Does rock speak to you in the same way as jazz, or is it like two different languages?

Pat Metheny: Honestly, I have never really worried much about anything like that. Whenever people start talking about terms like “jazz” or “classical” or “rock,” or what my thing is etc. … my eyes kind of glaze over. I just don’t think like that. For me, music is one big thing. And in the general area of back-beat type music I think you refer to along the way in these questions, it seems like those sub-groups are almost more about a lifestyle thing or a dress code or some kind of cultural/political thing more than just the music. I never really cared about any of that. I always just focus on the music and anything that is really happening is interesting to me – whatever it is.

The musicians who I have admired the most are the ones who have a deep reservoir of knowledge and insight not just about music, but about life in general and are able to illuminate the things that they love in sound. When it is a musician who can do that on the spot, as an improviser, that is usually my favorite kind of player.

AD: MoonDial has a particularly intimate feel — was that a conscious choice? While I feel like all your music has a distinct emotional component, MoonDial seems to be more transparent than usual — was this something you worked to achieve, or just a product of your current artistic mode? 

Pat Metheny: Really most everything I have done in my life as a musician has mostly been under the banner of “bandleader” — but the kind of bandleader who is going to be the person who will use the band as vehicle for their own compositions. That has required a focus where a big part of my job leads to finding instruments and even musicians to create an environment that might get me to the kind of result I am hoping for, for whatever music I happen to be writing or thinking about at a particular time; and then doing recordings and tours that reflect that period, whatever it may be. Obviously, there have been a lot of different ways that that approach has gotten me to over the years. This period is that same thing. It’s just that in this case, I am the bandleader of a band where I am the only one in it. And in particular, in the case of this new guitar, my job as “bandleader” for this project was to find tunes that allow it to shine at its best, just like I always have tried to do with the folks I have hired to be in my various groups over the years.

AD: The low strings on the baritone guitar have a very synthy sound at times — is that something you had to work to achieve through tuning/production, or was it a natural effect of the special strings and guitar? Can you talk about what effect the instrument had on your composition?

Pat Metheny: The first baritone record was One Quiet Night, where I remembered a way of using a baritone guitar in a tuning that a guy down the street from where I grew up showing me when I was a kid in Lee’s Summit; where the middle two strings are tuned up an octave. With an instrument like that in hand, a whole new world opened up for me. I was able to get to voicings and sounds that are just different from a regular guitar, but also requires an almost completely different way of thinking. A baritone in this configuration is more like an instrument that within itself contains three two-string instruments that just happen to be sitting right next to each other; the top two strings being in the viola range, the middle two in the violin range and the bottom two in the cello range. 

Given the nature of how a guitar is laid out, that means the “viola” player mostly has the melody and the violin player is often higher in pitch than the melody, but playing an accompaniment role. 

That kind of thinking always suggested more like what happens when writing for a string quartet or something. That in turn suggests a more classical guitar type sound – but I was never able to find the right strings to allow that tuning on a nylon-string-classical-baritone guitar until just before this tour began. When I finally cracked it – it was like another universe opened up in that already different universe.

I do try to follow the path suggested by each setting. If I have a band and one of the musicians is really good at this but not so good at that – I will write music for that band that is going to be more about this. Same goes for instruments – the nylon baritone likes hanging out in the zone that the tunes I picked to record in. In various sound-checks along the way those were tunes I found myself playing, I kind of kept a list and those were the ones I was drawn to record.

AD: You revisit an old song: “This Belongs to You,” from 2016’s The Unity Sessions. What drew you to reinterpret it? How does it fit in with this particular collection of songs [which includes standards and a Beatles number]? The Unity Band version sounds so smoldering, while this version sounds both gentler and pricklier, somehow. How did this one take shape as you reconsidered it? 

And what about “Falcon Love” — does that have anything to do with the “The Falcon and the Snowman”? [The soundtrack to the 1985 film composed by Metheny and Mays that included the Bowie collaboration.]

Pat Metheny: This Belongs to You is kind of a rare tune that I wrote on guitar. I almost always only write stuff on piano, an instrument many times easier than the weird issues a guitar has. For that reason, I discovered in a sound check one day that it happened to play particularly well on the nylon baritone. And it has a blues form in the middle which was a nice place to get to for improvising. And yes, in the Falcon film there was a much bigger love story that ended up getting cut, and that was the theme for it that was never used. One of the best flamenco guitarists is Vicente Amigo — one of my favorite musicians. He has been starting his concerts with that theme for years and I had never played it at all, so this seemed like a good chance to look at it.

In many ways, my primary interest is always drawn to what might be possible in the realm of improvisation. But I think there is a kind of mythology around the word itself. When improvising on a composition, say a standard, there are a range of approaches that can work. Some may go far away from the original intent, while others may follow the elements of the original composition more closely, almost to the point where it sounds like an extension of the composition itself – so it gets blurry.

In a way, the better you get at that kind of improvising, the harder it is to tell whether it is improvised or not.  Like with sound and orchestration, I like having a wide berth to explore those varieties. But in this setting, with this guitar, it is more about going deep rather than going wide. 

If I had to play something like Giant Steps in all 12 keys with a great player like Mike Brecker or something, I would not be picking up a baritone nylon string guitar to do that. But what I can get to with this instrument in a solo setting is something that I wouldn’t be able to get to in any other way.

AD: I am also struck by the Return to Forever song (“You’re Everything”) — for something with such a wealth of potential, you kept it pretty short and down-tempo — you pack a lot of complexity into the second minute, but it’s still noticeably brief, compared to the original. What was your relationship to this song?

Pat Metheny: I think Chick [Corea, of Return to Forever, among many other rewarding ventures] was one of the greatest composers of our time. That tune harmonically and melodically is just packed with meaning for me. The first Chick recorded version I think you are referencing has the same elements first in rubato form and then in time. That is a tune that could be done a hundred different ways and still be what it is. That is the mark of a great tune. My version here is thinking about how much we all miss him.

AD: I’m a big fan of your albums with Derek Bailey and Ornette — have you considered doing another project like that, where you work with someone a little more out? Are there any contemporary players or bands (or producers) you’d like to work with? I’m particularly thinking of a band like Wolf Eyes, who recently played with Anthony Braxton and are named after a Paul Winter album, but who usually operate as a noise group — a band that would resonate with your more holistic approach to genre. Do you keep tabs on that stuff?

Pat Metheny: I am kind of permanently stuck on “output” because I have so many ideas and projects going at the same time, so my “input” time is limited. But I will check out the folks you mentioned. That is usually how it works for me – I try to listen to folks that someone might mention to me. I do keep close tabs on the new folks around NYC on instruments that might be candidates for something I might want to do. Those candidate folks kind of need to be complete kinds of players who know a lot about harmony and the elements that make up the language that follow what I guess we would call the bebop and post-bop thing. If that part isn’t there, it takes a visionary kind of musician to transcend to a whole other thing like the guys you mentioned — that is very rare.

AD: And how do you think about that sort of thing in relation to your more typical material?

Pat Metheny: I always try to let the music at hand decide what direction I go in in terms of orchestration. I am pretty happy to play in a really dense way, or a really sparse way, or really loud or really soft or all over the dynamic range, really inside the chords or outside the chords – it kind of doesn’t matter too much for me – it is whatever seems to sound best for what is happening at that particular moment.

So, John Philip Sousa and the Beach Boys and Ornette and Henry Mancini and Miles and the Beatles really had just one thing in common for me — and that was that they were people who I heard by way of record albums. Beyond that, I didn’t worry about it too much. It seemed to me that there was way more overlap than differences in how it was registering to me.

AD: You’ve been touring a lot lately — any plans for another live album, or perhaps official releases of old live tapes, like a Metheny Bootleg Series? I’ve only seen you once, but the music breathed very differently live, and it changed the way I listen to your music — the distinctions between vastly different styles become dissolved, for one thing.

Pat Metheny: A good musician I know in NYC said something to me the other night that got my attention. He said I needed to play at the Vanguard again for a month or something since, as he put it, “there is a generation of folks who have never heard you really play” … and I know what he means. Playing a concert in a big place, which is mostly what I do now, is a thing, but not that thing. I just recorded a bunch of trio concerts with Ron Carter in a small place, I think that will come out and is more like what I think my friend is talking about.  Regarding a bootleg series … I have so many things ahead in general, I don’t feel any interest in looking back. I suppose I do have tons of stuff though. There was a great tour in Japan from when [pianist] Brad Mehldau and I did that thing years ago – the officially released record [Metheny Mehldau, from 2006] we made was at the beginning of our time together, and that Japan tour was the end — we got a lot better as we went.

AD: I only recently learned that [jazz guitar great] Jim Hall taught guitar to Far Side cartoonist Gary Larson, and that Bill Frisell did the soundtrack to a Far Side animated special. [Larson also created the cover art of several jazz albums.] Have you ever met him, or do you have any cartoonist connections?

Pat Metheny: I got to meet Gary Larson one time which was really cool. He came to a concert and after the gig came on our tour bus for a little while. I joked with him that I could see him coming up with lots of Far Side material using a tour bus as a setting and could see his mind at work!

AD: [Jazz drummer] Bob Moses [who played on Metheny’s first album, 1976’s Bright Size Life] recently came through KC, and he told a story about being picked up once by someone in a car from your family’s old dealership, and they’d put a batch of fresh-baked cookies in the back so the whole car smelled delicious. Do you remember anything about this? Is there a musical equivalent to this, do you think? Do you look for ways to sweeten the atmosphere of a vehicle, or is sweetness the last thing on your mind, and you just want to take people/yourself to wherever they/you need to go? (Yes, I know this is a ridiculous metaphor.)

Pat Metheny: Yes, he bought a car from my dad, and my mom made cookies for him. I just contributed something to a new recording he is releasing where he asked a bunch of his friends to take a page of music he wrote for us all and “do something with it.” This is not unlike what I did with Zorn’s stuff for Book of Angels TAP. Moses’s is called Peace Universal. Regarding your metaphor … I wish I had the ability to do something like what you describe and kind of envy folks who might be able to do something like that. In my case, once I am down in the thing, it is the thing itself that occupies every bit of my focus. I can’t do the A>B thing.

AD: Are there any roads not taken for you? Do you feel like there are avenues you haven’t explored that you want to explore? What remains to be done/what have we got in store for us?

Pat Metheny: I can barely sleep because I have so many ideas and things I want to get to. Already I have three records underway, all very different from each other. I mentioned the Ron [Carter] thing and I have a new Side-Eye III thing that I have been working on for a while that feels really significant to me. 

AD: Finally, what all Aquarium Drunkard readers really want to know: Have you ever choogled?

Pat Metheny: Well, back in KC in the early ’70s, even on the more straight-ahead organ trio type gigs, we used to play “Proud Mary” at the end of the night. CCR … a good band.
