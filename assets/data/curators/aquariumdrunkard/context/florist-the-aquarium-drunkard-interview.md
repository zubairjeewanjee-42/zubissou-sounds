## Body

Emily Sprague knows the end is coming. It’s inevitable and always has been, no matter how good or bad things get. The finite isn’t a source of anxiety for the singer/songwriter, but a catalyst for wonder. Jellywish, the new LP from Florist, serves to promote “how awesome it is that we basically don’t exist in the grand scheme of things,” according to Sprague. Rather than being a cause for nihilism, it’s a reason to make the world a better place, to tune into the quiet wonder of the day-to-day, to live authentically. Death surrounds the album’s lyrics, but it’s not born from fear. The band responds with faith in questioning and a deep-rooted, collective belief in the magic of existence.

Across 10 songs, Florist captures metaphysical absurdity, beaming gratitude, and a bedrock of love, using the jellyfish as a prism through which to refract Sprague’s musings. The self-described “friendship project” has toned down the ambient sprawl of 2022’s Florist, absorbed Sprague’s celestial solitude from Emily Alone, and reinvigorated the controlled explorations of their early work. Jellywish is a cumulation of ideas that moves beyond the sum of its parts. There won’t be answers to the fundamental uncertainties faced by all of humanity. There will be a gentle invitation into “dark and multifaceted existential waters.”

Aquarium Drunkard spoke with Sprague about hydrozoa, absolution, skateboarding, empathy, poetry, and more. This conversation has been edited and condensed. | a eleanor

Emily A. Sprague: I’m having my second coffee and just kind of being…Wednesday.

Aquarium Drunkard: How does one be Wednesday? 

Emily A. Sprague: Well, it’s a personal thing, right? Like, just being in the moment of the day. I’d like to try and be in the actual moment of the day as it continues.

AD: I wanted to start by asking about the seeds of your musical practice. How did your connection with music initially form and how has it shifted as you’ve continued to explore and create?

Emily A. Sprague: When I was coming into my consciousness, as we do whenever we’re, I don’t know, 12 or 13…when I was starting to have my own interests and opinions, that was the beginning of art-making for me. I’ve always been drawn to different creative ways of expression. I was kind of a skate and snowboard kid when I was growing up. Watching skate and snowboarding videos and seeing that some of the skaters were also artists was one of the first times that I made a connection about life being dedicated to [making art], which for me was about creating emotions to flow out so other people get to experience their own. Throughout my childhood and teenage years, that became a dedication to a life of being a catalyst in the world for thoughts and feelings. When I was younger, it was much more of a tool for my own processing. I felt it was possible to be as melodramatic or honest as my feelings were in this socially acceptable way through making art. The friends I have now are people who can talk about things that are real, difficult, and emotional. I’m sure all of us who are interested in art have that. But when you’re starting to navigate the world and figure out how you want to live, it doesn’t always present itself as an option. You have to put that stuff aside. We have “entertainment time” and then we have “regular life time.” Entertainment and art are wrapped up in industry. 

When I was a kid, music was what I landed on. My feelings could blossom out of me through music. Music was also something I was able to do by myself in my bedroom. I wanted to paint. I tried painting, but the capital-A art world, the white box, that whole thing was… I was very anti-establishment and anti-authority even from a young age. It always irked me. Music feels like this beautiful, super accessible format. Anyone can do it, anyone can participate in it. Music is essentially free, which I think is beautiful. It was the tool that felt the most accessible. I wanted to write songs and one thing led to another. I was so young when we started touring and now I’m at the beginning of my 30s. Back then and throughout the last decade, I was just doing something that I felt super driven to do. But the stakes weren’t as high. There’s the philosophy that David Lynch called “the art life.” I feel committed to that. It’s a belief system and I want to spend my life [making music] because I believe that’s my role in the world, that’s why I feel I’m alive. I will keep doing it even if there’s nothing to do it for.

AD: When you’re young, you don’t have the language to describe something like that. You wouldn’t call it a philosophy, you’re just exploring these things that create feelings. As you continue down that path and as time progresses, you can assemble the points and guiding principles for your own art life, to use Lynch’s term.

Emily A. Sprague: I knew I would never stop, even at that age. It’s the only path in my life that I’ve never deviated from. It will always be there for whatever reason. 

AD: It’s an outpouring that we can continually summon and never really vanish, which I think is special. Since you make music that comes from both structured and unstructured points of view, comparing song-driven work in Florist and the ambient music you make solo, how do those approaches intersect and mutually inform each other for you? They are distinct, but creativity coming from the same person will naturally have some overlap regardless of form.

Emily A. Sprague: I can only speak for myself but I know the rest of Florist is more or less rooted in this mindset. For me, with making songs, maybe they’re in a sort of folk-pop genre if we’re being categorical, the goal was never to write a certain kind of song. I wanted to write something that felt like I was standing my ground, talking from my point of view, and trying to create feeling and thought through music. It’s natural to do it gently since I’m not an aggressive person. There’s a place for music that communicates its message with or without words, having something be really long or having an A section, B section, C section — it’s all just the same soup. I think of it as different versions of the same kind of effort. 

There’s a part of me that wants to be a bit disruptive. As humans, I think we’re restricted by the rules that we give ourselves for what people have to be, what music has to be, what art has to be, what a job has to be, and so on. I don’t see the point of all of these restrictions. I find all that totally unnecessary. It contributes more to the fascist risk of choking out our abundant, beautiful world just so people can have control. It’s also part of the philosophy of the art I make to have question marks and push boundaries or to encourage other people who are going to be paving the way for another kind of music. I want to challenge the norms and be non-conforming in all these different ways. It wouldn’t necessarily be obvious; for me, it feels powerful if it’s more subtle. That is a huge part of why there are improvised instrumental pieces in Florist’s music. We’re not restricting ourselves to any one thing. Everything that I’ve done so far musically feels like it’s just the tip of the iceberg. I’d like to make some really different stuff in the future.

AD: Humanity’s obsession with creating restrictions is like inventing a box to put yourself into just to feel like you have control over something, to have a space of false authority that crushes everything outside of it. The resistance to that is very subtle in the music, but it’s present in the soup. Questions prompt further questions, not necessarily answers.  Art and life are more implacable and subtle than that. Given your resistance to definition and how much awe there is in your music, often at very simple, daily things, how do you find yourself taking in moments of small wonder and how do they inspire your music and daily practice? 

Emily A. Sprague: I genuinely love being alive. I have my own struggles, but I think that being alive is absolutely a trip. It’s amazing that we’re here to play and have fun and look and be in awe and experience joy. I find it fascinating and I’m frustrated by how we have to fight to prioritize it even being a part of normal life. We can humble ourselves by thinking about what we are on this planet and in the universe and appreciating everything that’s around us. That contributes to finding my own sense of peace and happiness amidst whatever is happening. It’s a part of Florist’s music because it’s something that the four of us share. We all love the profound little wonders that happen in life, that can happen between people or just by looking outside. Florist’s music is hugely about capturing those feelings and trying to promote them. We try to promote how amazing this little illusion that we’re all in can be. There’s some power in that. We need to remind ourselves to do these things as well, to appreciate what and where we are. It’s a very grounding way of looking at existence for me. We can put another little drop in the bucket of trying to shift this world into a more symbiotic place. 

AD: Every paradigm shift consists of these small things, like a hundred million drops in the ocean. Does it feel like an obligation to have these tiny drops in the ocean appear as a result of your music? It feels very intentional even as it’s something you’re drawn to explore.

Emily A. Sprague: It’s how I want to live my life. I don’t feel like I have any kind of duty. I don’t see myself as this bringer of responsibility to the world. We’re all trying to give what we can and do a little bit for the paradigm shift, which is something that I still believe is possible with humanity. It takes a lot of different kinds of effort. I’ve always felt like my strength is not in being loud, but in being quiet. Florist will be contributing and speaking out in our own way with any political specifics, but there’s not going to be something like a song title that’s pointing to one issue. I can be a bit more effective in this way, nudging everything a little bit or planting those seeds for people who may or may not already be thinking about it. It’s all about empathy. Florist usually comes back to this idea and I feel like empathy is one of the most important keys to the utopia (laughs).

AD: Jellywish deals with feelings of cosmic insignificance. In the truth of it, we simply are that small, but it doesn’t mean we’re insignificant. You’re asking questions and leaving them unanswered. It’s not nihilistic, but it’s accepting of this inevitability. How did these ideas of smallness and endings inform the record?

Emily A. Sprague: So much pain comes from the fact that we don’t always remember [how small and finite we are]. For me, there’s so much comfort and beauty in it. It opens up doors to have so much fun in this life when I’m rooted in this idea of the blips that we are. It’s not that it’s meaningless, but it could be so much better. And it ought to be! Because of how small and insignificant it all is in the grand scheme of things. There’s so much pain and suffering on Earth because there are humans who feel like their consciousness or ego is the center of the universe. Oh man, it sends out waves of really awful energy. On the world stage, there’s so much active nihilism. We’re promoting how awesome it is that we basically don’t exist in the grand scheme of things, so let’s try to make everything better. Life doesn’t have to be mangled by all kinds of pain and suffering.

AD: The smallest things can and should be celebrated rather than mourned, and we’re letting people continue to mangle because the smallness can be so paralyzing sometimes. You’re inverting that — the smallness is actually incredible. We’re just blips and it’s wonderful. Insects aren’t too concerned about how small they are in the grand scheme of things. They’re just physically small and continue to live. Obviously, humanity has a bit more consciousness…

Emily A. Sprague: Maybe too much.

AD: The greatest danger to humanity is our own minds.

Emily A. Sprague: I’ve been thinking a lot lately about how we’re at this point in evolution where we have emotional intelligence as a species and we’re evolved enough to make it very difficult to be a natural thing here while also having the intelligence to seek what we want and try to find harmony within each other and the world. We’re not evolved enough to not be struggling with it anymore. It really feels like an odd place. It was probably better when everything was just a bit more primitive and maybe things will be better if we make it another million years. I don’t even know, a couple million years? How old are humans? 

AD: Maybe if we make it through another repetition of the cycle or even finish the cycle that we’re still tearing through. Humans haven’t found the nirvana we claim to be seeking. We’re still in the “punching the wall and getting confused and upset” portion of our cycle of growth. Hopefully, it’s interstitial. There’s also the lyric from “Gloom Designs” about how “the computer is getting pretty smart,” but technology is a whole layer on top of that. How have you explored the sense of magical and dreamlike interconnectedness, through Jellywish and beyond? How did it inspire the record?

Emily A. Sprague: First of all, I believe that we are multidimensional in our consciousness, in quantum physics and the study of time, all that kind of stuff. What we see and feel where we are right now is just the tip of the iceberg. I truly believe that there are worlds, possibly within ours, overlapping or interfacing, even going back and forth. I obviously don’t know, but I believe without a doubt that this life is not just what it seems. That connects back to all the themes of death in Florist’s music and all the existential…not woe or despair, but questions and curiosity and wonder and magic as well. The simple answer is that I’m just a fantasy kid and I love magic and fantasy, I love sci-fi. That stuff is so powerful, and it’s such a great way of looking at those worlds and how they relate back to ours. It’s part of the music because it’s one of my favorite ways to embed art with these things that we can promote a little bit more to be normalized. We are consciousness in a body, not a body with consciousness. It’s a tool to find a bit of peace and a way to make sense of all this. I find comfort in it, maybe not everyone does, but the goal of Florist’s music is to insert these ideas, not in a scary way but in a “this is actually really cool and wonderful” way.

AD: Incredible things happen and sometimes they’re inexplicable. We can ask existential questions without nihilism, not necessarily expecting a clear answer because there isn’t one or we haven’t found it yet. There’s firmness in believing that.

Emily A. Sprague: If we make these ideas more commonplace, in conversation and how we interact with each other, it brings us closer together. It’s a way to connect more closely, which is a very powerful tool.

AD: We’re stuck in the perpetual space between knowing and unknowing. Sometimes we grow closer to whatever capital-K knowing could be, but often that shared unknowing is what we hold in common. Especially in this record, a lot of the lyrics and ideas behind it are trying to be anti-dichotomy, if that makes sense. There aren’t these absolutes that we’re stuck in.

Emily A. Sprague: No absolutes. One of my number one rules. No, it’s not a rule…because no absolutes. 

AD: The coexistence of chaos and wonder is a throughline in your music. Are there any takeaways that you’re specifically trying to put through with Jellywish? Is there even value in spelling it out when it’s already imbued within the songs? There’s so much we don’t know about what goes into a piece of art, but that doesn’t mean we can’t feel the reverberations of those things.

Emily A. Sprague: I wasn’t sure if it was going to be clicking with people. I think so far, based on interviews and conversations with writers that I’ve had, I feel that it is coming through and that makes me really happy. I think everything that we talked about, all those great questions and talking points that you brought about what drives this philosophy, makes me feel like it was quite clear in the music. I would just hope that it’s not reduced to being pleasant folk music or something like that. Sad folk music. That’s reductive, that’s the one thing I hope doesn’t happen. Beyond that, if you’re listening, I hope it’s pretty clear. You know what we kind of stand for and what we’re trying to promote with this music, which is everything that we talked about. 

AD: It can’t be reduced to a thesis statement. There’s not hope for any particular thing, rather that the multitude of things explored on this record, both sonically and lyrically, are perceptible and that people will connect with them. Before wrapping up, since you’ve talked a lot about poetry, are there any poets you’ve connected with recently or while making Jellywish? I think one of the first times I heard about Ann Carson was by reading an interview with you and I’ve really loved her work.

Emily A. Sprague: [Emily holds up an Anne Carson book] 

AD: Hell yeah. 

Emily A. Sprague: Just happened to be right next to me. I’ve enjoyed reading this collection of writings called A Shelter for Bells. It’s by this early 1900s German writer, Hans Juergen von der Wentz. It feels like if Florist was an old German naturalist poet dude. I highly recommend it.

AD: I like ending every interview with the same question: what is something you love about your art and creative approach?

Emily A. Sprague: That’s a really sweet question. I think vulnerability is something that I feel particularly proud of. That’s a huge part of what it’s about for me. It’s a place for me to have this courage that I have not always had in my life and don’t necessarily have outside of this format. All four [members of Florist] are wearing our hearts on our sleeves when we perform and bring this music to the public.
