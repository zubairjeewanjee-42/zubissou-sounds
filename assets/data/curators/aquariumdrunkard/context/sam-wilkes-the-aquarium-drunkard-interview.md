# Sam Wilkes :: The Aquarium Drunkard Interview

## Body

Jazz in Los Angeles is blooming right now. Thanks in part to concert promoters like Yousef Hilmy of Minaret Records, people across the city are hearing a wide range of improvisational music styles in bars, stores, churches, and gardens that now moonlight as jazz venues. Sam Wilkes, a bass player, composer, arranger, and bandleader, is one of the most sought-after musicians in that scene. His cast of collaborators—Sam Gendel, Louis Cole, Jacob Mann, among others—are regional luminaries. In the late 2010s, he toured with Louis Cole and Genevieve Artadi’s band KNOWER, which plays a fast, virtuosic funk that verges on video game music. Recently, the restrained, improvisational ensembles he leads have reached a large audience and earned him a cult following. His composition “BOA” scored a scene in a film starring Zendaya, “Malcolm and Marie,” directed by the creator of Euphoria. In 2023, Wilkes assembled a band that had never performed together to play two shows in Japan, which he recorded and released as a live album, out this fall, titled iiyo, iyyo, iiyo. The quintet’s performances in Japan explored a broad swath of the American songbook, from a version of “Rain and Snow”– an early 20th-century ballad later popularized by the Grateful Dead–to “I Wanna Be Loved,” a jazz standard that Dinah Washington recorded with Quincy Jones. | b berke

Sam Wilkes: It’s pronounced “E-O, E-O, E-O.” “Iiyo” is a Japanese word. I actually think it means good, but it was taught to me as meaning something like, “alright.” There are many meanings and many uses for it in the language, but it’s very specific on this album. 

I’d been touring in Japan regularly with these promoters. The person who is my main point of contact, this woman named Manami Kuriyama–one of the last nights of this tour, we took Manami and another friend out for dinner.

One part about Japanese culture which I really connect with is just a consistent showering of gratitude for any kind of interaction. And so when I took them out, they obviously said, “Arigato gozaimasu,” and all the Japanese phrases of thanks. And I was like, okay, hold on, “How do I say, ‘No, no, no, my pleasure. Stop, please. It was my pleasure.’”

At first they taught me a very formal way to say it, which I forget now. But then they started laughing and they’re like, “iiyo, iiyo, iiyo.” Which is like, “all right, all right, all right. Enough.” And in that moment, I was like, if we have a record here, that should be the album title. 

Aquarium Drunkard: So the tour that spawned iiyo, iiyo, iiyo: it was one set in Tokyo, and an earlier set at Festival de Frue in Kakegawa? 

Sam Wilkes: Yeah. What I didn’t know was that we’d be closing the festival, which was wild. It was Deerhoof, then us. And I was like, wait what? 

AD: So it’s not like an American music festival where they save the most famous act for last.

Sam Wilkes: They actually like ending the festival on a quieter, gentler note. They like the last act to be the late night, put a record on kind of vibe. 

AD: I’ve heard some lore about how this band got assembled. Is it true this ensemble came together by accident?

Sam Wilkes: My friend Manami, who I mentioned, reached out to me, and she was like, Wilkes, we want you to come play this year with the One Theme and Subsequent Improvisation band. That was an album I put out in October 2021 that featured a big group of musicians that included Chris Fishman. I was like, “Okay, let me reach out to everyone.” Everyone except Chris Fishman was unavailable. 

I have this other quartet and I have this other body of work that I’ve been performing at Public Records in New York. And I was like, “Maybe I could bring that quartet out, and Chris could be the keyboardist for that.” But I’m nervous about it because that ensemble hasn’t really played together. 

So I’m like, “Hmm, or should I just do this other band that just recorded a new album, which I love, which was a trio.” And I just couldn’t decide. The concept of combining those two bands never occurred to me, because they’re very different styles. It’s all people who are such unique voices on their instruments and who are such brilliant soloists. The thought of having three of my favorite soloists in the world in one band who are also the rhythm section, essentially, especially for my first band leading tour, that wasn’t even an option to me.

I remember I called a friend of mine, a brilliant graphic designer, an artist. I asked her, “When you have a client, and you have to decide between two options that you love, how do you make that decision?” She goes, “I don’t.” And I was like, “What do you mean?” And she said, “The decision will appear. I know it’s lofty, but the universe will show you what to do. The decision will be made for you, and all you have to do is seize it.” 

So I send off an email to Manami [proposing either the quartet or the trio]. And they wrote to my manager. They were like, “Hey, so Wilkes is going to be bringing these musicians.” And it was the two groups combined. 

There’s your decision. I remember laughing. I was like, “I have to do this. I am freaked out about doing it. But I’m going to do it.” I was very anxious. And what I do when I feel that way is, well, I have to put it into the prep work, and so I just prepared as diligently as possible.

AD: When you’re talking about prep work, are you writing out charts for them?

Sam Wilkes: Oh, of course. But this is a band of brilliant musicians, all of whom can read, who say, “I’m not going to look at the chart.”

Look, this is my first time bandleading on an international tour, and to bring that many people who I admire and respect together, and to be steering that ship for my first time. It was like, “Well, there’s only one thing I can do, is I can just prepare, prepare, prepare, prepare, prepare.” I’m going to have, obviously, all my playing figured out, and know what everyone else is playing. And I’m going to just have this vision of mine that will be executed as clearly as possible. But, of course, music doesn’t really work that way. 

AD: What happened? 

Sam Wilkes: We only have one rehearsal, and it’s in Tokyo, and it’s the day after we land. We are all very jet-lagged, and my stomach is going crazy, by the way. This is all happening while I’m having, like, severe tummy problems. I think it’s because Chris wanted me to have Panda Express with him in the airport at LAX. 

We have five hours to go through the tunes. I’m very quickly realizing that no one’s looking at the charts. Everyone knows the material, but there isn’t the precision that I was expecting. I saw that there were two options ahead of me: is it everyone nailing these arrangements, or is it everyone’s personal voice being entirely unfettered, not connected to anyone or anything other than the moment? And I’m like, “It’s the latter. It’s not nailing the arrangement.” Because if we start doing that, everyone’s going to be looking over their shoulder for my approval. And that’s not the way this should go. 

AD: You were making yourselves vulnerable to failure, and at the same time, vulnerable to success.

Sam Wilkes: Yes, and that’s where really interesting things happen. That’s why this record, I think, sounds the way it does, because everyone is reacting in real time. The only thing that I truly prepared, that everyone was clear about, was where in the song certain people come in. The only thing that we’re super aware of is not playing at the same time, generally. That allows us to have a huge spectrum of dynamics, so that when we all finally arrive together, there’s this moment of arrival that has true gravity. I was ultimately just like, “You enter here. You enter here. You cut out here. This is our peak in terms of volume. And everything else is open to the individual.” 

AD: It wasn’t until I saw a video of the Festival de Frue concert that I saw how intense the energy was onstage. You’re playing chords on your bass, straight on the beat. Your touch is light, your tone is mellow, but you’re headbanging and jumping around in your chair. What does it feel like to channel that much energy into so few notes?

Sam Wilkes: The great bassist Daryl Jones, who plays with the Rolling Stones, and played with Miles Davis, Sting and Madonna as well—his nickname is The Munch—he came into the masterclass when I was at USC for music school, and he was talking about economy of language as a bassist. And Daryl describes it as, “If a note doesn’t make me groan, then it’s not really worth playing.” That hit me like a lightning bolt, because I felt that already. If I’m not feeling each note with my entire soul, then perhaps I don’t play that note.

AD: Once I saw the energy with my eyes, I could go back to the recording and hear it in the music, but the album originally scanned to me as much softer, easier listening.

Sam Wilkes: Everyone is actually kind of screaming with their mouth closed on this record. Except a few times some of us actually shouted.

AD: The energy is red hot, but there’s a lot of restraint in the way you guys are playing, so the energy has to come out another way. 

Sam Wilkes: Yes, exactly. 

AD: With some of your other bands, I’ve heard you play hard and fast. Is it difficult to switch between a band like Knower, which opened for the Red Hot Chili Peppers, and the band on iiyo iiyo iiyo? 

Sam Wilkes: Hard and fast is an important part of life, you know what I mean? Just like restraint is. With Knower, there’s a level of precision and rhythmic accuracy and focus on pocket. That’s all totally present on iiyo Iiyo Iiyo, but it’s shrouded in space and restraint. 

AD: Do you listen to much ambient music?

Sam Wilkes: Not as much anymore, honestly. I used to be very, very into it. 

AD: I only ask because it seems like there are lessons you’ve taken from ambient music about leaving space in.

Sam Wilkes: Oh, undoubtedly, but I wouldn’t call it just ambient. The way I’ve moved through the world is really through studying music. It’s given me a purpose and an identity. And I just feel so nourished by learning about different musical traditions. And so studying the ambient tradition, and my love of John Coltrane and Pharaoh Sanders and Alice Coltrane and Herbie Hancock, and then getting into Peter Gabriel’s So, and learning about [Daniel] Lanois, and loving the Talking Heads, and knowing about Brian Eno, and then finding Laraaji through Eno. And then coming back to Alice Coltrane’s music after listening to Laraaji. And then learning about new music: Stockhausen, Steve Reich, John Cage. And Miles [Davis], of course, and Joe Zawinul. Oh, you can just go on and on, and you’re weaving in and out and — oh my god, wait, like, you know, I love the Dead. And on Jerry’s first solo album, Garcia, there’s kind of a pre-Lanois ambient moment on, “The Wheel.” And whoa, like, that’s life changing, you know? And so, is there an ambient music through line here in this music? Oh, my God, without a doubt. But just like there is with any other style I could think of, truthfully. Do you know what I mean? 

AD: You’re describing a “you are what you eat dynamic,” and you’re talking about what you eat.

Sam Wilkes: Exactly. It’s just infinite, you know, the amount of different influences that come in through the study of tradition. And everyone in the band is coming at it from that place. We’ve all studied so many different traditions.

AD: How would you go back and connect this phrase “iiyo iiyo iiyo” to what the records means to you?

Sam Wilkes: This really special thing happened. We were so happy we could go to such an amazing place like Japan, and be met with such openness and love. And the fact that we got to record it, and that the recording fidelity is great. The whole experience is kind of like a gratitude explosion. But then it’s ultimately still like, alright, alright, alright, come on, move on. Levity and a sense of humor is so important to me in all art. It’s an antidote to how serious the study of any craft can be. And so yeah, it’s kind of like, “We did it. Let’s go now.”
