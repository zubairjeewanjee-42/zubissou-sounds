## Body

“What they want you to be — yesterday’s hero, yesterday’s ghost,” Dean Wareham sings on his latest record, That’s the Price of Loving Me, released this spring on Carpark Records. But the album’s 10 masterful tracks prove beyond a reasonable doubt that Wareham isn’t fading away anytime soon. Bolstered by chiming guitars, sweet string arrangements and gorgeous backing vocals from Luna bassist Britta Phillips, it’s another masterpiece in a career full of them, stretching all the way back to Dean’s days with Galaxie 500. 

And speaking of which, That’s the Price of Loving Me is notable as Wareham’s first full collaboration in decades with genius producer Kramer, who lent his magic touch to those G-500 records from the late 80s and early nineties. All these decades later, Kramer’s skill for elegant arrangements (not to mention his keyboard skills) bring something special to the proceedings, giving Dean’s musings on politics, friendship, mortality, Gibson guitars and airborne toxic events a sparkling backdrop. During a recent chat, Aquarium Drunkard got the scoop on all of that and more. | t wilcox

Aquarium Drunkard: I saw Luna at the Teragram Ballroom right before New Year’s — and then a couple of days later, Los Angeles was on fire. How’d all that go for you? 

Dean Wareham: Smoke and ash. Just bad air quality and a layer of ash on the car and in the garden. But not too bad where we were. It looks like, “Oh, LA is on fire.” Yes and no. It’s such a huge city that, yeah, there were areas that were devastated, but for most of us, life goes on.

AD: I ask because the last track on your new album has an ominous vibe that seems relevant. Uncomfortably relevant, maybe!

Dean Wareham: “The cloud is coming for us all”! Yes. 

AD: I heard that one and thought that it was like a Don DeLillo novel in miniature — and then when I gave it a closer listen, I realized that it’s specifically based on his White Noise. 

Dean Wareham: Yeah, “The Cloud Is Coming” was written for Noah Baumbach’s adaptation of White Noise. Britta and I actually performed it in the movie —a very different version. We played two hippies in the evacuation center from the toxic spill. I wanted a song that used bits and pieces of dialogue or paralleled what was going on in the movie. And then Noah said, “Oh, just go look at some ’80s tabloids.” So that’s what I wrote the last verse about—”Behind the curtain East Berlin, they put a human brain into a chimp” [laughs].

AD: I hadn’t read White Noise since college, but I picked it up again after hearing your song. It’s funnier than I remember, but also sort of uncomfortably on the nose. 

Dean Wareham: DeLillo has a way of picking these topics that really resonate. The idea of the daily toxic spill, the “airborne toxic event.” And, of course, right after the movie came out, there was actually a train derailment in Ohio, where the toxic spill was just like that. And Underworld is about garbage collection—you know, the 21st century and the problem of waste management, which is looming over the planet, right? 

AD: It feels a little grim at times, but musically speaking, your new record is one of the most purely beautiful records you’ve ever made, I think. 

Dean Wareham: Oh, thank you. The cello player, Gabe Noel, classed it up a little bit. Britta and I had played a gig with him in town—a couple of songs as Harper Simon‘s backup band. And I told Gabe I was working with Kramer, and he said, “Oh, I’ve got to be there for that. I’m a huge Low fan.” So he came to the studio. He’d never heard the songs, and he just put cellos down on like four songs in three hours. And then Kramer asked, “Can you do another?” And he’s like, “No, I want to talk.” And he just wanted to tell him about how, when he was 15 years old, the Low records had literally changed his life and made him become a musician. And by the end of it, he was actually a little weepy. It was emotional. It was nice. 

AD: But yeah — Kramer! This is the first time you’ve really worked together for quite some time, right? 

Dean Wareham: It’s been in the back of my mind for about 10 years, because he kept reminding me: “It’s crazy we haven’t made a record together!” Right. I don’t know, life gets in the way, and everyone is doing other things. Finally, during the pandemic, he mixed a song for us—a cover of the song “The Carnival Is Over” by The Seekers. And he did a really great job. And then I just thought, “Let’s do this.”

AD: Did you send him demos? 

Dean Wareham: Before we started, I offered to send him demos. He basically said, “No, I don’t want to hear demos. If I hear demos and start thinking about the songs beforehand, then whatever ideas I have for arrangements, they just start to become more stock and more boring. It’s better if I just hear it and then whatever pops into my head, I go with that.”

AD: I guess you can trust him. 

Dean Wareham: There were times in the studio when I asked him: “Are you sure that works?” But he wouldn’t really brook any dissent. I told myself, “You know, it’s fine. Just go with it.” And in a way, it’s actually more pleasant. Instead of sitting around going back and forth with a producer, arguing about this and that, it’s more pleasant when someone really takes charge and says they know what they’re doing. Like, “No, we’re doing this, and then we’re doing this, and then we’re doing that. You’re going to play this.”

AD: And Kramer had you play all the guitars, right? Just like in the Galaxie 500 days? 

Dean Wareham: Right. Maybe that was his thinking. He’s just like, “No, no, you can do this.” Why? I don’t know. But again, I just went with it. That’s fine. And it’s quicker that way too. 

AD: How do you describe Kramer? What makes him an interesting and special person to work with?

Dean Wareham: When he puts his mind to it, he’s a brilliant musician and arranger. I would say that “arranger” is almost the top thing. He’s always saying “arrangement.” He’s thinking about that from the moment he hears a song. I mean, before we started, he said, “I’m not coming out here just to push the record button, right?” I told him no. 

I’m thinking about “Bourgeois Manque,” which is my favorite on the record. I had played Britta a demo of it. “This isn’t anything, is it? It’s just two chords, and I’m just sort of talking about this idea I have about politics.” But she said, “Oh no, that’s nice.” Then we got in the studio and started playing it, and it sounded great. And then Kramer’s like, “Okay, go out there and play the lead on an acoustic guitar.” So I started playing lead. He’s like, “Oh no, that’s too many notes. Keep it real dumb [laughs].” And then he added some more stuff, like this instrument called the tiple, which is like a mandolin, but it’s got 10 strings.

And then “That’s the Price of Loving Me” changed hugely. The demo of that song is really kind of quiet and subdued, so everything changed. For Kramer, making a record is about what happens in the studio. It’s not about trying to get to some pure idea that you have for the song or what you’re doing, right? It’s about embracing the accidents. Sometimes with a guitar solo—well, actually, with all the guitar solos—I had basically two takes. And I’d be like, “I don’t know. I could do that better.” And he’d say, “Nope, that’s it. We got it.” Well, he would let me do two of them. “We’ll do two,” and then he actually used both of them, creating kind of a blend. 

AD: Those Galaxie 500 records you made with Kramer way back when still sound so good and unique—and you guys recently released the Uncollected Noise New York collection. What’s it like hearing that stuff now? 

Dean Wareham: You know, even if those weren’t our best songs—and they were not our best songs—there’s something exciting in each song. It’s live. It sounds like us in that room. That brick wall room—Noise New York. That guitar sound. People always ask, “What pedals did you use?” I had one pedal. A Boss compressor, which then used the overdrive of my amp. It had an overdrive channel, so instead of an overdrive pedal, I just had that channel. Just those two things together, turned up too loud. Sometimes all you gotta do is dial in the right sound. And even if you don’t quite know what you’re doing, it’s almost like the guitar starts to play itself. If you let it. Right? There were many moments tracking the Galaxie 500 stuff where I’d be like, “Oh, I’m lost. I don’t know what I’m doing.” But you know what? Maybe it’s better if I don’t know what I’m doing.

AD: Speaking of Galaxie 500, I have to ask: is “You Were The Ones I Had To Betray,” which opens the new record, about the band? 

Dean Wareham: I didn’t sit down to write a song about Galaxie 500. But I was reading a novel by Julian Barnes, and he was talking about a close friendship that falls apart, the notion that one friend completely betrays the other one. Being in love with someone or being good friends is supposed to make you behave better to each other. But in reality, it often doesn’t work out that way. And you might say that that close friendship invites betrayal. So, then I was thinking: “Well, who have I betrayed?” I don’t really think I betrayed anyone. I just went on with my life. But obviously, some people, when you go on with your life without them, consider it a betrayal.

AD: It’s just a good, intriguing line: You were the ones I had to betray.

Dean Wareham:It’s a good opening gambit, I guess. John Fogerty says that about songwriting, right? The things you need for a song—you need a riff, you need a title. Maybe you just need the title. The title is the thing.

AD: Speaking of titles, I love the title track of the new one. I was trying to think if there’s anything that you’ve done in the past that was even remotely similar. What’s that song about? 

Dean Wareham: The first verse — “I dreamt that you were coming home / In a sprinter made of chrome.” It was sort of about our tour manager, Kiko, who died a couple of years ago. He used to pick us up in King’s Cross. He lived his life on the road, and it took a toll on his personal life.

Then, the second verse is more about me and Britta being on the road. I used to think when I was younger, “Oh God, that’s such a tired cliché— life on the road.” But hey, you know, hopefully, there’s enough poetry in it—like the “Ballad of John and Yoko.” That’s one I like.

AD: That one’s got [Fassbinder heroine] Veronika Voss in it, too. There’s a little German thread running through the album. 

Dean Wareham: Veronika Voss to rhyme with King’s Cross, yes. And then there’s the Nico cover, which is pretty obscure. “Reich der Traume,” which translates to “Realm of Dreams” or “Kingdom of Dreams.” It’s by Lütz Ulbrich, and Nico sang it on his solo album. It gave me a chance to practice my high school German.

There were only like four people in my high school German class. Nobody wanted to take German—I think they did away with it after I graduated. But the teacher had us reading Thomas Mann and Bertolt Brecht. Brecht was a major influence on my life, his poetry, the plays, and the politics. Sometimes you don’t realize until later what a major influence some of these teachers are who come into your life when you’re 15 or 16 years old.

But the German thing comes up again in “Bourgeois Manque.” The Thomas Mann story Tonio Kröger is one of his earliest stories. It’s about a writer—he goes back to his hometown, and they think he’s maybe a criminal. I think the police come too. But nobody recognizes him, nobody remembers who he is. At one point, his friend says to him, “You’re a bourgeois manque,” meaning, you’re an artist, but you’re also sort of bourgeois, and you’re not quite happy in either world. You’ve got one foot in each and you’re not comfortable around either kind of people.

AD: There’s a song about your guitar on the new record — a very tender love song!

Dean Wareham: It’s almost dirty. “I reamed you. I cleaned you. I waxed you. I rubbed you” [laughs]. I’d seen an ad for a 1968 ES-335, and it was like $5,700. And then they dropped the price considerably. So Britta and I went to the big Guitar Center on Sunset Strip and they had the guitar out on the counter, and there was a guy talking to the sales guy, and they had the pickups out of it, and they were examining every little thing—they were going through it for like half an hour while he hemmed and hawed. And finally, he said, “Nah, I’m going to think about it.” And I just walked over and said, “Did he buy that guitar?” The salesman said no. “OK, I’m buying it.” And then apparently the guy came back half an hour later [laughs]. I had snatched it out from under him. But I was going there to buy it, so I don’t feel too bad.

Later on, I was reading this poem from a book of French poetry that was about polishing a piece of furniture, a cabinet. “I’ve waxed you, I’ve given you my pain,” or something like that. And I was like, “Gee, that would be funny if I could write a song like that, but instead I could write it about this guitar and how I had to restore it.” And I know “reamed” sounds sexual, but there is such a thing as a reamer. It’s a tool to carve out the peg holes to make them a little bit bigger. 

AD: I wanted to ask you about something that’s not on your new record — “Snow Is Falling In Manhattan” by David Berman, which you, Britta and Sonic Boom did on your recent winter-themed LP. Did you know Berman at all? 

Dean Wareham: We weren’t close friends, but I’d hung out with him and corresponded a bit. A few years ago, I ran into him in Portland when we were up there on tour. He was there, hanging out with Stephen Malkmus, and they came to the show. Berman was like, “Oh, I’m making this record. But you know what? Nobody can make a good rock record after they’re 50 years old. It can’t be done” [laughs]. I think that was when he was recording with Dan from Destroyer, and that eventually fell apart, more or less. 

But when the Purple Mountains record finally came out, I sent him an email. “David, you know, you said that it couldn’t be done after 50, but this is your best record. This record is amazing.” He wrote back, “Well, thank you, but I think this guy had it right.” And he sent me this really mean review from Spin. 

But yes, singing “Snow Is Falling In Manhattan” in particular—there’s a verse in it that gets me every time, when he sings: “Songs build little rooms in time / And housed within the song’s design / Is the ghost the host has left behind.” It gives me chills every time. He’s the ghost and he’s the host. He left us this clue inside the song.
