## Body

“And don’t get it twisted, my heart’s still in it,” trills the Canadian songwriter Jennifer Castle in “Blowing Kisses,” the lead single from her seventh and latest album. Her voice is watery pure and slippery with vibrato, sliding in and amongst the words. 

That’s the song that made a surprise, lengthy appearance on the red-hot Hulu series The Bear, and also the one that best describes her instinctual, open-ended approach writing. “No words to fumble with/I’m not a beggar to language any longer/A state of mind/Only a god could come up with,” she sings, evoking the flow state of creativity that produced Camelot. 

In the following interview, we dive into that creative process, Castle’s love for gymnastics, her work with Owen Pallett, the myth and mundane-ness of Camelot and the way that great songs require an active collaboration between songwriter and listener. 

“I don’t want to teach anybody anything with a song. I’m not trying to steer anybody towards anything with a song. I’m not trying to be manipulative. I’m trying to let it out,” she says. “I must want it to come into being, so I just try to let it out as honestly as I can and then work from there.” | j kelly

Aquarium Drunkard: I’m hearing is a lot more pop and rock than on your previous albums, especially in songs like “Figure 8” and “Mary Miracle.” Do you think that’s fair? Is that something you were consciously aiming for?

Jennifer Castle: Yes, I think that’s fair to say. They’re a little bit hookier. I did want to push the tempos a little bit more. That could translate as bangers. I’ve written a lot of songs, and I’ll think I recorded it fast and then I’ll put it on and it’s like, no, it’s really chugging along. These feel a little more decidedly rocking.

AD: Was there anything going on in the background as you were writing these songs and thinking about them and recording them that shaped what this albums sounds like? In terms of what was going on in the world or in your life or things you were listening to or books you were reading?

Jennifer Castle: Yes and no. I was at home and not playing live so much. They were written in a quiet time when I was at home consistently, as I had been touring a lot before that. 

These songs weren’t bounced off anything.I didn’t sing a couple of them when I was out on the road. They really remained in my head until they were recorded. I still haven’t played many of them live. They remained kind of in this vacuum of my own imagination.

I think that gave them a quality of athleticism. I wanted them to leap places and get distances, because I wasn’t really getting places myself. They took on more of a geographical landscape quality.

AD: Since you weren’t playing them with other people, was it your vision for all the instruments?

Jennifer Castle: Oh, yeah. When I put this band together, they hadn’t heard anything.

I made a few demos.I made them very unlovingly, because I’ll get attached to demos. I don’t speak musical composition language, but the demos were just so they had some sense. And again, a handful of the songs, they had never heard, so we just went in for five days and really recorded them on the fly. I just had to kind of translate, “This one sounds like this.This one sounds like that.” They added their interpretations to that.

AD: Were they people you had worked with before?

Jennifer Castle: Yes and no. The singers yes. Paul Mortimer on guitar, yes. Mike Smith on bass, yes. Carl Didur and Evan Cartwright no. I did want specifically for there to be new people playing, and I didn’t play as much as I usually do. I just played acoustic guitar in the session. Carl played everything on keys and oftentimes I’ve played piano on records. I really stuck to the rhythm guitar. I wanted to play the way I’d been playing these songs at my kitchen table. And Owen Pallett on strings. I’ve worked with him before a few records back. So yeah, it was a bit of a mix.

AD: I’m hearing a lot of disappointment in other human beings in the lyrics, like in “Some Friends” and “Trust” especially. Were you feeling disillusioned about people?

Jennifer Castle: Well, not maybe with such a big brush. I did notice that myself but I felt more like I was standing up for myself. Although not every song was centered exactly around a personal experience. The narrator was standing up for herself and just kind of making a bit of peace with things.

I feel like “Trust” starts out with very personal, not just to me but to anybody listening. It starts out with personal emotions that we can all experience. I don’t want to say that I’m saying all those things and other people are not. I’m not the person. I could very well be the hypocrite. I could be experiencing jealousy.

Still, that song started out very centered on internal emotions and then it starts to ripple outwards into corporations and government and all these things. I was really linking our personal, emotional state of being with how that ripples out further into the world.

I think what you’re picking up on maybe is a kind of paranoia. I’ve written songs before that reconciled that feeling. I wanted to unreconcile it, to have that unreconciled state be a part of this record. To not necessarily make peace with that experience or get to the end of it to the point where you’re like “And then I had a revelation and everything’s fine.” I wanted to write from a place where you’re sitting in that confusion, that conflict. I haven’t really done much of that before.

AD: Tell me about “Lucky 8” and especially the video with all the archival footage of the gymnasts. What’s your connection with gymnastics? Did you do that when you were younger?

Jennifer Castle: I did. Just recreationally, but I really loved it. I really loved all those gymnasts and lots more. I love the Olympics, but not necessarily the corporate Olympics. When I was younger I loved the Olympic goal of all the best people in the world—whether it’s shotput or gymnastics or something else—gathering together for this good sportsmanlike, healthy competition.

When I recorded “Lucky 8” I really felt like it was tumbling. There was a breathlessness to the lyricism of the whole record. It just kept cascading.

Then it was time to make some videos, and I was making videos myself for the first time. I’ve never done that before. I walking and thinking, and I had that really quick idea.It’s gymnastics. It’s tumbling. I went home and started looking through the images, and that was the first video I ever made. I made it probably in a day.

When I was a little girl, like really young, I was really interested in flying. I would think, really, when I was young, that the key to flying was you jump before you land. Like there was an action you could take that you wouldn’t have to land. I felt like there was a real kind of girl spirit with that video. That they’ll just keep going. It really linked up with the energy of that song lyrically. It just keeps unraveling. I liked that. It really synced up. And then I thought it was really great to have that Olympian energy representing a part of this record.

AD: Very cool. Now you’ve always been loosely categorized as a folk artist. I feel like that’s less true with this album and was maybe never exactly true. How do you feel about that category?

Jennifer Castle: I think as a songwriter I’m not necessarily bound to being a folk artist. I think of myself more as a songwriter who could write any kind of song. I think that when I travel, it’s folkier. When I’m solo, you get the version of the song that’s written on an acoustic guitar, and all I have is what I can carry on my own back and sing with my own voice. The songs become this essential version of themselves. It becomes easier to categorize it as a folk show when I’m on my own. I just kind of share the songs in that way.

But on record, I feel like it isn’t bound to folk music, but then I’ll write a pure folk ballad, no problem. I’ll say that, and then I’ll be like, “Here’s a collection of folk songs.” But I’ll also write pop songs. I’ll write ballads. I’ll write songs that sound like they’re part of my inner rhythm and melody.

AD: You have such a beautiful voice. It’s really strong, and you have this tremendous belt when you need it, but also super clear and crystalline. I was wondering how you’ve evolved as a singer. Do you have formal training or are you self-taught? How did you get to where you are as a singer?

Jennifer Castle: I’m self-taught. It’s a lifetime project. I’m only starting to understand that I carry the instrument within. Because I started out being much more interested in lyrics and just saying the words. But I’m always singing. I probably sing more than most people I know in just the sing-song talky way. I’m around kids a lot. I think it’s an awesome form of communication. I think melody is an amazing gift to help another person understand quickly what you mean. It’s a tone.It’s a gift of tone.

Now as a person in my middle age, I do breath work, and I treat my body like it somehow has to carry the air. I do diaphragm breath work most days. Without overthinking it, but I do that regularly and I find that it really helps, keeping my diaphragm in good enough shape to be able to belt. I do like to belt.

AD: What do you like about it?

Jennifer Castle: I like to cast my voice. I don’t speak in musical terms, but I visualize distances and shapes when I’m singing. I think about how I have to reach my voice way over there. Or I’ll make sure it crosses a landscape that only I can see. I really do think in spatial terms. I’ll think, well, I have to get right up there, and then I have to come back down to here. I see it visually.

AD: I wanted to ask you about Owen Pallett. You’ve been working with him since Pink City and you do it again in “Earthsong,” which is one of the singles. How did you first connect with him, and what do you like about what he does for you?

Jennifer Castle: Well, we come from the same musical community in Toronto, so I’ve known him for a really long time. He’s been part of many different projects that I’ve listened to. I know him as a person. And then with Pink City, that was co-produced by Dave Clarke and Jeff McMurrich and me, and Dave Clarke was instrumental in talking with Owen. He said Owen is interested in helping with string arrangements. We sent him a batch of songs, and he sent us back sketches, and then Owen, when he sets his sight to contribute, he really takes it from there. He sent back the Czech Philharmonic. He was doing a session with them, and he slipped my songs in at the end of that session. I really, truly couldn’t believe it.

With Pink City, the strings have a hallucinogenic quality. I think of it as the songwriter sitting there with the guitar and the piano and then hallucinating these potential orchestrations. She can’t really make happen on her own, but she can think them. She can imagine them. I thought he really fulfilled that beautifully.

Then with this record, we’ve all grown. Years have gone by. When we asked him, he came back with some ideas, and it was so classic and understated and supportive. And then I was working a job in Toronto last summer on a commercial, doing production. He sent me a zoom link and said, “Check in at two.” I checked in, and I had no idea what was happening, and it was the Estonian Philharmonic, playing along to some of my songs. I mean, Owen must be uncomfortable now, but I’ll give him a big bear hug. You bring me orchestras. I don’t know how you have that magic skill, but somehow you bring orchestras to play on my songs, and it to me feels sort of miraculous.

AD: Yes, but he must have a different approach to music than you do, in that he probably writes it down and is connected more with this classical tradition, whereas you’re more instinctual about things.

Jennifer Castle: Yeah, totally. But he really values the instinctual. And he knows my music. He’s a listener. I think his arrangements on Pink City were grainier. They’re more cerebral, than on Camelot. I feel like the Camelot ones are more comfortable and a little bit more classic. I don’t know whether that speaks to where he is or whether he heard that in my songs, but that’s what he offered and I was like, oh yeah, that’s perfect.

AD: I’m intrigued by the image of Camelot and the song that’s called “Camelot,” which juxtaposes this really idealized image, whether you think of it as the Kennedysor King Arthur’s court, with lyrics that are about mundane and transitory things, “sleeping in the unfinished bedroom,” and so forth. How do you feel about the connotations of the word “Camelot” in the context of that song?

Jennifer Castle: I really thought a lot about that, and there have just been so many reasons it’s been satisfying to name it that.I’ve been writing kind of in a stream of consciousness. That line, “Back in Camelot, I really learned a lot,” came up in the song. Then I wrote that song, and then I let it be. It was a line in a song, and it kind of had a cool feeling to it.

Then I went to name the record, that was quite a bit later. I didn’t know what the name of the record was going to be. I was mixing, and as I was mixing that song, I thought, “Why did I write that?” And then I thought it kind of worked.In my last record, all of the songs fit into one vibe, but this one has all these different energies. I thought Camelot was cool as a place. Then all these regional qualities, all these provincial qualities could fit together under this one Camelot umbrella.

Camelot is this idealized place, a large myth. But when we think of things in mythical proportions, they’re often made up of mundane, daily realities. I thought that was really interesting for this time, this particular communal time that everybody’s in. That feeling is so mythically large, but everyone has their own myth, and they rub up against each other. There’s so much division but there’s also so much in common. I sat well with me when I said, okay, the name is Camelot.

AD: I was also struck by the image of a fractal canyon. Fractals are a mathematical phenomenon that occurs in nature. So, the term has an organic as well as a scientific resonance. The lyrics are about the natural world and the landscapes being wrecked by humans and their garbage. What does that title, “Fractal Canyon” mean to you?

Jennifer Castle: I’ve been hiking a lot over the last few years. I tried to keep disciplined and structured in my days, as so many things fell apart, so I tried to walk. I nicknamed a place I go up behind a certain cliff bluff, the Fractal Canyon. I think I called it that originally because of this particular day where, you know those ferns, fiddleheads?

AD: Yes.

Jennifer Castle: You know how they are literally fractals as they come out? And I must have been there in the spring. So, I would think to myself, oh, we’re going to hike into the fractal canyon. Referring to this one place near my house as fractal canyon. And then I just really liked that, and it came up in song. It’s kind of magical. I really like science and math. I’m not good at it. But I really like how it rubs up against universal principles. I like to listen to mathematicians that are like, “The supreme math is god.” It kind of rubs up against the spiritual and I really like that.

AD: “Blowing Kisses” is the big single and the one that got a really nice showcase in The Bear. How did that happen? They have such good music in that show, but a lot of it is kind of dad rock, male oriented, not very current. Why do you think yours worked with it?

Jennifer Castle: I had finished “Blowing Kisses,” so it was just sitting with the record which was, for the most part, finished. This was last fall. I had watched The Bear, and one of my dear friends is on it. Matty Matheson is one of the brothers, and he’s also a producer on it. And I watched the show. It was the summer, and I started sending him messages, like “Oh my god, you’re amazing,” and “I love it.” Giving him accolades on his acting, since he’s actually a chef.

I noticed the music, too. I was like, hey, it’s such good music. It’s really good for the show. It’s not trendy music. It sounds like a collection of somebody’s favorite songs.

AD: It’s a lot of Wilco, some R.E.M., the Replacements…and you.

Jennifer Castle: It sounds like a mixtape that you found. My manager that I work with, Victoria, said, “You should send ‘Blowing Kisses’ to Matty.” I didn’t want to put Matty in any kind of spot. Like hey, here’s my song. Is there anyway you can use it? But I didn’t feel that way because “Blowing Kisses” was unto itself finished and beautiful and cinematic. It was going to come out anyway. It was going to have its own life anyway.

So, I sent it to him in the spirit of, “Here’s this song. It’s not released yet. I don’t know if you want to use it. But I’m going to offer it to you first, because your show is so special and I just finished watching it. ”He was so sweet. He was just like “I love it, and I’ll take it to the producer.” That was last fall. I didn’t know how it was going to go, because I knew it had to fit. It has to go through so many people at that point. So I did not follow up, like hey did you hear anything? What will be will be with that. The show came out at the end of June—and we heard at the end of May. We got an email. I didn’t think it was going to be in it at that point. Because The Bear was coming out, and I was like oh well, whatevs. And we got an email that said, yeah, it’s in.

AD: It wasn’t just a snippet either.

Jennifer Castle: No, it was a beautiful collaborative moment. They really put it in a beautiful spot. I was floored. But then we had to suddenly turn it around really quick. It was coming out in three weeks. Our record wasn’t lined up to come out until now, so we were like, let’s roll this song out in three weeks. I made the video a week and a half before.I was just like, here we go, and “Blowing Kisses” got to come out. I was really happy that “Blowing Kisses” came out with The Bear. I was really ready for a way to share music that was different from anything I had ever done. And I thought that was really special. Oh man, it’s on a show. That’s so fun. I’ve never had that experience. I was really chuffed.

AD: Have you gotten new listeners from it?

JENNIFER CASTLE: I think so. At first, I was receiving messages from people that listened and Shazam’d it. Jeff McMurrich, my producer, was like, I looked on Shazam, and it’s famous person, famous person, famous person, Jennifer Castle. People were Shazam’ing the song.

I would receive Instagram messages and YouTube messages from people that found it. That makes my day. As a writer that thrills me. Like I still haven’t seen Matty, but he’ll also get a big bear hug when I see him. It’s so cool. They didn’t have to use it. I’m in a good enough place where I get that like, if it’s not right, it’s not right, and that’s not something personal. He’s making his art and if it doesn’t work, don’t worry about it. But things lined up, and I’m really glad.

AD: Do you have any favorite bits or lines or moments on this album that really stand out to you?

Jennifer Castle: To me, lyrically, there’s lots of great moments. There’s lots of good zingers. But I like “Earthsong,” I like when the lyric says, “I belong to this world.” I didn’t realize until I wrote that line that I needed to say that, for myself. I’m not exactly sure why but it felt satisfying to say, “I belong here.”

I like “Full Moon in Leo.” I like the sentiment. I like the way it shakes and the way the lyrics kind of throw it all away. I like the zinger at the end, where she says, “The dream is alive and well.” I like that. It’s almost an attitude you can’t sustain, but I get it every once in a while and I’m glad I caught it. 

AD: What do you think makes a great song a great song? What’s the difference?

Jennifer Castle: Hmm, just in general?

AD: In general, or if you can think of a great song and what makes it great, that would work, too.

Jennifer Castle: I think it’s really a collaboration between writer and listener when a song is great.

If you can really give the song what it wants and not judge yourself out of writing it and just let it out and not edit it too much while it’s trying to come into being, and you give it that good work and you give it that good channel, and you reach for it. You reach for the stars when you write. Even if you don’t make it to the stars, try your best.It’s okay if you don’t hit that note. There’s something really cool about sounding like you’re trying to hit it.I think there’s a good earnestness that ought to come through.

Then I think it’s a co-collaboration that determines where it lands. Because I can discover songs still and say, oh gosh, this is an amazing song. At that point, it’s really collaborative. What songs get canonized as great or where somebody is personally at when they’re listening. That part is out of my reach. The only thing that I can do is give it my best shot at coming into being when I feel like it wants to come. I may ask some questions of it later. I hear the songs first too when they’re being written and often say, “What? What does this mean?” I often find out what they mean later, or at least I discover what sorts of things they could mean. They don’t really have absolute meanings. But let it out.I don’t want to teach anybody anything with a song. I’m not trying to steer anybody towards anything with a song. I’m not trying to be manipulative. I’m trying to let it out. You cook what you want to eat. I must want it to come into being, so I just try to let it out as honestly as I can and then work from there.
