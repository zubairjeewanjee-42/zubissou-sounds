## Body

I’ve known Sergio Sayeg, better known as Sessa, for at least a decade. I even saw his first band, Garotas Sueças, play a small gig in San Francisco before that. We crossed paths briefly in New York and then he shared some tracks he was working on. With some minor tweaks, these songs would end up making up his debut album, Grandeza, when it was released in 2019.

I interviewed Sergio for Wax Poetics in advance of his second album, Estrela Acesa, released in 2022, enthusiastically following his artistic evolution as a truly unique voice in contemporary Brazilian music. Like many notable Brazilian musicians before him, Sergio straddles continents and cultures, having launched his career in the U.S., but calling São Paulo home (again). When Sergio asked me if I could write the promo copy for his new record, Pequena Vertigem de Amor, I jumped at the opportunity, eager to hear the new directions he was taking his music. The following interview is lightly edited from our hour-long conversation in July 2025 while he enjoyed a brief respite from parental duties, as his partner was out of town shooting a film. | a thayer

Aquarium Drunkard: I was thinking back to your first album, released on a small label and then you toured your ass off. Then you eventually jumped over to some major distribution and support with Mexican Summer for your second record, which is still pretty remarkable and unexpected when you think of it – I mean you’re singing in Portuguese – there’s not many artists doing the same thing on a U.S. label, at least when you launched your recording career.

Fast-forward to now and you’re releasing your third album – building on the previous two, so it sounds different, with lots of new sonic elements, but it’s still a Sessa record …

Sessa: I’m curious to know what you think.

AD: Well, the instrumentation is, shall I say, conventional in a way, sort of rock instrumentation in terms of keyboards and the drums and the bass, yet it still maintains a distinctive sound that is clearly your own.

And then with the lyrical themes, it’s sort of like your “dad rock” album, right? Fatherhood is a major, major theme and it’s very sincere. I had to look up what “Dodoi” meant. And I was like, Oh, shit, this is all coming together.

Sessa: Yeah, that word is pretty deep in the vernacular. Yeah, it’s sort of like, “boo boo,” right? And the kids say, like, “Daddy, can you kiss my boo boo?”

AD: On each of your records, you expanded your musical palette, like the first one was the instrumentation and sound you could get with the limited resources available to you as an unsigned, relatively unknown artist, right? And then for the second one, you had explained to me this concept of the Brazilian jazz trio as the band with the addition of string arrangements, that’s the real innovation on Estrela Acesa.

Sessa: Yeah, that was the dive that I did, I was kind of crazy about trying to get that sound it was very conceptual, especially because it’s not my thing – I don’t know how to arrange for strings – and you could just go so many different directions to get that sort of lushness and wash, kind of like thing from the records that I love. It had a serious commitment to the thing. I just love the sound, how do I get the sound, so I dived in pretty deep and it was also during the pandemic, you know, there was some kind of, like, “still time,” that allowed me to do this stuff. So it was a lot of midi keyboards, a lot of zoom calls with arrangers, time to conceptualize, which is so different from how I’ve done this record. You know, the nature of time just changed so much with the kid. And so with this record, it was just kind of like diving in.

AD: So, you didn’t have an intentional premeditated concept for Pequena Vertigem de Amor?

Sessa: Of course there were themes and concepts, and I sort of brought in a few keyboard sounds – there’s the piano, there’s the electric piano, there’s some synth lines, and this was something I was eager to explore.

[The making of this record] also had to do with establishing myself a bit more. With having a kid you know – it’s so classic – you settle down a bit. Biel [Basille], my studio partner and co-producer on the record – we finally had a location for the studio and all this stuff connected and that has to do a bit with the process.

It’s funny that you brought up the previous records, and this is sort of like my first record where I was just recording as I had the songs. Before, I was chasing it so, so hard, you know, and I toured really heavy. And I was coming from touring with bands, as a musician for years.

And then I had the second record and still chased it on the road a lot, even though I was a father already when the record came out. And then it’s almost like this thing, you chase it so bad, you know? I wanted to do music. I wanted this to be my life where you record after hours, when the advertising studio where your friend that is an engineer closes and he lets you in at ten and you walk out at four with tape, you know, all this stuff . . . and eventually it sticks. You know, eventually that’s like what I do, you know, without all the conflicts between the material needs of having a kid and the uncertainties of being a musician, but eventually it sticks, you know, I’m a musician. And that’s kind of like, what I do, I make my records, and then there’s, like, an interesting shift in perspective, because music occupied my life, and I wanted it so badly and then you have a kid, and then, the most important thing in your life shifts and then you find yourself kind of like doing the music in the cracks, you know, chipping away as you can the “Dodói” song is a little bit about that, you know?

You know, I have deadlines, I have sessions trying to get something going, I’m playing while I’m hanging out with my kid. And I probably didn’t look at him for a few seconds, and he fell.
“Can you kiss my boo boo?” and like, wow! Also, I was attuned and had my ears open to song writing, that type of mode of listening and then a line comes to you and it clicks.

Like the flower that grows in the crack, you know? It’s kind of like life is going on and cars drive over the pavement and people are rushing by and the flower pops out. The kid, the timeline, the schedule, the relationship, the bills – it’s a lot of pragmatism. You know, which is a bit dangerous for the poetry, but you have to find it in it, and when you do, it’s rewarding to insane levels. I feel very, very happy about the music I made on my first two records. But, you know, I think this is my best record, not as some kind of evolutionary narrative, but because I’m like, I really didn’t know how I was gonna make it. And when I signed up for the task, you know, okay, LP three is in the making, and I’m like, ‘fuck,’ you know? So, then you figure it out, and you know, ‘woooo’ that’s like a real high.

AD: So, it’s almost like a relief that you can still do it in this new, challenging context?

Sessa: When you have a kid, you have to mature so fast, so much responsibility. It’s so moving, but it’s limiting. Then at some point you’re like, ‘fuck, man,” life changed so much, but at some point you see that it changed, but you’re still yourself in the middle of life, right? And, and for me, what clicked was the ambiguity of the experience, which always has been something that stimulated me to write about. It’s been on my records: the longing for sexual encounters, and the sort of inherent loneliness of chasing the encounter, and or the pleasures and pains of intimacy, of living together, it’s always there and then I managed to access the ambiguity [in parenthood].

It’s difficult to do, because it is so ordinary. When I was growing up, I didn’t question it, adults had kids. And there’s space to question that, especially related to the role of women, but I don’t want my records to be a pro-life advertisement or anything like that, it’s just what happened to me. It’s so ordinary, it’s so common, but at the same time, the experiences that you have, the kid absorbing language, you know, saying stuff wrong, getting it, being fascinated with the world, it’s extraordinary! But it’s also so mundane, it’s such a day-to-day thing. So, when I managed to distill it all, to kind of like, let it happen, that’s when I encountered the poetics [of it]. When I was younger, I wanted to be a musician. Eventually I became a musician. And then other things in life happened, and then you still do it. This record has a bit of this tone, to me.

AD: Tell me about the studio you started with Biel?

Sessa: We have a studio, people come because of the sound on my records, because of what we do.

AD: Are you recording other people? What are you doing in the studio this week? Are you working on your own music, or other people’s?

Sessa: We’re recording a singer, producing a singer. On this record, Biel and I are co-producing. The studio is less a place where people come for us to hit “record.”

Things are going so AI, there’s a natural interest in things that are not AI. So people look for that a little  extra bit. It’s not such a widespread thing in Sao Paulo anymore, to have a reel-to-reel working tape operation that’s not at some super expensive studio.

I don’t know if you saw a record called anaiis & Grupo Cosmo that came out. She’s a British singer. Actually, she’s French/Senegalese, but she lives in London. That’s the record we produced that we’re the most proud of.

AD: Do you play on other people’s records in the studio?

Sessa: I play guitar, nylon guitar. You know Soyuz, that Belarusian group? So you know, Alex [Chumak] arranged stuff for my records and he came here to make his next record and I co-produced that.

We have a bit of an open code relationship. Like when he approached me, I was already interested in strings, and he was arranging and recording with a really cool sound. So we talked a lot, and it got to a point when I did a tour, just me, through Europe and there was one show where I had to have a band. So, that’s when we met. We met at the sound check, you know, for the first time. I didn’t even know how tall he was, because we just talked online. And he’s like really shy and awkward. You know, it’s like the polar opposite of Brazilian culture, he’s from the post-Soviet world, where the same guy has been president since Alex was born.

You know, not that my place or your place are doing much better, but you get what I’m saying . . . so he was like, if I come to Brazil, can you make my record? And we made it happen.

AD: You referenced some of the influences or inspirations for the new album. You mentioned Sly and Shuggie and RAMP – where does that come from?

Sessa: There’s definitely, like, a soul thing. It’s hard to slap a genre on stuff you know, but when I fell in love with music, that’s what I was listening to. Of course, there was also the rock n’ roll, you know, the Sonics, the MC5, but you know, so much of that is black music anyways, right? When I worked for Joel [Stones – owner of beloved East Village record store Tropicália in Furs], I would go through stacks and stacks of 45s just sitting at the counter.

It was a hip store. But, you know, like at noon on Tuesday, there was no one there. There’s a lot more time during the day that the shop was open then, then the parties, you know?

I have this theory, which always manages to prove itself: the music that you fell in love with when you were a teenager through your early twenties, it stains you, you know, it stains your soul and and you’re always in some kind of dialectic relationship with it.

At some point it became pretty clear to me that I’m not the funkiest, you know? And so I let go of that a bit and also I lived abroad, and I was sort of responding to, to my nostalgia for Brazil and the sound of the nylon guitar. Then I took the nylon guitar, I played so much, you know, played hundreds of shows, I wrote so many songs, and I kind of became decent. I studied with some heavy players and I became a decent guitarist. And then I was like, fuck, what now?

And that’s where the piano comes in, in the context of having the studio – the keyboards are always hooked up. Also Alex [Chumak] came to play keyboards, but the acoustic piano is Marcelo Maita, you know him?

AD: No, but I love that piano.

Sessa: He’s Amado Maita’s younger brother, much younger. He’s probably like sixteen years younger.

AD: How did you meet him?

Sessa: It was because of the first daycare that my son went to. It had a cool people kinda vibe, a more raw space, Décio from Bixiga 70 had a daughter there too.

So, I’ve been chasing this true samba jazz-style stabby acoustic piano. How the fuck do I get this sound? You know, who’s the player? Like, piano, it’s so hard, you know? The thing speaks in the lows, speaks in the highs, every genre has a piano – it’s so easy to mess up.

So, I’m chasing it, chasing it. And then at some end of the year party at the school, I mention this to Décio, asking him ‘who’s a piano player?’ And he’s like, ‘you gotta talk to Marcelo.”

I hit him up and we became friends. He was in a session here yesterday. I mean, the guy’s amazing, man! He played with [Wilson] Simonal, he knows the stories of the making of Amado’s record, which is, like my favorite. Amado Maita and [Erasmo Carlos’] Sonhos E Memorias are the kind of records that, like, fucked me up forever. It’s almost forbidden to play these records in my house because Helena [my partner] can’t stand it anymore.

AD: “Dodói” where did that groove come from? That guitar groove at the beginning is so good! You kind of looped it – it’s ready to be sampled!

Sessa: I’m waiting for the day they’re gonna sample my music.

AD: You were saying you might not be the funkiest guy, but, that’s getting there, man.

Sessa: Thank you.

AD: Previously, you described a jagged or sort of crooked groove, or swing, or something like that. I think there’s something Brazilian about that off kilter timing – it’s not that sort of precise classic American funk, you know, everything on the one . . .

Sessa: I think the guitar role in this record is a bit of what I said earlier. I feel like exhausted by the instrument and the way that I was doing the voicing, so I just kind of tried to shove my hand in a different direction, because the chordal part of the song – it’s kind of like the bread and butter of my songs – it’s kinda like how I make the shapes, but the rhythm came from somewhere else.

It’s hard to name a favorite song. I mean, I’m so happy with the record, but what I managed to communicate and those piano fills, kinda going really crazy . . . I remember walking out of the session that day, like ‘WOW’ because that’s like, completely live, you know – drums and piano in the same room, and the bass, and I’m in a separate room, like singing and playing. Serious energy in the studio. It was so amazing!

AD: So you’re tracking the vocals while you’re playing guitar?

Sessa: Yeah. I mean, sometimes I overdub, but a lot of the stuff is live, like drums, bass, guitar and vocals.

This is also the record where I returned to playing electric guitar, even though everything is through a wah wah. There’s guitar on two tracks.

AD: And you play electric piano on “Vale a Pena,” right?

Sessa: I do, yeah. It’s a Suette [pronounced Swetch], which is a Brazilian electronic piano. It’s a late-seventies,  early eighties [instrument]. There was a luthier here that would make it, you know, it’s very artisanal. I think it’s rare. I mean, it’s much cheaper than getting a Wurly or a Rhodes here, which is like a piece of furniture, but it was a one-man type of shop, and it only ran for a few years. It was a small factory, but I guess they’re not that many. It has such a lovely sound. I wrote “Vale a Pena” on a piano and I shamelessly played keys on the record.

AD: Each record of yours has a different combination of instruments. It’s like you have an equation right, and then, and then the next one adds a variable right, and then, and then, and then the next one adds a different variable. And then, because of these new variables, you know, it sounds very different. It’s not just an addition, you know it’s greater than the sum of its parts. As someone creative, I assume that you feel compelled to do something different with each statement. Is that true?

Sessa: Yeah, I think there is some kind of subconscious conceptualization of where you’re gonna aim to, like with the strings on Estrela Acessa, because, I was trying to aim for this nostalgia and this lushness, this sort of ultimate beauty that melts your heart when you when you hear it.

With this record, I was aiming at something, but it’s not that easy to pinpoint what it is. It’s a bit more driving with more rhythms. It’s a bit more upbeat, the production. It’s slightly glossier, you know, I mean, I think it’s a stretch to call my records glossy.

When I did Estrela Acesa, I didn’t really have a house. I was on tour. There was a pandemic. The tape machine I had had six working channels, rather than eight and then we took it to an island with questionable power and electricity. While on this record, the studio situation is a bit more solid and established.

The studio still has its edge, but a space communicates things. It also has the keyboards, which is something I was down to explore, the synths, the acoustic piano – I was trying to paint a picture with these colors. Maybe the immediate references [for this record] are a bit later in the seventies than my other records.

But honestly, on this record I was less motivated by that idea of specific references or instrumentation. I feel like with Estrela Acesa, I was really into crafting a perfect universe of sound, that, like, hints at some, like, ‘69 session at A&M studios, type of thing, I was obsessed with this concept of beauty as the creator of the music. And I think the string arrangements kind of represent that on Estrela Acessa.

With this, it was more like in the trenches of life type of record, but it was liberating for me to let go of the ideas and the concepts and stuff, and just do the music. And with the cards that I was dealt, I tell the story.

AD: I imagine you recorded stuff that didn’t make it on the record. Did you sort of end up picking ones that sort of held together more in a similar aesthetic?

Sessa: Honestly, that’s the extent of my output in the past two years.There’s maybe a couple songs that didn’t make it, but I usually cut things off in the writing process. There is one track that I like that I couldn’t really push to completion.

AD: Did you find yourself talking to Biel or Marcelo, the bass player, about what the vision was, or did it just sort of happen based on the songs you were writing?

Sessa: No, with Biel I exchanged a lot. Biel is definitely like in the passenger seat type of co-producer, he was very present for all the sessions.

Sessa: You know there are layers, like, there’s some really deep thoughts that go on when you’re making a record – maybe he didn’t see it all, but he definitely was with me on a lot of the things involved.

I think the other thing, and I don’t know how it sounded to you, but for me, like the real exploration is also the vocals on this record. I was kind of pushing it more, and kind of like exploring a bit the expression through my vocals, the keys and ranges and phrasing in new ways.

It’s also good, because it’s kind of like, you make a record, people seem to like it. You make another one, people seem to like it, you know, and maybe there’s this illusion that you’re rocking really hard. And for the artists, I think it’s important to be vulnerable and kind of question, which I do a lot, and I question my shit a lot, but the singing is such a place of vulnerability, which was really familiar for me from my first record, before which I never sang.

And then I stuck to the same style for the second record. And on the new record I was throwing myself somewhere unknown again. And it’s really hard, you know, because there’s all these things about singing, like, on key, off key, with air, without air, all these kinds of details. It’s so subtle, you know, like a perfume, the things that make a vocal performance, a vocal performance. It’s really hard to pinpoint, so it was a real trip for me as a singer.

AD: One last thought – if there’s one artist that I could say this album reminds me of, it would be Jorge Mautner from the late seventies and I know that you covered one of the songs from his record Mil E Uma Noites De Bagdá.

Sessa: Man, that’s the only record I have an alert for on Discogs.

AD: I think the similarities are the maturity, the poetry, you know, it’s sort of smooth, but still funky, And he also has a very unconventional voice, in terms of his delivery.

Sessa: One thing I think you would find interesting, so we are potty training our son right? So I got into this thing while he’s sitting on the potty. First, we would show him the world map and just like talk about places and stuff like that. But then I started showing him Wax Poetics magazines, looking at them while he sits there for a bit, and we look where each musician comes from on the map, and now he’s like, ‘I want to see music magazines.’

AD: Super nerdy three year old. I love it. What’s his favorite music? Aside from yours, of course . . .

Sessa: I mean, it’s hard with kids’ music, so much trash, but there’s a group here that’s really famous called Palavra Cantada, and their nineties records are really good, you know, because the production aged well – some good stuff there.

AD: Back to your album, which is not designed for children, but inspired by parenthood, this is an interesting challenge: how do you promote this album? Can you make parenthood sexy? Will these themes appeal to people who don’t have kids?

Sessa: I know! A marketing sticker came from my label Mexican Summer which said “inspired by early fatherhood.” My instinct is always to deviate from it. You know, it’s kind of a boring kind of thing. But like, I feel like there are so many people who have kids too, you know, there must be something about it. I think it could be a bit more universal, because it is a lot about changes and embracing it. You can fight and be nostalgic about the things you lost. Time moves for everybody, that’s my point. So, the theme of nostalgia for the past, it’s available to everyone. It’s just more intense when you have kids.

I don’t know, man, is there anything more beautiful than the kids, the hope in the future in this fucked up world. I gotta own the message too. I’m not gonna hide behind some imagined inspiration – honestly, life changed.

AD: I’m not saying I’m the perfect person to do this, but having just had a baby myself, I feel like I can inhabit a similar headspace to where you’re coming from.

Sessa: A lot of the writing for this album came from earlier in fatherhood, so it was more intense.

AD: Okay, all right, get to your kid. I’ll talk to you sometime soon. Ciao!
