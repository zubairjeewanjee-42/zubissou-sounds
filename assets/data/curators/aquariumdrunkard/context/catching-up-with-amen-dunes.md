## Body

If you’ve been following Damon McMahon’s Amen Dunes project for any length of time, you might have a mental picture of echo-sheathed, mystic guitar folk with an otherworldly sheen (a la Through Donkey Jaw) or subtly electrified but still eerie skewed pop (as in 2018’s Freedom). You probably wouldn’t expect an intricately layered, sample-based work of music like Death Jokes, his seventh and latest album which owes more to J. Dilla than the Velvet Underground. And yet though the sound is very different—with snatches of comic sketches, ghosts of classical piano and threads of jazz trio woven in—the album pursues an ecstatic release that will be familiar to long-time listeners. 

Indeed, there’s an aura of spiritualism enveloping Death Jokes that reflects its mortality-haunted pandemic origins, the onset of fatherhood and a brush with the otherworldly through, of all things, a piano teacher. McMahon hired Jonichi to help him get up to speed on keyboards—he wanted to make the piano an important element of the new songs—and found that his tutor was also a medium. Jonichi had studied with another celebrated piano teacher, Nadia Boulanger, who was as noted for her spiritualist teachings as her musical ones. “As he taught me, he couldn’t help but talk about her and her lessons and philosophy. He ended up being a big inspiration on the album,” says McMahon. A sample of Boulanger’s voice, in French, can be heard on the album-defining cut, “Round the World.”  

McMahon admits that his latest album may be a reach for some fans, with its complicated musical and cultural references and its hip hop influenced sampled density, but he hopes they’ll take the time to listen carefully.  “I just really hope that people take the time to pay attention to the details, because there’s so much in this album. Even my dearest friends are saying, ‘I didn’t understand it until I’d listened to it five times,’” he says. | j kelly

Aquarium Drunkard: Your album is called Death Jokes and the title track includes some clips of comedians, Richard Pryor and Lenny Bruce. What do you see as the conjunction between death and humor?

Damon McMahon: I don’t find death humorous. 

AD: But it depends, right?

Damon McMahon: Yeah, yeah, I suppose it depends. But the title came to me during the pandemic, and its meaning to me changed over the course of the album. 

AD: From what to what?

Damon McMahon: It’s pretty complicated. I don’t think I could answer that in a quick one sentence response. I really want the listener to see what it means to them. There are a bunch of different ways of looking at it. I’m wondering, how did you interpret it?

AD: In that bleak humorous way, that everything is a joke and death is a joke, too.

Damon McMahon: There is that. 

I guess the first way I intended it was that death is personified in that title. The course of life plays with us in a surprising ways. It toys with our big plans and ideas and the way we think things should go.  Also there are some people and religious traditions that I’ve admired over the years. Spiritual traditions that look at our human existence as just one facet of a bigger experience.

AD: Right, and that’s another running thread, this idea of spiritualism in the album. 

Damon McMahon: During the pandemic, the world was forced to look at its own mortality. I struggled during that time as well. There’s tradition, in particular there’s a Hindu tradition that looks at the importance of life differently. I was taking inspiration from that. That was the first meaning. And then the meaning changed as it went on.It connects to my usage of those comedians’ work. I didn’t realize that there was a second meaning or a third meaning to the title until years after I started making the album. 

AD: I understand you consulted a psychic while you were making this album?

Damon McMahon: No, no, no, that’s incorrect. I had a piano teacher who came to my house before the pandemic who also happened to be a medium. But that’s not why we met. We met to learn piano. But he happened to have that in his rolodex.

AD: But you did incorporate some spoken word from a spiritualist named Nadia Boulanger?

Damon McMahon: Yeah, and she was his piano teacher. 

AD: Can you tell me a little bit about her?

Damon McMahon: Yeah, totally. I don’t know that much. She was very celebrated pianist and teacher in France. I think turn of the century. She taught everybody from Stravinsky and Ravel all the way through Quincy Jones. My piano teacher was lucky enough to get a scholarship to go to Paris and study with her. As he taught me, he couldn’t help but talk about her and her lessons and philosophy. He ended up being a big inspiration on the album. 

AD: It’s weird that you have two people that are piano teachers and also spiritualists.

Damon McMahon: Yeah, it is weird.

AD: Is that a common thing? Is there some kind of link?

Damon McMahon: For me, this album is just full of coincidences and unexpected happenstance. But I think if you find a really good guide, they often have some kind of spiritual potency. 

AD: Are you interested in talking to the dead? What draws you to these people?

Damon McMahon: I’m not interested really in talking to the dead. I mean, I suppose I am if I’m really honest. 

The music has always come from another place when it’s working. I don’t identify with writing these songs. I’m drawn to a spiritual thing in general. I’ve been curious in pursuing it my whole life and then if you knock on a door hard enough, it starts to creep open a little bit. 

AD: I think a lot of people if they found out that you had written an album about death and the afterlife and spiritualism, they might connect it with COVID. But didn’t you start on these songs before that?

Damon McMahon: Well, no, part of it is connected to the pandemic. But the funny thing is that a lot of these songs that seem to be about the pandemic were written before the pandemic. Especially “Round the World.” That song came to me in December of 2019. It was one of those times when you’re writing a song, and you just start singing the lyrics. You’re not planning it. That whole chorus, “Let it rattle round the world, let it rattle.” I was singing about people struggling. That was in the air.

Then I wrote five of the lyrics during the middle of the pandemic. That was when I started to reflect on what was going on consciously. 

AD: And also, I think this is interesting, during this period you had a child, didn’t you? So there was death but also new life. Was that an influence?

Damon McMahon: Yeah, it was and now there’s two. The first came in 2021. 

AD: Right in the middle of it. 

Damon McMahon: In the middle of it, but the heat of the pandemic had kind of burned off. So she brought a different flavor. She just brought a potency that injected the album with hyper fuel. But I won’t say I wrote about it. Except for one song, “Purple Land,” I didn’t write about it.

AD: I remember Spaulding Grey was talking about having his son, Forest. And he said something like, now I want to live forever. Now I could die at any moment. It’s interesting how having a child changes your whole relationship to death and life.

Damon McMahon: Do you have children yourself?

AD: I have a son who is almost 30 now. I’m pretty much done with the hard part. Do you have two girls?How old are they?

Damon McMahon: One that’s almost three and one 10 months.

AD: Oh, that’s a nice age. 

Damon McMahon: It is. 

AD: But having a baby during the pandemic would be so intense because you wouldn’t have any help. 

Damon McMahon: No help. It was very intense and scary at the beginning when you’re figuring things out. 

AD: One of the first sounds you hear on this album is the piano, and I understand that’s a new instrument for you.C an you talk about why it made sense to play keyboards this time out?

Damon McMahon: That part, the fundamental musical part, there’s nothing too magical about that. I bought a piano right before the pandemic when I started writing this album. I always wanted to have that tool. Every time I do an album, I have new tools to channel the songs.It could be gear. Or it could just be inspiration. But piano was one of them. 

AD: Did it take a while to get good enough to play on a record?

Damon McMahon: Yes.I’m a terrible piano player. Even all those lessons from Johnny Chee, through him Nadia Boulanger, didn’t crack the impenetrable inability to play for me. But I learned a little bit. By the way that piano at the beginning of the record, I wish I could say it’s me but it’s not. It’s someone playing a Schumann piece. 

AD: Another thing that is relatively new about this album is that there are a lot of samples on it and electronics. Why did that seem like the thing to do?

Damon McMahon: Totally. Every time I do an album, I’m always conscious of my previous inspirations but I’m always very aware of what’s going on in the music world. I find it very inspiring. I dig pretty deep to find new musicians that are inspiring. 

This time, I was inspired by modern music, and modern music is made with computers and software and DAWs, home recording stuff. I was deep in my computer life, and the computer started sending me stuff. But also before, I had pre-emptively wanted to make an album that was drum machine and sample-based because I grew up listening to a lot of electronic music. 

AD: Tell me about that.

Damon McMahon: When I was 14, I discovered electronic music and started going to raves here and there. I grew up listening to hip hop, ever since I was very little, like nine years old. And so I’ve always loved sample-based music, drum machines, electronics. But I didn’t have the means. I just never thought I could do it. And then I started writing songs, and I also loved folk music. I was a folk songwriter from 16 years old and that started to develop, and I don’t know why, I just believed I didn’t have the skills. I didn’t think I could do it.

AD: The Picasso of sampling, J. Dilla, was a big influence. 

Damon McMahon: Yeah. Totally. I have very fond feeling about him, not just musically but as a person. I grew up listening to rap music from when I was about nine years old and then in the mid-1990s, there was a group called Mobb Deep in New York. That was the last time I listened to that kind of music for a long time. 

The whole bling type, money, Jay Z rap music was coming up in the 1990s, and I wasn’t that interested in it. I also discovered the Velvet Underground and Captain Beefheart, these people that were very anti-material, and it wasn’t cool. So I quit listening to hip hop from 1995 until J. Dilla’s Donuts came out in 2006. 

I was at SXSW. I was staying at a hotel with my manager at the time. I was shaving in the bathroom in the afternoon getting ready to go. He put Donuts on, and I heard those sirens. These motifs appeared. I was totally blown away. I hadn’t heard anything emotional like that in a long time in that genre, so I just fell in love with him then. I was very inspired by it. It had a musique concrete, avant-garde aspect. There were these little interstitial moments. They weren’t complete. They were just feelings. And also he was able to convey feelings with no words. It was very bizarre music. It was very sad. And I knew he was in the hospital…

AD: It’s a terrible story about him. Just hitting his stride.

Damon McMahon: Yeah, and I was really touched by him. And then when I was starting to make this record, I was like, oh shit, I’ve never made electronic music. Let’s get drum machines and learn. And wouldn’t it be wonderful to make my White Album-slash-Donuts. So that was it. That was what I set out to do.

AD: Are there any moments or lines or sounds on this album that you just really like the way they came out, and every time they come up, you just go wow, that’s good. 

Damon McMahon: Honestly, this is the best thing I’ve ever done. I feel more true and more inspired and also more guided on this album than ever. So I would say—and this is a cheap answer—but the whole thing. 

But little moments… there are so many because it’s so layered. But the end of the song “Exodus” was very inspiring. Random and unexpected stuff would fall into the session. I would just throw stuff at the session, and sometimes it would just land naturally. A lot of times I wouldn’t even check the key of the song. I would just, you know, by ear, reference it and find music elsewhere and throw it into the session. Line it up quickly. And I would not move it. I would just let it engage, randomly. So there’s a jazz trio that I put at the end of that song that was in a completely different key and it just lined up perfectly. It felt so chaotic and beautiful and ecstatic. So that’s one. But the lyrics are a big part of this whole album.

AD: What are some of your favorite lyrics on this record?

Damon McMahon: I care about them all, but I would say “Round the World.” The last two songs on the album are my favorite because they encapsulate what I care about in this album, the message of the album. 

AD: I know you’ve done a lot of collaborating in the past, and I was pretty struck by the song you did with the Sleaford Mods. Are you working on anything like that now?

Damon McMahon: No, I originally asked them to produce this whole record, but they’re busy and it just took too long. That was a great experience. I worked with this guy Ariel Rechtshaid. It was very beautiful and kind of unexpected. But it just took too long and they weren’t available. 

I tried to collaborate with everybody under the sun on this album, because I was like, how the fuck am I going to program drums, edit drums, record myself, use samples? I don’t know how to do any of this kind of music. So there was a lot of insecurity going into it. I had to get help. I think we always think that we have to find some kind of guru to teach us and show us. So I tried. I tried to collaborate for two years, and it failed almost every single time. 

AD: Really? A lot of people found it was easier to collaborate during the pandemic because everybody was home and bored and open to things.

Damon McMahon: Not me. It wasn’t because of in-person versus virtual. I actually love virtual collaboration. Any collaboration that made it was pretty virtual. But no, it was that this music is so unconventional and the world has become so conventional. I brought it to all these people in the music industry and I was met with conservatism. I was bringing it to music people and they were saying, left and right, what are you doing? Are you crazy? What’s wrong with you? Why would you do this? Over and over again. Celebrated producers and big engineers. It was wild. And I went to some musicians to ask them to collaborate and sometimes it worked but often they didn’t understand. And these were avant-garde people. It was shocking. It was incredibly disheartening. It was like a zombie movie where your down-to-earth neighbor gets overtaken by an algorithm or something.

AD: Wow, that’s interesting. 

Damon McMahon: It was crazy, but it led me to do it by myself. Except for little bits and pieces that I used. 

AD: But the sampling maybe stood in for that collaboration?

Damon McMahon: Yeah, in many respects. But I was looking for a producer. I was looking for someone to hold my hand and say, this is how you do it. Broad stroke.

AD: Well maybe it was better that you had to do it for yourself and it turned out unconventional?

Damon McMahon: 100% Absolutely. It was so wonderful that I was rejected. 

AD: How are you going to do your live show? How are you going to incorporate this stuff which is very different from your older material?

Damon McMahon: It’s pretty cool. I’m going to replicate it to a tee. We have a ton of MIDI instruments and analog instruments, too. So basically, we have Ableton brains that will send samples and software to various instruments. 

AD: Are you going to play some of the older acoustic songs, too?

Damon McMahon: We’re going to integrate them. We’ve been rehearsing and it’s actually pretty wild to put them back to back. 

AD: Will you be changing those songs to make them more like the new stuff or no?

Damon McMahon: It’s a good question.In a very subtle way that most people won’t notice.

AD: It seems very different from your last record.

Damon McMahon: Very different and especially from Love or Through Donkey Jaw. I don’t play anything from the first two records, DIA and Murder Dull Mind live, but I play some Through Donkey Jaw and Love tracks.I think it’s just an energy. 

AD: How do you think your long-time fans are going to react?

Damon McMahon: Well, my long-time fans, if anybody’s been with me since 2009 when the first album came out, they’ll realize that Death Jokes is speaking to that album. It’s the only thing I’ve ever done that’s like the first album. The first album was called DIA and I recorded it myself. I played all the instruments. It was like total freedom, and I haven’t had that freedom since then. I think those folks will really like the correlation. But the people who discovered me through Freedom, I think they’ll be a little…

AD: Non-plussed.

Damon McMahon: Or just bothered. Maybe not non-plussed but bothered. 

AD: I know you’re probably working really hard on getting ready for the tour and doing promotion, but are you working on anything else, any side projects you want to talk about?

Damon McMahon: Yeah, we’re doing all the videos for this tour, but also, I’ve started the next album. I always think about the next album. As soon as an album’s done, or even as it’s getting done, I start thinking, oh, shit, I’ve got to work on the next one, I have two ideas.

AD: Are you going to let them fight?

Damon McMahon: Yeah, let them fight. And then one will be like, nah, nah, I’m the way. 

AD: If you could get one thing across about this album and your career in general, what would it be?

Damon McMahon: I just really hope that people take the time to pay attention to the details. I don’t mean like C sharp to fucking F major. I mean what I’m trying to say. Because there’s so much in this album. Even my dearest friends are saying, I didn’t understand it until I’d listened to it five times. Even to me, the message can be overwhelming. If you sit with this thing—look, I’m a music fan—I learned music by listening to music. I didn’t learn it by studying it. When I listen to my favorite albums of all time, I hear what those people are saying to you through careful listening. It’s not going to hit you in the head. There are references to other songs, and they’re copying riffs. Especially with sample-based music. 

So I would hope people would be able to listen to it and not just say, oh, he used a comedian. Do he like comedy? Is he trying to be funny? No not at all. I used them to speak to this larger theme. I hope they are careful and wonder and are curious, and then dialogue. All good art incites some sort of conversation. I hate this or I love it. It doesn’t have to be I love this. You know, fuck that guy. Why is he saying that? I’d rather have that than someone just liking it on Instagram and be like, ‘Ha, death jokes, samples, hip hop.’ I want people to care like I care.

AD: What do you think makes a great song a great song?

Damon McMahon: Hmm. That’s like a spiritual thing really. It’s sort of like a mathematical thing. The way nature is perfect. The great song, it moves…It’s very hard to pin down, but there’s a chordal, melodic fluidity. I think it’s actually an ineffable thing. The great songs, like “Sitting on the Dock of the Bay.” It’s not just that it’s catchy. It gets into your flesh. It gets into your body. You don’t know why it’s in you. Maybe you don’t even like it, but it makes you feel and move without thinking. Those great songs are just…Because Otis Redding, he just got a download of that. I’ve never heard him say that, but some artists just get a download from the universe.

AD: It comes from somewhere else. 

Damon McMahon: It’s like leaves on a tree.
