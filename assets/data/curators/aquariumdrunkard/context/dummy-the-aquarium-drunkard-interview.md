## Body

The LA trance-punk ensemble had a monster 2021. Their debut album, Mandatory Enjoyment , was met with almost universal acclaim, then the foursome embarked on a series of grinding, months-long tours, got picked to make a Sub Pop Single and, in general, worked about as hard as a band should ever have to work. They returned to LA and hit reset, taking time off for spiritual and musical nourishment.

They regrouped in LA after the break for the first of two recording sessions, then during another two-week hiatus, the band had an epiphany. It started with the discovery of Mark Van Hoen’s the Locust project and branched off into multiple channels of 1990s IDM and electronics. What if Dummy made an electronic album with rock instruments, they asked? What exactly would that sound like?

The result was Free Energy, a driving blast of dance floor physicality. The new record was full of guitars, but not so much guitars that sounded like guitars. Its beats were more intricate than before, not just a motorik pulse. There were flutes and a sax and the sound of bubbles. It was trippy and euphoric, but also set the body in motion.

We caught up with all four members of Dummy about this sonic transformation and their new record and found still figuring out where the new direction would lead them. Said, guitarist Joe Trainor, “We tapped into some things that I feel like we’re just scratching the surface of, in terms of what we could do in the future.” | j kelly

Aquarium Drunkard: The new album, which I like a lot, drives a little harder than the one before it. Can you talk about how and why you decided to change things up?

Alex Ewell: That came from our time playing live. We were really digging out all of the feeling we could out of every song over the course of months of touring and practice, and by the end of it, I just really felt that whatever I was going to do next I wanted it to be a little more…to have a little more to dig into and to have a little more power behind it.

AD: More amplification or harder drums? How did that work out in practice?

Joe Trainer: Because Mandatory Enjoyment was written during COVID and we were all inside, the physical aspect of the writing process, in terms of playing live and practicing, was just completely gone. But with this one, we had the 150 days of being on tour under our belts and then just practicing twice a week. All of that coalesced into where the record ended up sonically.

Alex Ewell: A big part of it for this record was us learning to produce and record stuff ourselves. Just learning Ableton. We recorded a lot of stuff ourselves before we went to the studio. It seems like we had a lot of stuff worked out before we went into the studio.

Emma Maatman: I think we were a lot more open to bigger production on this album. Bigger sound. We don’t really like a big, huge, super crispy clean sound anyway, but I do feel like on previous releases, we were trying to shrink the bass down to nothing. To purposely make everything sound a lot more compressed and pulled back. But I think we were a little more open to letting things sound big and full and have some more weight to them.

Nathan O’Dell: But that just naturally happened while we were recording. When we started, we thought of this record as something small and tape-sounding, messed up in the high frequency range. But once we started putting tracks down, the sound came out heavier. It kind of just happened while we were making it.

AD: So, it was more of a tactile, organic thing, rather than thinking about influences and really plotting out how you wanted it to sound.

Joe Trainer: We just really followed where the songs took us in the studio. Three songs on the record we had played live, but the rest of it was not played live before the record. I think it was like Nathan was saying. We were thinking it was going to be more self-recorded, if anything more lo-fi and experimental in terms of the sound of it. But then, like Nathan said, when we got to the studio, something happened and we were like, “oh, this just sounds bigger and more expanded than we thought it was going to.” It just happened.

Alex Ewell: We were less influenced by a specific band or sound. We kind of had a whole different idea for what we wanted to do initially. The influences that did come into this record were from totally different musical styles. A lot of electronic music and pop.

Joe Trainer: The things that inspired us happened while we were recording the album. We tapped into some things that I feel like we’re just scratching the surface of, in terms of what we could do in the future.

AD: What were some of those things that happened while you were in the studio?

Joe Trainer: We referenced it before in the press stuff. The Locust record, Morning Light, and Transient Wave.

Alex Ewell: We ended up coming across all this music that we hadn’t listened to. Or had heard before but not really gotten into.

Joe Trainer: Like the Colin Newman record.

We were in the middle of making the record, and we had a two week break, and these things started popping up out of nowhere. Then, Nathan in that break wrote the basis for “Null Space,” the first single. And we took what he had for the groundwork and turned it into the kind of dance-influenced song that ended up being on the record. That song is directly influenced by listening to that stuff. It really, really made everything coalesce together. We knew that we wanted to make a record that was more rhythmically diverse and more physical. We knew we didn’t want to rely on the rhythms that we ground into the ground on previous releases. That was the most conscious thing we did, make sure we weren’t retreading that rhythmic territory.

AD: In between those two albums, you had a pretty amazing ride. You had really good reception for the first record. You were playing a lot of shows in all different kinds of venues. You did that Sub Pop single and got into a little bit of trouble with the Bandcamp article. How did all that chaos feed into the process?

Alex Ewell: I don’t know if it did that much, honestly. We were really unsure what to do. At least I was. When it was finished. When we got back from tour, that was the end of what we had planned.

Joe Trainer: The well was pretty dry at that point.

Alex Ewell: Yeah, I was feeling so emptied out. I needed a lot of time to listen to music again and get into something new. And yeah, by the time we really started writing the songs that ended up on the album, all that stuff felt pretty far away.

Emma Maatman: We had a nice big break. We were on tour for what seemed to be three months straight. We definitely were like, we need to take a break. We took three months not even thinking about trying to write anything. I think that did help do a little reset.

Joe Trainer: But I also think in terms of the chaos and all that stuff, and playing the shows, I think this record, even more so than Mandatory Enjoyment is a reaction to what we saw and heard. All the touring and all the shows we played. And then also the music that we were hearing that was coming out in the world. We weren’t finding that much in terms of our immediate surroundings that inspiring. And so, we had to really dig in within ourselves more than into external influences.

AD: What does that entail digging into yourself? How would that manifest itself?

Joe Trainer: I don’t know.

Alex Ewell: I think for you, Alex, you made a lot of demos. That was a big part of how we started writing stuff. You had made a lot of demos.

Joe Trainer: I had demos from that three-month period that I worked on. For me personally, it emptied me out. It got the lingering things, the preceding ideas out. Once that happened, the world felt more open. As opposed to the stringent lines that we kept everything within before.

AD: That makes sense. I feel like you’ve always done this interesting thing where you combine this really giddy, floaty, Stereolab-ish hum with a very propulsive punk energy, with a little My Bloody Valentine thrown in for seasoning. But this one seems almost more rave-y, I was getting almost a Happy Mondays vibe. It seems more trippy and shambolic than the one before. Is that fair or am I hearing the wrong things?

Joe Trainer: I think the shambolic aspect of it, for sure, because that would come from the Flying Nun influence that goes through the record. Also, I think for me personally, in the studio, I get intensely anal about everything sounding perfectly in place. In a way that only I can perceive. And for this record, we let things be more human sounding in a lot of ways and not try to over…

Alex Ewell: We wanted it to feel more like performances and have that passion behind it. On our first record, we were really trying to get everything super locked in to get that machine sound.

Joe Trainer: But as a rock band making that kind of dance/rave music. Marrying those two ideas. A band using rock instruments to make what we would consider, to some degree, a dance record.

Alex Ewell: I would say 1990s dance music was a bigger influence.

Joe Trainer: But more so in terms of IDM and trip hop. The Manchester stuff isn’t really an influence on us. At all.

Alex Ewell: More like Aphex Twin. I was always super into Boards of Canada as a teenager. That stuff’s always been a big influence. Ambient music and IDM and electronic music in general has always been a big listening thing for me.

AD: That’s interesting because you’re saying that the process was more live and yet the influences were electronic, which is not typically what you think of when you think of a rock band.

Joe Trainer: That’s what we wanted to do.

Alex Ewell: I feel like rock is in a sorry state right now. It needs some kind of other thing going on in order to….

AD: It’s always dying and always being born again.

Joe Trainer: All of us really like making music. Our earlier stuff, people were like, yeah, it’s euphoric or blissed out. With this one, I feel like we actually made music that was euphoric and blissed out. More for the live experience, to get you to move your ass. Not the kind of stuffiness that a lot of guitar-driven music tends to be. And emotionally satisfying at the same time.

AD: It seemed like you were working with more different instruments this time. There’s a sax solo in “Opaline Bubbletear.” Are there actual flutes in “Suddenly Flutes”? Who was playing the woodwinds or did you do that with synthesizers.

Alex Ewell: That was our friend Cole. Who we just asked them to…we didn’t really know what we wanted to do at the end of that song, exactly.

Joe Trainer: The wall of flutes

Alex Ewell: We had an idea for a flute section there.

Joe Trainer: Alex originally thought it would be funny, after this aggressive, punk sounding song, everything stops and then it was flutes. That was the idea. And then Cole took it and did what they did with it.

Alex Ewell: But I would say, if anything, there was more guitar on this album. We really tried to use guitars in different ways that maybe aren’t guitar-sounding.

AD: What would be a good example of that?

Alex Ewell: We have a number of songs with washy looped guitars that serve as a drone.

Joe Trainer: That in the past might have been a synth drone. We play it on guitars.

Emma Maatman: I feel like I’m playing a lot less of the Stereolab-constant organ drone thing. It’s happening a lot less on this album than it was on the last one. That was a pretty heavy pad throughout Mandatory. We’re using it a little more sparingly and with purpose now.

Joe Trainer: There’s definitely more dynamic use of the organ. Also, the guitar stuff in “Null Space.” The main driving force in that song is a guitar, but it’s a loop of a guitar that’s two chords laid over each other. This is getting in the weeds but …

AD: I love the weeds.

Joe Trainer: Yeah. We tried to use the guitar, as Kevin Shields would say, more as a noise generator. But at the same time, a lot of the songs have more chords and melodic resolves that feel more emotionally satisfying for us, and for the listener.

AD: Tell me about “Null Space” and how it came together. You said it was influenced by these electronic albums that you had been listening to.

Nathan O’Dell: I was just kind of, out of necessity, that song came to be. I had written one song for Mandatory Enjoyment, and I was like, “It’s time for me to write a song for this record.” We had a two-week period in between recording sessions, and I was like, all right, let me just demo this out. It originally started as a more Yo La Tengo-y sounding, straight up rock song. And as Joe said earlier, once the guys heard it, they were like, “Oh I hear it as this other synth-electronic song.

Joe Trainer: That song, in particular, gave such a nice emotional pay-off. Like the melody in the middle and the bass together make a very satisfying sound.

Alex Ewell: I had this idea to do a song, because I’d been working on this bubble sound on the drum machine. I kind of jokingly wanted to use it somewhere. And then I heard Nate’s demo, and I was like, this is perfect. I was able to cross the wires and make sense of the two ideas together.

Joe Trainer: The basis of the song is Nate’s voice in melody and the bass part. If you stripped everything away from it, that’s the song. Everything else is built on top of that foundation.

Alex Ewell: But it was crazy because it came together in a day or two.

Joe Trainer: Which is not typical of our songs. I think that song and “Soon-ish” are the ones that came together quickest and everything else was a long, drawn-out process, subtracting, adding, subtracting.

Nathan O’Dell: It’s kind of funny because the lyrics of that song are about taking two different worlds and mixing them together. I didn’t think it was going to have this electronic world meeting the standard rock world, so it ended up working out in this beautiful way.

AD: Do you all write songs? I know Joe does and it sounds like Nathan does as well.

Emma Maatman: I’ve been asked many times. They’ve all asked. Joe and everyone has been very encouraging, but I just cannot bring myself to demo out a song. It might be that I trust my bandmates so much. Why would I mess up a good thing? I would much rather focus on vocal melodies and lyrics and being a part of the process. All of the songs start out as a demo, but 90% of them end up being so wildly different from the demo. I get involved in the actual soup of the writing when we’re all together, rather than come forward with a “Here’s my song idea.” I like to be in the mix of it.

Joe Trainer: Alex definitely writes…

Alex Ewell: I’ve written mostly ambient tracks.

Joe Trainer: You write the ending tracks.

Alex Ewell: Right, both of the final tracks. Everything else is so collaborative. Usually, Joe has the original chords. And then we go from there.

Joe Trainer: And then we’ll try to blow up whatever that is.

Nathan O’Dell: There will be a composition where this part goes first, and then, oh no, I think it should go later in the song. We all have our hands in once the demo is sent out.

Joe Trainer: The demo is the dumb, Neanderthal part of the writing. Everything else that goes on top of it is what elevates the base of the song.

AD: It’s where the magic happens. Do you have any favorite bits or lines or sounds or moments on this album?

Joe Trainer: When Emma goes “Yeah,” or what do you say, “Huh”?

Emma Maatman: “Yet,” actually.

Joe Trainer: In the “Unshaped Road” at the end of the chorus. This “Yet.” The first time I heard that, I was like “Fuck yeah!” I got so excited. I’ll never forget hearing the first time Emma did that. I was shook. Also in “Godspin” when Alex brought it all together.

Alex Ewell: My dark horse pick would be “Minus World.” That song I feel like we…it was almost a back-burner song when we were recording, but as we put it together, the vocals made it, for me. They gave it so much character.

Joe Trainer: Nate’s verse in that one is another dope moment. I just love that Roy Orbison-esque moment. What are Emma and Nate’s favorite moments?

Nathan O’Dell: I was going to jump on “Unshaped Road,” the end of that song where all the extra toms and drums start coming in and it’s this cacophony. It feels really nice.

Emma Maatman: I would probably say “Godspin.” I’ve said it before, but whatever. That’s an Alex specialty to close out the record. It used field recordings that we had all taken on tour. Some were mine. Some were Nate’s. Some were Joe’s and Alex’s.

AD: Is that the one with the ocean sounds in it?

Emma Maatman: Yeah. It’s got Nate and I tapping on some big bell in Boise in the center of town. But also, when we were in Europe you can hear a choir of people singing in a cathedral. That’s what the noise is. It all came together so beautifully. The first time I heard it I got a little tear.

AD: Neat. Probably means more to you than to an outsider, though?

Emma Maatman: Completely. It’s something for us. Maybe people will find something in it. Hopefully they do.

AD: I was wondering about the tour. Are you going to do another punk DIY run through things or are you getting too big for that?

Joe Trainer: We’ve had an agent since the Horsegirl Tour, and so he just books everything, and our agent in Europe does the same. We still are very focused on curating shows that have the DIY spirit. Bands that are still definitely DIY bands on the shows. But they’re in more quote-unquote professional venues. We’re still playing a house show on our west coast show in Olympia, so we’re not above that. We don’t care. We’re not too commercial for anything yet.

Alex Ewell: We’ve played in some extremely strange situations. We’re ready for anything.

Nathan O’Dell: We’re all punk. It’s fine.

AD: But just in terms of comfort and not wrecking yourself on tour, it’s probably easier to do it the way you’re doing it now?

Joe Trainer: Yeah. We definitely know that this time, we want to space out the tours in a more deliberate way. Just for our own sanity. And just not destroying the songs. Not having the songs be ground to nothing as quickly as they did before. Which I guess even then, it wasn’t that quick.

Emma Maatman: In terms of us hating them.

Joe Trainer: For our upcoming tour, I think we’re not playing any old songs. We’re just playing stuff off the new LP. We’re being naughty in that way. But every time we play an old song, we’re just like ugh.

Emma Maatman: We’ll probably have some older stuff for the European tour. We’ll have to, because they like a longer set over there. We’ve been trying to shoehorn in some older tracks into the set, and it just doesn’t feel as good as the new stuff.

AD: Since it was written with a more live approach, is it pretty easy to adapt it for performance?

Joe Trainer: No.

AD: What do you have to do with it?

Alex Ewell: It’s really the opposite. We have to teach ourselves the songs now, and they’re way more complicated than we thought they would be. It’s a lot of playing a pedal or playing an e-bow on the bass. It’s almost like a performance in and of itself.

Joe Trainer: Rhythmically, they’re more complex, in terms of the drums. The motorik beat is easy.

Alex Ewell: I just got really used to playing that, so it became easy for me. Now that we have diverse beats, it’s a lot more confusing and a lot more work to learn them.

Joe Trainer: There are some songs like “Minus World,” that were pretty easy immediately to play. But then “Null Space,” because of the bubbles and the rhythm and because of how despite there being a lot of sound in it, there’s also a lot of space. I’m having to rhythmically play a loop pedal perfectly in time. You know, human error and a machine bashing up against each other. It would be easier to use samplers and computers to do a lot of this stuff, but we’re pretty dogmatic about that, and want to do as much as we can live.

Also we want the live experience and the experience of listening to a record to be two separate things. For us, as we’ve been practicing for the tours, the songs have definitely been more difficult. We’re slowly getting them to where they feel impactful for a live performance. There are differences in what we’re doing in the live show. It’s finding the balance. If it feels good to us, we’re pretty sure it will feel good to the people watching it. If it doesn’t feel good to us, it’s like, eh, we can’t really do it.

AD: Are you working on anything else right now? Do you have any side projects, or other arts that you do?

Joe Trainer: Nope. Dummy takes up all our creative…

AD: Some people are potters, and others are writing books, and all kinds of things.

Joe Trainer: Well, Nathan’s been working on DJing, right?

Nathan O’Dell: Yeah. I recently got into learning how to mix using a DJ controller. That’s been a lot of fun. I’d never realized how crazy of an art form it is. I always thought, you know, start track one. Track one ends, here comes track two. But I didn’t realize there was so much more that you could do to mix two different songs in different keys and accelerating or decelerating a song to match tempos. I’ve been getting into that, but I feel very new to it. But it is an exciting creative endeavor.

AD: Do you see that feeding into what you’re doing with Dummy?

Nathan O’Dell: I definitely see it working in, probably more in the future, just because I’ve been getting into it a lot more. Just techno music in general. It’s yet to make its way there. Stay tuned.

Joe Trainer: Definitely with the kind of influences that we started finding mid-way through recording the record, I feel like with Nathan finding a rave and techno scene here in LA will fit. Alex and I, we don’t like going out and being around people, but we’re finding a similar kind of electronic/dance influence. It’s from listening, instead of being a normal person and going out in public.

AD: What do you guys think makes a great song a great song? What’s the difference that makes a song really work or not?

Alex Ewell: I think it’s pretty complicated. There are a lot of elements that have to work in harmony. That’s what is so impressive about a good song. It’s hitting on a lot of different fronts.

Joe Trainer: But it also has to feel effortless.

Alex Ewell: Yeah. A gracefulness.

Joe Trainer: Something like…a song by JPEGMAFIA, the way that song is written is certainly different from the way Ronettes song is written, but they both sound effortless in their execution. But they sound so different. One is a very complicated, layered production, kind of messing with the listener. But it still sounds correct. But the Ronettes song, the chords and everything just rolls together in this beautiful linear fashion.

Alex Ewell: I think what you’re getting at is that there’s a spectrum of what purposes songs can serve and what feelings they can evoke. There’s obviously music that’s incredible that’s just drone-ambient and minimalist techno stuff that’s not necessarily a good song…

Joe Trainer: But beautiful in its own way.

Alex Ewell: Right.

Nathan O’Dell: I feel like the word intention comes forward, and that’s all that I have to say about that.

Joe Trainer: You hear a Neil Young song like “Tell Me Why,” and you think that’s a great fucking song. The chords and the lyrics and the emotional quality of it. And then you hear something by Pole and it’s just a little “boop…boop,” but it’s the intentionality of it.

Alex Ewell: Something that I get excited about is finding an artist who’s doing something way ahead of their time. They were inventing their sound. Even hearing it 40 or 50 years on can be really exciting.

Joe Trainer: Things that are ahead of their time even still. What would be a good example?

Alex Ewell: Mark Van Hoen and Locust. Not that he was inventing IDM. But I feel like he had a sound that I didn’t know existed then almost. And then I heard it, and I was like, how did I not know about this?

AD: There’s something that everybody recognizes about a great song that is just incredibly hard to put into words.

If you could tell people one thing about Dummy, if you could get one thing across, what would be the most important thing for people to know about you?

Alex Ewell: Like that we’re just normal people.

Emma Maatman: Normal music.

Alex Ewell: Just in the sense that we’ve been trying to make music our whole lives and it’s kind of been a lifelong project. But obviously we all have normal jobs, and we do that, too. That’s not always the case for a lot of musicians.

Joe Trainer: I think being working class people and making music, we just really valuing a general authenticity. The art that we’re making feels very authentic in a world that now feels very inauthentic. It doesn’t feel like the effort is being put in. Authentic is something that we really care about. And I think that gets pooh poohed a lot.

Alex Ewell: That word is kind of vague.

Joe Trainer: It is but it isn’t.

Emma Maatman: We joke about things like “This song’s going to blow up on Tik Tok,” but we’re not trying to make the kind of music that gets thrown in your face all the time. We all come from diverse backgrounds, but we’ve all been music lovers and musicians for our entire lives. This is the first musical project, for me anyway, that’s gotten to any kind of level like this, where people want to interview us and talk to us about it. We work really hard.

Joe Trainer: We work harder than we probably should. We feel compelled to work really hard on our art. And that’s not just on the musical side. It’s the videos and the art that Emma makes and the visuals that Nate makes for the live show. It’s all very intentional. The other shit, outside of our little bubble, we don’t really care about that when we’re making the art. We’re just making what we think is good and cool and interesting.

AD: I think that’s the only way to do it. I don’t have any other questions. Is there anything else that I neglected to ask you that you’d like to talk about? Anything important that we didn’t cover?

Joe Trainer: Maybe the lyrics? If Nate and Emma want to talk about that?

AD: What was your favorite lyric on this whole thing, Emma?

Emma Maatman: My favorite lyric. “With Little Wings.” it makes me laugh, but I love it.

Nathan O’Dell: Lyric-wise, compared to Mandatory Enjoyment, I definitely approached this album with a more internal or close-to-me view. A lot of the lyrics on Mandatory Enjoyment were about things I saw going on in the world. This is definitely more immediate.

Emma Maatman: I think all the songs are more personal this time around.
