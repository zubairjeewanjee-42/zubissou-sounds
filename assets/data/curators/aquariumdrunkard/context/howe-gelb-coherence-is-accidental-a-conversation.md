## Body

Poet philosopher Howe Gelb is a natural storyteller. And it turns out the Tucson-based songwriter doesn’t even need his signature voice—husky and low, a rumbling, phantasmagoric presence fronting his genre-crossing band Giant Sand for more than 40 years—to get his tall tales across. On his latest, Weathering Some Piano, Gelb’s voice does make a few brief but welcome appearances, but the focus is on his piano playing—solitary, unadorned, self-recorded at his home in Tucson “during random moments of weathering.” Featuring 13 improvised, or spontaneously composed, piano ballads, it draws on a wellspring of gospel, blues, jazz, and folk forms, all while maintaining Gelb’s idiosyncratic feel and phrasing. He joined us to discuss the record, the “illusion of coherence,” and co-writing the 2022 animated sci-fi film Quantum Cowboys. The conversation has been edited for clarity. | j woodbury

Weathering Some Piano by Howe Gelb

Aquarium Drunkard: About a decade ago I helped host an in-store performance with you at a record shop, Zia Records in Tucson. We dealt with some fascinating electrical issues, and you told me about strange electrical “gremlins” that sometimes messed with you technologically. 

Howe Gelb: Yeah, that’s mainly a Tucson thing. The trick is not letting them get to you.

AD: You just roll with it?

Howe Gelb: I guess at this level of entertainment, it’s all do-it-yourself, intentionally. So when things go wrong, you’re kind of used to it. Because things are always going wrong when you’re doing your own thing. From the very beginning, with our punk band, you’d set things up yourself. There would be a like-minded friend helping set up local shows and they would be doing everything themselves. You got used to things going wrong because like there’s nobody to blame—you just know this is how it goes. You’re not doing it for economic reasons or attention. You’re doing it because you have to do it, otherwise you feel like you’re going to wither.

AD: You’re talking about dedication. I think people are interested in how to tap into that passion on an individual level right now, because I feel like a lot of the algorithmic qualities of our online life feel like they’re whittling away people’s ability to connect to things that mean something to them. We get distracted by everything else. Is that how it feels to you?

Howe Gelb: Yes, it’s been a steady eradication. Things are so easy. The less of your own energy and time you put into something, the body and mind deems it less worthy than something that you had to have patience for and really put in the time and days. I think it’s across the board. You used to have to search out bands you liked, either on record or when they played live, or if anybody ever wrote about them. Somehow all that effort translated in your whole system, body and mind, into a worth. You had gone on an odyssey to seek it out. From what I gather from my younger friends, the same thing is going on with dating, with all the dating apps.

AD: You know everything about somebody before you even meet them, right? 

Howe Gelb: In the old days, if you saw somebody you didn’t know, somebody at a cafe and they pondered you a little bit, you might be like, “Oh, I want to go meet that person.” But you don’t know where to go find them. And if you do find them, that’s a whole other thing: to get the phone number and then call them—but maybe they’re not home? You don’t hear from them. Maybe they don’t have a message machine? Et cetera. It took so much effort and time.

And then, you finally get to hook up with them, and by hooking up, I mean meet up with them…and you have to see if it goes anywhere. It took so much energy and time to put into that person that I think you gave things more of a chance back the. Now it’s all over and done so quickly, from what my younger friends say. We have a supper club, you see, and in this supper club it’s ages 29 to 73. So one woman tells us of her dating experiences. She’s in the middle of all. It’s fascinating to hear how it’s going down, but it kind of reminds me, it’s like music anymore. It’s too easy, too easy to get to.

AD: When you read old personal ads, they’re like these evocative little missives. “Hey, I saw you on the G train.” I follow this Instagram page called Long Lost Personals, and even when there’s like a little bit of an overt or creepy or pervy quality to some of those things—and sometimes there is—there’s also a mournfulness, this idea that you missed something potentially. 

I’m sure you’ve had experiences where you hear a song and then you go to look for that song again, but you can’t find it. This has happened to me. You find things that seem like they should be the song, but it’s still not. With streaming? You’re going to find it again. Because they’re just going to keep serving you similar things. I don’t think that squares with your trajectory in music. You once told me you took the “Neil Young path,” where you follow your eccentricities and muse wherever it leads you, like from punk stuff, to a record like Weathering Some Piano, where it’s you alone at the piano and all the mood comes from the way you capture these sounds. It seems like you follow your own whim. I think that when we talk about algorithms and stuff, it seems like they’re intent on breeding “whims” out of us.

Howe Gelb: Well, yes, but also I think you have to stop using the word “they.” I mean, you see “they” used—and I don’t mean in the pronoun sense—I mean “they” used in the conspiracy sense. People use “they” every which way, even in commercials: they don’t want you to know about this hack. 

AD: Right, “Your doctor doesn’t want you to know this one weird trick.”

Howe Gelb: When you’re thinking about how to appeal to a consumer and get them to bite, “they” is one of the most successful means. So I don’t know if “they” even know how the algorithms are set, I think it’s “it.” It doesn’t want you to know this or to know that. But I don’t want to say “they,” because that immediately starts separating us again and there’s so much separation going on between people that it really has driven the population insane. 

AD: It’s true. In the liner notes you say that “Nature is, in and of itself, is in itself an improvisation.” I’m sure you plink away on the piano all the time. How did you shape these improvisations into the coherent statement we hear? 

Howe Geb: Well, I think the coherence is often accidental. Because people tend to see patterns in chaos. They see patterns in stars, clouds, patterns on stains on the wall. 

AD: We look for stories. 

Howe Gelb: That’s the way our minds work. It’s you. If you help it along a little bit, it’ll seem as you suggest. But really when you step back far enough from anything, it seems more coherent. Get further from the planet and everybody looks like they want the same thing, everyone looks like the same person. So I think the further away you are, the more coherent something seems. I don’t know if it’s an illusion or if it’s a law of science. As far as the piano goes…hey, let me know if I’m just blowing too much hot air here, because…

AD: No way, I’m digging this. 

Howe Gelb: People have short attention spans. 

AD: It’s true but we like to think of Aquarium Drunkard as something for people who still enjoy paying attention to things. But I know what you mean. I don’t want to say “they” have short attention spans, but I know I sometimes have a short attention span. I don’t want to externalize it all, I struggle with it too and it’s getting worse. 

Howe Gelb: Exactly. We’re fucked. 

AD: We’re fucked. [Both laugh] 

Howe Gelb: But look at any film. When you give yourself over to that film, that movie is always different from any other in the sense that there’s a certain logic in play, where the director is kind of like a god. He made a world where it makes sense to the viewer once they start understanding the logic embedded in that film, or the reasoning embedded in that film. It may lean on a few basic principles like, I don’t know, gravity, greed, love, things like that, or chase scenes, but the director can’t help but have a certain way of thinking embedded in the film. Like Wes Anderson or even John Ford. The actors pick up on it a lot of times, like in a Woody Allen film, where many of the characters end up doing a Woody Allen impersonation. So in that sense, when you go and part in a piece of art, that director’s personality or logic is speaking to you. You can understand it and work with it, then you get to extract from it whatever you can, depending on how you feel toward that particular director, consciously, subconsciously, or even unconsciously. 

So the same thing happens with an album; there’s a certain logic embedded in it. And you’re either like, “No, I don’t get it,” and you walk away from it. Or, you begin to get it and then you get it more and more with every listen. It can actually deliver comfort to you or become the a soundtrack to your life. That’s the illusion of coherency—thinking those things that you heard in that piano record coalesce like that. 

The other the only other thing is that because it was done in such a relatively small, amount of time—just a few months, not years—you end up with a period piece. You know, “The guy’s going through his blue period or he’s going through his rain period or he’s going through, you know some type of phase or season.”  

AD: If we’re talking about Neil Young, “The Ditch Trilogy” or whatever, or now he’s doing Trans it’s like synth pop  whatever. 

Howe Gelb: And he just makes that shit up. Everybody in in the arts has, in a way, had to go into the arts because of the way their brains function. Or have difficulty functioning. You use your so-called instinct, and make art that’s favorable first to yourself. Then somebody asks you about and you’ll then take the time to think about it and come up with, “Oh, this why I did it.” But when you’re doing it, I don’t think you’re that aware of it a lot. 

Some realists are—some people have that capability. You know there’s various formats, the whole spectrum is there. You can be a realist and have planned it all out and measured the outcome in advance. But others just kind of throw caution to the wind and go, “I’m gonna survive this one way or the other.” Maybe that’s what it comes down to in the end. How you’ve grown up? Have you allowed yourself to take a chance, without focusing in on what it can get you or where you’re going to end up with it, or where even where it’s going to end up period. 

AD: I know the 1976 Polanski movie The Tenant was was hugely important to you. You seem to speak and think cinematically. In 2022 you co-wrote the animated sci-fi film Quantum Cowboys with Geoff Marslett, which you also acted in. What was it like making your own movie? 

Howe Gelb: Wicked and wild. It was it was a lot of fun. It came at a time where I didn’t see it coming. The collaborative exercise of writing a movie was engaging in itself, especially with somebody who’s done it before and knows what they’re doing. So with Geoff, it was basically a volley where we would get together and hash out some ideas and jot them down. We’re doing that at a distance, he’s from Texas and we would be writing emails with these ideas, or talking on the phone. You get spasms of storyline inspiration and it just flows out of you: Then this would happen and then this and then we’ll have this. And how about this? That that kind of exercise is not only a lot of fun, but it feels like it’s a muscle you’re exercising in depiction and the dance between fact and fiction. Because a lot of fiction becomes fact. A lot of science fiction became fact. It’s almost like it’s a manifestation or like humans, with our imaginations, are picking up the smoke from a future fire so to speak.

AD: It’s a pretty mind-bending movie. 

Howe Gelb: I heard this story about these hikers in the ’70s who stumbled upon some sort of ark in the Arivaca mountains, a story I picked up from a Tohono Oʼodham person, so I took that premise and ran with it. But Geoff actually had to make the film—I just got to come up with ideas, but he had to actually film them. So it was a like a surprise to see it revealed. But here’s my thing, I don’t think there’s ever been a movie that really depicts the way music really sounded in the Southwest in the mid-to-late 1800s. There’s never been that kind of soundtrack in a western film. Because it had to have sounded bad. 

AD: [Laughs] 

Howe Gelb: Think about all the instruments. It was so hot and there’s no place to get out of the sun. Everything is dirty all the time and you couldn’t get parts, the right strings. You know, drums would have been dented, horns would have been dented. You would have had mismatched pieces. Things wouldn’t stay in tune because of the severe heat. So everything is rattling, it would have been a cacophony of sound when you tried to play anything with a group of people. That was the original inspiration for my idea to make a film: “Let’s have a western that shows how incredible the sound was back then, when people began to play music.” 

Some of this did make it into the movie. You have one person singing acapella while hanging laundry, and that sounds fine. Neko Case does that in the film, that sounds bird-like. Been then imagine in the next scene two people trying to play together—it’s already starting to get a little funky. We got a little of that into the film when John Doe and Alex Coc are playing in the saloon, Cox on that out of tune piano and John trying to play slide with a broken beer bottle. I think that was as close to my original concept as we got. But imagine it, like 12 people playing out of tune instruments together, like on a Saturday night dance in a barn. It would have sounded like Sonic Youth with acoustic instruments, just din, a cacophony, and people would have been fine with it, because they just wanted to hold another human and dance and have a sound other than the wind.
