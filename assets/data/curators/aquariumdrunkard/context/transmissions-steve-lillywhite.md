## Body

Welcome back to Transmissions with Jason Woodbury. This week, Steve Lillywhite, a producer who’s had as pivotal a role in shaping your host’s musical taste as anyone. In this conversation, Lillywhite opens up about working with artists like Dave Matthews Band, U2, Phish, XTC, The La’s, Marshall Crenshaw, The Killers, and more. From The Joshua Tree to Billy Breathes, from Before These Crowded Streets to Field Day, Lillywhite speaks about it all, the influence of dub, his production approach, and more.

Lillywhite is somebody we’ve wanted to have on this show very long time, and we were excited to ring him up. Big thanks to Grayson Haver Currin, the incredible critic and writer you are no doubt familiar with, for helping connect us to Steve.

AD: I love when you can turn a post-apocalyptic scenario on its head a little bit. 

Steve Lillywhite: It’s funny that David Byrne and Dave Matthews write lyrics in a similar way. They both write in this way of just— they don’t write poetry and then sing the poetry, you know? It’s not like an Elvis Costello or a Bob Dylan, you know. They just sing whatever comes out of their mouth, and then that eventually turns into words.

AD: Yeah, you know, it’s funny because thinking about the way that thread runs through a lot of your collaborators. Eno was sort of a pioneer of that too, of just sort using vocal sounds that somehow end up forming into lyrics. When I listen to some of the stuff from his early, more lyrical kind of work, you know what I mean?

Steve Lillywhite: Yeah. Oh, Brian is fantastic, but I don’t know whether David Byrne took it from his influence working with Brian, but certainly with U2—I mean, Bono writes in that similar way, and they hadn’t worked with Eno until the fourth album. And I know that Bono wrote lyrics in that way from the first album onward. He’s never been… I mean, he would try to write poetry and go, “Steve, listen to these words.” And he’d write, and he’d talk them to me, and I’d go, “Okay, Bono, that sounds great, but go in and sing them.” And he’d go, “Oh, okay.” So he would go in and sing them, and they sounded terrible because it didn’t work with the music. He can sing anything—he’s a brilliant singer—but he has to believe in the lyrics that he’s singing. I mean, he’ll sing in gobbledygook. He has this whole language that he calls “bongolese.” But then he has to make the bongolese work for himself. Whereas some—David Byrne sings in this gobbledygook style, and he doesn’t really need to make it make sense as much as Bono does.

AD: When I think about a lot of the U2 stuff that I’m most drawn to. Your early records with them are super captivating, you know, because they really just have so much energy. And I remember being a kid and working backwards from The Joshua Tree to hear some of that earlier stuff and really being way, like, into the early stuff that you were doing when it was more what my young teenage brain could sort of read as punk. You know what I mean? Which, I know, is the world you kind of came from too, right?

Steve Lillywhite: Yeah. It was the testosterone because now, as I’m older, I listen back to those records, and they sound so almost tiring. You’re only 40—I’m 30 years older than you—so it is like, oh my God. But at that age, you need that sort of thing coming.

AD: I’ve always been drawn to the more spiritual or mystical aspects of music, and you do get the sense of, at least for me, I hear in the early, early stuff the kind of apocalyptic purity that only comes from that, like, age of being a boy growing up in the specific zones of conflict that they had grown up in. 

Steve Lillywhite: I also prefer, if you say, “What’s your favorite Dave Matthews song?” I would go towards “The Dreaming Tree,” “Crush,” you know, those sorts of ones. And also with U2, I loved “Drowning Man” and these more sort of whimsical, sort of sonic soundscapes rather than—I mean, I love “Vertigo,” I love “Beautiful Day,” you know, I love all those sort of hits, “New Year’s Day.” But yeah, I like the more sonic landscapes that I pretty much put together for them.

AD: Well, I was thinking about the contrast of the young, strident Bono of the early work with something like “Who’s Gonna Ride Your Wild Horses?” I mean, to me, that’s one of the high watermarks, where the poetry that Bono’s aspiring for is perfectly matched with this melody that he’s found—not a single word feels out of place in that one. And then, like you said, sonically, it’s incredible that you can chart your relation over the course of this time period—how much even you guys have grown in your own collaborative spirit.

Steve Lillywhite: Yeah. I mean, I worked with them last year, actually.

I went to the Sphere, and I was consulting with them on the sound for their gigs. And halfway through the rehearsals, Bono said, “Steve, we’ve been recording this song called ‘Atomic City,’ but it doesn’t give us that essence of U2 yet. Can we go into a studio and just record it with the band?” So, even though they were doing the gigs with the other drummer, we went into Sound City Studio with Larry midway through the rehearsals, and for the weekend off, we cut this song, “Atomic City.” And it was like, all of a sudden, I was like, “I’ve got this song with a great guitar riff from Edge,” which—I’m always looking for the guitar riffs to be brilliant on a U2 song because the singing is always going to be the singing. But I think the ones that make them better than some of the other songs are Edge’s contribution—it is the music. And this was a really great guitar riff. So I went, “Yes.” And I remember walking around the studio going, “Oh my God, I’m in the studio setting up with U2 again to do a record.” I mean, what would Steve Lillywhite do? I actually found myself thinking, “What would Steve Lillywhite…? Ah, yes, that’s what he would do.” So, it was quite funny—like an out-of-body experience where I was—and I hate talking about myself in the third person, but I did that.

AD: You sort of have to when you’re faced with those moments of collapsing time—time collapsing in on itself. Who was that guy? Was that guy me? He was you, and he is also this other guy now too.

Steve Lillywhite: This is one thing—and I will be long gone when you get to my age—but there is something that I’m sort of experiencing now. It’s like your life gets stretched, right? As you get older, it goes like that, and more and more, yourself at, say, age 24 becomes like—it gets stretched further and further away. So you think of yourself as being less and less that person until, actually, it gets to a point where it almost snaps off, and you see that person as a completely different person than who— And I’ve had sort of these—I sort of put my life down into three different lives almost. There’s that first life, that middle life, and now I’m on that third life. And I’m very lucky to have gotten to the third of life. But it feels like, certainly, life number one is now sort of out on its own. I don’t feel a connection to life number one. So I can look back and go, oh, actually, that was pretty good. That guy—that guy was pretty good. Because my ego is not involved in it at all, because I am not that person.

AD: Yeah. You know, it’s funny because one thing I wanted to—it’s funny—something that I wanted to sort of lean towards is that question of so much of your work. Let’s say, something like with U2 or other groups—it’s so collaborative. There’s always been a collaborative element, even just beyond you working directly with the band, but also you working with other producers as well. It’s a multi—it’s a multi-part thing. Was the ego—sometimes I wonder how you navigated. There’s a lot of egos in the room. You know what I mean?

Steve Lillywhite: I never work with other producers. My situation—when it says “Produced by Brian Eno, Daniel Lanois, and Steve Lillywhite,” for instance—I’m never in the studio with them. Imagine it much more as a linear…what’s the race where you pass the baton? Relay race. Imagine it like a relay race, and Brian Eno and Lanois start it. Then they pass me the baton, and I’m running the last leg of the race. That’s it, really, because I’m never in the studio making decisions with those guys. They basically start it, and then they hand it over to me when the band have basically broken their backs.

I’m still, deep down in my soul, a believer that if the person starts a record and finishes the same record, it’s more of a complete, meaningful journey. And weirdly, with Dave Matthews Band, there’s only been one album that I have done everything from beginning to end, because Under the Table and Dreaming, they decided to—it was at those days where everyone used a different mixer. It was when a guy called Tom Lord-Alge was deemed the guy who was good at mixing. So I basically handed in my record to RCA and went back to England, and they decided that they wanted Tom to mix the record. For me, that meant that I’d failed, because up until that point, I’d always seen the record from beginning to end. And of course, when I heard the mixes that Tom did, it was okay, but it wasn’t my vision—my vision from beginning to end wasn’t.

Now, I can say that my mixes, I think, have been lost. I would love people to hear my version of Under the Table and Dreaming. So, I was told that the album was doing well, but I was back in England, and my marriage was failing, so I was feeling pretty down about my life. And so I was surprised when they said, “We want Steve to come back for Crash.” Great. They enjoyed their part working with me. So I went back and did Crash. And they didn’t—to be honest, the record label didn’t say, “We want Tom Lord-Alge to mix it.” But when it came to that point, I thought, well, maybe I could go in the studio with Tom, and we could work together, because I’m such a collaborator that together, he can mix it, and I can be there with him with my vision. But he can get maybe more of a different sound. And we tried that, and honestly, he did three or four songs on that album, and I mixed the rest on my own. And no one knows the difference. That’s the thing—there’s no way you would know the songs that he did and the songs that I did on Crash. So, the only Dave Matthews album that was my vision from beginning to end was Before These Crowded Streets.Yeah, and that was when they really started evolving. But still, I think Before These Crowded Streets is my favorite Dave Matthews album because it’s the most challenging. I mean, it is a fantastic record. And all the little commercials—Dave said, “I want to do these little thirty-second pieces.” We wanted it to feel like a complete experience, almost like our version of Dark Side of the Moon.

AD: Well, let me just tell you—just direct from the heart—you guys succeeded. That is, hands down, the one for me. In eighth grade, it was my Dark Side of the Moon, and I’m sitting there with my headphones on, having maybe listened to the Goo Goo Dolls or something in the hour before or whatever. But now I’ve got that on, and I’m hearing the Kronos Quartet sort of do this haunting little concerto in the middle of this thing, where I’ve just heard this sort of Al Green-meets-Sting-esque R&B ballad. Anyway, all I’m saying is that album really does…I mean, I think in some ways, it’s the peak record for me. I think of that band as a statement, not to mention just the harmonies on the record. And it’s interesting—essentially, previously, you had sort of… you didn’t want to put an emphasis on the electric guitar element with that band?

Steve Lillywhite: That’s all me. I never wanted to, because I take so much—so much—from my very first experience with a band. When I see a band live, if I love them live—and I did this with Guster, for instance, and I did it with Dave Matthews Band—I just saw them and went, it’s all there. Everything that I need to make a great record is there. It’s up to me now to find the best way of making that work on a record. 

So on Under the Table and Dreaming, Dave said—because when I saw them, Tim wasn’t in the band. Tim was this friend. And Dave said to me when we had our first meeting—he says, “I’ve got this friend. I want to bring him in to do overdubs because we only have one acoustic guitar.” So the idea was Tim was going to come in and do his thing on these songs, and I said, “No, don’t bring him in for overdubs. Bring him in right at the very beginning.” Because I wanted to have—I see everything like a football team—I wanted to have two acoustic guitars, left and right. And I think Dave, even on the credit, said, “Dave Matthews, left guitar.” And I didn’t want Dave to do double tracking, because that is so normal. If you have two different players playing, they can be—they can play something different, and it won’t make a difference because it’s two different styles. So it’s not sitting there making it completely like an Eagles record…

AD: It’s like Verlaine/Lloyd. It’s like that Television record, Marquee Moon—that sort of, yeah, attention, interplay, but each occupying its own thing, right?

Steve Lillywhite: Own space. Yeah. So Dave said, “Okay.” So we got Tim in, and one thing it really did—it enabled Dave and Tim, who are hilarious together, as you’ve seen at Dave and Tim concerts. They had this…

AD: Oh, yeah. Live at Luther College. They’re so funny together. Yeah.

Steve Lillywhite: Yes. Well, it’s like that all the time in the studio. And this was the beginning of this sort of joy that making records with them was. It was a joyful experience—one, two, and three, no question. It was a joyful experience.

And when it came to four, I said to them, “Okay, guys, beware.” Because one, two, and three—we’d gone to Woodstock twice, and then we went to The Plant in Sausalito, California. So those three albums, it was like going on holiday, right? Time. We went away and had this experience of being away from our home. That’s very important. I love that. But I said, “We’ll make a record in Charlottesville. Build a studio, but you must be aware that there are red flags with this.” And I told them right from the beginning, “There are red flags because you are not going away to make a record. You are at home, going to work.” 

And I said, “Mentally, when you are going to work, you start to complain about it. Everyone complains about going to work, and it’s your right, because it’s not a vacation. It’s not something that you love. So I said, ‘It will take longer.'”

And of course, I was absolutely right. And I’ve heard stories like, “Well, the studio wasn’t great.” Doesn’t matter about the studio. It’s the mentality of the people making it. And I tried to explain to them that those first three albums, it was a magical, joyful experience, but…

If you really want to push through this idea of working in your hometown, you have to be prepared to be tough and to battle it out because it’s not going to be so easy.” 

And I was prepared for it. I was ready. I could see the big picture. I could see that it was not going to be the same as the other three, which was great, you know. 
And Dave was, yes, admittedly, in his sort of dark period, which had started with Crowded Streets and was continuing a little bit more. But that is a valid emotion. Those things can be great in music. Look at “Philadelphia” by—I mean, Bruce Springsteen when he did that.

AD:  Oh yeah. “Streets of Philadelphia.” Yeah, incredible.

Steve Lillywhite: It was brilliant. And this was an artistic, creative statement that I felt was very good. Now, it wasn’t finished. I said to them, look, it’s—but all of us, they suddenly were living at home. And so it became like going to work. Big difference—going to work, where there’s a never-ending job, to going away and doing it for a specific amount of time.

But great art is timeless. So the timeless—the amount of time you spent doing it—doesn’t matter. It’s the mental resilience to keep pushing it forward, to make that creative statement that, honestly, I feel I had. But they lost their nerve.

You know, it’s sad. Because it wasn’t even the band who lost their nerve. It was Bruce Flohr who went behind my back and got them all to mutiny against me. And honestly, Dave was like, “Well, Steve…” because I said once to Dave that the band is the most important thing. This was during Crash. Dave was in a bad way, and he was going, “I’m not sure if I want to be in a band anymore.” He’s like, “I can make records. I don’t have to be in a band.”

I said, “Dave, please, the band is the most important thing. People love bands, and you’ve got this wonderful, eclectic group of people, which is unusual. You can go and work with as many session people as you want, but you’ve got this crazy violin player. You’ve got this sax player who is like a genius, although flawed. You’ve got all this ragtag—it’s a bit like the E Street Band—and you’ve got this band, and that’s why everyone loves Dave Matthews Band, that lineup, because it was where the sum was greater than the parts, right?”

And now, you’ve got incredible brass sections. You’ve got Tim playing loads of electric guitar. It’s okay. It’s good, but it doesn’t have—it’s not special like it was then.

AD: Well, it’s certainly a different beast. 

Steve Lillywhite:  A different beast. Yeah, yeah, yeah. There’s no question.

AD: That’s for sure. But it’s funny, that thing you’re talking about—like what people love is a band. When I look at your vast sort of discography and think about the through lines, I think that’s something I recognize often, right? It seems like you do—be it Guster, be it Phish. You did Billy Breathes, an incredible record. And that’s my favorite Phish studio album. But then you even came back and did Joy with them in 2009, and that’s a good record.

Steve Lillywhite:  “Backwards Down the Number Line.” I like that song. There’s some good stuff there. Billy Breathes was this—this, I mean, actually, it’s—that sits together as an album, as one of the best albums I’ve done in the way that it goes through this journey. Again, it’s a journey album.

AD:  That makes such a huge impression on me—similar to Before These Crowded Streets—the way there’s a flow together as a statement. I know people wonder now, has the album outlasted its time as the sort of—I guess I want to say—ingestion form for music, whatever you want to say? Has it outlasted that? Are we going to come back to a singles age where we’re back in like that? I don’t know, but I’ll never—nothing will ever beat the experience.

Steve Lillywhite: It depends. Depends on how much weed the American public smokes. Because honestly—I mean, I’m being honest—if weed was completely… because there’s something about smoking a joint and sitting back and listening to 40 minutes, 45 minutes of music that is just as good as it ever gets.

AD:  I mean, I think that’s what Alan Watts once said. He said some line—it’s best to be sober. A hilarious thing for Alan Watts to say, by the way, notorious boozer Alan Watts—but like he said, it’s best to be sober, but if you’re going to smoke some weed, at least listen to music while you’re doing it. And I… yeah, so I think—

Steve Lillywhite: You know who Alan Watts is then?  

AD: Oh, yeah, yeah, yeah.

Steve Lillywhite:  Yeah, I’m a big fan.

AD:  Me too. I think he’s absolutely brilliant. Absolutely brilliant. But I do think that you’re right, and I think it’s also the pace of social media that has created this rush to consume—to watch 30 seconds of something and then move on. And I think that maybe an album sort of format is at odds with that, but I just think there will always be people who want to carve out space for that, to take that time and to invest in a narrative, invest in a journey. And I think that that’s something that a great deal of the records from your catalog have—that “we’re going somewhere” thing. And the way Billy Breathes ends, it’s like… it’s just a triumphant finish to the epic fantasy movie you just watched. Do you know what I mean?

Steve Lillywhite: Yeah. And, you know, the great thing about streaming and the ability to listen to anything at any time means that these records don’t get lost—because we all need it, and because it’s all about word of mouth. You get these groups of friends who still… all it needs is one of them to have heard it from their brother or something like that to discover something. And then they like it, and then they tell their friends, and then there’s this… and it spreads. That’s the best advertising for your art—the fact that it’s good. You know, you can almost… you can take it to the election—that the fact that those ads meant nothing. People didn’t… I mean, eventually, they decided that wasn’t what they wanted. It’s nice to see that Kari Lake lost in Arizona.

AD: Well, I’ll tell you what.

Steve Lillywhite: The one little piece of…

AD: No, we could go down that road, but we’ll—I’ll avoid making a public enemy of Kari Lake right now. But what I will say, though, is that idea of people discovering the work on its own or evaluating it in different contexts. You were talking earlier about feeling like the younger you is almost like a different person. Something that I found very curious is—I’m a fan. A fan. To say I’m a fan is putting it mildly. Another record of yours that I’m just absolutely obsessed with is The La’s album.

Steve Lillywhite: Oh, wow.

AD: And the funny thing about that is those guys, they were so annoyingly critical of the record itself.

Steve Lillywhite: Yep.

AD: Does that complicate your feeling with it when somebody sort of does that sort of thing, right? How do you come back to that record? Does it come with a little chip on its shoulder? 

Steve Lillywhite: No, no. You know what? He was—Lee Mavers is the genius, the songwriter, and the main guy of this band. I think he was probably right because he was so talented, and he was so—I’ve always said, probably, if you lined up all the artists I’ve worked with, gave them an acoustic guitar, and said, “Okay, Bono, sing a song. Dave, sing a song. Lee, sing a song,” or whoever, and have this—and you sit there marking out of 10—I think Lee Mavers would win that battle because, at his best, all of these people at their best… And honestly, the fact that the album was not huge almost validates his point. Now, I love it. And people would go and see them live and go, “I don’t understand what he’s talking about,” because it’s a faithful reproduction of it, it is what they do. It’s that sort of sound, and that’s how I saw it. I saw them. That’s what I do. I try to record a version of what I see them do live. Because—and that seems like, “Oh, well, then you just record it.” No, there’s so much more art to it than just recording it because…

You have to make it an album that works rather than just recording what they do live. I mean, Guster, for me, is a great example because any other producer would’ve seen them live and said, ‘That’s bongos and two acoustic guitars. You’ve got to have real drums.’ And I said, ‘No, this works.’ When I saw them live, it was just incredible. I mean, Brian doing The Thunder God—it was just great. And I thought, my job is to make that work on record. So I basically dismantled every single drum part, and we built it up from scratch with all these different sounds to make it interesting. And it goes right back—having… and for me, I see live as the sort of—what is the template for what I have to do? So anyway, that’s—I want to make that on record, and that’s an art form in itself. That’s all I’m saying. 

Because a lot of people will say, ‘Well, you just—’ but I see it as a limitation. And I think all art needs these limitations because otherwise… because when Peter Gabriel said to me, “I want no cymbals on my album,” I didn’t see that as a problem. I saw that as a problem to be solved. How do we make it good with no cymbals? I don’t see it.

Like, no, we’ve got to have cymbals because every single successful record in the last 20 years has had cymbals on it. No, I didn’t see it like that. I saw it as a challenge to make it work without cymbals, and I saw Guster as a challenge to make it work without real drums. I saw Dave Matthews as a challenge—to take these 20-minute jams and turn them into songs that were not too short, like pop songs, but not too long that they were boring. That was all down to me, to make them viable for the format—viable formats—and that’s my job.

AD: Wait, you mentioned “Drive In, Drive Out” earlier, right? That’s the one at the end where somebody says— you say— ‘Cut this bad shit.’ Is that what he says?

Steve Lillywhite: “As bad as shit,” or something like that?

AD: Yeah, he goes, ‘cut this bad shit…”

Steve Lillywhite: I used to love leaving those things on records—lots of little clues—because…
