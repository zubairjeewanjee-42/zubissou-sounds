## Body

Lagniappe (la ·gniappe) noun ‘lan-ˌyap,’ — 1. An extra or unexpected gift or benefit. 2. Something given or obtained as a gratuity or bonus.

Simon Joyner is this week’s guest on our Neil Young podcast, All One Song — but he’s not just that! With his friends the Nervous Stars, he also generously recorded a new Lagniappe Session for us … and it’s all Neil Young songs! What a gift. Simon, of course, is one of our greatest singer-songwriters, with a deep discography stretching back to the early 1990s. He’s one of those guys who just seems to get better with age; to wit, his upcoming album Tough Love on the Sophomore Lounge label is one of his very best, a deeply felt collection that feels timeless and timely all at once. We’ll have more to say about it in the upcoming weeks, but for now, enjoy Simon and the Nervous Stars tackling some of Neil’s very best songs. Here’s Joyner in own words below …

Simon Joyner & the Nervous Stars :: After the Gold Rush (Neil Young)

I chose this one for obvious reasons, the first Neil Young song I heard from my father’s record collection. And the one I memorized at a tender age and sang out loud to myself from my front step until I got to my south Omaha grade school most mornings. I remember timing it so I could sing the last line “flying mother nature’s silver seed to a new home in the sun” just before I got within earshot of the crossing guards. Sometimes I’d sing “Somewhere Over the Rainbow,” another song about escaping bad situations. In retrospect, my unhappy parents and their impending divorce was weighing heavy on me and both those songs really helped me feel a little less alone.

Simon Joyner & the Nervous Stars :: Too Far Gone (Neil Young)

This is one of my favorite slice of life songs from Neil. It’s about a relationship that succeeds and fails right out of the gate. I love how he taps into the bittersweet memory and takes most of the blame for why it didn’t work out. When a song makes you want to go out and get your heart broken, it’s doing something right. I like the earlier versions of the song, the version on Freedom is probably my least favorite rendition but it’s hard to fuck up a song this good. There are hardly any lyrics, Neil at his best is better than Hemingway with the word economy. And it’s a great song to sing around the campfire, everyone wants to take the blame when the chorus is that easy to sing!

Simon Joyner & the Nervous Stars :: Safeway Cart (Neil Young)

Sleeps with Angels from 1994 was the Neil Young “comeback” album that really rocked my world. When I was recording my album Heaven’s Gate in 1995, I took inspiration especially from “Safeway Cart” for my song “Obituary.” The idea of building a stark narrative around two chords. There’s a little descending single note thing on “Safeway Cart” that I tried to do on “Obituary” as an acoustic guitar overdub too. The lyrics are impressionistic and very dark, another isolation and perseverance song. Maybe about America? 

Simon Joyner & the Nervous Stars :: Love in Mind (Neil Young)

A perfect, understated song about how love can get you through the day. Again, a little slice of life with some personal observations about how uptight and self-denying society expects us to be thrown in on the bridge. And a great solo Neil at the piano song too. Like most of Neil’s greatest songs, there’s an aspect of the loner in this song, everything is from his point of view and there are no other characters, only feelings and thoughts about others. But, carrying those feelings and thoughts with him is how the loner is never alone, it seems.

Simon Joyner & the Nervous Stars :: Everybody Knows This Is Nowhere (Neil Young)

I wanted to try and do one of Neil’s undisputed classics, also an obvious favorite of mine too. This is one of Neil’s simplest songs, a reverse grass is always greener on the other side type of song. There is only one idea here and you might say that idea is the foundation of most of the great American novels of the 20th century, i.e. getting the fuck out of town. And Neil accomplishes it with fewer than 65 words! Huck Finn has over 110,000.

Simon Joyner & the Nervous Stars :: Journey Through the Past (Neil Young)

Another beautiful and deceptively simple Neil solo at the piano song. On the surface it’s about moving on from one love and one life to another. He’s at peace with what’s been left behind and he’s thinking of it fondly, not bitterly, and hoping the feeling is mutual. I really love how Neil juxtaposes these feelings of loss and change with his feelings about leaving Canada and moving to the United States to build a new home here. It’s all about visiting people and places through your memories so you haven’t really lost them after all. Not easy to pull off without coming off saccharine but it’s all in the tools employed and withheld and the risks Neil is willing to take to be honest which are inaccessible to lesser songwriters.

Simon Joyner & the Nervous Stars :: Last Trip to Tulsa (Neil Young)

I’ve always been a fan of this epic nightmare from Neil’s first album. There’s a style of psychedelic writing he was messing around with in Buffalo Springfield and a little on this first album that he never really returns to, and this is one of my favorite examples of it. Maybe an attempt to do what Dylan was doing in songs like Gates of Eden or something. The song “Broken Arrow” from Buffalo Springfield Again is another great example of this style but I love how unhinged all these particular vignettes are. It’s a real end of the 60’s apocalypse folk blues ride over the cliff and very cinematic, sorta Taxi Driver meets Electra Glide in Blue meets Wanda. There’s a great strain of impatient, end-of-my-rope feminism running through it too, which is refreshing. The narrator is a woman who has been messed with in every aspect of her life and the ending of this song is one of the most satisfying of Neil’s catalog! It’s not instant karma because it’s taken 87 years, but it is a kind of symbolic justice I can get behind as a listener. It’s really hard to believe the same man wrote “Welfare Mothers” and “A Man Needs a Maid” and a few others I don’t want to recall!
