## Body

Seemingly out of nowhere, an all-instrumental album appeared on Bandcamp called Cease & Desist, attributed to a band called Jungle Boogie. It turned out to be a duo consisting of Britt Walford (Slint, The Breeders) and Ken Brown. Brown, who also has been known as “Bundy K. Brown,” is an original member of Gastr del Sol, Tortoise, Pullman, Directions in Music, and has also amassed countless engineering and remixing credits. 

Emerging out of the pressures of non-musical responsibilities, Brown and Walford first came together to serve as the rhythm section for two different guitar players named Tim — Ruth and Barnes — but eventually settled into recording as a duo, making minimalistic music by themselves and for themselves. Their playing on Cease & Desist could be described as being “in the pocket” if the pocket was actually an endless hallway, such as the spooky, haunted groove of “Crumbs As Coals.” The loop-powered “Another Gadget” sounds like flares from a radar, tracking objects moving in deep space. The songs all have extended running times, allowing for the music to evolve or, in some cases, devolve into new ideas. For example, “Wizards Only Fools” materializes out of a quiet pairing of throbbing bass and cymbals but then a new groove emerges in which Brown moves through what could be mistaken for a series of different alien languages. There’s a through line that connects the music of Jungle Boogie with the duo’s previous work in Slint and Tortoise but Cease & Desist also stands apart as it completely erases all context. 

Aquarium Drunkard spoke with Brown about the mysteries of how Jungle Boogie came to be, how the project fit into Brown’s trajectory as a musician, his interest in food, and more. This conversation has been edited and condensed. | s bunn

Aquarium Drunkard: This is a duo record with Britt Walford and it’s clear from the liner notes that you value playing with Britt. What is it about him as a musical partner that brings you back?

Ken Brown: Brit and I have known each other for a long time, but only become closer as friends more recently. I moved to Louisville in 2009 from Chicago, and I had met Brit many years before, and so we knew each other but we actually had not played together until fairly recently. Brit and I have drifted in and out of pursuing music very seriously over our lifetimes. I think, for Britt, it presented a lot of challenges to be involved in music in the way that he wanted to be. He had parenting responsibilities that prevented him from doing that. He was kind of a single dad for 18 years and didn’t really have the bandwidth to do music in the way that he had in the past. When [his daughter] got older, it was something that he was able to have back in his life. 

It was circumstantial that we started playing together. It’s something that’s weird about being in my 50s and trying to be involved in music is [that] I don’t have a problem with playing with younger people, but it’s hard to meet people from a different generation and find ways to connect. It was really easy for Brit and me to say, “Hey, let’s get together.” 

When I listen to the stuff that we put out on Bandcamp, I feel like Brit and I have a really complementary sense of time. He’s often pushing really hard behind the beat. He has this really languid playing style, and his sense of time is very laid back. It’s very in the pocket, and it’s dragging. I like playing with that kind of time. It’s very different than other drummers that I’ve played with. [John] McEntire was super precise as a drummer. Johnny [Herndon] also has a very unique sense of time that can sometimes drag a little, but it’s very different from the way Brit plays. As I’ve listened to those recordings, I feel like there was a way that we both like to lay back, and there was a chemistry there that I hadn’t otherwise had with a lot of the other drummers that I’ve played with. I’ve been super fortunate in my musical life with nothing but excellent drummers — John, Johnny, Dan Bitney, Doug Scharin, and now Britt — great drummers throughout the course of my life. 

AD: Can you explain a little about the process that you all as a duo would make this music? Did it start with a beat from Britt? A bassline from you? How much improvising did you do? 

Ken Brown: Yeah, it was all impromptu. We would get together once a week for a little over a year, and we never had any set plan. Sometimes we’d sit down, and I would start making some noise, or sometimes Brit would start something. It varied every time. I have a lot of pedals that I’m playing with, so I was able to create loops and then play over them. When it became obvious it was just going to be me and Brit, I knew I needed something else. I was using looping pedals and different kinds of electronic devices to build a lot of sound and things to play off of. We would talk a little bit about things in very broad terms. There was a period where I wanted to experiment with playing very quietly, and then that kind of turned into a thing where we found that keeping the overall volume of what we were doing low, it worked sometimes. But then I was like, “Okay, if we’re going to play more loudly, maybe I’m just going to turn my amp up and as loud as possible, but I’m going to continue to touch the bass in a very quiet way. I’m not going to really dig in and play hard.” We would experiment with ideas like that. 

I subscribed to my friend Peter Margasak’s Substack, and he was writing about a drummer from Vienna, Martin Brandlmayr, who plays in a number of different groups, and some of it is improvised; some of it is electro-acoustic music. And he talked about how either Martin or one of the groups he’s associated with, would take us a small musical gesture, like a really small gesture, and then they would exploit every possibility of playing with that gesture. I loved that concept. And I remember there was at least one conversation that Britt and I had where we were playing, and I was like, “Let’s think about that. Let’s have that in our mind.” So, there would be broad ideas that were kind of abstract. And we would say, “Let’s see what it’s like to just play with small gestures.” Or “Let’s see what it’s like to play quietly.” Or, then other times we would show up and then do whatever we were in the mood for. 

AD: In terms of trying all the different possibilities of a motif, a particular track on the album, is that a slice of everything that you had done in a day?

Ken Brown: I would say, for each of the pieces that’s represented, yeah, almost every one of them was one piece of one day. Each of them is a snapshot of a thing that happened in one day. I’d say about a quarter of a third of them have a little bit of editing in them. It might have been a 20 minute thing, and turned it into an 11 minute, more digestible thing, that still captured all of the interesting stuff that we did. And I’m 90% certain it’s in the chronological order with which we played them.

AD: And did you do any overdubs at all?

Ken Brown: No overdubs. It’s just live to a little digital recorder in the room. 

AD: On the liner notes for Cease & Desist you go through the different stages that you and Britt went through, first working with Tim Ruth and then with Tim Barnes. How does the music on the album differ from the other music you were making with the two Tims? 

Ken Brown: With Tim Ruth, it was very much he had ideas, but nothing that was a complete song. Sometimes it was just a riff. We were trying to figure out how we wanted to figure out how we wanted to arrange it. But the way that we worked with Tim Ruth was very interesting in that even though they were relatively firm ideas, they constantly changed. We never played them the same way twice. Tim passed away last fall, and I don’t want to speak for him. On one hand, he had these kernels of ideas and these riffs that he wanted to work on, but I think he didn’t want it to be like, “Here’s how it’s going to be.” He wanted it to be a collective thing. And I hadn’t picked up my bass very much in years and I didn’t feel very confident asserting an opinion about the way things should be structured. And I think Britt kind of felt the same way. We were in this weird limbo. I think Tim might have appreciated a little bit more of us stepping up in that regard, and we didn’t. Maybe that’s some part of how that group fell apart. 

The stuff with Tim Barnes, it was weird because about half of the time the stuff that we played with him was super abstract; very much In that sort of contemporary improv thing, very textural playing. I was doing a lot of stuff with my looping pedals, where it was a wash of sound instead of discrete rhythms. A lot of that material was more impressionistic. And then some of it seemed to bounce between two worlds, where it would either be very improvised, very electro-acoustic. And then other days, Tim would be in this mode where he wanted to play a very strict backbeat-y, almost krautrock-y thing. And we would spend two hours with him playing like a beat, and then we would play around it.

Some of that material is also going to see release in 2025. There’s quite a bit of material coming out under Tim’s name, and a good portion of it features stuff that Britt and I played in that context of the three of us playing together. But almost all of it was embellished later with overdubs from other collaborators. 

AD: You write in the liner notes that playing with Britt helped “push me out of the corner I’d unintentionally painted myself into at that point.” What do you mean by that? 

Ken Brown: I hadn’t been playing bass in a long time, or guitar, or any of the things that I like to do. For so many years, I wasn’t interested in playing music with other humans. I’d kind of moved away from that. After I quit Tortoise, I didn’t do anything full time. The first Directions thing with Doug [Scharin] and James [Warden], I think we played together a total of four or five times. Same thing with Pullman. Pullman was just kind of an idea. We got together and played for a couple days and made a record. Then everybody went back home, which was how both records happened. And even the tour, we had a day or two of rehearsal, and then we did a week of shows, and then it was done. And that was when I was generally very active. Then I had kids of my own, and I had other things that I was pursuing outside of music that were sucking up a lot of my energy. 

I hadn’t touched any instruments in a while, and I would stay involved in music in different ways. I joked with a friend of mine who said, “Oh, you should come over. We hang out and we play, and we jam.” I was like, “I don’t play music with other people. I just fuck with my computer.” Because for years, I had been doing remixes, or I had been engineering other people’s sessions. I had not picked up an instrument in a long time. I didn’t have the bandwidth to go and play with someone. The two nights I had off, I wanted to sit on the couch with my wife and watch a movie. Circumstantially, I had stopped playing with other people, I stopped creating in that way. The awesome thing is doing remixes, making stuff in the computer, opens up a whole world of nonlinear possibilities, but it takes your mind out of that “We’re in a room together making sounds in real time collaboratively.” I hadn’t done that in decades. 

AD: There seems to be a finality about the liner notes. You write about the duo with Britt in the past tense, hinting that maybe performing live had brought about the end of the project. Though, I saw that Jungle Boogie has a gig opening for BASIC soon. What’s the status of Jungle Boogie? 

Ken Brown: I’m using Jungle Boogie for my solo performances. I don’t know that Brit and I aren’t going to play together. We still spend time together. We talk about music. He had other pressures coming to bear on him. It’s hard to find the time to get together once a week, every week, the way we were doing things. If we didn’t do that, it would be very hard for us not to play regularly and do a gig. If you think about it, there are 10 pieces on the Bandcamp album, and we played once a week for over a year. There’s so much more material that was not worth having other people hear [laughs]. So, if we couldn’t find the time to practice regularly, continuing to do shows didn’t make sense. I’m still doing some solo stuff, and some of it is based aesthetically on similar ideas to what is [on Cease & Desist]. 

AD: I want to ask you about a few of your other projects from the past. A few years ago, Directions in Music re-released a 12” called Echoes. What prompted you to go back to that project? 

Ken Brown: It was purely Jeremy [DeVine] from Temporary Residence asked if I wanted to do it. I suspect it might have had something to do with Kieran Hebden [of Four Tet]. He’s a big fan of that record. There was a period of time where he was playing it a lot in his DJ sets. Jeremy approached me and said, “Hey, this has been out of print for a while.” And it’s funny because I joked with him at the time, I was like, “Dude, you can reissue it if you want, but there’s copies on Discogs for like a buck. I don’t see a huge demand for this record in the used world, so I don’t know that it merits a reissue.” But I think Kieran playing it a lot created some demand for it, and yeah, it did pretty well. 

AD: I was listening to Pullman’s first album and the song “Two Parts Water” was playing and my wife said that it’s the intro song for the radio project StoryCorps. Is there a story of how that came about?

Ken Brown: Yeah, they had reached out to us before even StoryCorps was a thing. One of the original guys who started StoryCorps had done a bunch of independent films, and he had licensed a little bit of Pullman music for that. And he was a big fan, and it fit with his work, and he wanted it for the StoryCorps project. We were like, “Yeah, we love what you do. Go ahead.” I love the StoryCorps project. My parents loved hearing it on NPR, because my dad was a big folky and so the Pullman music probably spoke to him more than any of the other music that I made and he also listened to NPR all the time, so that was very satisfying.

AD: I wanted to ask you about the Instagram account you have devoted to food pictures, and I’ve seen references to you as a food writer. What’s the story there? 

Ken Brown: I’m not sure that I would call myself a food writer, but I have a project that I’m working on that I don’t know what form it’s going to take. Like a lot of creative people, I worked in and out of the hospitality industry over the years, and cooking is a thing that I do. I don’t cook professionally anymore. I do what I call mercenary cooking. A friend of mine, who’s a chef, just called me. He’s working at an event, and he needs some extra hands, and I’m going to go cook for two days. I don’t need that to be a regular part of my life. I’m tired of my hands getting cut and burned and my back getting ruined and obsessing over the way certain things look on the plate and all that kind of crap. The writing project is something that I started after the pandemic. I found out that a friend of mine had written a book, and she didn’t publish it. She didn’t attempt to have it published. It was about the process. It was like a lot of my music has kind of lived in that realm. it’s like, “I’m going make this thing, and I don’t need to sell a million records.” It’s something I do to kind of keep myself busy when I’m not working on music.

AD: Lastly, this is a little pedantic, but how do you want your name listed? Ken Brown, Bundy K. Brown — who are you these days?

Ken Brown: I’m very comfortable with any of the above. It’s convenient for me because, depending on what people call me, I have an idea of when I met them in my life. There was a period where I was kind of disavowing the Bundy name, but I don’t care about that anymore. That’s the other challenging thing about the little niche that I’ve carved for myself is that I did a show and solo show here in January and the guy who put it together was like, “Well, how do you want to be credited?” And I was like, “I don’t care. If you think people will recognize Bundy Brown, then please put that out there. Otherwise, I don’t really care.”

I don’t think anybody knows who Bundy Brown is. I don’t have a tremendous presence out there. I went to a music festival in Chicago, and I walked into the venue, and Bettina [Richards] from Thrill Jockey and my friend Peter Margasak were standing there and one of the musicians from the festival was standing there — Pat Thomas, an extraordinary piano player. A round of introductions was made and blah, blah, blah, just chatting. Then it’s like, “Ken here was an original member of both Tortoise and Gastr del Sol.” This guy had no clue. As soon as that came up, the whole conversation changed. I’m sleeping in the bed I made. I quit Tortoise before anybody knew about the band. We had made a record, but we weren’t big. And similar with Gastr del Sol. I quit Gastr before they were signed to Drag City. I think the Gastr del Sol box set goes to show that Gastr was a tremendously important band, historically but, contextually, there’s a lot of young people who make music that’s deeply indebted to them, but it’s not part of their musical history. The box set helped to recontextualize that. But I’m only on two songs on that whole box set, so my moment with that band was even outside of that. I don’t know that using Bundy or Ken or either one of them makes a difference at this point.
