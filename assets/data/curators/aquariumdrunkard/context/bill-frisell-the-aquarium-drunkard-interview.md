## Body

In 2022, Phillip Watson authored Beautiful Dreamer: The Guitarist Who Changed The Sound of American Music, a biography devoted to Bill Frisell, whose distinctive playing—a blend of blues, country, western swing, ambient, and other Americana mutations—have placed him at the fore of modern jazz. 

In the book, Watson details a dream Frisell once had, three or so decades back. In it, the guitarist enters an ornate American Gothic mansion. Once inside, he encounters three “nice little people” dressed in dark brown hooded robes in a library. The “miniature monks” tell Frisell they are going to show him “stuff, the real stuff, how things really are.” After revealing to him the true essence of color, the monks decide to reveal the true essence of music next, beaming the pure sound like an arrow into Frisell’s brain, as if pumped through some tube, directly in. Suddenly, Frisell wakes up.

By his own admission, Frisell says he’ll never get close to the sound he heard in that dream. But his latest recording, a live/studio mashup called In My Dreams, nonetheless points to what that unadultured sonic flow might have felt like. Joined by longtime collaborators, including his familiar rhythm section, bassist Thomas Morgan and drummer Rudy Royston, and a string ensemble—violinist Jenny Scheinman, violist Eyvind Kang, and cellist Hank Roberts—Frisell sounds as calm and dialed in as ever, manipulating live on-stage recordings with light overdubs in the studio.

Frisell joined us to discuss the dream realm, the influence of Duke Ellington collaborator Billy Strayhorn, and Gary Larson’s The Far Side, among other fascinating topics. Settle in for a waking dream with Bill Frisell. | j woodbury

Aquarium Drunkard: Congrats on this album, and ahead of your 75th birthday next month, happy early birthday.

Bill Frisell: Well, thanks. Yeah, that’s wild. How did that happen?

AD: Getting ready for this interview, I was reviewing your discography. You’ve kept up a high level of productivity for 40 of those years. It’s really something to behold. 

Bill Frisell: Music is incredible. It keeps getting better and better. People say, “Are you gonna retire?” I can’t even imagine. Why would I want to stop? The music is the best place to be. 

AD: I saw Pharoah Sanders play well into his 70s; he played right up until then end. Then there’s Marshall Allen of the Sun Ra Arkestra, still playing at 101. 

Bill Frisell: It’s incredible. I saw this thing of [Marshall] jumping off the stage. Music is a miracle. 

AD: Does it help keep you young in some way?

Bill Frisell: Oh, for sure. I really believe that. The travel and all that, it’s hard. It’s absurd when you travel for 12 or 24 hours to get somewhere and you play for an hour. That stuff is really hard. You just get wiped out. I’ll be backstage thinking, “How am I going to do this? I can’t even stand up.” Then as soon as the music starts—it’s not even just the adrenaline. Music is like food; it gives you the most incredible energy. I just feel so lucky to be able to do that all the time. The music really heals you and gives you strength. I just can’t imagine what the world would be without it.

AD: The notes for this album detail a crazy dream you had. It feels like a scene from a fantasy novel. But I wonder: do you ever have mundane dreams about music? Like you’re changing strings, sorting pedals, that sort of thing? 

Bill Frisell: I dream about having to get to a concert. I’ll be in a hotel and I realize, “Oh no, I forgot my guitar.” Those are the ones that I remember these days. There’s this moment right between dreaming and waking up where you’re in a place. And in that moment you remember it, but then it evaporates when you wake up. 

It really reminds me of when when we’re in the midst of playing and the stuff is really happening. It’s like the moment that you realize what’s happening, you know, it’s not happening anymore. There are a lot of similarities going on there. When you’re in the music, it’s like you’re in a dream. 

AD:  I mentioned going through your discography, and I don’t know if there are any other examples of what you’re doing with In My Dreams, where you’re taking live performances and adding studio touches over the top of them. 

Bill Frisell: I’ve never really done a record this way. It’s mainly live, but we the technology now is amazing. We had multi-tracks of those live sets, so we could really mix it. 

AD: It feels cohesive, like a full album statement. 

Bill Frisell: That’s what I hope. There were no rules. I wasn’t trying to be “true.” I don’t even remember the order of songs we played originally. It was like, “Okay, now we’ve got all these materials. Let’s just make something out of it.” I overdubbed very little; I think I stuck an acoustic guitar on one thing. I wasn’t being precious about, “Oh, it’s got to be pure live this or that.” We just wanted to make something interesting. 

AD: You tend to record live in the studio too. Do you ever have that thing happen where you start playing, close your eyes, and the performance is done?

Bill Frisell: Oh yeah. Time will expand and contract. Sometimes it can go in both ways, in a positive and negative way. Sometimes, two seconds of the music can seem like a lifetime. Like, “Please let me out of here.” [Laughs] But more often than not, it’s like, “Wow, what? This was just amazing. How did this happen?”  

AD: You cover Duke Ellington and Billy Strayhorn’s “Isfahan” on this album. Strayhorn is a huge influence on you. What makes you so interested in him? 

Bill Frisell: One thing about getting older, one of the good things about it, is that more and more is revealed. There’s never been a time when I didn’t appreciate Duke Ellington. But as the years go by, the more I listen, more and more is peeled away. More and more is revealed. And Billy Strayhorn, I just started putting his songs together as I listened. I spent decades working on “Lush Life,” a song he wrote as a teenager. How is that even possible? It’s taken me years to have the confidence to play that song in concert.

AD: There’s an incredible part of Duke Ellington’s Second Sacred Concert, which was recorded during the “jazz mass” fad, where jazz artists were playing churches, like Vince Guaraldi’s At Grace Cathedral. Ellington does a spoken word part about Strayhorn. He discusses Strayhorn’s “four major moral freedoms.” “Freedom from hate, unconditionally, freedom from self pity. Freedom from the fear of doing something that might benefit somebody else more than it benefits him, and freedom from the kind of pride that makes a man think that he is any better than his brother.” It’s remarkable. When you hear something like that, you end up being able to somehow hear it in the music too. 

Bill Frisell: There’s a recording of Ellington playing “Lotus Blossom” alone on the piano in a recording session for …And His Mother Called Him Bill. It’s all Strayhorn music. The whole band is in the studio. And they left the tape rolling, and you can hear the band shuffling around, packing up their instruments. And then Duke Ellington goes to the piano and he starts playing “Lotus Blossom” by himself. It’s so soon after Strayhorn’s passing. It’s heavy. It’s like “woah.” 

AD: Do you find yourself still thinking of your collaborators who have passed on sometimes when playing too?  

Bill Frisell: Every day, yeah. There are a lot. Paul Motian, all the time. There’s a song on the album. “Curtis.” That song that’s was for [trombonist] Curtis Fowlkes. He’s someone like that for me. There’s Hal Wilner, who passed away at the beginning of the pandemic. I think about him a lot. I leaned on him a lot. And I still do, but it’s not quite the same as when I could call him up. It’s pretty different now. I feel like an orphan or something.

AD: You have a tendency to play with people for a long time, building long relationships. One of my favorite records of yours is Quartet, which is celebrating its 30th anniversary. 

Bill Frisell: That was with Ron Miles and Curtis Fowlkes. 

AD: Exactly. It’s so cool to trace people’s journeys together through a discography as vast as yours. Quartet brings to mind Gary Larson, since some of those songs were features in his animated Tales from the Far Side special. I found this incredible drawing he did of you.

Bill Frisell: The one with the stuff coming out of my head?

AD: It’s got a tube going into your head like that dream of yours, only it’s going from your guitar to you head. Or the other way around. [Laughs] It’s like your dream a little. One of the best Far Side comics ever is “Cow Tools,” which has come back into the news as scientists are discovering that cows can and do use tools. It’s almost like, did Gary Larson put that into the world? Did he insert that idea into the world? Life imitating art is such a funny, beautiful thing. 

Bill Frisell: Oh wow, yeah, I probably told him about that dream. 

AD: It’s beautiful how your discography to showcases friendships and long term collaborations, with people like Larson, Thomas, Motion, Rudy, etc. I can imagine seeing time and relationships represented in the work like that is a headtrip. 

Bill Frisell: There’s just something about putting all that time together. Then, everyone goes in different directions and comes back, bringing new things into it.
