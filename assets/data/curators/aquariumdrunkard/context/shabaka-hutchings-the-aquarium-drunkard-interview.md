## Body

Though he’s known for his fiery, raging performances with groups like Sons of Kemet, The Comet Is Coming, and Shabaka and The Ancestors, Shabaka Hutchings eases into a contemplative zone with his debut solo album, Perceive Its Beauty, Acknowledge Its Grace. Released on Impulse! Records and recorded at the legendary Van Gelder Studio in New Jersey—where John Coltrane cut A Love Supreme and many other jazz classics were committed to tape—the album finds Hutchings setting down his sax in favor of a variety of flutes. Joined by guests including Saul Williams, Euclid, Esperanza Spalding, Floating Points, Laraaji, poet Anum Iyapo, and fellow flute devotee André 3000, Hutchings drifts into a gentle, new age-inspired zone, blending spiritual jazz expression with ambient sensibilities.

“What does it mean to have music of spiritual substance?” Hutchings says. “What does it mean to be spiritual? What is spirit? A lot of the times these words are used and no one ever stops to define them apart from a weird reductive dictionary definition. For me, spirit is simple: it’s an energizing force.” He joins us to discuss that force, his shift toward the flute, the influence of Outkast, and connecting with his father on a creative level. This interview has been edited for clarity and cohesion. | j woodbury

Aquarium Drunkard: When we last spoke, you mentioned a quote you grew up hearing: “The aim is to know more and more about less and less until you know everything about nothing.” I feel like this new record ties into that somehow; there’s an effortlessness and openness to it, it feels like you’re arriving at a new place, like a debut record in some ways. 

Shabaka Hutchings: I’ve spent a lot of time on it. It’s been about two years in the making. It’s really made me think about what it is to live with a body of work and what processes go on in terms of actually refining what you think of it. So what happens when you spend a lot of time on the work isn’t that you add more and more layers to it. You actually become more selective about what you think isn’t appropriate or isn’t necessary. I just found myself going back and just taking out or cutting back a lot more and then arriving at a point where the only things that’s left are the things that I think are the most essential.

AD: Perceive Its Beauty, Acknowledge Its Grace is very contemplative. People know you as a saxophonist, and your records are often energetic and even furious sounding. Was it the flute itself that lent itself to the more subdued palette that you’re working with on this record?

Shabaka Hutchings: When you’re really getting into the flute and [playing] in nature, [playing] outside, there’s a different dynamic and a different approach to how you place your sound within other sounds. I guess it’s all up to people’s individual temperaments, but I want to hear is a blend. I want to explore how sounds fit together. When we recorded it, we recorded it in the same room, without any separation or headphones. The musicians had to listen in a really deep, sensitive way. 

I’ve done a lot of thinking about this transformation into the quieter dynamic, and it feels like it’s always been there, on the peripheries of all the albums. There have been these moments of quietness, but they’ve always taken a backseat to the more bombastic elements. But with something of my own, I just felt like this is what I’d like to portray as being the representative of where I’m at at the moment. [I’m] trying to explore that different emotional palette.

AD: I’m curious about the shift away from the saxophone. I don’t want to be too reductive, but did you feel burned out on the saxophone?  

Shabaka Hutchings: No, not at all. I actually feel really invigorated by the saxophone. I feel like my saxophone playing has been getting better and better. Some of my most satisfying experiences playing saxophone came in those last shows. It’s not that I’m tired of the sax and it’s not that my sax playing is waning. It’s just that, artistically, there’s something else that I need to dedicate my focus to and it’s got to be a unidirectional focus, at least for a particular period of time. I really felt it from the point where I made the decision personally and then publicly that I was going to stop playing the sax. I really love playing the saxophone, but it’s not always about doing what you love. It’s doing what needs to be done for the purpose of artistic growth. It’s tough. It took a lot of soul searching and questioning. The result is me just making a proclamation and saying, “Actually the sax is over, because it doesn’t represent where I want to go on a deep personal level.” You can articulate these things in words, but actually it’s a feeling—it’s an intuitive understanding. 

AD: “I’ll Do Whatever You Want” is a really beautiful song. I love what Laraaji does on that, it’s almost a Leon Thomas-type thing. When did you first run into him and were you familiar with his music coming up?

Shabaka Hutchings: Not so much. I only became familiar. Carlos Niño actually invited him. He said, “Laraaji is in town, would you mind if I invite him to the studio?” And I was like, “Yeah, sure.” After that, I actually checked him out and realized, “Oh my God, a legend has been invited.” 

AD: That song signals a sort of turn in the album. The first half of the record is more tentative, but the second half gets more and more inviting as it goes on. 

Shabaka Hutchings: This is something that I really worked on. I’m a really big fan of arcs, the arc of the record. How does it move forward and how does it resolve itself? There’s a dark tension in the beginning. It gets so tense that by the time the first beat comes in “Body To Inhabit,” it just feels like it’s this weight that comes off of your shoulders. And the beat’s only a hand clap, a single hand clap that goes through the whole tune. But because there’s this dark tension that goes all the way to that point, it feels a lot more significant. From that point, you kind of float off. 

AD: Obviously anybody who’s making a flute record these days is going to get asked about André 3000, who appears on the record. It’s funny, you guys on these parallel flute journeys. When did you you become aware that he was playing woodwinds too? 

Shabaka Hutchings: It’s quite incredible. I think the first thing that I saw was when he released that duet with James Blake, “Look Ma No Hands,” a 20-minute improv with him on bass clarinet. I thought it was cool free improv stuff, but to then hear it’s André 3000, someone that’s known for something else completely, it just made me think, “That’s a real courageous spirit.” I just think it took a lot of courage for him to be able to say, “This is where I’m at. I am a creative artist, and I’m finding out what that means by just doing it.” 

AD: Growing up in Barbados, you’d often practice your clarinet along to hip hop records. Was Outkast was in the mix back then? 

Shabaka Hutchings: I’m a massive Outkast fan. I really am the biggest André 3000 fan, which is why this whole situation is completely surreal to me. I literally listening to Southernplayalisticadillacmuzik when I was in Barbados growing up and Speakerboxxx/The Love Below. I think there’s something special about how he uses cadence. Even on that last Killer Mike album, the song “Scientists and Engineers,” there’s a verse where André does something in the middle of his rap where he just flips the cadence around in a way that’s just mystical. I think is the best way of putting it. You could probably get an explanation if you try to analyze it, but the joy is actually in just how it makes you feel and the impact that it gives to you.

AD: The joy is in the mystery to some degree. That’s really nicely put. You have so many incredible collaborators on this one. Saul Williams, Esperanza Spalding, Moses Sumney, Lianne La Havas. Everybody fits in a way that it doesn’t feel like, “Okay, and now here comes this feature, it’s going to bring this whole different thing.” It doesn’t feel that way at all. 

Shabaka Hutchings: I just feel like I’m incredibly lucky. This felt like stars were aligning in so many ways.

Ankh Anum · Song of the motherland

AD: The final song on the album, “Song of the Motherland” is a new recording of a piece your father Anum Iyapo put out on his 1985 album Song of the Motherland. I hadn’t heard his album, and so I was getting ready for this interview and realized, “Oh, wow. He did album art for people like King Tubby and the Heptones and all sorts of people, these legendary reggae records.” How did that idea for that recording come together? 

Shabaka Hutchings: The idea came together [from me realizing it’s] a real travesty that no one knows how badass he is. Ithink it’s just due to there not being a solid codified recorded archive of what he’s done. It’s all very stretched out. He wanted to come to the studio actually to just do something else to record. He does a lot of ceremonial work, so he wanted to just record some ceremonial drumming. And I knew he was coming, so I brought one of his poetry books. I had the basis of the track and when he came I was like, “I’d really love for you to read this poem.” And he was like, “OK, I’ve not really performed poetry live in about 15 years, but I’ll do something.” And he just did one take. The poem finished on the last beat of music completely organically. We’re reissuing his album Song For the Motherland, on my label, Native Rebel Recordings.
