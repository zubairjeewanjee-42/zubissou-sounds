## Body

27 years on from her recording debut, Kathleen Edwards has carved out a wandering and unique path. While her first full-length LP Failer remains dear to the heart of longtime listeners, it was 2011’s Voyageur that gave her her biggest chart success both at home in Canada and in the United States while also being shortlisted for the 2012 Polaris Music Prize. But in 2014, Edwards unexpectedly stepped away from music, opening her own coffee shop and only intermittently releasing songs and rare live performances until she re-emerged in the heart of the pandemic with 2020’s Total Freedom. Last year’s Jason Isbell-produced Billionaire and a previous EP of cover songs made it one of her busiest years for new music in a long time.

Aquarium Drunkard sat down with Edwards to talk about her early career and the connections that it has made and the changes that have happened, especially since her return to music in 2020. We talked about how she feels about her debut EP, her tendency to work with musician producers, how she ended up recording a covers album because of the algorithm, and why believing yourself while performing a cover is crucial to making it your own. | j neas

Aquarium Drunkard: I want to actually start with a pre-Failer question, which has to do with Building 55, your EP from back in 1999. I remember the first time I ever heard it was when Aquarium Drunkard posted it, because it doesn’t exist except out there in those 500 or so physical copies. I know that’s something that’s never been re-released. How do you feel about that super early stuff? Is the fact that you’ve never put it back out there related to your feelings about it or was it just something you didn’t feel like revisiting?

Kathleen Edwards: I think the reason it’s just so far outside of my interest or at all is just because it’s really the first songs I ever wrote. You know, when I first started playing, I was doing the classic sort of, learning how to be on stage for three sets and being paid $40 for the night at a local college pub. And so when the transition came from learning everyone else’s songs and playing entire sets of covers to inserting a few of my originals, those were literally just the first songs I ever wrote. And I think there are even one or two songs on that EP, if I remember correctly, that the words were actually from another local band guy who was a writer and he had written me something. And so I put music to it. And I think, I just don’t think of them as songs. I just don’t have a relationship with them or think of them as part of my catalog. I just think they were my first attempts, like the first steps I ever took.

I was saying this to somebody the other day that when I made Failer, it was like two years, three years between the time that I wrote those songs and when I recorded them as sort of a demo in the same studio where I made Building 55. And then all the sort of stuff happened and then eventually Rounder [Records] put it out and it had been remixed three times and the whole sort of packaging and massaging of making it into its best possible sounding demo, essentially.

But I remember it was probably five to ten years that went by that I didn’t ever listen to [Failer] because I was playing it every night. Why would I want to put it on? And one day, you know, I remember being like, ‘oh, I haven’t heard that a long time’ and I would play it and I was like, ‘who the fuck is that?’ I don’t remember her. I know that’s me, but I just don’t have a relationship with that version of me anymore. And it’s not a criticism or looking down my nose, it’s just, it’s so funny how it’s this, it’s sort of this document on record. Literally.

But Building 55, I just don’t have any. If I ever listened to like half a song, it kind of was cringey. and in the way that, maybe it’s like the first time you put on makeup and you’re just kind of a little embarrassed that, you know, you filmed the process. AD:  I was going to ask if Building 55 was a way to get those songs out of the way and that freed you up to write what would eventually become Failer, but you said those songs were written a couple of years before as well.

Kathleen Edwards: Yeah, the songs on Building 55 were definitely reminiscent of my early-90s folk influence. Ani DiFranco was my hero. For five years she was the one and only. And Indigo Girls and the Cranberries, those were my influences. And then, what happened after Building 55 was I discovered Wilco and Whiskeytown and a little bit of Sheryl Crow, Aimee Mann, for sure. A little bit of Lucinda [Williams], and Son Volt, but the sort of emerging alt-country thing was everything. I just suddenly  loved it so much that that felt way more like me than the percussive Ani DiFranco sort of disciple that I had been.

So when I started listening to that sort of movement of songs, that was when I started writing songs for Failer, and I think that that’s why I shifted into a very different, what I consider a completely, almost like a totally different personality of writer.

AD: When you say you don’t recognize that version of yourself on Failer, or that you kind of feel separated from that person, is it lyrically? Is it performatively? Is it just kind of everything wrapped together?

Kathleen Edwards: You know, it could be like, is it well written? Is there a wording here that’s too conventional? Is it too, sort of, what’s the word, basic? You know, how is this better than average songwriting? And so I think in the ways that my songwriting and my brain kind of worked when I was making new songs after Failer was, of course, I wanted to continue to be a better songwriter and whatever way you get to that.

But with Billionaire, I think because I had listened back to Failer, and thought, God, I don’t know who that is anymore, but what I really wish is that I was as uninhibited and unworried about whatever I was writing and the outcome of that. You know, I think I was a little too inside my head for the remainder of many of my records. For good or for bad, it’s not a criticism, but I loved that the kid who wrote Failer was just like, ‘I don’t fucking care if this is the word I’m putting in this song.’ I don’t care if I’m writing a song that says, “I’m a little bleeder with white pants on” [a line from Failer‘s “Westby”]. My current self hears that, and I’m like, ‘ew, like, ew, who wrote that? Not me.’ But I did, and I like the reminder that actually, oh yeah, I really put myself out there, and now I feel a bit self-conscious about who that person is. And maybe that’s the way I regressed, and now, I’m much less concerned about that. I’m not as self-conscious as I used to be, and I’ve kind of gone back to the Failier kid. I think [that version of me] was just like, I want to show everybody I can do this. And so I was completely uninhibited and just went for it. And I didn’t care what the consequences would be because there was nothing to lose. I had nothing to lose because it was essentially my first record.

AD: One of the things I’ve noticed is kind of a throughline is that you’ve had other musicians who co-produced a lot of your albums. Not all of them, but a fair bit of them. And you did Failer with Dan Draves. And so one thing I noticed is that Draves also plays on the album. Jason Isbell, of course, plays on the new record Billionaire that he co-produced for you. And then I started looking at the other albums in between, and I don’t think there’s a single one where the producer wasn’t a musician who also contributed to the playing. I could be wrong.

Kathleen Edwards: I think Jim Scott might be the only one who wasn’t actually a physical musician who produced.

AD: Yeah, thank you for clarifying. But I feel like a lot of times I see a lot of records, and there are lots of producers who don’t play. They’re not really musicians who play. Is that something that you commonly sought out in people you decided to work with on these albums? People who could kind of lend a hand from the point of view of a musician?

Kathleen Edwards: That’s a great question. I don’t think anyone has ever asked me that. It’s funny, every time I’ve made a record, the person who produces the record with me or as the singular producer, it just was an easy decision. I didn’t have a lot of hesitation on the choice. It just was like, this feels great. Every person that produced the record with me, I had some sort of relationship with already.

So Dave Draves on Failer, I’d already made Building 55 with him. And he was the only person I knew who had a studio and I liked going to his studio, and he believed in me and was incredible, and he still is running a studio and has young kids go in there all the time, which is really like a gift; it’s really special what he’s been able to help people do. And then Colin [Cripps, producer of Back to Me] was my partner who had produced other records and he was like, I can do a knockout job for you, and it was just an obvious thing to me.

Jim Scott [producer of Asking for Flowers] had mixed Back to Me, and I enjoyed so much working with him and knew he had worked on so many great projects that, when I inquired about him being involved, he immediately just took the producer chair in a way that was, like, my job is to make it easy for you to just play your songs and be surrounded by incredible talent that elevates you. I was so lucky to be with somebody of that incredible experience. And he was so effortless in that role because he’s such a talented recording engineer. Jim Scott was on my dream list. His name was on [Tom Petty’s] Wildflowers and his name was on [Lucinda Williams’] Car Wheels on a Gravel Road, and he’d mixed all these records that were incredibly formative to me. He was on a level that I never dreamed would be in the same room with me. He was a uniquely special relationship.

And then Voyageur was a little bit of a weird one because I originally had started making the record with somebody else who was an old friend from Ottawa who plays music still, and he just did not end up having the bandwidth or the time. And so then Justin Vernon was my boyfriend, a new boyfriend, we had only been together a couple months, and he just sort of was like, I can help if you want me to help. And then he made it very easy, easy to make Voyageur. Voyageur was a very hard record to make because it took a long time and it was a real departure for me, and I was making it in several different places, and Justin was so committed to helping me make the record, but also he was really busy. And really, it’s an incredibly dense record in terms of how many tracks are on every song and it just took a long time, but Justin played on so much of it, and I was so lucky that he came into the picture when he did. I was able to make the record I really dreamed of making, even when stuff that he was throwing up there wasn’t how I’d hoped to hear it. And then you’re like, oh my God, that’s so good. It’s so different.

And then Total Freedom started out actually not unlike Voyageur, where I started making the record with Ian Fitchuk, who had just produced and won all these Grammys with Kasey Musgraves, and it was because we had met doing a co-write with Maren Morris. And I just liked Ian, as he was such an effortless musician. But I knew that he was also wearing a producer hat and he kind of was like, I’m a huge fan of yours. I realized that as much as they were so generous in their, you know, treating me like they were incredible fans of mine, I just also got so lucky that people with the sort of a fresh take on making music wanted to help me get where I was going. I think also Ian went from sort of a Nashville session guy who had been working on a bunch of creative projects that were all really, really good to being a guy who produced the biggest record of the year. And I think a lot of people were knocking on his door. And I’m sure it was quite overwhelming. And so I think it was a very amicable sort of thank you so much for starting this, I think we’re going to go here, and I think he was incredibly gracious about it, and he had lots on his plate, so it wasn’t ever a negative thing.

But also full disclosure, when I started making that record, I had been in a relationship that was, really, really bad. So we started making the record and about a month and a half into the recording process, this sort of bomb went off in my life, and it just took a while to kind of say, okay, I’ve got to move forward on making this record. I mean, I genuinely was excited about making a record. And I just, I was not up for going back to Nashville and continuing. But my longtime bandmate who had a home studio who I’d worked with for years, Jim Bryson, I could just go down the street and go and sit there and record some songs in front of a microphone and resume some of the things that I had started. And, I had been with somebody who ended up being, he was abusive, not physically, but he was an abusive person, and when you extract yourself from somebody, when you realize that you actually have to, you actually have to find a way to get yourself out of a relationship before they know that you’re planning on extracting yourself for your own safety and your own well-being. It thwarted the “Total Freedom” album recording and then that’s why I kind of stayed going just to my friend’s place and working on it from there. And from there, Jim really helped make the record. You know, he really helped me finish it, and put his own production touches on it too, so we stayed kind of local for that reason.

And then Jason [Isbell] was the only person I’ve ever made a record with who I didn’t know. I didn’t know him at all, and we’re not friends. It’s not me saying anything negative, but I didn’t know him except from opening for him a couple times. And we didn’t hang out on tour or anything. We had a few conversations, and I was incredibly grateful to him for having me open some shows. And then when he and I were just very casually texting on a social media platform, like Instagram or something – it was because I had covered [the Jason Isbell song] “Traveling Alone” as part of a little covers project – and he just said, so what are you up to? And I was like, well, I actually want to make a record, but I have no idea who could produce this record. And then he said, ‘maybe I should do it.’ And I was like, oh my God, what? I was hoping he’d be like, ‘oh, I know this great guy who would be perfect for you.’ And instead he said [himself]. And I was like, my God thank you. So we walked into the studio, day one, and, I mean, we hadn’t gone for lunch, we hadn’t talked on the phone, there was no pre-production except the work that I had done and sent to him. So Billionaire was very unique in in that respect. 

AD:  Going back to Failer, back in the day, albums used to have diverse international release dates. So Failer came out earlier in Canada. The Canadian release was in late 2002, I think, and then the American release was January of 2003. Was there a difference in the reaction between the two? Did it start catching fire earlier in Canada or did the American release make a difference there?

Kathleen Edwards: Honestly, it’s so wild. My being noticed in the States results in my being noticed in Canada. And Failer was the first thing that happened. I put out the record. I think it came out in like September ‘02. Maybe it was October, but I think it was September. And like, the phone was not ringing, you know. I did a couple shows. My album release was in a little town called Guelph, Ontario, at a college bar and there were 10 people there. And, you know, I wasn’t expecting some great hurrah. I was still pretty much an unknown artist. And, you know, in Canada, there is one radio station, and it’s the CBC, and I’m sure notionally they played me a little bit, but otherwise, nothing was happening. And it was Rounder Records who, they generously said, just put out the record in the fall in Canada, knowing that frankly, that wasn’t going to have any effect on their marketing campaign.

And so they were setting up Failer for a January release. And the person who ran their marketing department, I’m still friends with, a guy named Jeff Walker. And Jeff got me a booking on David Letterman. I think the label had just also had that incredible few years where O Brother, Where Art Thou? came out, and Alison Krauss was really one of the big Americana acts of the moment. And Norah Jones, too. So the booker on Letterman played the record and said yes, and not only that, she then gave it to Dave. And this is what I’ve been told, so if I’m wrong, someone please correct me, but that Dave loved the record. So when I got booked and when I played the day my record came out in the US, Dave then gave me an introduction on the show that said, ‘I love this record. I’ve been listening to it all week, it’s excellent.’ And people were just like, well, fucking ring that bell. And then Canada paid attention. And then I got asked to do national press and then people were like, oh, we should pay attention. So it really did happen that way; nothing was happening in Canada at the time for me.

AD: I made a friend in 10th grade; she moved to my hometown here in North Carolina from outside of Toronto, somewhere around there. And I had learned a lot of Canadian music from watching 120 Minutes on MTV. I was a big fan of Hayden and different people like that and my friend was also. She’s the one who turned me on to the Tragically Hip and a lot of other Canadian bands. But I was thinking the Hip are a good example of a band that, they were huge there in Canada, but then didn’t ever quite make that impact through the U.S. But for so many other artists, I think, it’s the exact opposite. It’s kind of the experience that you had, I imagine, of having that success in America that reverberates back home.

Kathleen Edwards: Yeah, yeah. Which is a great example of something that is now more and more absent in Canada. It’s really interesting. I think it’s true around the world. Ireland, especially. Ireland’s a perfect example, and yes, it is a very small country relative to the United States. But Ireland has tons of artists who do very well and get tons of radio play and build these national audiences and then don’t actually leave Ireland. They don’t need to. Maybe they want to, but they actually have an incredible fan base that keeps them going in Ireland. And Canada does not have that. We somehow, because we’re so dependent on the US for sort of the media and the sort of like the pop culture sourcing, we’ve taken our foot off the gas.

It’s really heartbreaking to see, to be honest. If you think about Canada in the early 90s, we had the Barenaked Ladies – they had not come to the U.S. yet – but they were nationally known by then in Canada. A band called The Spirit of the West, Loreena McKennitt, the Tragically Hip, Blue Rodeo, Crash Vegas, Junkhouse. There were numerous acts that were known that had national followings who were considered sort of rock and roll stars. And none of them were stepping foot in the U.S. to do that. Sarah McLachlan put out three records before anything happened for her in the United States. And all that stuff is really heartening, and it’s absent now, and I think, we need to find a way to bring it back, if only for the success of our culture. Not because we can’t give U.S. credit for breaking our artists, but because it’s just good cultural, foundational, legacy work.

AD: You got a lot of comparisons to Lucinda Williams early on, and I felt like it became like a little bit of a bone of contention. I didn’t think of you as similar songwriters. I think it was just that there was this similarity in the kind of voice earlier on, to some extent, and I feel like that is where people made that comparison. Was that something that you remember happening a lot, and did that bother you or did it feel flattering?

Kathleen Edwards: The thing I was worried about when I saw that and saw it so much, I thought, well, surely Lucinda Williams is tired of fucking hearing that. Because, you know, like when you get called like ‘the next Lucinda Williams,’ you’re like, how do you think Lucinda Williams enjoys hearing that? The next Lucinda Williams? Like, Lucinda Williams is fucking great, and she will always be great because she’s incredibly good.

And, you know, it’s funny, I met Lucinda for the first time just a few years ago. We got to meet, and I remember feeling like, I mean, I almost, I was like, don’t cry, don’t cry, don’t cry. And she was so lovely and gracious, but you know what she said to me was she kind of apologized to me for having to hear [that comparison] so often when I first started. She’s said ‘because you were so good just on you, and you kind of got compared to me, and I’m sorry that that happened all the time.’ And all I could think of was like, I’m sorry that you probably had somebody calling [me] the next Lucinda Williams, like, that would be like a stab in the back to somebody who’s like, what? I’m not going anywhere. I think she thought maybe I would probably feel a little resentful that I had to live in her shadow, and I was worried that she’d be resentful that I kept being called, you know, her successor. So it’s really an interesting thing. I mean, I was a huge fan. The thing that I think the only thing back then was like, well, but she’s not that big of an influence. I would say now, that’s less true, because I didn’t really know Lucinda very well. Car Wheels on a Gravel Road had come out, and I really, I loved it, but I didn’t love it the way that it kind of took off and made her sort of a much more mainstream audience. My favorite record of hers was Essence. That was the record where I was like I get it now. It took me a while. But I was like, yeah, Lucinda Williams is great, but I actually really love Aimee Mann more. I could name four other women that were bigger influences than Lucinda, but also the comparison was a huge compliment, like a huge compliment. I felt that way then too.

AD: The word “Failer” is in the song “6 O’clock News” obviously, but is that the sole reason it’s the album title? Was there another connection to it outside of it just being a part of the lyric of one of the tracks?

Kathleen Edwards: I think it was just that thing, it happens to me every time I make a record where I have to figure out what I’m going to call it. And I think there was just one day where I was thinking of what could I call this, what could I call this? And when I wrote “6 O’clock News,” I remember, I never really put much thought into the word “failer,” I just was kind of emulating a southern accent when I wrote, like, “she says her baby’s a failure,” and I just liked the joke of it in my mind was like, you know, you use the word “Failer”, you can’t even spell failure. So I just thought it was funny to me. I thought it was sort of a tongue-in-cheek, and that’s why. It was self-deprecating, and at that point, that was my biggest asset was that I could self-deprecate all day long and I just went with it.

AD: I know that song has kind of lived on as one of that album’s bigger songs, I think, because it’s such a great, little vignette. I mean, it’s a story. It’s a short story of a song, which, I love those kind of things. But I feel like too, I feel like “Mercury” has oddly become a big song from it. Not odd because it’s not a good song, but just, it feels more like a deep cut, not something I would’ve picked out in that way.

Kathleen Edwards: Oh, it is odd. That’s how I met Justin Vernon, was somebody sent me a message saying, ‘oh my god, Bon Iver covered “Mercury”.’ And I was like, who’s that?

AD: Have you had people talk to you about feeling a particular connection or just their reasons why they felt drawn to that song?

Kathleen Edwards: I don’t know why, in the same way that, maybe just certain songs just kind of capture a moment. I’m not sure, you know? That song was inspired by Richard Buckner. I think I was trying to be Richard Buckner when I wrote that song. Whenever I think of “Mercury,” I think that’s the song I wrote trying to be Richard Buckner.

AD: Right after Failer, or I guess it’s the next year, technically, I think, you put out the Live at the Bowery Ballroom EP that I think has “Hockey Skates” on it. It’s all live.

Kathleen Edwards: “National Steel” for sure is on it.

AD: ”National Steel,” yes, and then you did a cover of AC/DC’s “Money Talks,” which I really enjoyed your version of that. When you’re beginning as a musician, of course, covers are a big part of your repertoire because I imagine that’s a big part of building your stage presence, like learning how to be in the moment with a song that you already know rather than kind of worrying about, is the crowd connecting with my own songs? But once you’ve built your own body of work, did covers remain a part of your live repertoire?

Kathleen Edwards: Yeah, we won’t do a cover if we have like a festival set usually, but definitely for the long shows. I love having a cover and actually sometimes when I don’t have one, I feel a little bit like we got to give the person who didn’t know me tonight something to go home with.

AD: Total Freedom came out in 2020, and then there was a gap of a few years, and then all of a sudden one day on my streaming service, because I have you flagged for it to ping me when you released new things, it said ‘Kathleen Edwards just released “Only Lie Worth Telling”,’ and I thought, did she cover Paul Westerberg? And then for a while you were releasing a cover song every month.

Kathleen Edwards: That’s right.

AD: So what made you decide to do the Covers EP? Was it an intentional kind of warm-up for Billionaire? Did you have Billionaire kind of in mind the whole time?

Kathleen Edwards: I had “Billionaire” written, but I had no idea at that point. I hadn’t even talked to Jason [Isbell]. So, you know, the honest truth of it was that I had, you know, I had quit music [following Voyageur] and I had had to reassemble a team. So I had a new manager before Total Freedom. I’d signed to Dual Tone. I had a booking agent and a publisher. And so we sort of had this team and then we made Total Freedom and then, you know, it was the pandemic and it was a huge disappointment. I think I was a little bit naive to how sort of not a release it would be. I think I was a bit naive about, you know, what the long term implications would be. And the woman who I was working with, who I really liked, and I was very glad she was my manager, decided to stop managing. Not just me, but she had Hayes Carl as well, and she basically called me one day and was like, I can’t continue. And your manager is, so foundationally important. It’s so hard for a person to juggle all these people, unless you want to remain sort of doing really DIY stuff, and I just, I can’t handle all that. I suppose I could if I was just aspiring to play to 75 people a night, but I don’t. I want to try and build.

I spent two, three years trying to find a manager, and because the bottom had fallen out of the music industry, especially with people who were in these sorts of jobs, they were  all sent home, like crew people and riggers and managers and their teams. No one could afford to keep people on, because ultimately, there was no income coming in from all these venues, the avenues that they were used to making money from. So really, people were suddenly just kind literally half staffed, and I was looking to have a manager take me on after putting out a record that really, you know, everyone had gone through a similar experience if they’d put out a record.

AD:Yeah, yeah. How long had the previous manager, how long had you been with her?

Kathleen Edwards: About four, three, four years. So we did all the setup, you know, like there was a huge amount of setup, which is so disappointing because they put in so much time, like the amount of work that is required, it’s like a startup. I mean, it’s no different than a startup of any kind, right?

And so there was a real, it felt like a real disappointment. And obviously I really like this person, my former manager, Holly. She’s excellent, but she ended up leaving management. And so I was pretty committed to spending that time to just try and figure it out on my own, but then in the meantime, search for a manager and that too was kind of a tough slog. I had had a really good manager from early on who was there from Failer to Voyageur but going back into that management situation was not an option. And, so, you know, I had to call people and just try and see if anyone was interested and I knew that people knew my name, but I think nobody really knew what I was doing.

And eventually, for about a month and a half, I found somebody. And so then that manager suggested, look, you know, I think you need to do some covers because, you know, your algorithm is dead in the water, because I had stopped playing music right when streaming started and he wasn’t wrong, you know, it was a fair point. And so I called Jim Scott, the only person I really still had a relationship with in any kind of studio recording producer life. And I didn’t have a manager who could pick up the phone and call 30 people and be like, would you do this? Would you do this? And Jim Scott said yes.

And so I took all these songs, including “Only Lie Worth Telling,” a Flaming Lips song [“Feeling Yourself Disintegrate”], “Crawling Back To You” by Tom Petty, just songs that really had always sort of been in my covers repertoire. And I thought, well, let’s just do a really nice job on them. And when I delivered the covers selections, the answer that I got back was ‘this wasn’t really what I thought you were going to do. I thought you were going to cover like a Kacey Musgraves song’ and I was like, oh. Well, I don’t think that was what I ever intended on doing or communicated that I would do. So in the end, we didn’t remain in a business arrangement and I want to press all the cover stuff and still try and just find a producer for a Billionaire.

But yeah, it was a really tough three, four years to be honest. And I know that’s true for so many people in all different professions and people’s personal lives. The pandemic really had a ripple effect, but for music, for me, it was like, I put all this setup work into relaunching for 2020, and that didn’t happen. And then the ripple effect of so many people exiting the music business. You really need good administrators. You need managers who have teams, who have a part-time bookkeeper and somebody who can handle your social media and so many people had to just scale back their management that they wouldn’t take on new people. So maybe someone who would even think about taking me on just knew they didn’t have the manpower.  And that’s why I made a covers record.

AD: Had you covered that particular Paul Westerberg song before?

Kathleen Edwards: I played it once at Park West in Chicago, and I really liked the song. I listened to that record a lot when I was commuting between Ontario and Wisconsin when I was dating Justin [Vernon] and I really fell in love with that Stereo record. And of course, the song was reminiscent of exactly the sentiment of my breakup with that guy. So it’s like, you know, the only lie we’re telling is I’m in love with you. And I was like, well, that’s a perfect cover song. It embodies the moment, this moment in my life. So I might as well, I can own it as my own, you know, that’s always an important part of a cover is like, do you believe yourself when you’re covering it?

AD: In the song “Glenfern” on Total Freedom, there’s that line where you talk about, all the kind of great things that came along with success, but then there’s that line…

Kathleen Edwards: “It almost killed me.”

AD: Right. And so, do your memories of that Failer time, the success that came, is there a bittersweet memory of that stuff because of the troubles that came with it, or has enough time passed that you take the more positive things and kind of leave the crap behind, or how do you kind of live with that record at this point?

Kathleen Edwards: I think I just give myself a lot more grace for the things that I thought I was blowing at the time. You know, I was put in so many situations that my team or my band were like, ‘this is so exciting, this is a big deal.’ And I was just hoping to get through it. My band was there with me and they were wonderfully supportive. And you can’t criticize everyone doing the best that they can in the moment with the tools that they have. And I did that, but I also felt like half the time I was, I was struggling to enjoy it. Because I was put in so many environments where now in retrospect, I know what to ask for to make it better for me. So many bars I had to play where I couldn’t even hear myself for the whole show. Like, how am I supposed to enjoy playing if you can’t hear yourself? How are you supposed to get better at singing if you can’t even hear what your vocals sound like? And that’s just the nature of that learning curve, but I give myself a lot of grace for nights where I probably seemed like I was just grumpy. The thing is, all of those experiences have fortified me and made me the best version of my musician self today. I can’t redo that stuff. I only wish I could just go and stand in my shoes when I was 25 and just know what I should have asked for to enjoy it more. That’s the one thing that I wish I had, but I have it now. And maybe I wouldn’t know what to ask for if I wasn’t put in those situations. 

But you know, Colin Cripps is the subject of that song, “Glenfern.” Thank God he came into my life. He was suggested as a replacement guitar player for somebody who was doing their own thing at the time who I’d been playing with at the beginning. And Colin really loved the demo version of Failer, and he was a huge champion, and then I just was like, oh my God, who is this unbelievable guy? And we became a couple. And with that, he brought all of this experience and he was so much more grounded. You know, he was 17 years older than me and he had done a lot of recordings and a lot of touring and he really helped me from like driving that fucking van off a cliff. Like he kept me from becoming a drug addict and an alcoholic. You know, because I had somebody who was steadied beside me helping me navigate that stuff. So when I write that thing about “Glenfern,” you know, really, had he not been there, I probably would not have made three records. I probably would have fucking blown every bridge or just, you know, not known how to handle these intense situations I was put in.

I was looking at Aquarium Drunkard before we talked, and I scrolled through some of the people you’ve interviewed and so many, there’s so many people making really interesting work. And some of them don’t have large audiences, and some of them do, and some of them probably subsidize their passion in music by doing other work to pay their bills, and in that sense, up until the pandemic, I was self-sufficient. I was making enough money to pay my bills with music. You know, nothing crazy but enough, which is a victory.

But I was also thinking about all these artists and their pictures and their stories, like the, the first paragraph of a lot of them, and it immediately evoked like, an insecurity in me where I was like, I’m sort of trying to be a mainstream songwriter, and these people are doing real art. Like those are real artists and he’s talking to me and I’m kind of just like trying to be on the Grand Ole’ Opry and put on some fake lashes kind of songwriter. I mean, I know I’m not, but that’s sort of the insecurity in me that bubbles up because I’ve had to confront some of the realities of things that I think I did wrong at the beginning or that I didn’t have somebody help me be a little bit more prepared and polished. Like, I don’t feel like I give myself grace for that, but I wish that I had been a lot more confident in understanding that I actually was a really naturally good songwriter from the very beginning, even if I said something stupid on stage or, I did that because I didn’t think I was, and I, it was like the self-deprecation was a real monkey on my back. And I wish that I had found a way to confront that sooner and let that go so that I could enjoy the fact that people cared about me and that people were interested in my music because if I did, I know more people would have felt drawn to my work as opposed to like being kind of shitty and drinking too much on stage. I have persisted and endured, doing this a long time, and the effort that you put in versus the reward. It’s just, the reward, the certain types of work get small, but the joy of doing it as you get older and with time, you also realize, like, what a gift.
