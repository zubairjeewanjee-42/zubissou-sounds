# The Telepathy Is Evident :: Nels Cline on Consentrik Quartet & His Old CD Wallet

## Body

With his fourth album for Blue Note Records, guitarist Nels Cline introduces the Consentrik Quartet: Cline on guitar with saxophonist Ingrid Laubrock, bassist Chris Lightcap, and Tom Rainey on drums. Gathering noir-ish soundscapes, agile jams, and what Cline dubs “abstract and floaty ballads,” Consentrik Quartet bristles with energetic currents. Yes, Cline’s fiery and arcing runs are immediately recognizable no matter what group he’s with—be it Wilco, The Nels Cline 4, or the Nels Cline Singers—but here he and his collaborators seem to melt into the songs themselves, which were largely written during the pandemic, when Cline found himself in upstate New York with nothing but time. 

Cline joins us to discuss forming Concentrik Quartet, the influence of Jimmy Giuffre and Paul Bley, the unpredictability of Wilco, and the contents of his old CD wallet. | j woodbury

Aquarium Drunkard: Thanks for taking time to talk about this great new record, Consentrik Quartet. I think this is my favorite of your solo albums in a long time. This one really is in the zone. 

Nels Cline: Thank you.

AD: We talked for the Transmissions podcast during quarantine. I understand that’s when this music started to first germinate? 

Nels Cline: That’s when I started actually writing it. It was maybe germinating a little ahead of that, but during the lockdown era, it was all about the Singers album, Share the Wealth. But then by March of 2020, even though Share the Wealth didn’t come out until November of that year, my wife [Yuka Honda of Cabo Matto and solo fame] and I found ourselves in the woods in upstate New York quite suddenly, and we soon were just twiddling our thumbs. So yeah, I had gotten a grant the year before through Ars Nova Workshop in Philadelphia to write music for a quartet that would then perform the music in the eastern United States. So I thought, well, I got a grant to write music, I might as well write it even though we’re not going to perform anytime soon. So yeah, about half the material on the record was written during that phase of life.

AD: We’re now a few years past that lockdown moment, and so it feels like maybe we’re just starting to be able to get a little bit of perspective on what it meant and what was happening then. But I’m curious, what was the mood as you were working on this stuff? I hear a lot of openness and obviously a lot of freedom in this record, but it’s also so compositional. Was there something about that moment that gave you space or maybe having the calendar clear for probably the first time in a long time for you, did that inspire any of maybe the tone of the record at all?

Nels Cline: In some regards, I think my writing and conceptualizing process hasn’t changed much over the years. What’s changed is who I’m writing for. I think about the players. Except in the case of, for example, the opening track: “The Returning Angel,” it’s completely composed. There’s no improvising. But the last track on the record, “Time of No Sirens,” I didn’t really know what to do with that piece, to make it a Concentrik Quartet piece, for a long time. So I just let it percolate in my imagination for maybe a couple of years as I struggled to figure out just what to do with it. And definitely that piece is born of that moment that you’re asking about. I have a lot of pieces that have a lot of space. I joke that when people hear my records, they’re skipping over these floaty and abstract ballads. [Laughs] But in the case of “The Returning Angel,” it’s an overture, that is, completely composed. That brings us to a kind of accessible jam out tune called “The 23,” and the 23 is a Delaware County highway number. I think that if I’m writing for different instrumentation, the music would come out differently. But the balance of spacious to grooving and free, from intense to quiet and relaxed, that’s something I strive for in all the projects I do.

AD: In terms of players, this band features people—saxophonist Ingrid Laubrock, bassist Chris Lightcap and drummer Tom Rainey—who you got together with pre-pandemic to play at John Zorn’s venue, The Stone, is that right? 

Nels Cline: Yeah, it was The Stone in Brooklyn, which he did for a brief period there in Crown Heights, in an art gallery there.

I did two nights. The first was a trio with Tom and Chris, and the second night I added Ingrid, and it was all improvised. In terms of what I was playing guitar and setup wise, it was pretty stripped down at the beginning of that trio. I was in a Gábor Szabó frame of mind, playing with virtually no looping or effects—this acoustic electric guitar and a lot of drone jazz. 

But when we improvised at The Stone in Brooklyn, I had more of my kind of familiar setup; it came out really nice. So when I got this grant, initially, Mark Christman at Ars Nova was thinking of my group, The Nels Cline 4 with Julian Lage, Jorge Roeder, and Tom Rainey. But Julian being Julian, I had to tell him, “I can’t wrangle Julian anymore. It’s a miracle I’ve gotten this much scheduling time with him.” 

AD: He’s busy. 

​​Nels Cline: For good reason, he’s got a really fantastic career. So I said, “But I’m starting this other quartet that I want to do more with.” And I described it to Mark and he said, “Okay, well that sounds great.” So I wrote my justification for why I should get this grant and what I wanted to do, and I got it. So then it was for real, it was going to be a band.

AD: Reflecting back on those nights, what struck you about the combo, what elements did you want to expand on? 

Nels Cline: I don’t know if I can come up with any specific impressions but certainly playing with Chris and Tom was already “a thing. “ And I had admired Ingrid playing in various settings, particularly her duo with Tom. They’re married and they do a lot of duo music. And during the pandemic, they were actually posting streaming performances from their tiny apartment in Brooklyn. I paid attention to that. 

I’d already wanted to play with Ingrid. But that duo was kind of a powerful impetus for me. And I heard her at the Vanguard with Mary Halvorson Octet and there was an aspect of her playing: super articulate in a melodic sense, but also…the way she plays on the Concentrik Quartet record, her handling of the harmonic structure and her melodic sense in her tone. It’s beautiful saxophone playing. And then she has the extended techniques and a kind of mad intensity when she wants it as well. That’s ideal for my music.

AD:  We already talked a bit about Julian, and a throughline between you too is Jimmy Giuffre and Jim Hall. When did you first start to get into Giuffre?

Nels Cline: I started listening to The Jimmy Giuffre 3, the 1961 version, in the ‘70s, when I became aware of Paul Bley. I became a Paul Bley fanatic. So when I learned he and Steve Swallow were in Giuffre’s group—their record Fusion, on Verve, it’s still a desert island disc for me. The writing is so concise and the aesthetic is so dialed in. The telepathy is so evident and it has this restrained quality and instant compositional aspect that makes it, I think, just a go-to listen almost any time, an “always be inspired” kind of recording. And then the two records with that band are equally important to me. 

And then years later, ECM released the first two Verve records on a double CD called 1961, and did a lovely remastering job. And that was something that I traveled with back in the day when we used to travel with CD wallets and stuff, that along with Ravel and Erik Satie piano music, those were things  I always had with me on tour as a kind of oasis.

AD: A sonic refuge from probably the stresses of being on the road. 

Nels Cline: And just the constant sound. So that’s always back there, the Paul Bley approach to improvising, whether in a trio or doing solo piano or whatever. The Annette Peacock ballads, the Carla Bley compositions—these are things that I’ve been listening to and trying to, in some regard, reflect in my own writing since the ‘70s. 

AD: I was thinking about the Hot Sun, Cool Shroud EP Wilco put out last year. A song like “Livid,” it’s like “What the hell are these guys up to, it’s a very different register. Wilco is such an established thing, but you guys still surprise, and I feel like that must be a testament to the fact that everybody involved is interested in pushing forward and not necessarily repeating the same thing that you’ve done. 

Nels Cline: I think you’re absolutely right. I think that really that’s coming from Jeff Tweedy, not somebody who likes to repeat himself. And he’s also insanely prolific. But “Livid” is definitely a Jeff creation. But this openness, this unpredictability—all these things are why I even joined Wilco when I joined almost 21 years ago now. I had turned down a couple of tours  with other bands and things that I probably should have taken, because I was so broke, but they weren’t musically interesting to me. And I knew that Wilco would be this not only very humane situation, but also stylistically wide ranging and quite often unpredictable.
