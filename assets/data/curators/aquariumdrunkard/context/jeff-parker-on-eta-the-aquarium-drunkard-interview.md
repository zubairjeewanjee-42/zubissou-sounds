## Body

For years, guitar player Jeff Parker shared details on his social media accounts about his weekly gig at a bar called ETA in the Highland Park neighborhood of Los Angeles. Known for his always searching solo oeuvre, his varied collaborations, and production background as well as his revolutionary work as a member of Tortoise, the opportunity to see Parker play live in small, intimate space made more than one Instagram follower consider permanently relocating to Southern California to regularly catch these gigs. 

Mondays at the Enfield Tennis Academy, the 2022 release by Eremite Records, gave those that didn’t overturn their lives a window into the music that Parker played at this residency with the ETA IVtet, the name given to Parker and his regular collaborators: saxophonist Josh Johnson, bassist Anna Butterss, and drummer Jay Bellerose. On November 22, International Anthem issues The Way Out of Easy, the latest from Parker and the ETA IVtet. 

Whereas the Mondays album was made up of selections from various dates over the course of the band’s residency at ETA, The Way Out of Easy exclusively features music from one specific Monday. The album consists of one original composition by Parker and three group improvisations that presents the ETA IVtet moving through an array of sounds, including big fat grooves, hazy ambience, spooky dub, and exploratory solos. Throughout the band demonstrates how patient, tight, adaptable, and transcendent they can be. 

Aquarium Drunkard spoke with Parker about the beginnings of the ETA residency, the evolution of the ETA IVtet’s playing, his history as a bandleader, future releases by him and Tortoise, and how The Way Out of Easy represents a denouement. | s bunn

Aquarium Drunkard: How was the residency at the ETA established? Did you have goals for the band when you all started out or was it just an opportunity to play?

Jeff Parker: It was an opportunity to play. It was early when I first moved out to Los Angeles, and people had started calling me to play around town. One of the early gigs I did was at the Soho House in West Hollywood with this drummer named Matt Mayall and a guy came up to me at the show, who’s a Tortoise fan. He knew some of my music and he was like, “I opened this bar in Highland Park and just come by and have some drinks and hang out sometime.” His bar was ETA, and it was Ryan Julio who had the club. Then he started having music there, and the same trio that I played with at the Soho House played at ETA. I was just like, “Man, I’m not really playing that much around here.” So, I asked him if I could play there every week, just on a Monday night. I got Jay Belleros and Anna Butters and Josh Johnson, and we started playing there every week, just kind of as a place for me to keep my brain working and keep my chops up a little bit. We just played standards, tunes that we all knew, or if there was something that we were checking out. It was no original music or anything, just tunes from records, like as standards or bebop tunes, jazz tunes. 

Sometimes people would be out of town, and I had other people fill in. But for five years it was pretty consistent with that quartet, and we were just playing standards and then the music started to open up over time. Eventually, I think it was just one week where I was just like, “Man, I’m going to bring my looping pedal, my sampling pedal.” I started to bring synthesizers and stuff, in addition to my guitar setup and I told them, “Let’s just play open this week.” I set up drone-y things, and the music started to get really interesting. After a while, I was like, “Let’s just do this every week, rather than dealing with tunes.” We started with improvising in a pretty intentional, deliberate way. Starting with these drones and dealing with loopy stuff, and then if we exhausted that, then a kind of plan B, we’d play a tune. This was a really natural evolution of how the band stumbled into a specific way of improvising.  

AD: Do you think that evolution was because of the gelling of the band, but also the familiarity of the venue and a regular audience, or was it a combination of all of those things?

Jeff Parker: Yeah, man, it was all of it. At the same time, [the residency] was concurrent with the compositional music that I was making with my New Breed group. Josh Johnson plays in that band. Honestly, all of us work together in different projects with other people outside of the ETA IVtet. To make my New Breed music, Josh Johnson, he started to incorporate more electronics into his saxophone sound and keyboard sound in the New Breed band, and that bled over into when we would improvise with the ETA IVtet. Jay’s also on my New Breed records. And Josh and Anna, they play together in Josh’s band and on Anna’s music. There was a lot of overlap. With the ETA band, there were all these other experiences dealing with music that people were composing. So, when we would improvise, all of that other stuff was informed in what we were doing. Jay Bellerose has a lot of success as a session musician around Los Angeles and in Nashville. He plays on film scores. He’s a very colorful drummer. He can just show up with a snare drum and a kick drum and a cymbal sometimes. But if he’s improvising, sometimes he might show up with a 30-inch bass drum and these weird instruments. He has drums with springs on the inside of them so that they make echo and crazy, super thin cymbals that sounded insane. So, it was all these other experiences that were kind of bleeding into what the band was doing. Very natural. 

AD: Have you ever done a residency like this before moving to Los Angeles? Did you do this in Chicago?

Jeff Parker: Yeah, I did. I played at a place in Wicker Park called Rodan for almost 10 years. I did it at first with a bassist named Jason Ajemian, who was the bassist in Fly or Die, jaimie branch’s band, and the drummer named Nori Tanaka, who lived in Chicago for a long time, but went back to Japan. He lives in Fukuoka in Japan now. We did it for probably four years, until Jason and Nori moved away, and then I kept doing it with Joshua Abrams and John Herndon. Joshua Abrams played bass, and John Herndon, who’s one of my bandmates in Tortoise, played drums. And we did that for five years, every Tuesday night. It was the same thing, we just improvised. 

AD: How do you think you’ve changed now as a band leader, not only with this quartet, but with your solo work too? 

Jeff Parker: I mean, I just kind of stumbled into it, you know? My relationship with International Anthem has certainly heightened the visibility on that front. It’s made me be a bit more professional about it, whereas before, even the gig at Rodan, I never really saw it as my gig. I mean, it started out as Jason Ajemian, the bass player, who was the person who got the gig and then asked me to do it. As a trio, I’m the melodic instrument, so people just kind of assumed that I was a band leader. I guess I’ve kind of assumed some responsibility of guiding things in a certain direction. It’s taken a while of finding a community of sympathetic people who get where I’m coming from, at least on some level and I get where they’re coming from too. We develop these musical relationships and a support system. 

AD: The last ETA record was pulled from lots of different performances. For this album, you only drew from one performance on January 2nd, 2023. What was it about the music on that particular night that made you want to release it? 

Jeff Parker: We recorded the very last night of ETA, which I think was December 23, 2023. Scottie McNiece from International Anthem really wanted to put out the last night of us playing at ETA, which was a Saturday night. The place was ram-packed, the place was crazy, a line down the block. Most of the people who tried to come couldn’t get in. It was Saturday, it was the last night. And we always played an ETA on Monday; it was low key. This night, there was a lot more energy. There was emotion behind it; it’s the last night we were going to be playing in the place. Everybody was celebrating the end of it, and the music just wasn’t that good, you know? We listened to the recording, and it didn’t have the introspection and patience that we would have on the Monday nights. Bryce Gonzales, the genius recording engineer who recorded almost every night of everyone playing at ETA. Not just us on Mondays, but he was there most nights recording everyone. So, he had pretty much every Monday that I had played at ETA. I scoured the tapes and it was just one night. Everything about it was really cool. It sounded amazing, and we played really well. It wasn’t anything special about that particular date, or anything. It was just a really great documentation of when our band was at the top of our game. It just happened that the whole night was good. That was something that International Anthem wanted to differentiate from the Eremite Records release, which each side was from a different night. International Anthem wanted to have one night of a whole album, rather than it being curated in that way. 

AD: Since you’ve got all of these recordings, do you think you’ll put out anything else from the quartet’s time at ETA, or is this kind of it?

Jeff Parker: I don’t know. That’s a good question. I mean, this might be it. There are a lot of recordings of that band, yeah, but it might be more valuable just to have this be it. [Laughs] And maybe kind of move the band in a different direction. Maybe start writing music for the quartet, so everything’s not just improvised. 

AD: That’s what I was going to ask: what’s the future of this band? It sounds like you all have really connected. You talked about all the different projects sort of bleeding into each other, but do you think you would do a studio recording or another residency? What do you see as the next steps with this band? 

Jeff Parker: Oh, another residency seems impossible at this point just because everyone’s so busy. Anna plays in Jason Isbell’s band, and they tour a ton. Jay is always very busy. I mean, the residency was just serendipitous because Jay was T Bone Burnett’s drummer for everything. So, he had a nine-to-five. He’s in the studio all day in LA and then, at night, he was around town basically. And now T Bone is kind of retired, I think, and so now he’s back on the road. He tours with Robert Plant and Alison Krauss. And Josh, he’s everywhere now. He won that Grammy with Meshell Ndegeocello, and he’s got his own thing happening. It’s basically almost impossible to get everybody in the same place. We have some gigs coming up in December, but for us to play someplace every week like we did in the past, I don’t see that happening again. Maybe make another studio recording. I think the only thing I could see is just to book a tour, but I don’t want to tour. Maybe just play a few times a year, wherever people can have us. We’re playing at Big Ears in March, and we’ve got one night in Zebulon in December and then we’re playing December 2 in Brooklyn. But that’s it. That’s all that we’ve got so far.

AD: Over the past few years, you’ve been very busy collaborating with other musicians, production work, and playing with this band. Your last solo release was Forfolks and before that, Suite for Max Brown. Any plans for your solo work? 

Jeff Parker: I’ve been trying to make another New Breed album. Full disclosure: I’m happy that this ETA record is coming out, but there was supposed to be a New Breed album. I did some sessions, and I wasn’t able to finish that record in time, and in order to keep things on schedule, this album is coming out, which is great. This album would have come out anyway at some point. But I have another New Breed album in the works. I’d say it’s 70% done. I hold myself to high standards. I mean, I could put stuff out, but it’s not done. So, I’m working on it. [Laughs] 

AD: I gotta ask about Tortoise. Are you guys working on new stuff? 

Jeff Parker: Yeah, we are. We’re really close to having a new album done. Yeah, the band’s reactivated. We just played two shows two weekends ago. We played in Chicago and then played in New Orleans. It’s nice to have the band active again. Yeah, we’ve got some new stuff coming.

AD: I love The Way Out of Easy, and I love this band, so I hope there’s a future for you all, but it feels as though this album is sort of the end of an era for the quartet. 

Jeff Parker: Yeah, it’s hard. It’s a bit difficult. I was kind of in shock when Ryan [Julio] was like, “Yeah, we’re closing in two weeks.” I kind of committed to that place. People in other places would ask me to play in L.A. and I’d be like, “Well, no, if you want to hear me play in town, come to ETA and hear me play on Mondays.” I committed to it and built it up. And even in 2024, this year, I deliberately started the year really slow, because I had a really busy couple of years, especially after things opened back up with COVID. After COVID, I was doing a lot, and I didn’t really take any time off. So, my plan in 2024 was to hang out at home and play at ETA and then, he told us two weeks before the end of the year that the place was going to close, so I was stuck without a place to play in LA. It kind of threw me off, man. For real. For a while, I was in a state of grieving. 

The place was gone, and there was a community around it. It was a creative space for me as an artist, and I didn’t have it anymore. Man, I went through some stuff, certainly around the place. To mark it as an end of an era, for sure, is certainly accurate. I do think that the quartet is a special group, I mean, we have a real rapport. It grew out of that residency, and the place is gone. That record is a document of what we created with the community. You listen to that recording, that place is packed. It’s a bar, it’s a tavern, and it’s totally fucking packed with people, and it’s pin-drop quiet. Nobody’s saying anything. While we’re making that music, it was a special moment, certainly for me. I’m very grateful that I got to be in that experience.
