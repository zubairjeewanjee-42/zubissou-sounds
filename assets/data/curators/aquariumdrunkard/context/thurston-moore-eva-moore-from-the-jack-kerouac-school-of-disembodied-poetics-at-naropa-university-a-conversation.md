## Body

It only takes a moment outside of the Jack Kerouac School of Disembodied Poetics at Naropa University for the conversation to turn esoteric. There is no shortage of lore to draw from here–from tall tales of the Beat generation to the exploits of artistic luminaries at the Hotel Boulderado, the de facto haunt for visiting lecturers and performers. This year, Naropa is celebrating its 50th anniversary, a landmark for both the university and its co-founder, poet, activist and scholar Anne Waldman. After meeting Eva Moore in the school’s parking lot, we are immediately on our way to the lobby of the famed hotel to meet Thurston Moore.

The release of his latest album, Flow Critical Lucidity, finds Moore once again collaborating with Eva, AKA Radieux Radio and their mostly London-based ensemble which includes Deb Googe, Jon Leidecker, James Sedwards and Jem Doulton. His ninth solo effort, the Sonic Youth-founder and Sonic Life author adds seven more tracks to what can only be described as an ever-growing body of experimental music and literary work. But Moore is quick to point out that oversaturation is of little interest.

As we take our seats in the main lobby, I tell Thurston that the room feels appropriately literary. It is, he tells me, since Ginsberg used to stay here. That evening I would return to Naropa for a poetry reading which included Moore, amongst others, reading to a captivated audience of students, artists and Rocky Mountain litterateurs. But for now, I’m in the lobby of the Hotel Boulderado with Thurston and Eva Moore, discussing the writing process, how collaboration can subdue anxiety, the legacy of James Grauerholz and that time Henry Rollins grew out his hair. | n lekas

Aquarium Drunkard: 1991 The Year Punk Broke was the first important concert film for me. I could recite the dialogue…

Thurston Moore: [Laughs] The only thing I didn’t like about 1991, and I think it is a fine document, is that it was at a period that I was ignoring lyric pages. I would always have lyric pages taped to the monitor, cause I couldn’t remember my own lyrics for the first week or two. By the third week, I was getting it. And the fourth week, I was there. But those first couple weeks, which is really when this movie was made, I kind of thought, maybe I don’t need to sing the same lyrics every night anyways–just like a spontaneous conjuring of lyrics. In the movie, “Teenage Riot,” [sings] “There’s a bird…and a cloud up above and a ray is going to come someday and a teenage riot…” and I didn’t think anything of it until I saw the cut and there it was, me singing these ridiculously lyrics, it’s embarrassing and it’s caught in perpetuity.

AD: As a young guitarist, the spontaneity and just fury of it all was intoxicating, it took me a bit to uncover just how delicate many of those songs really were.

Thurston Moore: When you talk to a Japanese noise artist like Masami Akita, his whole thing was fetishizing the noise parts, getting rid of all that songwriting stuff. [Laughs] So it’s kind of funny. It’s like the free improvisers taking the stuff they were hearing in free jazz and getting rid of the jazz idioms around it–just focusing on the interplay of free improvisation, Europeanizing it in a way.

AD: I just wanted to jam a drum stick in a red Jazzmaster and call it a day. 

Thurston Moore: I still want to do that too.

AD: As time went on, the layers of songwriting presented themselves. I’ve found that to be true of Sonic Youth but also of your solo records. During the pandemic I listened to Demolished Thoughts a lot. I heard it in a way that I hadn’t before–a deeper layer. I also revisited Rock n Roll Consciousness and Trees Outside the Academy quite a bit.

Thurston Moore: Trees Outside the Academy I did in Northampton on J Mascis’s top floor. J Mascis lived in this house in Amherst Massachusetts, still does, painted purple. It’s the house that the Thurman family lived in. The Dalai Lama would stay in the top floor when he visited. Robert Thurman was this Buddhist scholar who entertained the Dalai Lama. J went to high school with Uma, she was a little younger than him, he knew the family a little bit just locally. The house went up for sale after all the kids moved out and J bought it. He’s lived there ever since. He created a studio on the top floor where the Dalai Lama used to kip, I was one of the first people to go up there and actually work.

Eva Moore: Do you think that recording was blessed by his holiness?

Thurston Moore: I think so. I felt Trees Outside the Academy had a certain vibe to it. It was informed by that space. I was in Northampton because I lived across the street from Smith College. That title came from where I would walk along the river on the campus with my dog. Outside the university were these very purposefully planted trees, and I just thought these trees are all about being complete in their knowledge of what they are, and they are outside of this place where humans are trying to sort of educate themselves and each other, where as the trees already transcended that–at the beginning of time. I liked the fact that they were in a row as guardians around this establishment. There was a lot of that going on in my brain when I was making that record. It’s sort of warm whiskey music in a way,

Eva Moore: That’s one of my favorites. It was Psychic Hearts, Trees Outside the Academy, and then Demolished Thoughts. Then Chelsea Light Moving which was kind of an oddity because you didn’t use “Thurston Moore” for the project.

Thurston Moore: Well it was right when Sonic Youth stopped, and there was a big fallout obviously with the whole narrative of Kim and I breaking up, and I was like, “Can I just erase my name and go out and do something?” No, you can’t really do that can you? But I thought I would create a project called Chelsea Light Moving, for better or worse it was what it was. I was spoiled so I refused to do press, I didn’t want to talk about anything, especially because I knew I would have to talk about what was happening and I just really wanted to be in the music. I liked the group. The songs were written really with a sense of immediacy. We booked a tour and we didn’t do press, it just said Chelsea Light Moving and yeah, 30 people would show up. [Laughs] I was like ok this is cool, back to the egg playing these gigs.

The rest of the band, after the gig, they could just hang and have a beer and I couldn’t do that because everyone wanted to have a piece of the Sonic Youth guy. I couldn’t escape that. I would go in the van and hide in the backseat. Everyone would come back happy like “Let’s go find a Motel 6 somewhere” and I’m going insane. So, I did that for a year, and it was at a point where Eva and I were sort of trying to define our lives together. Eva moved to London and I followed her. People are like, “When are you going to do it again?” And it’s like, I didn’t move it with me.

The name came from Chelsea Light Moving, Philip Glass and his brother, or cousin or something, and Steve Reischl. I met Philip Glass, Eva and I were on tour with Yoko Ono doing little contemporary art museums across Europe, the smallest ones across the farthest reaches because that’s what she wanted to do, bring her thing to places who don’t normally get her thing. I was asked to perform with her, mostly this abstract stuff based on these pieces she had.

Eva Moore: I was her guitar tech.

Thurston Moore: I wrote a guitar piece that I gave her called “Hugging Piece,” where two people wear guitars and they are walking towards each other. The guitars are on full blast but you don’t really hear them because you aren’t touching them, you kind of hear the detritus of sound, and then when they get close they embrace and it’s like [makes loud feedback noise]. Eva had to put it on [Yoko], and Yoko is like, “This is the first time I’ve ever worn a guitar.” We’re like wait, you’re the fifth Beatle and you never wore a guitar? It was great, Eva was showing her films, Yoko was showing her films. So there was a dinner, Eva, myself, Yoko, Yoko’s assistant, and Philip Glass and some of his musicians. And I’m sat next to Phillip, and I had met him maybe a long time ago but he wouldn’t remember me from Adam. But I certainly knew who he was. I said to him, I have to tell you I had this group and we had this record, our name was Chelsea Light Moving, and he looked at me and said, [excited] “You’re the one!” He had gotten wind that there was a band called Chelsea Light Moving, “You’re the one, I wish my brother was alive, he would’ve been so happy that somebody called themselves that.”

Eva Moore: After Chelsea Light Moving is The Best Day with the British band.

Thurston Moore: It was fortuitous for Ev and I to be living a ten minute walk to Cafe OTO, kind of the critical listening room for experimental music in London for the last 15 years, and that became our den you know. We were there all the time. The programming was incredible, continues to be. The first record in London is The Best Day, but this is the beginning of where Ev and I really started collaborating together. Eva is bringing lyric concepts to the record, which formulates a lot of what defines the records to me. Our partnership really starts blossoming there creatively. We had been creative doing book work together, but making records was a new aspect, it continues to be really rewarding.

Eva Moore: I remember actually, it was here at the Harry Smith Print Shop that we printed up on those sacred machines the lyrics for The Best Day.

AD: Was that type of collaboration different from how Sonic Youth worked lyrically?

Thurston Moore: There wasn’t that much collaboration lyrically in Sonic Youth. Most of the songs were recorded instrumentally with some regard that lyrics will exist, and then in the recording process it’s like we have these 8-10 songs, which one do you want to sing? Initially, it was just Kim and myself, Lee’s song would always be something fully formulated by him. Whereas for Kim and I, it was never really that way. Maybe more for me and less for her, but that’s kind of how we worked. You were sort of tasked with this instrumental song and you would have to wear headphones and drive in your car like “The moon in June coming out tonight” you know? We all had our own strategies. Mine was either sort of like channeling or actually going into notebooks and finding things that I had written.

AD: So now it is more collaborative in the sense that you are working together on individual lyrics and concepts?

Thurston Moore: I’m just writing the music. In a lot of ways I have anxiety about writing lyrics to music, and I’ve always liked the idea of singing words that I didn’t write. When Ev first started giving me some pieces of papers it was like, well here is something, and it’s like that actually works let me just sort of plug that in. I modify it a little bit sometimes. I really enjoyed that collaboration, it was truly collaborative. And I didn’t feel like I had to express myself lyrically all the time, I feel like I’ve done enough of that. I didn’t feel like people must hear what I have to say, I never had that feeling like I had to prove something. When push comes to shove, I can write fucking lyrics but knowing that Eva is consciously thinking about lyrics to what she is hearing in the studio really alleviates a lot of anxiety for me, I can just focus on the instrumentation.

AD: What was the process for Flow Critical Lucidity like?

Thurston Moore: I was composing the record in Switzerland, at a residency, we had visual access to Lake Geneva and the mountains surrounding it. That informed a lot of the vibe of it. I was writing a lot of it on electric guitar with headphones on and Eva was writing lyrics. It was probably more collaborative in a sense where all of a sudden pages were in front of me, I was writing with these pages in front of me instead of pages coming afterwards, that was really a new thing with this record. Also Eva suggested introducing keyboards and piano.

Eva Moore: For the arranging of the record it was just you and me, James [Sedwards] and Wobbly [Jon Leidecker]. We left the rhythm section out because we knew that was going to come later. A couple things happened on the lake while we were there, including a man who was exploring the sea drowned, and so the last song on the record was for him.

Thurston Moore: The Diver.

AD: I wanted to ask you about Rewilding also, it has a Gary Snyder vibe to it.

Eva Moore: Can I quote you on that?

AD: Of course.

Thurston Moore: Gary Snyder, yeah, Snyder is still around. I would love to see him someday, I never met him.

Eva Moore: There is a movement in the UK, I think it is really beautiful, the rewilding movement. But also we made friends with these guys who were growing coral. They are called Coral Morphologic, they are kind of like turning coral into this art. But they also have underwater cameras where you can watch the coral. It is really beautiful. So you know, it’s really inspiring to see these real movements happening. So that song is inspired by both of those.

AD: You write lyrics under a moniker, can you tell me a little bit about Radieux Radio?

Eva Moore: [William] Burroughs had a boyfriend named James Grauerholz. I saw a picture of James Grauerholz like 25 years ago and just decided that he was the most gorgeous man alive. I feel like when I met you [Thurston] I found my James Grauerholz. I found that ideal love. I don’t know what it was about that relationship where it was like extra romantic, because it was these two men who love each other. When I started writing lyrics, I am writing about women, I am writing about men, I’m writing about my love of all kinds of intergender romance. I kind of feel like that voice, Radieux Radio is very much like a gay man writing about his beautiful gay lover. Yeah, so that disembodied poetic, that’s who that is.

AD: The idea of love beyond eros, romance beyond the romantic. 

Eva Moore: It’s like agape, brotherhood.

AD: If love is consciousness, can we transcend the physical tether right? Love as consciousness. That is actually something I thought about while listening to Flow Critical Lucidity, and Sonic Youth always seemed to be pulling at the tether of the tangible in a sense. That is something I’ve always been drawn to in your work, as in Burroughs or Kerouac, in many of the artists we talk about.

Thurston Moore: You talk about somebody like James Grauerholz and his relationship to William Burroughs, Burroughs is a couple decades his senior and what was, for me, always interesting in a relationship like that was James was kind of like a guardian, making sure William who he adored and loved would not be taken advantage of, would not be exploited. And his narrative would be truthful. 

He wrote a long thing online about how William felt about the narrative of when his wife was killed by his own hand. This is the narrative that Bill and I want to share. It was really important for him to do that. It was something that certainly haunted WIlliam Burroughs ever since. His biographer Barry Miles, who wrote the Call Me Burroughs biography, the one to read, he says that incident defined his psychology from that point on. You can look at it in any context you want to look at in, cause it’s a terrible thing. He is the perpetrator, and he went to the grave as the perpetrator of this incident, and for James Grauerholz [to say] I need to set the record straight, I’m going to put this out there, I find that to be, that’s really important.

Eva Moore: Also, we have a lot of sources that have told us about this party trick coming off successfully before. It really was an accident.

Thurston Moore: A drunken accident.

Eva Moore: A lot of psychology though to conquer after that.

Thurston Moore : The karma of it. When something happens in your life that is that critical, why does that happen to your life? I had an interview with one of the students in my conference, dealing with all this karmic chaos, and I had to be like this is more than me being in a conference about your writing.

Eva Moore: So much writing is therapy.

Thurston Moore: Then I thought, why am I meeting this person today? This person is living in a zone way beyond what I’m living in as far as desperation goes, and is here at Naropa, writing about this intense reality of their lives, their life that is challenged completely. This was a call to empathy that I rarely have. And I finally got this person to smile, it was kind of amazing.

Eva Moore: It gets really intense with the students. You are grateful for these moments when someone can trust you.

AD: William Blake wrote about the poetic genius, Neil Young talked about dropping everything when the muse calls. I was curious, what are your thoughts on the muse?

Thurston Moore: It’s always there. I don’t have any anxiety about writer’s block or anything. If I have any anxiety, I’m afraid to get to a place where things actually take off in the creative realm. I have no anxiety about the muse all of a sudden being there. In some way, my anxiety is about entering into those zones, because these zones usually result in this kind of pleasure head DNA thing, and I get to sleep at night and I feel energized, and I feel good. I know that happens, sometimes I feel it’s something I get resistant to, because maybe I don’t feel like I want to be in that zone of feeling that way, I’m kind of addicted to my doldrum. I don’t know? When Gerhard Richter talks about the practice of painting he’s like you paint every day. You have that time every day where you go in and practice, that’s how you become more of a painter, more of an artist, that’s how you become better. You pick up a guitar every day and play better and better. I find that’s true of course. But I also find if I step away from that practice, I know that it’s going to be waiting for me. That contemplation is always there, and in some ways maybe it is better that I didn’t play every day because I would’ve been in this same stream. What if I step out of the stream, climb over the mountain and go to some other stream? That’s what happens if I take a week or two or a month and re-enter into something else. Both things are fine, both things work for me, so I never have any sort of issue with that. My only issue is that time is ticking. But then it’s just like, well, who cares?

AD: I’ve heard artists like Nick Cave talk about going into his “Nick Cave world” to write. Do you think about making a Thurston Moore album or by stepping out of the stream, do you also step out of that pressure?

Thurston Moore: I never think about my name. Or even what I look like. That scares me more than anything. When I’m in a situation where I become sort of the big cheese in the room or something. I don’t ever walk into a room, knowing that, or thinking that, or wanting that. Then all of a sudden I realize that’s what’s going on, it kind of freaks me out a little bit. I am a Leo but I don’t feel like I ever desire that. Even at a young age I was like, I’d love to be in a band that was on the cover of Circus Magazine. But it was because I liked that community. I wanted to be part of that community that is up there freaking people out. I’m ok with that. I’m ok with that notoriety or whatever, being recognized, but I certainly don’t feed off of that. It’s not what makes me want to do work.

The work is kind of its own thing, it’s never about satisfying anybody else. It’s like when Henry Rollins came out on stage with Black Flag and the band had just decided to grow its hair long. I saw that when they came to the East Coast. Henry was like the king of DC skinheads. All of a sudden he goes to the West Coast, kind of starts taking acid and listening to the Grateful Dead, and they all start growing their hair and they’re like “Fuck punk rock, we’re something else.” Black Flag is something else. But their audience is like teenage skinheads, they were just like “Cut your hair!” Henry did this great thing, I’ll never forget it. He reached down his pants, and pulled his cock out. He held it in his hand and said “I’ll grow my hair down to here before I satisfy you.” 

And I was standing there like right on dude. That’s the best comeback I ever heard. He quieted this crowd of the rowdiest fucking skinheads, they all just kind of looked at each other like… the king of skinheads just grew his hair out and told everyone that he was going to grow his hair all the way down to his cock before he satisfies you. I always remember that.

AD: That’s hard to argue with.

Thurston Moore: It’s not about satisfying expectations at all. It transcends satisfaction. It transcends the exchange of acceptance–walking into a room and expecting acceptance. I’ve learned how to play gigs or do readings and not really go out there with any kind of fear of not being accepted.

AD: Acceptance and attention I imagine.

Thurston Moore: I already went through being young and waving our hands in the air. Like “Look at us!” I don’t feel at all like I need to do that. In fact I’d rather not be in a situation where there is some kind of expectation because of my history or whatever, hence Chelsea Light Moving. When I started recording under my own name, the first responses were “They sound like Sonic Youth songs” well, OK, there is a reason for that. That’s my vocabulary and it’s not like I feel I should step away from that. It’s always interesting, it’s always inviting, it’s always intriguing. Maybe I feel a little guilty when I’m not doing it for a long period of time, but why do we constantly have to exhibit ourself, display our work?

AD: At every turn, at every level, I think there is very modern pressure to be prolific.

Eva Moore: There are poets at Naropa who take a vow of silence and say, my name is appearing in too many poetry journals, I want to step back. They removed their ego from their community, purposefully, and I think there is something really beautiful in that too. Knowing when to edit yourself.

Thurston Moore: I write a lot of music on my Zoom recorder that nobody will ever hear, and I don’t care. You know, sometimes you over saturate people with your work. But then I think of Margaret Randall, great writer, poet, thinker, feminist, she’s been on the frontlines of so much of what has happened in the timeline of the Western world, and she’s written close to 200 books. If you’re going to collect Margaret Randall, you’re in for it. She is constantly putting something out there–like Guided By Voices who put out 300 records this year. In a way you can make an art of that kind of over-saturation, I’m not really interested in that.
