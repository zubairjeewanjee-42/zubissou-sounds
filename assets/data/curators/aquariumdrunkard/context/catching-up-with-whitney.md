## Body

It feels strange to have a new Whitney record to become acquainted with against a winter backdrop. The luminosity of the Chicago duo’s musical expression, both in vocal delivery and golden-tinted guitar licks and soft percussive accompaniments, personifies the warmth and carefree essence of long, sweltering days. Small Talk, which finds Whitney returning to their breezier sensibilities following their fascinating and successful foray with incorporating more electronic textures throughout their 2022 release, Spark, presents a marked maturation of the band’s approach to lyric writing, a confidence in their artistic expression that’s also reflected in the magnificent elevation of their idiosyncratic musical style as they create bigger spaces for more textures to play together. 

Guitarist Max Kakacek and singing drummer Julien Ehrlich packed a lot of living in the three years between albums. From break-ups, being roommates and, crucially, sitting with the advice that it was time for them to fully take the reins on their sound and that self-producing a record was well within their reach. Setting up a make-shift home studio in Oregon, not far from where Ehrlich’s dad had been storing recording gear, the pair spent three weeks putting a shape on Small Talk’s effervescent eleven-song tracklist. Having played under the Whitney name for a decade now, with a handful of years in Smith Westerns before that, Kakacek and Ehrlich are so finely in-tune with one another that they’ve cultivated a space to comfortably veer left-of-field with their projects (be it covering a Willie Nelson song with Waxahatchee or making an electronic-focused LP) that has ultimately strengthened their creative muscles, as evidenced on Small Talk, their most accomplished body of work. Here, a gorgeous 1970s polish glistens in the dynamic instrumentation and general buoyancy of tracks like “Dandelions” and “In The Saddle”, while melancholic piano-led moments such as “Silent Exchange” and “And Love” demonstrate Whitney’s success at deftly merging a decade’s worth of work, ideas and curiosity cohesively under one roof.

Whitney’s Max Kakacek and Julien Ehrlich paused what they were doing—recording a cover of Robert Lester Folsom’s “See You Later, I’m Gone”—to talk to Aquarium Drunkard about their experience making Small Talk, how watching the NBA on mute while recording was encouraging and continuing to embrace a homemade sound for future Whitney records. | z hedderman

Evangeline (feat. Madison Cunningham) by Whitney

Aquarium Drunkard: The new Whitney album, Small Talk, feels particularly special within your discography because you self-produced it and recorded some of it at home. How was it approaching this one from the perspective of being totally in the driver’s seat for it? Did you miss having the external voice of a producer to bounce ideas off?

Max Kakacek: Mainly during the mixing process, it would have made it a little easier because we became a little tweaky, and we’ll fiddle with things over and over again. Or, move stuff up and down that most people wouldn’t even care about. We really get lost in that bubble. In terms of the process of writing, we extensively demo, and that’s always been part of our writing process. So, you know, I think it was pretty much the same as when we got into the studio, we were kind of just on our own in that sense. 

AD: Something about these songs that I enjoyed was the broadening of the arrangements and how big the album sounds. I loved the richness of the orchestration on “Evangeline” and “Back to the Wind”, in particular. What were some things you were excited to play around sonically with that had been lingering in your mind for a while, and wanted to try in the arrangements that you finally got around to with this record?

Julien Ehrlich: Honestly, the saxophone was a pretty big addition on this record. Even though it’s not necessarily always featured as a lead instrument, the texture really goes a long way. We wound up putting it on three more songs than we really intended on working on. That was something we were excited by. 

Max Kakacek: It’s funny to say that “Evangeline”, honestly, is the most playful song we’ve ever done. When we were writing that song, the chorus was so grand. [In the past] we would have been like, ‘This is too much for us’, or something. That was a moment where we let ourselves have fun with how big it got, and with those three giant chords, which is something we don’t normally do. 

AD: How has it been translating that in your live shows? You were playing a lot of amphitheatre-based venues during the summer. How was it playing the new songs in a setting like that? 

Julien Ehrlich: It was great. We always play with a trumpet player who’s playing through a harmonizer. Our band, I feel, is suited for both theatres and amphitheatres, actually. It’s funny, we’re about to start rehearsing because we’re going to play the record front to back next Sunday, and then again in New York on actual release day, and we have no idea how certain songs, specifically the end of “Silent Exchange”, there isn’t really another moment on the record like that, so figuring out the guitar arrangements and how we can make it feel as overwhelming as it does on the record is going to be interesting. Max and I played “Evangeline”, a totally stripped-back version of it, at a show about a month or so ago, so we know that that song definitely works, at least, in a bare form. I feel like trying to recreate the actual choruses, how they are on record, will also be a bit of a challenge. 

Damage by Whitney

AD: A fun challenge, hopefully. With Small Talk, it also feels like you were full of ideas and confidence in developing the instrumentation. Coming from your previous record, Spark, which had more of an electronic-tinge to the songs and also outside of that recording different covers, something that you were doing today, too, it seems like the last couple of years, leading up to this record, have been filled with you exercising different creative muscles. How do you feel that the last couple of years, with those different ways of approaching music, have helped strengthen your musical expression? 

Max Kakacek: You know, coming back to the recording process, there was a lot of rediscovering the genesis of the project or something. We started recording ourselves at our house and with organic instruments, leaning into tape saturation, and just recording on tape, especially for demos. It’s not as crunchy; we polished it up a little bit. After Spark, returning to that process was really inspiring again, you know, we went really extreme one way and coming back to where, or how we started everything and fell in love with the project, I think that reinvigorated us to make as much music as possible. We wrote maybe 20 songs for this one. That’s the most we’ve written for an album, so we have a lot [of songs] that we’re sifting through now.

Julien Ehrlich: There are a few that should probably make the next record, and then some other ones that should definitely come out, too, at some stage. 

AD: Did you find it hard to whittle the final tracklist down from those 20 demos? 

Max Kakacek: Honestly, it didn’t end up being that hard. But again, to speak to our tweaker mode, there was a point where we were like, “Back to the Wind” doesn’t fit on the record’. We were thinking that it’s too much. Then, there were two weeks we were like, ‘It’s going to be 10 songs and “Back to the Wind” won’t be on it!’ Now, that’s so insane to me. 

Julien Ehrlich: We wrote a rockabilly song, as well, that we all really love. I still really love that song. I could see that one being on the next record. It was maybe a tiny bit too heavy-handed or something in the context of some of these other songs. That song being on the same record as something like “Islands” is funny to me. 

AD: You mentioned how you wanted to bring it back to the genesis of the project, which is interesting given that you started Whitney, obviously, out of being in Smith Westerns, a decade ago. You lived together during the making of the album, and I read that you watched some films together, notably Poor Things, which you jammed over, right? 

Max Kakacek: Yeah! One day, we were going through different movies as we were recording, and I’d never seen that movie, so we were recording with the sound off to watch something. It’s a pretty visceral film. 

Julien Ehrlich: I mean, it’s pretty much soft-core porn for 20% of it! 

Max Kakacek: And so, I was sitting there being like, ‘What the fuck is this?’ because I had no context of anything that was going on!  I can’t remember who put it on. 

Julien Ehrlich: It’s such a really visually striking and weird ass movie, especially some of the settings towards the end when Mark Ruffalo and Emma Stone are on whatever trip, and it’s so clearly shot in a movie studio, but they just make it look so fantastical. It was super cool. 

Max Kakacek: For “Back to the Wind”, we were watching old skate videos, which is why we referenced that in the video for that song. Depending on the song, we’d try to choose a mood somehow, but randomly. 

Back to the Wind by Whitney

AD: While you were living together and watching movies while recording, I have to imagine it was tough to switch off from thinking about the songs and the album. Did you have any rituals that you had together during that period to unwind? Were you cooking a lot?

Julien Ehrlich: Yeah, we did switch off cooking dinner, just based on whoever was working. If Max is sitting down focusing on a bass line, I’ll cook dinner, or if I’m focusing on melody or something, vice versa. If we’re like in a mode where we know we’re going to go hard and really push to try to actualize an idea that we’re working on, beers or some bottles of wine will typically make an appearance at around 6 or 7 PM. Then we can stay up until 3 or 4 AM. We did a lot of writing throughout the winter, and there was a TV behind the computer screen, and we usually just had the NBA playing in the background! That’s always really fun. If we’re working on an upbeat song and they’ll always show the cheerleaders dancing, and we’ll have the sound turned off, so it’s fun to watch people dance to the song that you’re actually in the process of creating! 

AD: Nice. In exploring and experimenting with new things for this record, what were some of the main challenges you faced in the writing or recording period? 

Max Kakacek: The chorus on “Dandelions” took a really long time. When we were able to unlock that part of the puzzle, or when we’re having a successful day, we felt like very inspired, happy people. But when we’re having a bad day with a song, we tend to be like, ‘Should we throw away this whole idea?’ There were certain points where we almost threw things away. “Dandelions” got to that point at a certain time. We unlocked the chorus, and once that happened, we were like, ‘Oh, this has to be on the record!’ We have a lot of those up and down moments where right before something seems like we can’t figure it out, but we’ll somehow find a way to make it into something that we like. 

AD: With the lyrical side of the album, I enjoyed how emotive and transparent you were about some of the experiences that shaped the themes. How was it writing these songs, and how has it been returning to them with the distance of time and hearing things from a different perspective? 

Julien Ehrlich: Overall, we both knew that Spark was very much a pop record to us. That’s reflected in some of the lyricism, where it’s a bit more, I don’t want to say surface level, but it’s more direct. There aren’t necessarily layers to think about in all those songs. We were just trying to write hooks. When we were mentally, not even preparing but thinking about what the next record should be, and feeling like we were ready to return to a bit more of the Whitney sound that we started with, we knew that we had to add a seriousness to it. We had to dig as deep as we could for the lyrics. We knew that we couldn’t come back and be singing about the trees and the rivers and shit. We needed to figure out what more of the true Whitney personality is and all the different sentiments that that entails. I feel like we were at least somewhat successful. 

AD: Yeah, absolutely. In searching for what the Whitney personality is through this process, it does feel like you had a lot of breakthrough moments in that regard, but also on a personal level. How has songwriting, and particularly crafting the lyrics of these songs, helped you get a better understanding of yourself, your place within the world and in the relationships in your life? 

Max Kakacek: It changes often, but in our writing partnership, what’s fun is, say, with the song “Darling”, for example, we wrote that when I was probably a week fresh after getting dumped. I was so fucking sad, I was break-up sad, you know? We’ve all experienced something like what that feels like. We wrote that song almost as a joke break-up song. It’s so simple and honest. The lyrics remind me of a Dolly Parton song or something, where it’s a 1950s or ’60s sad song. It was a moment where we got to play with or have fun with the sadness that we’re living through. Even though it doesn’t fix anything, it was funny to us or just a good place to put that energy. Instead of leaning into sadness, like, ‘Oh, I need to make a really sad song to express my heartbreak,’ we made something that we thought was cute and that cheered me up at the time, because it was fun! The Instrumental at the end of that song is so uplifting and optimistic. That felt like this is what a breakup is; it’s sad, but then there’s something optimistic near the end. 

AD: A light at the end of the tunnel. Sonically, there’s a strong 1970s feel to the arrangements and some of the tones incorporated in there. George Harrison was someone I kept thinking about. The album artwork even feels like a nod to Tapestry, in a way. What were you listening to while you were recording that, intentionally or not, that trickled into your melodic sensibilities? 

Julien Ehrlich: We were definitely listening to some Nick Drake. Always listening to The Band, really. We were leaning into both of those influences at certain points in the record. Neil Young’s “A Man Needs a Maid” was a direct inspiration for “Evangeline”. 

Max Kakacek: I don’t think it inspired any of the actual textures, specifically, I don’t think we had heard it and then were like, ‘Let’s make this!’, but the Cindy Lee record, the feeling of it and embracing old textures in a new way and the confidence behind that record was inspiring. It’s one of those things that you hear and go, ‘Damn, now I want to make music like that. ’ That was an inspiration for its incredible artistic weight. The amount of great songs on that record was inspiring to us. Also, just embracing something that’s a take on music that’s been made before. It’s a great testament to that approach and sound having validity in 2024, or when it came out. 

Dandelions by Whitney

AD: Completely, yeah. I mentioned earlier that this year marks a decade of Whitney. Looking back on that time, how would you say that your bandmate has pushed you forward into the musician and writer that you are today?

Julien Ehrlich: There have been some pretty direct moments. When we were writing “Damage”, we had a couple of different placeholder vocals, lyrics and hooks in place for the chorus, and then Max would tell me if something didn’t feel right. It’s those moments where it’s just like, ‘All right, fuck, as frustrating as this feels right now, we need to do this instead. ’ We’re pretty direct and do push each other. I do think a lot more of it is unspoken. At this point, we’ve put so many reps in in the last 10 years or whatever that we both know when something is right a little faster and, also, we know how to get there a little bit faster. 

Max Kakacek: Julian got married last December, and I had to just say some shit at that wedding, and I’m not a good public speaker; it’s not my thing. The way the wedding worked was that there were a lot of speeches throughout, and we were drinking and eating, and I ended up giving my speech at midnight. At that point, we’d been drinking wine since 3 PM, and basically, all I could pretty much stutter out was, ‘I could never do this without you, bro’, that was basically my speech! That’s very true, I couldn’t do it without him.

AD: Finally, what do you think Small Talk has unlocked in you both for the next album? 

Max Kakacek: I think we’re just starting to get the studio going. Recording today, I felt that doing a cover recording is a way to start opening up the studio and see what comes out in a low-pressure scenario. Also, I could see us doing a lot of it ourselves again, maybe it’d be a little crunchier. I don’t know. 

Julien Ehrlich: Or maybe a little more homemade feeling, or something. The newest songs that we’ve made genuinely feel very inspired to me.
