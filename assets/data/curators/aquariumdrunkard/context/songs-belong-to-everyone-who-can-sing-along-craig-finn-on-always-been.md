## Body

The cover of Craig Finn’s latest solo album, Always Been, directly nods to Randy Newman’s 1977 lp Little Criminals. Shot in the same location, on the West 7th Street overpass over the I-110, it presents The Hold Steady frontman posed exactly as Newman is on his classic album. And like Newman’s songbook, Always Been is filled with character studies, a cast of people who aren’t quite sure what to make of their lives and the directions they’ve taken. 

Produced by Adam Granduciel of The War on Drugs, Always Been presents the story of a man who becomes a priest despite a lack of faith. It’s accompanied by a book as well, Lousy With Ghosts, which presents further stories which expand and draw into focus other characters and action on the periphery of the record. The result is a narrative that’s as expansive and detailed as Finn’s yet created. “I did a lot of writing,” he says over the phone, speaking with Aquarium Drunkard about the new record, book, and related inspirations, from Bruce Springsteen to the late Scott Hutchison of Frightened Rabbit. Our conversation, edited for length and clarity, is presented here. | j woodbury

Aquarium Drunkard: There’s a lot of Bruce Springsteen in the air in 2025; so my first question is: did you get a physical edition of Bruce Springsteen’s Tracks II? 

Craig Finn: I have not. The size of my apartment has got me out of record buying mode for a bit. But I have been listening to it. I love it. The synth stuff’s great. I really liked Western Stars, so I’m drawn to Twilight Hours, where he’s in Glen Campbell mode.

AD: I’ve always wanted a whole record that sounds like “Streets of Philadelphia” and “Secret Garden,” so I’m especially excited about that side of it. And there are just great songs throughout. The War on Drugs covered “Unsatisfied Hearts,” and to start off this talk about Always Been, I wanted to ask about when you first met Adam Granduciel and the crew? 

Craig Finn: It was around ’09. I heard the first record, Wagonwheel Blues, and really liked it. We asked them if they wanted to go on tour in Europe and they said yes. They flew over early to do an early show without us, their own show in London or something. And on the eve of the tour our guitarist Tad Kubler got very ill and went into the hospital with pancreatitis. We had to cancel the whole tour. We stranded them in Europe, which was I’m sure very harsh, but the next spring, we asked them out in the US. We went pretty much across the country and it was a fun tour. They were a three piece at the time. It was Adam, Dave Hartley, and this drummer Mike Zanghi. They were not the juggernaut that they became yet. They were pretty scrappy. But they were super charming. 

AD: When you and Adam started talking about Always Been, did you start with the idea of Adam taking the lead sonically? 

Craig Finn: 100%. 

AD: I feel like Adam brings a Jeff Lynne-ness to his projects, and by that I mean wherever he goes, you can tell it’s him there. 

Craig Finn: Sure. I had the songs going with an acoustic guitar. I’d gone through the process of taking the fat of ‘em. I knew when Adam heard them he’d be like, “Yeah, we got this.” For me, it’s just a matter of recording with good people. When you watch Behind The Music, people are like, “This is a very difficult record to make.” But this was the opposite of that. I don’t remember ever beating our head against the wall. We tinkered with the song “Fletcher’s” a lot, but other than that, man, it was just kind of a matter of just recording and generally having a good time doing it. 

AD: Like Adam, you can always tell when you’re involved in a song. You have a recognizable voice. But “Fletcher’s” is definitely new territory for you. 

Craig Finn: With that one, I kept saying it needed a non-vocal hook; I kept referring to it like a hip-hop song, it needed something to hook you. It ended up being that descending counter melody, but I remember just searching for that and we ended up coming up with something very simple, but searching for that kind of motif that could happen between the verses. 

AD: Something that isn’t new territory for you is creating rich worlds, but between this record and the book, Lousy With Ghosts, I think we’re looking at your most fleshed out world, with scenarios, characters, and all of that. 

Craig Finn: That’s why Lousy with Ghosts was able to come out. I did prose writing where I sort of mapped out more about these characters and more about their story than I was even able to fit onto the record. So I knew them really well. It was a really cool exercise. In songs, you’re just showing the tip of the iceberg. But when you really know the characters, you know there’s a lot more going on underwater, their backstories, their motivations. You might not be able to fit all that into a song, but I realized that storytelling wise, this is the most expansive thing I’ve done. 

AD: You’ve got a character here who used to be a priest. And as he grows more and more detached from his calling, he becomes more bitter, to the point that he’s “pissing off a petty vengeful God.” It’s a funny, really cool, non-didactic and sneaky way to introduce this idea that the shittier we feel, the shittier we imagine God must be. We are always projecting ourselves onto that mirror, and it works forever in both ways. We imagine the world as we imagine ourselves, and maybe we imagine “God” the way we imagine ourselves. 

Craig Finn: I also think that he needed to suffer to actually reach out for God, and he becomes a slight believer once it comes crashing down, rather than on the way up. I don’t know if I’ve really said this anywhere, but my partner is a nurse. I remember when she was going to school, she said, “I’m happy to have a job where when you tell someone what it is, they understand it.” If you say, I’m a nurse, people have a vision of what that person does. And I think “priest” has that too. He puts on a uniform and that’s about finding a place in this world. The power is some part of it. Respect is some part of it. But it boils down to wanting to have a role. 

AD: With a work this expansive, it’s almost like the Craig Finn Cinematic Universe. Do you feel like all your songs take place in the same world? 

Craig Finn: I don’t know. I think they’re similar worlds. I think maybe Hold Steady songs take place in their own world, and Lifter Puller songs take place in their own world. 

AD: That gets me thinking about things like A Complete Unknown, the Dylan biopic, or Deliver Me From Nowhere, about Springsteen making Nebraska. 

Craig Finn: Right. Springsteen making Nebraska, that’s a fascinating topic. 

AD: I feel like for a music movie to work, it needs to have that narrow focus, vs your music that feels like a movie, where it can sprawl out more. You brought up Glen Campbell earlier, and I feel like Glen Campbell’s one of my favorite “movie” directors. I’m not sure exactly what “Wichita Lineman” fully means, but it’s totally cinematic. But biopics where you try to tell an artist’s whole life? They almost never work, unless you’re going to do something kaleidoscopic like Baz Luhrmann’s Elvis, which I actually liked. 

Craig Finn: I loved it. 

AD: Wow, I thought I was alone. 

Craig Finn: Who hated it? [Laughs] 

AD: Nearly everyone I’ve talked to. [Laughs] It’s almost like an impossible ask, but if for some reason Hollywood turned around tomorrow and said, “Hey, we secured millions and millions of dollars to produce a fully fleshed-out movie based on your songs.” Who’s the dream director? 

Craig Finn: I think the Cohen Brothers, because they’re also Minnesotans and they’re my favorites. I think that there’s an unease in a lot of their work that I really love. 

AD: Great choice. I could see Kelly Reichardt doing a nice Always Been film. I wonder, is it challenging to invest so much time and energy into creating a world with a book and a record and know that on some level, people are going to listen to the songs out of context, in a playlist or maybe just via a short social media clip? 

Craig Finn: I think ultimately you’ve got to make music for listeners that are like you. I am going to be 54 this summer, so I’m definitely not the target audience on TikTok. I don’t really engage with that platform, which, you could say from a career standpoint, is limiting. But spiritually, it doesn’t feel right for me. I mean, I think that there’s no doubt that a certain age group or something might not be interested in this, or it might not want to invest the time. But you write for the people who will. 

AD: And for them, a project like this really rewards the imagination. Like Frank Sinatra’s Watertown, that’s a movie playing in your headphones. It might not be all spelled out, but it’s a feeling and a vibe. And the listener in that case becomes almost a co-creator of the world. Does that excite you as a creator? 

Craig Finn: I find that thrilling. There’s a site, Clicks and Hisses, that annotates Hold Steady lyrics. They do a really good job, except sometimes they’re very wrong. And I think my favorite parts are the wrong parts! [Laughs] I’m not writing in and saying, “Hey, you got that one wrong.” oftentimes they are spot on, but I actually like the idea of the user taking control. There’s always going to be room for human error and a misinterpretation. To me, that makes it more fun. 

AD: And it’s a reminder that when you release a song, it’s the world’s now.  

Craig Finn: Remember when Scott Hutchinson from Frightened  Rabbit passed away? I was part of a tribute to. He was a friend of mine, so we did a show. It was myself, Ben Gibbard, Julien Baker, Aaron Dessner, and Kevin Devine. We were all kind of holding it together, and it went going pretty good. Then at the end, the front of house guy put on the Frightened Rabbit track “The Modern Leper.” And everyone in the room started singing too. It was all very sudden. Backstage, we all started crying. Really sobbing.  It made me realize “These songs? They are everyone’s now.” Scott is no longer with us. Maybe parts of the songs belonged to him before, bu now they’re truly just out in the world. He’s left them behind for us. It was a really profound moment. Songs belong to everyone who can sing along.
