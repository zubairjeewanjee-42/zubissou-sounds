## Body

There was an eight year gap between Nottingham, UK band The Soundcarriers’ 2014 album Entropicalia and 2022’s Wilds, but thankfully the retro-pop combo have returned after a much shorter wait with Through Other Reflections, a spellbinding collection of beatific harmonies, motorik pulses, reverberating flutes, and fuzz guitars, all eased along by the haunting vocals of members Leonore Wheatley and Dorian Conway. Multi-instrumentalists Paul Isherwood and Adam Caan join us today to discuss the new album, creating music for the sorely missed television program Lodge 49, and the enduring influence of Scott Walker. | j woodbury

Through Other Reflections by The Soundcarriers

Aquarium Drunkard: 2022’s Wilds came after an eight year break between records, but Through Other Reflections comes relatively quickly after that last dispatch. What helped facilitate the quicker turn around?

Adam Cann: Well there’s always a bit of an overlap with stuff we are working on and as Wilds took quite a while to take shape there was always tracks being worked on so we had quite a few things almost ready to go by the time Wilds comes out.

AD: In the album bio, Adam Cann says, “The songs explore a disillusionment with the way things are going particularly after 40 years of neoliberalism.” Soundcarriers are a band that utilize retro sounds, but the lyrics of this record are more focused on the right now. How does that dichotomy play out for you all personally, balancing nostalgia with a postmodern critique?

Adam Cann : That’s interesting and something I would need to think about! I guess our musical influences sort of end where neoliberalism begins, but yes there’s a contradiction in that we all live in the postmodern-era and that’s what allows us so much freedom to access things and pick and choose influences and do what we want. The price has been a bit of a hollowing out of cities and communities and a dissipating of some of the energy culture/music/whatever had. I think there’s a physicality that you will always need. I think some of the lyrics on the album where fueled a bit by these contradictions

AD: The initial writing for this album took place in Berlin; how did wandering the city inform the tone and feel of this album? 

Adam Cann: I think it was just a couple of tracks which came quite quickly and managed to get to a piano and get the rough idea down. It was more the change of environment that always helps with getting new ideas, just to be away from the day to day concerns is always helpful and definitely there’s a lot of space in Berlin compared to other cities which helps. Also the way the city was divided is just so crazy and creates a surreal feeling and when you look into the history of it like in the East they could receive TV from the West. It was a bit of a catalyst for those particular ideas anyway.

AD: 2014’s Entropicalia was in part inspired by J.G Ballard’s The Drowned World. Do any particular authors, science fiction or otherwise, inform Through Other Reflections?

Adam Cann: Definitely dystopian fiction and films are still  a strong source of ideas on Through Other Reflections. I liked Station 11 by Emily St Mandel; always love John Wyndham. It goes back to the postmodern thing and I think everyone likes the ideas of being able to start again in someway or create their own version of society picking out he bits they like.

AD: “What We Found” has a really fantastic vibe—it brings some folk-rock, Britfolk elements into the mix. Could you share a little bit of background regarding that one?

Paul Isherwood: Pish. I had the bass line for ages for the that track, initially the idea was for a slightly heavier track but after trying a few things out it kind of went in a different direction, more folky and a bit lighter sounding with different textures coming in. When Leonore and Dorian were putting vocals down they instinctively came up with a really nice harmony, almost country-esque, and it just kind of morphed into what’s on the record. It’s quite experimental but it had a definite structure that was easy to work with and it stopped it getting too crazy.

AD: The late Chris Dedrick of the legendary orch-pop band The Free Design wrote liner notes for 2009’s Harmonium, writing, “Maybe The Free Design planted the seed for the magic garden of intricate group harmonies that blended this softer sound…It takes a weird inner strength to pit flutes and quiet voices against drums and electronics and the rest of the world.” How did you connect with Dedrick, and what was the experience like of reading those notes for the first time?

Paul Isherwood: That was a great moment of synchronicity, I had recently been in Japan just before we started recording Harmonium and stumbled across the Free Design album Heaven/Earth. I was really blown away by the track “Dorian Benediction.” I saw it was on Light In The Attic, so a friend of ours at the time managed to get in touch with Chris via Light in The Attic and asked him do the liner notes. It was beyond amazing reading them for the first time, I’m so pleased he actually got back to us and it really captured that moment.

AD: Soundcarriers music tends to be classified as “cinematic.” How often are you all visualizing scenes in your own heads—is any of that imaginative work conveyed between band members directly?

Adam Cann: I think when we improvise or are working in the studio we will often refer to the feel of a particular  soundtrack or film like that’s the sound or feel we are going for so it’s good to have that shorthand of things we are into.I guess it’s one way of starting a track or idea. It takes away a bit of the blank page feeling and takes some of the pressure off if you look at it that way. It’s always interesting the way music and film combine and it changes both.

AD: I got hip to Soundcarriers via Lodge 49, a truly great show. It seemed like the music was tailormade for a program about a semi-enchanted southern California. How did working on the show inspire what Soundcarriers are up to now?

Paul Isherwood: That’s great you like the show. I just wish more people knew about it. It should be seen. It was a really nice diversion and I particularly enjoyed working on some of the cover versions for it which sadly didn’t get used. We did brilliant versions of “We Have All the Time in the World” and “Nature Boy.” We generated quite a lot of ideas and arrangements which did go into some of Wilds and on the new LP included our attempt at the theme song “Sonya’s Lament,” which was fun to do. I think we’ve always loved that sort of faded exotica surf sound and that kind of aesthetic which the show has.

AD: The band covered Scott Walker’s “The Seventh Seal” for Lodge 49. Could you reflect a little on what impact Walker had on you?

Adam Cann: I think the first thing I liked from Scott Walker was Sings Jaque Brel and played that to death. Definitely a big influence in terms of arranging the music , just some really sublime moments. I draw the line at Tilt, though! Love the bass on “The Old Man’s Back Again.”
