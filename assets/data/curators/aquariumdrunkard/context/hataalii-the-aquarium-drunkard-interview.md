## Body

There’s a sign on the cover of Hataałii’s Waiting for a Sign, bearing the words “authentic real deal.” It’s the kind of billboard familiar to anyone who’s driven through the large swaths of Native-held land in the American Southwest. Signs hocking goods, promising hearty meals, beckoning you off the road and into some cafe, trading post, or casino. There’s also a coffee pot pouring forth coffee onto a woman the size of a mountain. We’re not going to guess what’s going on there. 

Hataałii, or Hataałiinez Wheeler, doesn’t always spell out his messages. But within this cryptic, gnomic songs, there’s plenty to wonder at. Waiting for a Sign follows his 2023 album Singing Into Darkness, which found the Window Rock-raised songwriter presenting his songs, with their signature blends of new wave, indie rock, country, psychedelia, and deadpan vocals, to a wider audience.

Sign finds him in even stronger form. Recalling a dusted Destroyer, a desert-baked Dirty Beaches, or King Krule on a lonesome Navajo Nation biway, the songs here are filled with mournful pedal steel, drum machines, and reverb-drenched guitars, grappling with American paranoia, loneliness, and perception: “I think that maybe/We should start to try/To see this place through/Through different eyes/Because this pair still gets me down,” he songs on “Brown Fool Eyes.” It’s an “authentic” recording, as “real deal” as it gets, but that’s only reading on the surface. We rang up Hataałii to dig deeper, discussing “Alex Jones,” songwriting, and his nods to Elvis and Alan Vega. | j woodbury

Aquarium Drunkard: I love this album cover. How did it come about? Did you commission it?

Hataałii: It’s by Ryan Singer. Yeah, I did commission it. Because I was doing a lot of drawing at the time, I just kind of gave him the idea of what I wanted and then he made it into a painting. 

AD: Did you sort of sketch out all of the individual elements and select all the images? 

Hataałii: Most of it, but along the way certain things he would add on his own or I would chip in. It took a while, a couple of weeks, three weeks, or even longer.

AD: The coffee pouring on the woman—that’s one of the most interesting elements to me.

Hataałii: Yeah, that was cool. She was originally supposed to be wearing a traditional Navajo outfit with the tsiiyééł, a Navajo hair bun but I think we were just running out of time. 

Aquarium Drunkard: I would say like without a big marker like that that exactly defines who that character is, you kind of don’t know, which I think is cool.

Hataałii: People can interpret it as something personal. 

AD: Do you really have an old Chevy like that or have access to one to drive around?

Hataałii: My grandpa has one, but I don’t know if he would let me drive it around. But the songs in the album span from many different time periods, with a lot of different kinds of songs. If anything, I wanted the album cover to represent that, kind of all flying all over the place.

AD: The opening song, “Alex Jones,” might be the first time I’ve heard the term “UAP” used in a song. What made you opt for that designation over say the classic “UFO?” 

Hataałii: I used UAP instead of UFO because I think I made the recording around the time that stuff was happening, [wide adoption of the term UAP following the congressional hearings of 2022]. I kind of just wanted to make a song that was real dirty, the sound is just real nasty with the lyrics. The song is just a conjured up collection of all the terrible things about—things that I don’t really mess with.

AD: So do you have an interest in stuff like UAPs? A pop cultural interest in extraterrestrials or whatever? 

Hataałii: I don’t think so. I think UFOs are cool, I like aliens but I’m not really into it that deep.

AD: It feels like all that is part of the mythology of the American Southwest. It’s tied into all sorts of things, including American psychosis. And I feel like when you invoke somebody like Alex Jones…if you love him, if you’re a big Alex Jones fan, you view him as a sort of brave truth teller. But for others, he just looks like the most cartoonish articulation of American paranoia ever. And I’m always interested in where that paranoia sort of stems from.

Hataałii: I mean, I think that this song is about that. What it stems from, which is fear. Fear just kind of makes you crazy. I felt like a countryish, nasty sound was a good way to go with that. It sounds like a dog dry humping something that isn’t even alive. I wanted to make it sound like that, nasty, gross, and I wanted it to be very strong as well.

Waiting For A Sign by Hataałii

AD: I love “In My Lawn.” I think that’s my favorite song on the album, and it might be my favorite song that you’ve done so far. Those guitars—was that all you in the studio? 

Hataałii: I made that song, I think maybe right after or right before I made “Alex Jones.” That I was just in my house that I used to live in with a couple of roommates, and I was just kinda messing around, you know, on the guitars like always. That just happened, that one just happened.

AD: I love the way you paint scenes. When you’re writing songs, are you often imagining a setting or visualizing? 

Hataałii: It depends on what era of the songwriting it was in. A song like “Alex Jones” or “In My Lawn,” those ones kind of are just sort of more or less like spitballing a story or a scene together. Whereas like “Love is Over”, that’s a really really old song for me. And that was at a time where I already kind of knew what I was going to make a song about. Yeah. It just depends on when I made the song. 

AD: So some of these songs go way, back, stretch way back to earlier in your songwriting? How far back are we talking? 

Hatałii: I’d say “Love Is Over” was the earliest I made that song. I was like 17. 

AD: Do you still write a lot of songs, or write regularly? 

Hataałii: Yeah, yeah man. I think I have two albums ready, two more albums ready to go right now. 

AD: You really cover a lot of ground on this one. I love the New Order, more new wave elements. But other moments are more country, more psychedelic. Do people ever sort of bring their own expectations of what one of your songs is going to sound like to the table and express that to you at all? 

Hataałii: It’s only happened a few times, where people have been listening to my music since I was in high school. Somehow I’m still connected to this indie music. I think that that is the thing that people know me for. But with that being said, it’s not like I’ve ever run into any problems because of that. 

AD: How did your collaboration with Earth Surface People, “Rude Boy,” come together?

Hataałii: Dakota Yazi, he knew my music. That was actually where we recorded the album, in Santa Fe. That’s how I found out about the studios that I recorded with Earth Surface People there. I just liked Alex Simon’s vibe and the vibe of the studio, it was a good decision. I could bring all my instruments and stuff up, no problem.

AD: I love the sort of little shout out you do at the end of “In My Lawn,” the almost sort of radio announcer thing. Was that song inspired by a radio interview?

Hataałii: Ah no, I was trying to be like Elvis or something. I was trying to be like Elvis or like Alan Vega…something like that.
