## Body

Lagniappe (la ·gniappe) noun ‘lan-ˌyap,’ — 1. An extra or unexpected gift or benefit. 2. Something given or obtained as a gratuity or bonus.

Those familiar with this corner of the Internet are likely familiar with the music of Jack Cooper, as we’ve fastidiously covered his recorded output in one form or another since 2014. And for good reason. Following the dissolution of his previous band, Ultimate Painting, and the sole release under his given name, Sandgrown, Cooper has channeled his efforts into the potently shapeshifting outfit Modern Nature. On the heels of the band’s latest LP, The Heat Warps, Cooper and co. return with their third entry in the Lagniappe Sessions, this time paying tribute to a pair of Beatles’ chestnuts. Below, Cooper’s reflections on Beatledom, the power of childhood memory, and more . . .

I don’t remember not knowing about The Beatles. As a person growing up in the north of England during the 80s and 90s to a mother from Liverpool, The Beatles’ music was as hardwired as numbers and words. My Mum would play their records, we would sing along in the car and when I was old enough, I began collecting home-taped cassettes, CDs and seven inches we’d find in charity shops.

When I was about 8, my family were visiting a family friend in Ascot, outside London. As we were leaving breakfast at a Little Chef and returning to our car, a small King Charles Spaniel ran over to us. The dog’s owner was nowhere to be seen, but when we asked in the restaurant, the manager said, “Oh, that’s Lady Di. I’ll call Ringo and get someone to come and get her,” My Dad asked, “Ringo Starr?” and the manager nodded. I was already well aware of Ringo at this point and so this was too much to comprehend.

We stood outside waiting with the dog for the owner to arrive, only to be disappointed by a dishevelled guy in a pick-up truck. Ringo’s fried assistant chatted to us for a couple of minutes and drove off. It turns out the Little Chef was next to Tittenhurst, the estate Ringo had bought off John Lennon in the early 70s. We stood there in silence, having come so close to a Beatle.

When I started playing guitar in the early 90s, I would play Beatles songs. Before I knew what chords were or how to tune the thing, I worked out the melody to ‘Hey Jude’ and figured that I was ready to go. How to arrange songs, how to sing harmonies; it all comes from listening to The Beatles. I adored them.

The Beatles were the reason why my last band Ultimate Painting happened. James and I were equally obsessed, and the band was formed around hours of us bonding over them. We covered them back in 2014 for this very website; a version of George’s ‘If I Needed Someone’.

When the band ended in 2017/2018, I found it difficult to listen to them for a number of years. I would side-eye Beatles news and releases like the Get Back documentary with something approaching jealousy.

In 2024, when our daughter was approaching the age of three, she and I watched A Hard Day’s Night on a bit of a whim. We both sat there enthralled; the action punctuated with occasional questions like “Who’s that?” and then “What’s John doing?” Over the next few months, we started listening to The Beatles a lot and when we weren’t listening, we’d talk about them, look through my old Anthology book and roleplay me being George and her being Paul. It was exciting to fall in love with them again and see them through her eyes.

At the end of 2024, a few days after finishing the most recent Modern Nature record, my family spent a few weeks in LA and in the evenings I watched the Get Back documentary for the first time. I’d just had the greatest time recording with Jim, Tara and Jeff, so watching the documentary prolonged that experience. I recognised the camaraderie, the tension, the love and the excitement of creating something from nothing with friends. I couldn’t get enough of it.

During my trip to Los Angeles, I had coffee with Justin Gage, this website’s creator. We chatted about our new record a bit and he suggested us doing another Lagniappe Session, so when Modern Nature reconvened at the start of 2025, I proposed the band recording a couple of Beatles songs and the others agreed.

When we stayed in Los Angeles, I became fixated on George’s ‘Long, Long, Long’ off The White Album. A newly released mix that I’d missed during my Beatles hiatus shone new light on it and I think over the course of our trip, I listened to it nearly 50 times. It’s a beautiful song that was done a disservice in the 60s and treated as an afterthought on subsequent reissues and remasters. It might be George’s greatest song?! “How I want you… oh I love you” – beyond perfect.

‘She Said She Said’ is one of John’s finest songs and one of my daughter’s favourites. His songs seem to get better the more I hear them.  When I was growing up, we’d often see Paul, George and Ringo on TV. John had been murdered when I was tiny, so he was always an abstract presence in my head. I think part of the reason why The Beatles and John in particular have recently taken on this heaviness for me is that I’m older now than when John died. I don’t have to explain why that resonates, but it does.

Like a lot of three/four year olds, my daughter’s concept of death is beginning to form and, like mine, aspects of it are being defined by having heroes like John and George whose music is still so alive. It colours everything I’ve ever thought about it.

I was texting with my Mum the other night while I was listening to John Lennon/Plastic Ono Band. We were chatting about The Beatles as we often do. We said goodnight and then she messaged me again five minutes later; “I still feel sad about John.”
