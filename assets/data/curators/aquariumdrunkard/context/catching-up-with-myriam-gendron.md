## Body

Myriam Gendron is balanced between opposing forces: between songwriting and poetry, English and French, acoustic folk and chaotic post-rock. She works within these parameters with grace, emerging from the rubble with a consistent poetic vision. Her records straddle these ideals: the first, Not So Deep as a Well, reinterpreted Dorothy Parker’s poems; the second, Ma délire, revisited traditional folk melodies. Her third album, Mayday, is a continuation of this balance: a meditation on grief, sorrow, and most importantly, survival. Ahead of its May 10 release, we sat down with Gendron, discussing Leonard Cohen, recording with Jim White, and the intersection of poetry and songwriting. | b arthur

Aquarium Drunkard: What was your first experience with music?

Myriam Gendron: I was born in Ottawa but I didn’t really grow up there—I moved when I was one. I grew up in Quebec. So I grew up in French, just the other side of the river in a town called Gatineau. There was a public school in my neighborhood that was kind of a music school. All the kids went there, but there was a special program during kindergarten where we all learned the violin. After a year, they picked one child out of three to go into the music program. You had to be ok at violin, but you really had to be a pretty fast learner. I was picked, so I studied the violin, piano, choral singing, percussion—all sorts of instruments. It was nice. That was kind of my awakening. We moved to Washington D.C. when I was ten and few years later, I picked up the guitar and taught myself how to play. I’m not sure I would have done that if I hadn’t learned theory as a kid.

Aquarium Drunkard: Do you remember the first record you really gravitated towards?

Myriam Gendron: I’m going to skip all the Backstreet Boys and Spice Girls stuff. [Laughs]  When I was 12, Nirvana was really important. I became kind of obsessed with the Kurt Cobain figure…that’s probably my first experience of good music. I was in a metal band when I was a teenager, playing guitar.

AD: What first led you into this sort of folk space?

Myriam Gendron: I became really interested in lyrics and literature. That became a really important part of music for me. All the great folk singers like Leonard Cohen: it was poetry put into music. But yeah, I was into grunge and punk, but when I heard “Suzanne,” I was maybe 14 years old, and I was like, okay, it doesn’t have to be angry, musically speaking, to be interesting. There’s other ways to express these emotions. It’s not just folk music, obviously. When I say poetry, it’s in the very large sense of the word. I include like novelists in there, like Henry Miller. But yeah, Dorothy Parker. I barely knew her when I picked up that book. It was just a beautiful object. I picked it up and I read the first poem and that became the starting point of the first record and my career in music. I don’t know if I would have done anything else if I hadn’t found the book.

AD: How do you go about putting poems into arrangements? How do you work between those? Do they belong together?

Myriam Gendron: No, I think that most poems can’t really be successfully put into song because they have their own music. They don’t need anything else. I’m not saying that Dorothy Parker’s poetry needed me, obviously. But I think she was well aware that she was writing songs. She says it often. They have a very regular structure and rhythmically, it’s just obvious that it’s a song pattern. But most poems don’t have that; don’t need that; it would actually hurt the poem to try to put it to music. So no, I don’t think they necessarily go together.

I think songwriting is a very specific type of poem writing. Yeah, Leonard Cohen wrote this poem called “Queen Victoria.” I think it’s the only one that started off as a poem and then he put to music. He never recorded it in-studio, there’s just a live recording of it that’s absolutely beautiful. For some reason, he managed to make it work, but when you read the poem, it doesn’t feel like a song at all. It’s very strange.

AD: Are there any other poets that have stuck out to you with that same structure; that have the potential to be translated into song?

Myriam Gendron: Yeah, there’s another poet that’s the same generation as Dorothy Parker. My good friend Glenn Jones gave me a book of poetry and said, “you know, maybe it could be like Volume Two.” They have that same structure that could make good songs, but I decided not to go further in that direction. The book is right here: Sara Teasdale. Maybe one day I’ll do that.

AD: How did reinterpreting Parker’s work influence your songwriting?

Myriam Gendron: What I learned with Not So Deep As A Well is to use what other people have done to express my own voice. They were her words, but they were my emotions. I didn’t feel like I was stealing anything. It taught me that you’re never really original, you know? You’re always working with what’s already there, even if it’s not as obvious. When you’re writing a song, you’re always channeling something; you’re always reshaping because nothing’s really new. These emotions are universal. It was kind of a way to accept that as a part of the process, because we have this idea of artists and creation like, you have to be new. But you’re never new. 

When I worked on my second record, I had that in mind. I didn’t feel obligated to make original songs because the idea of original songs didn’t mean anything to me. And that allowed me on Ma délire to write my own songs. It’s acknowledging that that’s what we’re doing. We’re just reshaping. So that’s what I did with traditional music. But then once you accept that, maybe it’s easier to make your melody, your words. 

AD: Talk to me about “Dorothy’s Blues,” off your latest record —  why return to her? What inspired that song?

Myriam Gendron: It was a beautiful day outside: the beginning of spring, around this time last year. And I felt miserable. It was a very hard year and I was trying to express this contrast between how I was supposed to feel and how I felt completely incapable of happiness or lightness. I had this this line from Dorothy’s poem “Theory” in mind—“into love and out again”—and I just worked around it. I wrote a verse in English and I was trying to write a second one and French came up. So I have this verse in English and I have this one in French. And what do I choose? Let’s just keep both.

AD: Is it hard to work between those two languages? Is there a tension there?

Myriam Gendron: I never really thought about it like that. I don’t know how English and American people perceive French songs. I hear people say, “Oh, I love it when you sing in French, it’s so romantic.” Sometimes I’m worried like, maybe since you don’t understand the words you’re bored. But, people have told me, “we hear you,” even if they don’t speak French. The emotions: they go through. But some people are kind of annoyed by it, too. When I play in the States, I usually play less of my French songs. So I feel like maybe it’s a problem, but I don’t know.

AD: How was the recording process?

Myriam Gendron: Jim [White] and Marisa [Anderson] came in studio. We recorded three tracks together, but two of them, they just played on what I had already recorded. So there was only one track that we really recorded together in the studio. I had never recorded professionally before. I didn’t feel really comfortable because I didn’t have a lot of time in the studio–only two days with Jim and Marissa. And I didn’t know if I would be good enough to finish it all. That’s why I recorded my parts earlier, so I was sure that I had something good that they could work on. I’m happy I did that because it allowed me to be more of a producer.

AD: Can you talk about balancing the acoustic folk songs with these freak-out instrumentations? How’d you manage those things? Where did that idea come in from?

Myriam Gendron: When I made Not So Deep As A Well, I had no expectations—no one knew about me. It was a shot in the dark. But I kept receiving messages from people who liked the record and it did pretty well in a small niche of avant garde, but it was very surprising that it’s not necessarily folk people who liked my record. When I played shows here and there, I was often opening for Body/Head, Dinosaur Jr., Godspeed You Black Emperor. Once in a while, I also opened for regular folk singers—regular as in traditional, run-of-the-mill—and it didn’t work as well. Really early in the process, I got the feeling that my audience was not the typical folk audience. It was all about this tension: tension between soft things and violent things. That’s what people were looking for in my music. I’m not sure exactly why. But I like that because I come from that, you know, I was in metal band as a kid. I don’t want to be folk singer. That’s not how I see myself. I listen to all sorts of music and I can be pretty angry at times. When I worked on Ma Délire, I had guitar and vocals that I recorded at home. I was like okay, we have to bring some really nice grungy stuff on it. So I sent Bill [Nace] and Chris [Corsano] tracks and we made it work. I was really happy with the results, so I decided to keep working in that direction. 

Mayday is a bit more intimate, I would say, but I’m still building tension. I think the whole process of taking old songs and you know, it’s like, you’re anchoring your creativity and in a very, very, very old tradition, but it’s not about looking behind. It’s a way of talking about the present and the future but with the lessons of the past. It’s both very ancient and very modern.

AD: Returning back to the album, you mentioned “Lully Lullay.” I was just wondering where you found that song and why you decided to do that.

Myriam Gendron: Yeah, originally, it’s dated 15th century, from England. It’s about what became known as the Massacre of the Innocents. When Jesus was born, King Herod ordered the killing of all children under the age of two because he heard that the King of the Jews had arrived and wanted him dead. So “Lully Lullay” became a Christmas carol, but it’s a lullaby that a mother’s singing to her condemned child. And the Appalachian version of the song—I heard it from John Jacob Niles—is a completely different melody. And it’s the melody that struck me first, it was so beautiful. I kept humming it.

It’s a goodbye song. It’s a grieving song. So I decided to switch it around, so that I’m saying goodbye to my mother. I was playing it with my guitar and I heard some very loose instrumentation in the background. I felt like it had to be Jim and Marisa, and I would just sing on it. I wanted free drums—I wanted really something very violent, like a storm. I talked about this with Marisa and she said, “you know, usually people choose between the lullaby and storm, and with this song, you’re doing both at the same time.” Even in the title, Mayday. It expresses you know, those days that are so sunny and beautiful. But Mayday is also a distress signal. So there’s, there’s all of that. My mother died in May and my daughter was born in May. It’s a month of many contrasts.

AD: I found it interesting that the last note on the record isn’t your voice or guitar, but instead it’s that wailing drone—why’d you chose to do that?

Myriam Gendron: That’s the last song I wrote. I thought the record was over. I had nine songs and I was happy with them. Nathan Salsburg—he’s a great musician and he works at the Alan Lomax Digital Archive. They were working on an exhibit of lullabies. He sent me a few and they said, if you’re into it, you could pick one and make your version and then write about it. So I was writing this text to accompany the song and it was just so scary outside. The sky was very threatening – there had been a tornado watch and I just had a flood in my basement a few days earlier. It’s like nature was angry, right? My kids were scared and I was like, “how do we soothe our children when everything is so threatening?” It’s not just the weather. I mean, you know, the wars and stuff. It’s scary, right? It’s hard to tell your kids that it’s all going to be okay. I was haunted by this idea. So I started playing chords and singing a lullaby. I was like, okay, this works, but it needs the tornado. So I reached out to Zhou and told her what I wanted, and she was like, “Oh, I love that. I want to do it.” When she sent her solo, I was like, “Oh my God. This is exactly it.” It’s completely unexpected — you don’t think it’s coming. It’s very loud. But then it has this almost gospel feeling to it. There’s something very hopeful. There’s a lot of light and I thought it was a good way to finish the record: on a hopeful note. It’s sad. It’s very sad, but it’s hopeful.
