## Body

Ethan Iverson is a rare bird, a jazz musician who’s just as adept at writing about the form as he is playing it. As a member of The Bad Plus, the recorded a series of adventurous albums with the trio between 2001 and 2016, incorporating covers of artists like Radiohead, Aphex Twin, and Pink Floyd along the way. In 2017, he departed The Bad Plus, turning his focus to albums like the ECM outing Temporary Kings with saxophonist Mark Turner and 2021’s Every Note Is True, Iverson’s debut for Blue Note, which found him joined by bassist Larry Grenadier and legendary drummer Jack DeJohnette.

Earlier this year, three decades after his solo debut School Work, Iverson released Technically Acceptable. Recording again for Blue Note, it pairs the pianist with two different trios, bassist Thomas Morgan and drummer Kush Abadey and bassist Simón Willson and drummer Vinnie Sperrazza. Tearing into standards like “Round Midnight” and “Killing Me Softly With His Song,” the record is balanced but propulsive. Iverson joined us to unpack it and reflect on both his jazz and crime literature inspirations. | j woodbury

Aquarium Drunkard: Congrats on your most recent record, Technically Acceptable. The title track is one of my favorites, it has such a captivating feel, like it’s at ease, but somehow disorienting. You’re technically very accomplished, so I wondered if you could tell me a little bit about where the title comes from and alludes to? 

Ethan Iverson: I read the phrase in a couple of books by Lee Child, author of the Reacher series (which is now on TV). “Technically Acceptable” is the “only standard military engineers understand.” I thought it was memorable, amusing, and somehow suited the piece I was writing. It’s just a mid-tempo rhythm changes, but it has a bit of a mysterious vibe.

AD: You work a lot in a trio setting. A trio has always struck me as having the quality of cutting a lot of the extraneous elements of a band dynamic out before you even start playing; what draws you to a three-piece presentation? 

Ethan Iverson: The piano trio lineage in jazz is simply awesome. I could make a desert island list of records simply with piano trios: Bud Powell, Thelonious Monk, and Ahmad Jamal for starters.  It’s not just about the piano, but it’s also about the bass and drums. I’m very happy with this new trio with Thomas Morgan and Kush Abadey, we will be playing more in the future as well.

AD: Your take on “Killing Me Softly With His Song” is really hypnotic. What did learning that song and coming up with your own version reveal to you about the root composition? 

Ethan Iverson: Thank you so much! I play the piece almost straight. It’s so beautiful, it doesn’t really need more than that. Roberta Flack is the one, but actually first heard it on another great piano trio album, Hampton Hawes At the Piano with Ray Brown and Shelly Manne. For me that’s a classic LP.

AD: This is your second album with Blue Note, following 2022’s Every Note is True; as a massive jazz fan, a noted writer, and critic yourself, what does it feel like to have your own work featured on such a legendary label? 

Ethan Iverson: My whole career is a bit of a surprise. I never really expected to be leading groups on Blue Note and so forth. Most of us serious jazz heads would agree Blue Note has the greatest number of masterpieces in the back catalog. 

I must credit president Don Was for taking a visionary stance towards a celebrated brand. He told me that I wasn’t like anyone else, and that part might be true. 

AD: On the note of trios, Every Note is True featured that configuration; you were joined by Jack DeJohnette and Larry Grenadier, two artists whose CVs are mind blowing. They are very associated with ECM, and you’ve released music on ECM as well. What place does that label hold in your own conception of jazz/classical/minimalism? 

Ethan Iverson: Manfred Eicher is brilliant, and I love the ECM catalog. One could claim that Manfred is one of the architects of how we play jazz today—something one can’t say about too many producers. While the pandemic was so difficult for so many, Every Note is True was surprise gift of those troubled years. Since there were no gigs, Jack and Larry were free to meet me in the studio. 

AD: You’re a great interviewer yourself, and your site and Substack are full of fascinating pieces. Who were your music writing inspirations early on?

Ethan Iverson: For the interviews, I think of Notes and Tones by Art Taylor and Reflections from the Keyboard by David Dubal. As a practitioner/critic, I look to Charles Rosen as the benchmark. For prose style, Kevin Whitehead made an impression.

AD: How about literary inspirations? 

Ethan Iverson: I’ve always been a big reader, and in a modest way I suppose I am expert in crime fiction. The prose of Rex Stout (author of the Nero Wolfe mysteries) is pretty awesome, if I sound like Stout sometimes that is only to the good.

AD: One thing I really like about Transitional Technology in particular is the way you’ll diverge from talking about jazz and music theory breakdowns to writing about all the Mission: Impossible movies or science fiction. It’s a very much “man cannot live by bread alone” vibe. Are you a big cinephile? Have you watched anything interesting lately? 

Ethan Iverson: [Laughs] Yeah. Well this is going to sound pretentious, but I do relate genre to genre. Like if I’m looking at all of Mission: Impossible that is somehow like looking at all of the performances of a jazz standard like “Stella by Starlight.” What is the frame? How do we fill the frame? In that sense I regard it as all relevant, although I will always know more about “Stella” than Ethan Hunt. 

I’m not a cinephile but last night I re-watched Memento by Christopher Nolan. It made an impression in the theatre years ago but I haven’t seen it since. Like most who think about genre, I am attracted to but also skeptical of “meta” frames for a crime plot. Memento is still good, though.

AD: You’ve written about and knew the late, great writer Donald Westlake. His body of work is so vast, I’m curious where you tell people to start with him? 

Ethan Iverson: After I read all of Westlake’s 100 books, I sent him a fan letter. I think he was pleased to be contacted by a working musician who had no desire to ask a favor, so he and his wife Abby came the Village Vanguard where I was playing with The Bad Plus. I got to look at a hero up close and maybe imbibe something of how he did it.  

The Ax is Westlake’s masterpiece, suitable for anyone, you don’t need to love crime fiction to dig The Ax.

AD: I’m a big fan of the Parker stuff, especially the late Darwyn Cooke’s comic adaptations. And of course I love Lee Marvin’s Point Blank. Do you have some favorite Westlake adaptations? 

Ethan Iverson: Point Blank is one of kind. The Outfit with Robert Duvall is pretty good, on second watch it was better than I remembered. I can’t say too many others are up to snuff, and Don would have been the first to agree. Apparently some further things are being produced, which is great in that these cinematic adaptations will help keep Westlake’s name alive. Someone asked James Ellroy if he minded what the movies did the books, and he replied, “The books are still there on the shelf.”
