## Body

Practitioners of Vodún, a religion originating in West Africa, are said to commune with the spirit world. Antoine Dougbé, a Benin-born songwriter who drew from Cuban rumba, son, and Congolese guitar music, was such a committed vodúnisant that he eventually declared himself “the Devil’s prime minister.” Upon moving from Abomey to Cotonou, then one of West Africa’s musical capitals, Dougbé sought out musicians capable of keeping pace with his hybrid vision, quickly earning a fearsome reputation for driving players to their limits.

He soon found his match in Orchestre Poly-Rythmo de Cotonou, whose drummer and bassist were themselves Vodún initiates, and with the group recorded three albums and a string of singles in the late ’70s and early ’80s. A survey of these recordings has now come to light via the mighty Analog Africa and true to Dougbé’s spiritual affiliations, the sounds within are possessed.

Feverish and dizzying, the songs move relentlessly with phased guitars, reedy keys, balmy horns, and mesmeric call-and-response vocals. Muscular rhythms and slinky grooves wind beneath gleaming synths and wiry strings. A highlight like “Gnin We A Na Mon” drifts on cascading keys and Latin percussion before giving way to soaring horns and vocal bravado, while “Vile De Nan Gni Noude” rides a surging pulse of quicksilver guitars and layered harmonies. Press play and let this spellcasting music bring your spirits to a rise. | c depasquale
