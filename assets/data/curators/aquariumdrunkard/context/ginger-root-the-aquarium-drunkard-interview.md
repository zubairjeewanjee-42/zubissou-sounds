## Body

In the four years since his last full length record, Ginger Root (the moniker of musician and visual artist Cameron Lew) has been steadily building an all-encompassing new world. Since the beginnings of his self-proclaimed “weird, crazy music career”, Ginger Root has recorded and toured extensively, including opening slots for the likes of Khruangbin. Recalling a whirlwind of sounds from glistening City Pop, vintage soul and the obtusities of solo McCartney, the musician balances formative retro pastiche with a fusion of singular flare. A film school graduate and designer, Lew’s laborious build up to forthcoming album SHINBANGUMI has been the result of meticulous creative planning. Likening it to Ginger Root having its own cinematic universe, the album’s unique rollout includes a medley of interwoven music videos (shot in both California and Japan) that create one long, narrative-driven short film. Admirably, Lew’s filmmaking and aesthetic fingerprints are uniquely embedded in the project, while musically, the ultimate goal is to create a classic full album experience.

With an extensive fall tour on the horizon, Lew joined us from his home in Orange County for a nuanced conversation about all of the musical and visual components that go into the new record. We also discuss the intricacies of recording percussion in a dusty karaoke bar, treating the music videos like a television production, crafting songs with physical locations in mind, learning that he is a huge fan of Glen Campbell cover songs and much more. | m neeley

Aquarium Drunkard: How are you doing? Are you still in the Huntington Beach area?

Ginger Root: I’m fine, thank you for asking. And yes, I still live in Huntington Beach.

AD: You’ve described your upcoming record as being a showcase of your “new self”, and it sounds like the last four years since Rikki have been pretty intense with that classic album and touring cycle. Can you expand on that refreshed feeling?

Ginger Root: Yeah, sure. If we’re jumping back four years to my last full length album, it came out right at the height of Covid, when it was really happening. So I felt like with that album Rikki, it was kind of hidden to the world and nobody got to listen to it because we were all stressed with personal stuff, which is totally understandable. So during the rest of Covid and not being able to tour and everything, I made two EPs and really tried to just have fun again making music. I tried to figure out how to push the sound more, how to incorporate other influences and just kind of mix things up. And in doing so I think I accidentally came more into my own as a musician, and as a person too. After those EPs came out, like you said before, we started touring for real again and doing these recording/touring/recording cycles. 

To make this new full length album happen, I had gone through such a weird, crazy musical career in the past like 3-4 years since the last album. In a good way I felt like I was a completely different person, and I had all these new experiences I wanted to talk about and all these new anxieties I wanted to share and work through music as well. 

So I was really excited to take on the challenge of battling the current state of people’s attention spans and putting out a full record instead of singles and stuff like that. So I just think this new record SHINBANGUMI that’s coming out is kind of a celebration of the crazy journey and hard work that Ginger Root has gone through over the past four or five years. 

AD: Something I take away from your music is that it seems like it evokes certain locations and regionality. Similar to the music of Shintaro Sakamoto.

Ginger Root: Oh, I love that guy.

AD: It seems almost made for a hypothetical nightclub or lounge setting. Am I imagining this, or is that sort of thing ever intentional for you? 

Ginger Root: I think in terms of real tangible locations, it depends on where I recorded each song. This album was a cool opportunity because, it’s not like I set out to do this in the beginning, but it worked out that like 60% of the songs were recorded here in my home DIY studio and then the other half was recorded in Tokyo in DIY settings over there too. So I definitely think location influenced the composition and arrangement and mixing, to a certain degree. I think, too, that with Ginger Root having a strong visual and almost cinematic universe type of component, I think it’s only natural for me to kind of subconsciously think about where this song would be in terms of a scene, and what place would the song also be in, and what type of experience or situation would you be going through when you hear this song. I think all of that culminates together, definitely.

AD: Speaking of which, I had to ask about the song “Kaze”, which you said was recorded on a dusty drum kit in a Japanese karaoke bar.

Ginger Root: I’ll tell the lightning version, because it’s a long story. Basically, after our first tour in Japan, I stayed a few extra months in the country in a place called Asakusa. It was really late and there was nothing open, but this one bar across the street from my AirBNB was open. I went in there, and this guy comes down and sits next to me. He put his phone down and it showed what he had been listening to on Spotify, and it was my face, which was really embarrassing because I’m not one for public interaction. He looked me dead in the eye and was like “oh my God, you’re the guy!” So then the whole bar, because there were only like six people, got really interested in who I was. The bar owner started playing my music videos on the TV, it was really embarrassing. But the guy was like hey, I’m sure you’re very busy and it’s late but I have my go-to karaoke spot that I’d love to take you to to sing some songs. 

Being very introverted, I wanted to decline but I thought you know, this is a crazy, once in a lifetime adventure. So I went with him and his friends, and met the owner who was very sweet and nice. At that karaoke bar was this drum set that was just accumulating dust in the corner. So when I went back on a personal trip the next time when I was recording some of these songs, I reached out to the owner and asked, if I rent out the whole karaoke bar and pay you for like three hours, can I record drums in your bar? It was funny because since I reserved the whole place, they still gave me the house snacks and tea and drinks and stuff. It was a very refreshing record session, I’ll say that. So yeah I recorded it there, and there were bongos and a shaker and everything. Like 99% of that song was recorded in Tokyo and the drums and percussion were recorded in that dusty karaoke bar. 

AD: That’s a great story. I love how you compared it to Haruomi Hosono’s “Tin Pan Alley” sound.

Ginger Root: That’s right, thank you.

AD: You did something very unique with all of the music videos for this release, which all kind of stitch together with a narrative, almost like an short film. In this era where they can sometimes just feel like “assets” that slip into the ether, I thought this was a creative way to go about it. What was the genesis for those components?

Ginger Root: I love making music videos, at the forefront. I think nowadays as an artist, the way things need to be attention-grabbing, whether you see that as a negative or a positive, you know, music videos are a way for me to try to invite people into this world that we’ve been building for so long. In terms of the genesis of the idea, the past two EPs were kind of … I was kind of consciously or unconsciously creating this world with a lot of throwback eighties influences: eighties Japan, eighties America and everything. And so we kind of built this world and for this album, everyone got really accustomed to this setting and I felt like it would be a shame to start over and rebrand and create this whole new facade or whatever. So I thought to base it on the previous work as a foundation we already laid during those two EPs we released during the Covid time. 

It’s always been my dream to try to make a piece of music and a piece of visuals that complement each other, essentially making one giant movie. I went to film school and I’ve always wanted to make a feature film or a TV show, so this is kind of my way of trying to do that in a music video format. And I’ve learned that me and my co-collaborator David Getal who co-directed the music videos with me, have found out a lot about the limitations but also the beauty of making stories and telling narrative through music videos. You can’t have dialogue while the song’s going on, you can’t have sound effects. You can’t explain things like you can in other narrative forms like television and movies and stuff. I wanted to kind of create a longer piece of narrative music video content for people to buy into. 

Like you said, with how certain music videos sort of fall into the ether, I thought if we made basically one big music video, it’s got to hit somehow, somewhere. Growing up with videos that went insanely viral in the YouTube heyday … I’m talking about virality before like TikTok and reels and stuff like that, which are just like song-based and everything. You know, like The Marias or Men I Trust or Boy Pablo, all these videos that kind of just grabbed your attention, all these people. I felt like I wanted to give it a shot and do as many music videos as I can because I felt like the more I do, one is going to have to stick. 

AD: How long would you estimate it took just to create the videos?

Ginger Root: We actually shot it kind of like a TV show, where it’s like, alright today is all of music video two, and then half of music video three because there’s a scene that repeats or whatever. It was weird and kind of efficient to shoot it like a TV show where we have recurring characters and props and settings, so logistically that was kind of cool to figure out. We shot it over about five and a half rounds of film, where we should shoot about 10-14 days at a time. I think all in all, it was probably about two and a half to three months of total production time. 

AD: Cover songs have always been a big part of your musical DNA. Do you have a favorite or formative cover song memory?

Ginger Root: In terms of covers that I have done and were pivotal, I did this series way back in college where I would do a new cover every week. Originally it was supposed to be me making a new original song every week and then it was like no, I can’t do this [laughs]. I decided to do covers instead and the first one I did was “Never Can Say Goodbye” by the Jackson 5. That one felt so special because it actually opened the door and allowed me to be okay with not playing it true to the original and making it my own. Which is of course what a cover song is. 

But you know, there’s also cover purists of people who say you have to play it just like the original. Beatles fans, for example, and I am one of those. Or, it’s like you totally turn it into something barely recognizable or kind of recognizable. I think a couple of cover songs that aren’t mine that I wish I did are, there’s a Kero Kero Bonito song where they cover Oasis, “Rock ‘n’ Roll Star”. That’s a crazy cover that sounds nothing like the original but it slaps the entire time.

AD: Good timing to mention that one.

Ginger Root: Yeah, right? With the reunion and everything? There’s that one, there’s Dorothy Ashby doing “By the Time I Get to Phoenix” by Glen Campbell, that’s a beautiful rendition. I definitely spin that one a lot during DJ sets. And then, “Guess I’m Dumb”, which was another Glen Campbell cover by Tatsuro Yamashita and is really great. I’m a big Glen Campbell fan, I’m just realizing in real time right now. 

AD: You put these out in a compilation series called Toaster Music, are there any plans to do more?

Ginger Root: Well you know what’s funny, they’re called “Toaster Music” because I challenged myself to record mostly everything in my previously owned 2004 Honda Element, which looked like a toaster. That car got in an accident and we had to get rid of it, so the place to do it is no longer. But one of these days I would like to get another car that is suitable for that. I’d love to do that cover series during downtime between cycles or something. It’s a fun, creative exercise and release. It’s a low stakes thing, kind of just like doodling on a piece of paper and I still find it very fun to do. So yes, I’d like to one day. 

AD: We’ve discussed a lot about the intriguing philosophies behind this record. Ultimately, what are you hoping for when it’s released?

Ginger Root: I’m curious what people’s reactions will be, being that this is my first full length album in four years. That is a lot of time, a lot of days and a lot of hours. So I’m interested to see how people react. Especially, if I’m being quite frank as an artist, it’s really hard to break through the algorithm and the recommended content and streaming service algorithms and stuff. It’s really hard to stand out. So my one hope and wish is that people will listen and watch this thing that took, you know, two and a half years to make. I hope people enjoy it as much as I enjoyed making it. That’s the only thing I’d like to add.
