## Body

The universe isn’t hurting for more albums filled with gorgeous waves of melody and pillowy drones produced by synthesizers. But when the person behind said music is Peter Baumann, you’d be right to sit up and pay attention to it. 

Baumann is a living legend in the world of electronic music. He was a member of Tangerine Dream from 1971 to 1977, a period that saw the German group achieve its greatest successes both creatively and commercially. After releasing some solo work in the late ‘70s, he moved behind the scenes, opening a studio in Berlin and producing work for Conrad Schnitzler (Kluster) and Hans-Joachim Roedelius. In the early part of the next decade, he founded Private Music, the label that helped foster the New Age music movement and was partially responsible for Yanni’s huge career. Baumann remained with the label until it was sold in 1994. 

And while he continued to make music off and on, including collaborations with fellow Tangerine Dream alum Paul Haslinger, Baumann devoted much of his life post-Private Music to a study of the mind. He founded the Baumann Foundation, a think-tank that, according to its archived website, “explores the experience of being human in the context of cognitive science, evolutionary theory and philosophy,” and co-authored a book that explored both the human ego and the impact of the 9/11 attacks on evolution. 

When I caught up with Baumann in a Zoom call from his well-appointed home in San Francisco, we touched on all of the above, while homing in on Nightfall, his recently-released studio album that sets an appropriately dusky tone over nine tracks of smoldering ambient and gently experimental pop. | r ham

Aquarium Drunkard: Listening to your new album, I hear a spaciousness and an openness that suggests someone that has a lot of confidence in what they’re doing or at least a lot of patience when it comes to the music they’re making. Nothing feels rushed or overly excited. Is that something that resonates with you? 

Peter Baumann: Well, I hear you. I mean, it was always my history of having various spacious elements in the music. But I think that in today’s world, it’s so hectic, so crazy, so many stimulations that it seems to be even more quiet than it used to. Had I done this record in the ’70s, it would have been a very lively record compared to what we were doing with the band. 

AD: As you are writing new material, does it simply start as individual tracks or are you thinking thematically, as part of a potential new album? 

Peter Baumann: The commonality is possibly the mood I was after, and I think the title Nightfall captures that. But I have a ton of little sketches and ideas, and I basically see the pieces grow by themselves, organically. I work a little bit on one and then I work on another one, and they influence each other. But they grow together like a bunch of children. 

AD: That’s a great analogy. I contrast the spaciousness of most of Nightfall with a piece like “I’m Sitting Here, Just For A While” which has bits of rhythm that pop up for only a few seconds. As you are editing these pieces down to what we might call traditional song length, are you always seeing the big picture and knowing when to drop in little details like that? 

Peter Baumann: It’s really more of a feeling process than a thinking process. I don’t check out how long the pieces are or what elements they have. I really sense whether it’s too dense, or whether it’s too active. So, I’m really going by what the mood is. How I feel about any given piece. And that changes sometimes from day to day. So at one point or another, I have to say, “Okay, that’s it.” But they really are alive to that degree. 

AD: I feel like I also hear a bit of Middle Eastern or North African influence on the track “I’m Sitting Here.” Do you feel that part of the world influences your music? 

Peter Baumann: Not consciously, but subconsciously. I like music from all over the world and from different times, so I’m sure it all influences me to some degree. 

AD: With all due respect, you are not the most prolific of artists. Years or sometimes even decades elapse between your albums. Is that on purpose in some way? Do you need that time to let ideas develop and grow organically like you said earlier, or are you someone that just waits for that moment of inspiration to strike and then you rush over to your studio to capture it when it happens? 

Peter Baumann: I would say that I’ve never considered myself to be a musician, quite frankly. So, music is something I love to do, and I enjoy doing. When I have the time, I feel myself drawn to the studio. It’s not that, “Okay, now I’ve got to get the next record done.” I do it when I really feel like I enjoy doing it. The music industry is not what it used to be, and I got lucky to have grown up in a time where you could make a living. And then I had a record company that was very successful in the early ’90s. But today, I have many other interests, so it’s not my full time job. 

AD: True, because outside of music, you have done a lot of work in the fields of cognitive science and evolutionary theory. You co-authored the book Ego: The Fall of the Twin Towers and the Rise of an Enlightened Humanity, which is an amazing read. This may be a big question but looking at the state of the world today, how do you make sense of it through those lenses of evolutionary theory and cognitive science? 

Peter Baumann: Well, I’m currently writing another book called Some Days Are Better Than Others. What it boils down to is it analyzes why we feel pleasant and unpleasant feelings, and how they come and go and weave through our lives. There’s not a human being out of our eight billion compadres that has only good feelings or only bad feelings. There must be some kind of function to those feelings, and I’m analyzing what the function is. It’s quite insightful to recognize why feelings begin to evolve, to begin with pleasure and pain, and then feeling pleasant and unpleasant, even emotionally. If your status goes up, you feel good. If it goes down, you feel bad. If the stock market goes up, you feel good. If it goes down, you feel bad. It weaves itself through our lives and when we take a step back and not pay that much attention to feelings, then we feel more freedom because we don’t try to manage our feelings all the time. That’s what this book is about. 

So, to come back to your question about the state of the world right now, when you see it through the ebb and flow of feelings, you see it in a much longer arc. And I think that generally, we lead much, much more comfortable and better lives today than we ever have as a species, and the brain automatically tries to figure out, “OK, where’s the next danger going to come from?” We get bent out of shape with the smallest things. I mean, just a few thousand years ago, they were starving and half of your kids would die in childbirth. So, we have made much, much progress, but emotionally, we have not made that much progress.

AD: You joined Tangerine Dream in the early ’70s but I’m unaware of what you were doing musically or artistically before that. What were you getting up to? 

Peter Baumann: To begin with, I was very young. I joined when I was 18. I have been playing in bands since I was 14. I just haphazardly started playing in the band. It goes to show how arbitrary life can be. I was at an Emerson Lake & Palmer concert in Berlin, and the band was late. So you start talking to the fellows next to you and behind you. And there was a fellow with long, dark hair named Christopher Franke. We talked about it. “What do you do?” “Music.” “What kind of music do you do?” And I said, “Oh, I like to make experiments,” and this that and the other. He asked me if I would give him my address, and then a week later, there was a letter saying, “Hey, we’d like to talk to you. We’re looking for a keyboard player.” So it was a concert. And thank God, Emerson Lake and Palmer were late. Otherwise, who knows what would have happened. 

AD: I hope you had a chance to thank them in person for helping make that happen. 

Peter Baumann: Actually, I did have lunch with Keith Emerson many, many years later when I had a record company and we discussed doing a project. I didn’t bring it up at the time. 

AD: Tangerine Dream had some pretty impressive success during your time with the group. You sold a lot of records, especially here in the States. Was that something of a surprise to you the way things ticked up as they did? 

Peter Baumann: It was a total surprise. We did the music because we enjoyed it. I remember I was on a vacation with a girlfriend in Italy, and then I get a telegram from Richard Branson saying, “You’ve got to come to London to do interviews. Your record is in the charts.” And I said, “What charts?” It never occurred to us that we would end up in any kind of chart. We never considered our music to be commercial and that there were huge sales on the horizon. Never. 

AD: The Tangerine Dream live show was such a huge production. There was so much equipment being hauled to these events and there was a light show to go along with it. How did that develop? 

Peter Baumann: Well, the equipment just grew because there were more and more different instruments when we first started out. The Moog synthesizer was just beginning to get some traction. The very first concerts, Christoph was playing drums and Edgar [Froese] guitar, and I was playing keyboards. Then we expanded into anything that would make any kind of sound. Obviously electronics are the most versatile. It was a big outfit, but today, it would actually be small. Then we had a laser show for a while from Laserium in L.A. They just approached us and said, “We love your music. We’d love to do a laser show for you.” That’s how we connected. 

AD: I think about your live setup then compared to say, what Kraftwerk is doing now. You look at pictures of their live shows in the ’70s and ’80s and there’s gear everywhere. Nowadays it’s just four guys on platforms controlling everything with tablets. 

Peter Baumann: I’m not sure that they control everything. I think a lot of it is playback. They add a little bit here and there. I think it’s great stylistically. It’s very, very different music. It’s very mechanical. It’s not as analog as we used to be. That’s the fun part. You have something that hits you this way, and something that hits you that way. 

AD: One of the last projects you did with Tangerine Dream was the soundtrack to the film Sorcerer. How did that project come to you all and how did you develop the music for that? 

Peter Baumann: That was a great, great process. William Friedkin approached Virgin Records and said, “I love what these people from Tangerine Dream are doing. I’d love to use them for the next film I’m doing.” We talked to him on the phone and he said, “Why don’t you do some demos and we’ll meet in Paris.” We went to Paris and saw him in his big, fancy suite and played him the music. He said, “This is great. Just do some more of that and then I’ll play it when I’m shooting the film.” So we did, I think, an hour and a half, maybe two hours of different kinds of tracks, and he played them while he was shooting the film. We never actually made the music to the film. The music was done before the film even started shooting.

AD: Again, that was one of your last projects with Tangerine Dream. Why did you decide to step away from the band when you did? 

Peter Baumann: You never know exactly how you make decisions and how it all unfolds. It seemed at the time that it became work. It wasn’t like in the beginning. We never wanted to be commercial but then the pressure starts. You have bigger tours. You have commitments. There are more people and you want to deliver for those people. And then it becomes work. It felt like that phase, that particular phase, was coming to an end. I don’t regret it. I wouldn’t have met my wife. I wouldn’t be in America. I wouldn’t have had the record company. So it was obviously an impactful time in my life, and I thoroughly enjoyed it. I’m perfectly happy with how things went. 

AD: Let’s talk about that record company, Private Music, which you started in the ’80s. How did that come together? 

Peter Baumann: I did a couple of records for a company called Egg Records, and I constantly produced. I had a recording studio at the time, a full-fledged professional studio, and I had, literally, a shoebox full of cassettes. I went with my wife on our first vacation together, and it was very romantic. There was a fireplace, and I started playing these cassettes, and my wife said, “Oh, this is great.” And I said, “Yeah, I was thinking of starting a record company.” She said, “Oh, you absolutely have to do it.” I found somebody that could run the record company and it was called Private Music because it was an intimate feeling that I was after. It wasn’t dance music. It wasn’t for public consumption, but more for private consumption. 

AD: How deeply involved were you with finding artists for the label? 

Peter Baumann: Pretty early on, I had an A&R person, Doreen D’Agostino. She was in the music business for a long, long time. She’d field the tapes that were sent to us, and some of the music started to be played on weird radio shows. One day she was playing something and I was walking by, and I said, “Who’s that?” She said, “He’s an obscure guy from Minneapolis called Yanni.” I said, “Yeah, that’s cool, cool music.” So every artist we got involved with came to us in a different way. 

AD: Do you have a sense of what comes next for you, or any ideas for future recordings? 

Peter Baumann: None whatsoever. I have a little studio and, on weekends, I spend time there just fiddling around. If something sticks, I’ll put it into a particular folder. And if there’s enough in there, then I might do another record.
