## Body

By 1975, saxophonist Alan Braufman had immersed himself in the free music scene of New York City. Emerging from the loft circuit, he cut his debut album, Valley of Search, that year. Released by the India Navigation label and featuring bassist Cecil McBee and drummer Ralph Williams, it announced Braufman as a dynamic voice, earning a cult following in the years that followed its release.

Some fifty-odd years later, Braufman is still at it. In 2018, his nephew, Nabil Ayers, reissued Search, which led to 2020’s The Fire Still Burns, accompanied by his Search bandmate Cooper-Moore on piano, tenor saxophonist James Brandon Lewis on tenor saxophone, Ken Filiano on bass, drummer Andrew Drury, and percussionist Michael Wimberly. And now, a new album: Infinite Love, Infinite Tears. Produced by Ayers, it’s a more gentle set than the previous: vibraphonist Patricia Brenna sprinkles it with dulcet, sounding melodies, while percussionist Michael Wimberly and drummer Chad Taylor hold it down with returning players Filiano and Lewis. 

A spellbinding listen of post-bop, it finds the 72-year-old artist tapped into a primal creative spirit. He joined us to discuss the album, his writing process, his tour with the Psychedelic Furs, and more. | j woodbury 

Infinite Love Infinite Tears by Alan Braufman

Aquarium Drunkard: I’ve read in a few interviews that you don’t tend to go back and listen to your own records all that much.

Alan Braufman: No, I don’t. You hear ’em so much when you’re making them. You’ve got to mix them and everything and you’re kind of sick of ’em by the time they’re out. I get it. I know what I did.

AD: Infinite Love, Infinite Tears is a very open sounding record. It’s bright—I think it leans a little more on the infinite love side of things than the infinite tears side. But I’m fascinated by the dichotomy suggested by the title. Where did that come from? 

Alan Braufman: I didn’t write it with the title in mind. But I think the title expresses what music is saying there. At first I was thinking, “Yeah, ‘infinite love’ has a nice ring to it, but that’s been done a million times probably.” But there’s a sad side to it too; I was thinking about how love and tears are so intertwined. You don’t have grief without love. I liked the sound of it also for the title of the album.

AD: Chad Taylor is on drums for this record—he’s incredible. 

Alan Braufman: He’s amazing. Really one of the best drummers in the world right now. I mean, lucky to have him, he’s a wonderful musician.

AD: Patricia Brennan’s vibraphone touches are beautiful too. And you’ve got James Brandon Lewis on sax. He’s having a huge year—have you heard his album with the Fugazi guys, Messthetics? 

Alan Braufman: Oh yeah. He’s perfect for that band, they got the right guy. 

AD: I understand that often, your songwriting process involves you spontaneously singing a melody and if it sticks around, you might work it into a full song? 

Alan Braufman: That’s pretty much it. I guess most musicians have music going around their head 24/7. I think a lot of people do, not just musicians. People will sing a melody they heard on the radio to themselves. Musicians have original stuff going on in their head all the time. That’s actually the title of the opening track on the album, “Chasing a Melody.” I named it that because sometimes, I’ll be talking to somebody—it’s not happening right now with you—but I’ll be talking with somebody, music will go through my head and I’ll follow that melody. It might lead somewhere. But I tune out what the person’s saying, and I have to come back and say, “What was it you were saying?” 

I would say that 98% of what goes through in my head when I’m humming music myself is garbage. But once in a while that thing comes—and I’ll say, “Wait a minute, that’s nice.” Now let me follow that one, let me see what I can do with that. And those are the tunes that kind of get past the editing process. It’s kind of an insecure feeling writing that way, because sometimes the tunes are just shut off and I can’t find anything that I like for weeks. I can go and try to write something, and I do, and invariably what I try to write is never as good as what comes out naturally.

AD: Has that been your approach for much of your career?

Alan Braufman: It has, yeah. I don’t know if it’s really that unique an approach. I would imagine that other people do it too, especially pop musicians who don’t do stuff that’s very complicated. Often, it’s easy to hear in your head. But that’s the way this album and The Fire Still Burns were created. 

AD: There’s a narrative arc to The Fire Still Burns. You start with “Sunrise” and “Morning Bazaar,” and you close with “City Lights.”

Alan Braufman: And that’s something that came out when I listened to the music. I hadn’t conceived of it that way, but it just turned out: morning and night. 

AD: A lot of the songs on this new album are in major keys. 

Alan Braufman: Four of the six. 

AD: Sometimes with free jazz or avant-garde music, major key melodies might trigger a bit of suspicion. Were you surprised to find yourself operating that way, or did it even occur to you?

Alan Braufman: I was aware of it. But to go back to the beginning of the conversation, it’s almost like you’re going by what’s going through your head and picking what you like. I picked what I picked and oh, four of ’em are major keys. That’s interesting. But I didn’t try to do that.

AD: Of course there’s room for subtle shades and harmonies in a major key, but people tend to think of it as “happy sounding.” And there’s always a desire to push into new territory. 

Alan Braufman: Exactly. And minor is more “dramatic.” And of course, you can get into hybrid scales and scales from different music systems. Piano major scale can still do some good. [Laughs] 

AD: Simple melodies don’t mean uninteresting ones. I think about the Psychedelic Furs, which is a band you played with. 

Alan Braufman: One of my favorite tunes of all time is “Sister Europe,” and it’s two chords, a couple of notes, and the melodies are just perfect. [Note: a cover of this song appears on Braufman’s album Lost in Asia]. [Playing with the Furs] was a very quick thing for me. I was playing with Carla Bley at the time, and the Furs’ saxophonist, Gary Windo, had overstayed his visa. So the Furs asked me to do a Canadian tour, which I did. It was an absolute blast. I had never heard of the band before, it was 1982. Windo called me up one afternoon and asked if I could do the tour. I said, “Sure, that sounds fun. When is it?” And they said, “They’ll pick you up tonight, eight o’clock that night.” That night there was a big tour bus in front of my place on Canal Street.

AD: You studied at Berkeley in Boston, and then ended up in New York. You must have seen some incredible shows there. 

Alan Braufman: I used to go to Slug’s on East Third Street, between Avenue B and C. I was only 15 but they didn’t care, they let me in. Sun Ra played there every Monday. I would go see the Sun Ra Band a lot…I’d see Pharoah Sanders, Gary Bartz. Then, when I was 17, I got a job at this movie theater. It was called Bleecker Street Cinema, a little art house, foreign films and everything. That was right down the block from the Village Gate, and they had a reciprocal relationship. We’d let Gate employees in to see movies for free and vice versa. It would be Miles for a week, Rahsaan Roland Kirk for a week, Roy Haynes with George Adams. Everybody came through, I was very fortunate. 

AD: You recorded your debut, Valley of Search in 1975. Your nephew Nabil Ayers reissued in 2018. Was that re-energizing for you? 

Alan Braufman: It opened my eyes a little bit. When Nabil called me about how I’d feel about a reissue, I had no idea that people were still listening to it. It had a mini-cult following. People were paying 400 bucks on eBay for original copies. 

AD: New York plays a huge part in your story, and the song “Brooklyn” is lovely. But are you living in Salt Lake still? 

Alan Braufman: I’m currently in Salt Lake. I spent a lot of time in New York. 

AD: Do you find yourself missing New York? Is that song kind of a reflection on that feeling?

Alan Braufman: Well, I miss New York but I’m there enough that I don’t miss it that much. But I called that song “Brooklyn” because I had gone out to see some friends play in Brooklyn, and it was a nice summer, and I was walking home instead of taking the train. And that tune just came to me as I was walking. Talk about the soundtrack of your life. That just came to me when I was walking and I thought, “I think there’s something there.” 

AD: I love the idea that music is always bouncing around, but it can be hard to quiet your mind enough to hear it. Do you have a mindfulness practice you adhere to? 

Alan Braufman: I run a lot, and during runs, I’ve actually been writing in my head. 

AD: Wow, your body is engaged so your mind is free to wander. 

Alan Braufman: The thing is, I’ve run thousands of miles in my life, but I’ve never once run while listening to music. I listen to my own, the music that’s going around my head.
