## Body

Peter Stampfel’s 2025 collection Song Shards: Soul Jingles, Sotic Jingles, Vintage Jingles, Prayers, and Rounds features a whopping 46 songs, but compared to 2021’s Peter Stampfel’s 20th Century in 100 Songs, that’s downsizing. Despite struggles with dysphonia, it’s clear the The Holy Modal Rounders founder, one-time Fugs member, and solo singer has no trouble gathering up material. And thankfully, the 87-year-old folk singer is still able to share songs with his listeners. 

Song Shards offers a perfect encapsulation of his sensibility, combining vintage advertisements for products like Libby’s Canned Food, Pepsi-Cola, and Ajax with spiritual reflections and stoic wisdom like “Open Me,” “The Obstacle is the Way,” and “Maximum Love,” the latter being a song of praise to the very fabric of the universe: 

“Love to every single atom
And sentient being that exists
Love to every single atom
And sentient being that exists” 

Like his friend and collaborator Harry Smith’s epic compilation 1952 Anthology of American Folk Music, Song Shards casts a spell. With Stampfel’s distinctive yelp at the fore, it offers both levity and grace in an age that could use a lot more of both. He joined us to discuss the record, his spiritual practice, and reflect on artists like Bob Dylan, Willie Nelson, Smith, and Irving Berlin. This conversation has been edited for clarity and cohesion. | j woodbury

Aquarium Drunkard: I really enjoyed my time with Song Shards. I was thinking about how it combines both funny little jingles as well as deep spiritual reflections, which brought to mind the hermetic dictum, “As above, so below.” What was your concept for combining these high ideas with low, everyday commercial songs? 

Peter Stampfel: Well, it was serendipity. I had recorded some of the jingles with producer Mark [Bingham, guitarist] and my daughters, Lily Stampfel and Zoë Stampfel, and some friends. I showed the song charts to Mark and he said, “Let’s record them!” Then I started recording the other songs, and felt like there was a similarity there. So we combined the tracks and they were exactly an album’s length. I’ve always been fond of the juxtaposition of the profound and the goofy, you know? 

AD: Too much heavy, too much lofty, and you get lost. Too much goofy, no profundity, and it gets spacey too. You put the two together, and you’re really onto something.

Peter Stampfel: Exactly. 

AD: Many of the songs on this record are very, very short. Which is a function of jingles, of course. But I was also interested to learn that when you were in AA, you were given prayers to memorize, and one of the ways you did that was by setting them to music. Are music and spirituality tied together for you?

Peter Stampfel: I’ve always felt that music has a supernatural element to it. I sometimes sing to myself when I’m praying, which I do. It’s a funny thing. And more likely to happen if I go to bed when I’m stoned. 

AD: “Maximum Love,” with the lyrics “Love/To every single atom/And sentient being that exists,” was that one inspired by a bedtime toke? 

Pete Stampfel: Oh, no, I wrote that during lockdown. [Laughs] I decided to meditate. Or that I was going to retry meditation. Sometimes I’ll go on Google and try and find information, or go to my Facebook wall and ask for answers from people that Google can’t provide. I was asking about meditation, asking for hints and tips. And one person suggested a book put out by these monks, or semi-monks in the midwest. I sent away for their book and got it back, and I found their methodology for meditating was absolutely the most lucid and magical that I had seen anywhere. 

So you start by relaxing, and then basically thinking love at the universe. And tasked with thinking love at the universe as hard as I could, I tried to articulate that, and it came out, “Love to every single atom and sentient being that exists or ever did.” It was like, “You want me to love the universe? OK, well stand back!” 

AD: It’s hard to beat loving every single atom. Kind of covers it all! 

Peter Stampfel: I cover the waterfront.

Song Shards by Peter Stampfel

AD: I try to meditate too, and while I struggle with it sometimes—my mind loves turning and turning and generating thoughts—every now and then the conscious side of things slips away. And in those moments, I find myself in a state of awareness and in that state, I’ve thought, “If anything is holding the universe together, it has to be love.” 

Pete Stampfel: Yeah, exactly.

AD: “Hail Mary” is a beautiful one too. Did you grow up Catholic?

Peter Stampfel: Oh yeah, I grew up Catholic. And I quit. I was gonna be the first kid in the family to go to college. This was Milwaukee in 1956, and after being there for a month, I discovered my first bohemians, folk music, and the budding counter culture. I realized that the Catholic Church was just one option, not to mention being, like, ridiculous. My parents were going to be tortured forever because they missed mass once? You miss mass, that’s mortal sin. You got a mortal sin, you die and go to hell, right? And you’re tortured forever! Doesn’t sound like “God is love.” Not very loving. 

But I’ll never not be fond of Mary. I had a really good mother. Many people don’t. So I never had an prejudice against women. I’ve always felt that the musical version of “Thee Our Father,” was ponderous and clunky and really non-spiritual. I wanted to do a really good version, and have it dedicated to Mary. I’ve started working with these guys from Catalonia. Our album is in the home stretch right now. I sent them “Hail Mary” too, and we started cooking up a rockabilly version of that one. Like, how would Elvis do it? We also have a straight version of it, and they prefixed it with a snippet of a Catalonian hymn to Mary. They did a weird effect where it sounds like I’m suddenly talking in tongues. It goes into this Pentecostal pseudo glossolalia. 

AD: As I understand it, Elvis’ very first recording sessions were songs for his mother, right? So you’ve got this kind of cool divine feminine thread that’s running through it. I didn’t grow up Catholic, but I have always thought the veneration of Mary was beautiful. 

Peter Stampfel: My understanding is that Catholicism allowed women in higher positions of authority too, you know, prior to 1000 A.D. Barbara Ehrenreich wrote this book called Dancing in the Streets: A History of Collective Joy. Apparently, they used to really carry on during mass. People would do rhythm instruments and dance. In fact, they never even put pews in churches until a couple hundred years ago. Everyone was standing, you know, so mass could be mildly frenzied. The Inquisition was actually started to purge that element out of the church. That purged element became carnival. 

AD: I like the idea of a less staid, more active worship service. That sort of brings me to another favorite of mine from this set, “More Wack.” In it, you sing, “Please God/make me more wack.” Who doesn’t need a reminder every now and then to not to take themselves so seriously? Is that what you sort of mean by “wack”?

Peter Stampfel: I believe that goofiness is sacred. When Trump was elected for the first time, I started thinking, “How can we possibly fight that evil bastard?” And goofiness is the only thing powerful enough. It’s such a great evil, but if people could get together and be goofy together on a regular basis, it’s a huge bonding experience. It’s a huge release of tension. 

In my liner notes I cite a thing about [anthropologist] Robin Dunbar’s number [of social connections a human can sustain]. I read in a recent issue of The Economist—which is a good source of information, even though I don’t 100% agree with their politics—where Dunbar posited that one of the ways humans became what we think of as human was, well, they started getting drunk together, picking up fermented food from the ground. And getting drunk collectively was a social bonder that was extremely effective. People started being relaxed and goofy together, along with that they started cooking together, and suddenly they were sitting around, cooking meat and began conversing more. That’s two of the big social bonding elements that made humanity become humanity. 

AD: There are stoic threads that run through the record as well. “Be Strict With Yourself” is a really interesting one, because the lyric you sing is, “Be strict with yourself/tolerant of others.” Right now the prevailing mode, especially online, is to be strict with others and very tolerant of your own failings. It’s a trap I certainly fall into myself. And maybe that’s just human nature, but it’s good to push against it. It’s good to hold yourself to a standard, but you have carve out the space to show grace to others too. 

Peter Stampfel: Yeah, all those stoic lines are basically really powerful practices. If you do those things, your life works much better. Your interactions improve. 

AD: The music critic Robert Christgau wrote that next to Bob Dylan, you’re “the closest thing to a genius to come out of the New York folk revival scene of the ‘60s.” Do you ever get tired of hearing that? 

Peter Stampfel: No I never get tired of hearing that. [Laughs] It feels wonderful.

AD: I don’t think I’d get tired of hearing it either. [Laughs] In his book, The Philosophy of Modern Song, Dylan wrote about your Fugs collaborator Tuli Kupferberg’s song “CIA Man.” Did you check out Dylan’s last record, Rough and Rowdy Ways? What did you think of it? 

Peter Stampfel: That’s the one with the really long song about Kennedy, right? 

AD: “Murder Most Foul,” yeah. 

Peter Stampfel: You know, my dad is from Minnesota, and he grew up just 20 or 30 miles from Duluth. Dylan got to New York about a year after I did. I arrived in ‘59 in September, and he came in the winter of 1960. I met him in 1961. We actually played music together, and he really blew my mind. I was 22 or 23, and he was 20. But this kid was doing stuff that was so far beyond. He had rock & roll phrasing, but he understood the traditional stuff. I thought there was traditional folk music and rock & roll, but they were disparate. It was a “never the twain shall meet” thing. But he had actually combined them. His voice went electric before his guitar did.

I’ve always profoundly thought of Dylan as someone whose abilities are something to aspire to. As well as the fact that he’s done an amazing job of staying sane, considering all that stuff happening, you know? But I wish he was more melodically inventive. I mean, he could be, but he doesn’t care to be. That’s his prerogative, of course. “Murder Most Foul” is an impressive piece of writing, but nothing I’d want to listen to on repeat, you know? But he can still crank out a first rate song. 

I’ve always been, like, fascinated by the idea of why so many songwriters lose it. I just recently noticed that I’ve been writing songs longer than Irving Berlin was. And Irving Berlin wrote his last good song in 1950, or maybe the early ‘60s. But most of his songs are crappy, you know? 

AD: After that point?

Peter Stampfel: No, no, before that point too. I mean, like the original songs he wrote for The Cocoanuts, the Marx Brothers film. They’re all throwaways, you know? And that’s OK. But my point is…Willie Nelson still writes good songs. But no other songwriter that I know of is doing A-level material like Willie. And he wrote his first song when Bob Dylan was seven years old. 

One of the reasons is that he collaborates. He’s part of the very wise songwriting collaboration phenomenon. Did you ever read Dave Hickey’s Air Guitar? Hickey is probably the guy who coined the phrase “outlaw country.” Reading his book, I realized the fact there there’s a lot of collaboration going on in Nashville. Take a guy like Roger Miller. He’s famous for his speed ingestions, and when you listen to his songs, you can tell. But Nashville is a collaborative place. And a lot of that collaboration was speed-fueled. What percentage of the all time greatest country hits were written by popping speed together?

AD: Another person I want to ask you about is Harry Smith. I was reading the liner notes for the 1997 version of The Anthology of American Folk Music. I loved your assertion that the key of “G” is the people’s key. But also, you stated, “If God were a DJ, he’d be Harry Smith.” Harry obviously produced Virgin Fugs. Was he an inspiration for projects of yours like Song Shards and 20th Century in 100 Songs, in terms of gathering these songs and casting a magical spell with them more or less? 

Peter Stampfel: Absolutely. Have you seen Dust-to-Digital’s Excavated Shellac: An Alternate History of the World’s Music? That’s an inspiration too. And do you know about Radio Garden?

AD: That’s the site that allows you to tune into music from stations all over the world, right?

Peter Stampfel: Yeah! I’ve been really hung up on stations from Madagascar. Man, the music they have there. 

AD: I think of people like you and Harry as custodians of songs in a certain way. In the case of Song Shards, some of those songs are Pepsi-Cola jingles or old beer advertisements, some are sacred texts, but it’s all about how you combine them and sequence them—it ultimately takes the listener on a journey. 

Peter Stampfel: Have you heard my song about Harry, “His Tapes Roll On?” He recorded wire recorder back in World War II. He recorded on a reel-to-reel when the reel-to-reel was new. And eventually, he would use the telephone answering machine to record. Harry Smith is gone, but his tapes roll on. Bob Dylan said that if not for the Smith anthology, there wouldn’t be a Bob Dylan.
