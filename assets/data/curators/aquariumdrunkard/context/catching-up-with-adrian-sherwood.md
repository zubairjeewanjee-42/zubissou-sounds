## Body

Adrian Sherwood is one of the unsung innovators of contemporary music, a man who immersed himself early on in reggae, dub and Afro-beat and brought these sounds and production styles to post-punk, punk and industrial music. He had a decades’ long partnership with reggae pioneer Lee “Scratch” Perry and equally lengthy stints with Creation Rebel and African Head Charge. As a producer he’s worked with luminaries of post-punk like Mark E. Smith, Mark Stewart and the Slits, as well as industrial heavyweights like Einstürzende Neubauten and Minstry and psychedelicists including Primal Scream. 

But while collaboration has been a hallmark of Sherwood’s career, he is currently stepping out on his own with a full-length The Collapse of Everything and an EP The Grand Designer, his first solo efforts in 13 years. We recently connected with him to discuss this new work, his ever-evolving set of studio tools, his history in music and his lifelong commitment to learn and grow. 

Says Sherwood, “It’s like the Jamaican saying, each one teach one. It applies. I worked with all those great people, different people, good and great Americans and then lunatic soundscapers from England, like Mark. I learned quite a bit from everyone I worked with.” | j kelly

Adrian Sherwood: Why not? I’m really proud of the last few records I’ve made, the Creation Rebel, Sonic Boom and Panda Bear, the Horace Andy, African Head Charge. I’m proud of them all, but all of them, even though there’s a band or an artist or whatever, I still feel like they’re my records. It’s not some kind of ego thing, but I do feel they’re mine. 

But with this record here, I’ve got the opportunity to go on stage, do some events. It’s my shout, the whole record. 

I don’t want to rest on my laurels and just do more of the same. So, I tried to make this one so that I was ready to go and perform it live on on stage. I’m going to have musicians playing with me—Doug Wimbish, Alex White, Mark Bandola—and I’d like to enlarge it with a really good visual, a kind of tripped out semi-psychedelic sound and visuals. 

AD: You’re kind of swimming against the stream if you’re going to do a big tour with a large ensemble, aren’t you? 

Adrian Sherwood: Well, I don’t think it’s going to be a large tour. I’m doing three shows in Japan in November, which are quite big ones where I can afford it. I’m doing a very big one in London in February. I’ve been invited to do Big Ears Festival. 

AD: Oh, nice. Yeah, that’s a great festival.

Adrian Sherwood: I haven’t been there, but everyone tells me it’s good. For that, I’ll come on my own, but I’m going to bring the visuals, and I’m going to do quite a vicious live dub show with a multi-track on stage. But then when the budgets permit, I would like to enlarge what I’m doing. 

AD: I want to go back to something you said earlier about seeing the records that you produced as your records. That’s one way to look at producing, but there are other producers who feel that they should just kind of get out of the way of the sound. Can you talk about that a little bit? 

Adrian Sherwood: Well, Joe Meek always said he thought they were all his records. But I think with me, the Head Charge and Creation Rebel were originally studio projects which evolved into bands and that is mine anyway. I see them as my work as well. 

With Sonic Boom and Panda Bear, it was a collaboration. It’s called Reset in Dub. And the three of us did that together. So again, I feel it’s mine. It feels very part of me. I put everything into it. I’m involved from the beginning to the end, constructing the rhythms, co-writing the music. So, all those records have definitely got my sound on them. 

AD: So, someone who is familiar with you, it wouldn’t matter whether it was African Head Charge or Einstürzende Neubauten or whatever, they should be able to say, oh yeah, that’s Adrian Sherwood. 

Adrian Sherwood: Well, maybe not so much with Neubauten, because for that one, I was keeping things very dry and very tough. You can identify me, my presence, better with the more dubby tripped out stuff. 

AD: Your solo album, The Collapse of Everything and the EP are very much in line with that kind of sound, I think. 

Adrian Sherwood: I tried to evolve this one. I’m using new things that I haven’t done before and also introducing elements like the guitar with Bandola.

AD: I’m interested in that because you’ve obviously been in the industry for a long time and you want to keep it fresh. What are some of the things that you’re still learning and pushing and doing differently? 

Adrian Sherwood: Well, there’s currently, everyone’s going AI this, AI that, like it’s a bad thing. And it’s true that in the world at the moment, it’s just very scary how out of control everything it feels. But personally, I think AI can help in certain ways. 

For example, on the music front, I was using RipX to ghost my own tracks to create ambiences and then tuning things I was playing with. There are also lots of plugins. I don’t operate the computer. My engineer operates the computer. I’m like conductor a little bit and say do this, do that, and then do this, do that and get the players in. But there are new plugins coming that are incredible, and there’s also a new delay unit, there are new things coming all the time and I love them. So, I’ve been working a lot with new bits and pieces and also a lot of the analog machines. I’m really enjoying them as well. 

I also did some records which haven’t come out yet on using Dolby Atmos which is just so exciting. Also, having spent all this time not making a record for myself, I’ve got the chance to just do whatever I like when it’s just me. I’m not trying to please whoever I’m collaborating with on the record. So, I made a piece of music that’s a piece of sound that I think I’m very, very happy with . 

AD: Did you make this music all at once in a contained period of time or do these ideas come from all over the place? 

Adrian Sherwood: I did it over a 12-month period. 

AD: Was there anything going on in the background either in your life or in the world or in the music you were listening to or books you were reading that sort of fed into how this album came out? 

Adrian Sherwood: Well, I deliberately didn’t use vocals on this one. I deliberately made it instrumental. 

But, you know, the world’s in such a funny state at the moment. I just thought I’d theme it, and it’s like a kind of soundtrack for where my head’s at at the moment. It’s not all gloom and gloom. It’s quite joyous. There are definitely some things I’ve learned over the last ten years working on other people’s records, and I’ve applied that to this production. 

AD: The title, Collapse of Everything, seems pretty universally applicable at the moment, but I understand it came more from a personal loss? 

Adrian Sherwood: Yes, my friend Mark Stewart died. He was a very close friend, and I miss him dearly.

AD: I just penned a review of his last album, which was amazing.

Adrian Sherwood: I didn’t do a lot on that, just one track. But we did a song with Scott Crow, an American friend of ours, and part of the lyrics on it contained the phrase “the collapse of everything.” And I was missing Mark, and I thought, you know what, I want to frame that as the title. And that led to some of the other track titles which I thought were relevant to where we’re at at the moment. 

AD: He was the master of the startling title.

Adrian Sherwood: Mark was lovely. We had some lovely times, and I miss him greatly. 

AD: I never met him, but the music was just amazing. 

You can’t hear a track like “The Collapse of Everything” without thinking about your long history and reggae and dub. It’s got a lot of the same sounds and the same echoing kind of cavernous space. Do you want to talk about how your relationship to that kind of music has changed over time, if it has? Has it evolved? 

Adrian Sherwood: I was very lucky to be young during the golden age of reggae and dub and everything, and so I started with it way back. But the tonality and the space in Jamaican music is brilliant. You know, there’s a space where the bass is, and this element is here, that one is there. The picture, the sonic picture is uncluttered. In my work with Neubauten—and many other bands— I applied that tone and tried to create space in all the productions. 

If you notice, The Collapse of Everything is quite dense. There’s lots of things going on, then it clears a little bit, and it comes back and it’s very textured. It’s quite a lot busier than my normal stuff, but still quite clear. I was trying to make it chipped out with Mark’s guitars and get something different really. I didn’t just knock it out. I worked on it quite hard to get it. So, I mean, I think it sounds like a step forward for me and not just more of the same. I don’t want it to be more of the same. Yeah. Because we’re in danger. I mean, a lot of my stuff obviously sounds quite similar, but I don’t think that’s a bad thing. 

It’s just very difficult for anybody doing music now to get noticed and heard. I want to jump on a stand and say “Listen this is a fucking great record. Listen to it.” But so does everyone else. The last few records I’ve made, Horace Andy’s Midnight Scorcher, the last African Head Charge album, Trip to Bolgatanga, the Sonic Boom/Panda Bear record—they’re very, very good records. I just wish like 100 times more people had bought them. 

But now, again, we’re in a stage where it’s all about streaming figures. And as you get older, you don’t get the streaming figures that the younger artists do.

AD: Well, you’re also not making music to please the algorithm, which a lot of people are. I mean, the algorithm has terrible taste in music, my personal opinion. 

Adrian Sherwood: I agree. 

AD: I wanted to ask you about “Dub Inspector” which has almost an Ethio-jazz kind of slink to it. I mean, it sounds like that classic Mulatu Astatke. 

Adrian Sherwood: That was the idea with the horns. 

AD: Can you talk a little bit about your connection to African music and how that’s evolved over time? 

Adrian Sherwood: Well, I first heard Ethiopian stuff, funnily enough, on a cassette that I had about 1981. I started studying it. That’s very early. Shabazz music out of Addis Ababa. And it was amazing. It just sounded like reggae to me played backwards with the horns and reversed. 

AD: But there is a connection between Jamaica and Ethiopia. The whole Rasta thing is…

Adrian Sherwood: Yeah, but that wasn’t relevant. You know, the Ethiopians hadn’t heard that Haile Selassie was worshipped. But they were making this incredible jazz. 

I also heard some of the stuff coming from Ghana, the high life music. I had an album on Melodisc that was really interesting. Then as the years passed, Bonjo [Iyabinghi Noah of African Head Charge] had been born in Jamaica, but he was into the Afro stuff, and we started experimenting with African rhythms when we did the first Head Charge album. 

 Since then, I’ve done work with King Ayisoba and some other African artists, trying to help them to be honest. I’ve made some very lovely recordings recently with some African musicians. Yeah. It’s very frustrating because you’ve got this wonderful, wonderful stuff, but to most European ears, reggae kind of suffered a lot when they started getting into the black awareness and the roots stuff. People were asking, what’s this “jah this” and “jah that.” They just didn’t get it. They wanted the fun stuff like they had in the 1960s. Then Madness and the Specials, the two-tone bands, filled the gap.

It’s the same with the African stuff. A lot of it is absolutely brilliant and you can’t doubt the incredible playing but a lot of the white music fans can’t relate. They could maybe relate to Fela Kuti because he was singing in English and whatever. But if you’ve got an artist who is gruff and raw, a whole album of that, try selling it. It’s bloody difficult. 

The idea of psychedelic Africa, it’s still not been tapped. 

AD: There was a period in late 70s, early 80s when all these mainstream artists were putting reggae beats into their songs. It was everywhere, and we’ve never had that with Afro-beat. There have been little bursts of people, using that as an influence. 

Adrian Sherwood: With African stuff, it could be, something could really be done. But I think we need some young African people to do it. 

It’s like Cuban music. My other half, she’s a big fan of Cuban stuff. I’m playing the Cuban stuff, and I half like it, but it’s in three/four. And you’ve got big artists from South America who’ve got a quarter of a billion streams. And I listen, it’s like, I get it. But crossing to people who don’t speak Spanish, it’s not easy. It’s not easy. 

But both have such vast potential even here, 45 years after those records. What I tried to do on my own account was very trippy, you know, gone wrong African time. I try and have somebody who’s a little bit off-center just to to draw you in. I don’t want it all formulaic and clean and pretty. I want something mystical and magical. Yeah. That’s what I’m looking for.

 But I think in answer to your thing, my relationship with African music is one where I’d love it if somebody could say, look, Adrian, we’d like you to spend a year or two working with all these acts and we’ll finance it. It’s all the artists’ dream. They all think, oh, if I get a record out in Europe, I’m going to be, you know, it’s going to, everyone’s going to discover me. But a lot of people just can’t, find it very difficult to relate to it. So, something has to be done, I don’t know, to draw people in. 

AD: I did want to ask you to talk a little bit about your band on this record. I know there’s some really interesting people and also some interesting guests. Can you talk a little bit about the people you worked with? 

Well, Doug Wimbish is like my brother. We’re very, very close. So, I’m lucky to have access to such a great talent. He’s unbelievable, Doug. And I wanted to have Doug and I wanted to have Alex [White]. He’s young, 30. He plays with Primal Scream and Fat White Family. Mark Bandola lives in my town. 

A lot of musicians have moved where I am, this area. I live in Ramsgate, it’s in a place called Thanet, and it’s great. We’re only an hour aside from London, it’s beautiful here and much more affordable. You can breathe. 

So, in the town I’ve got Ivan [Hussey], a cello man as well, who I would like to bring on the gigs, but I can’t really get him involved to next year because to bring his cello and him to Japan, it’s involving two or three, two air tickets. I’m doing the first one a bit more stripped down. 

But the guests I’ve got on there are all key people in my life on that record. And they’re all, and obviously I’ve got Keith LeBlanc on it as well, who as you probably know passed away last year. So, it’s very important to me that I’ve got him contributing. I’ve lost so many friends. You feel like you’re the grim bloody reaper.

AD: I’m noticing that myself. Can you tell me a little bit about the recording process.

Adrian Sherwood: I did it right here. I’m on the music floor.

AD: I see Elvis behind you.

[There is a large poster of Elvis Presley hanging on the wall behind him.]

Adrian Sherwood: Great, isn’t he? Look, he’s crying. Yeah. All faded away. I got him for £10 in a junk shop, but he was perfect when I got it. Yeah. And the other side’s got him holding the mic facing the street. It means that I can have the light coming in, and no one’s staring at me. 

[He picks up the camera and gives me a virtual tour of his home studio.]

There’s the album. 

AD: Oh, yeah, there it is. Quite a lot of other albums, too. 

Adrian Sherwood: Yeah, well this is just part of the collection. I’ve got loads downstairs underneath me where I live and the floor above. But this is the area where there’s the old bar, which is a 1930s, I think I thought it was an Italian bar. But here’s like pure vintage gear. And in here, if we’re playing guitars or people are singing, and this is the control room, which is all vintage gear. You know, 10 head beats. Lexicon 224, AMS, Neutron, Grampians, AMS again. Yeah. It’s all wonderful. The mic we’ve got in here, I got it off Robert Plant.

AD: Oh, really? 

Adrian Sherwood: Yeah. It’s a top Neumann from 1967. So, what I’ve got is vintage mixed in with the computer and my good engineer. Yeah. We record it here or if I need to, I’ll go and record drums in somebody else’s studio or cross files on some of them that are there online. But the record was made largely in that little space there. 

AD: And this is where you live too?

Adrian Sherwood: I live here this house is 200 years old. It’s a Georgian terraced house and it’s a beautiful historic street here. You know, if this was in London you just couldn’t afford it. Yeah. But it’s, you know, all my kids and grandkids are in London now. So, I came down here to be with one and she’s cleared off another kid. But I love it here actually. It’s very peaceful and I’m not stressed out with the bullshit of a city life, you know. 

Where are you?

AD: I live in New Hampshire, which is, you know, north of Boston, New England. Yeah, it’s very pretty, you know. It’s out of the way a little bit and cheaper. We moved up here from New York when we wanted to have a family.

Adrian Sherwood: Well done. 

AD: Yeah, yeah. It’s a little dull, you know. 

Adrian Sherwood: Yeah, but at the same time it’s what you make of it. I mean, I can gather people and do whatever.

New York, you know, New York, I loved it. I went there in the 1980s and it was like first time and it was quite dangerous and fun. But you go there now, it’s just like a sanitized version of itself. It’s only really for rich people. 

AD: Yeah, and there’s no stores anymore. Like, it used to be so entertaining to just walk the length of Manhattan. It used to be a little person with their restaurant, their record shop… it’d be like a store that sold pipes or a store that sold ravioli. And there’s nothing like that anymore. 

Adrian Sherwood: But all of America is like that now. There are no little funny cheap shops. I’ve got some of my best friends in America. I feel really they haven’t got access to good food. A lot of them. I was there a couple of years ago before Keith died, we did a little weird, a couple of weird events. We were in Ohio I think, and it was like, there’s nowhere to eat. The nearest thing to eat was McDonald’s. And the corner shop only sold crisps and sweets and hot dogs.

AD: The Midwest is a frightening place. That’s where I grew up. I grew up in Indiana, which is right next to Ohio. 

Adrian Sherwood: Yeah, so you’ve got like a world of that. At the moment, it’s really, really terrifying because it’s happening everywhere. Corporations are taking over every government. 

AD: So back to the music, the EP, the Grand Designer? Did that come out of the same batch of material or a separate thing? 

Adrian Sherwood: It was all the same batch. I got a couple more from as well. But “Russian Oscillator,” which I really liked, it just didn’t fit with the tracks I had to put on the record. 

AD: What do you mean? 

Adrian Sherwood: Well, just if you listen to them and you listen to them and you listen to them, and decide, the feel of going between this to this to this okay. I couldn’t do that with “Russian Oscillator” so I just put it on a 10 inch which you can’t get anymore. I’ll keep that for a compilation or for a bonus track for Japan for the time being. 

AD: Tell me about the Lee “Scratch” Perry track on the EP. He’s another one we’ve lost recently. 

Adrian Sherwood: Well, Lee was my dear friend. I forged a very good relationship and upstairs in the house here we have a Lee Perry room. That’s where he used to stay, and I’ve got all this incredible artwork. Look, I look up there, look at that painting there on the wall. Yep. With George Washington and the Queen with the cross for her head. That’s Peter Harris and Lee Perry together. That’s an incredible painting, that one. I’ve got another room upstairs with all the Lee Perry art. 

I’ve also got an album, an unreleased album with Lee, that is every bit as good as Heavy Rain. Do you know the Heavy Rain? 

AD: Yes. 

Adrian Sherwood: I’ve got an album that I’m just going to sit on for a year or two, because I’m adding subtle little touches, but I don’t want to just rush out a record. The idea of that makes me sick, to be honest. So, I’ve got a couple of things with Lee on tape, and I put that vocal against the “Grand Designer” rhythm. It just fitted brilliantly. I even made another version with him where he’s doing an X rated lyric. 

AD: Do you have favorite bits or sounds on either The Collapse of Everything or the EP?

Adrian Sherwood: I mean, you know, we’ve got “Spaghetti Best Western.” Best Western’s a hotel chain. We thought Spaghetti Great Western was, it’s like, it’s quite a comedy track. I like that’s quite funny. 

But also, I think “The Collapse of Everything” is a real vintage track. I’m really proud of it. It’s got depth and color and everything to it.

I like all the tracks for different reasons. In the old days you used to deliberate about whether to make an album as opposed to a single. Yeah. Oh, I’m going to spend days making this an album. But that’s what I’ve done on this. I want it to be a proper album. Beginning to end, it’s like a little trip. If you put it on in your house, you wouldn’t want to be jumping tracks particularly. It just flows. There’s not really anything that jumps out. 

AD: You’ve done an awful lot of things, a lot of different kinds of music. What would you hope your legacy would be? What would you hope people would say about you? 

Adrian Sherwood: Doesn’t he look good for his age? 

AD: Ha, but is there anything that crosses over? Something you’ve always been trying to do, regardless of the context. 

Adrian Sherwood: Well, for a legacy, people read the front of the sleeve. Those who know know what I’ve done and then those who know who are influenced by what I’ve done. I know as a fact lots of other crews, musicians and people have been inspired, and that is the greatest honor I could have. You know, I might not have had the commercial success, but I’ve got lots of people who give me a nod. That’s a wonderful thing. Every time that warms my heart, you know, makes me feel good. 

There are very few records that have really lasted the test of time. So, I hope some of my work, some of my work now is, you know, like Dub from Creation, my first ever record. I don’t even like saying this but it’s approaching 50 years old. And it still sounds pretty good. I don’t know if in years to come it’s going to be held up as any… It’ll just be some curiosity. 

AD: I think the first time I ever ran across your name was with Sherwood at the Controls. 

Adrian Sherwood: It was recently then. 

AD: Yes, and you know, I loved all those bands and I didn’t realize that one person had shaped all those different sounds. 

Adrian Sherwood: I didn’t shape them. I was involved with all. No, I learned from a lot of the bands that I was working, you know, like… before, Mark [E. Smith of the Fall] was my friend, he was a difficult bugger, but he loved his anti-production techniques where everything was dry, no reverb, no delay. He used it later, but at the beginning it was everything natural. And I applied that. I’d have a really wet mix going on, a dub mix and I’d dry it right up. You could control the tension in the tunes by varying that, by having something really raw and dry, then expanding it. 

I learned quite a bit from everyone I worked with. It’s like the Jamaican saying, each one teach one. It applies. I worked with all those great people, different people, good and great Americans and then lunatic soundscapers from England, like Mark and whatever. And, you know, bless, I think legacy wise, which is your question, I think your legacy is if you’re a good person, you leave good work and good spirit in your kids, grandkids and your friends and try and make sure what you do is serious.
