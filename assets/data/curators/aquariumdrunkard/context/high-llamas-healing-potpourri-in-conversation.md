# High Llamas & Healing Potpourri :: In Conversation

## Body

Sean O’Hagan’s High Llamas project has always followed its creator’s whims. But with Hey Panda, the Llamas embark on their most unexpected journey yet, straight into the heart of the auto-tuned, hyper pop fixated social media feed. Featuring a cast of guests including Bonnie “Prince” Billy, Fryars, and Rae Morris and inspired by contemporary electronic composition, ’90s rap, and neo-soul, it’s a record that proces that O’Hagan is less interested in plundering the sounds of the past as he is in recontextualizing and expanding them, tugging on the corners to reveal obscured connections and dimensions. 

Simi Sohota is the man behind Healing Potpourri. On 2022’s Paradise, he joined with O’Hagan to create what we called “a pure chamber pop gem.” For this special In Conversation back-and-forth, Sohota acted as Aquarium Drunkard’s correspondent and spoke with O’Hagan to unpack the new album, consider the spiritual life of pianos, and reflect on the way the ’90s changed how musicians approach—and shatter—the concept of genre. The conversation has been edited and condensed for clarity. 

Simi Sohota for Aquarium Drunkard: How are you Sean? 

Sean O’Hagan: I’m very well, Simi. I’m really excited to be speaking to you about a record after our wonderful experience together on the Healing record. It’s really nice that we’re continuing our conversation. Just for anybody out there who doesn’t know, I produced Simi’s band, Healing Potpourri, two years ago now, isn’t it?

AD: One of the joys of my life, really. That’s why it’s really great seeing you again and catching up, because I consider myself one of the High Llamas’ ambassadors to California. I’ve been spreading the gospel of your music ever since I heard it. I feel like the High Llamas army is growing. It seems that more and more people that I talk to about music are talking to me about the influence of High Llama’s on their music. It’s growing.

Sean O’Hagan: I thought that influence engine died a long time ago, because obviously that conversation was probably being had in the ‘90s, but I didn’t believe it was being had after that. But you’re a different generation. I’m 64. I’m twice as old as you nearly, and a different generation, and for your age group to actually have that conversation is so beautiful. It’s so beautiful.

AD: I still remember the first time that I heard High Llamas. I don’t remember quite what year it was, but I was in my San Francisco apartment. I think I caught a cold or something and I just discovered Retrospective, Rarities, & Instrumentals. That was my first introduction to you: I laid in bed sick for two or three days listening to it over and over again, blown away by the chord changes, blown away by the melodies, and blown away by the lyrics, which I think are an underrated aspect of a lot of your music. Your lyrics are poetic, and it convey an image, but they’re a little mysterious, too. It looks like you’re in your studio, huh? You’re in your music studio at home?

Sean O’Hagan: Yeah, the place that you remember from a few years ago. Yeah, I’m still in that room.

AD: And is this where a lot of the magic happened for your new album, Hey Panda. [Drag City]l sent it to me ahead of time before this interview, and you sent it to me about a year ago or so after you finished it. So that’s where it happened? 

Sean O’Hagan: Yeah, this is where it happened. I recorded here, Press Play, Andy Ramsey’s studio, [and] I mixed in Ben Garrett’s studio. Ben Garrett—his artistic name is Fryars. He was big influence for me. I made a record with him called God Melodies, and I made a record with his wife, Rae Morris, and it was a changing experience for me. It really made me feel as though I wanted to participate in contemporary music. You can think about the High Lamas [having] these stately influences from all over the place, from Brazil, from the ‘60s. But most people think of it as influences from different genres and eras and I’m very aware of that. I was painfully aware of it actually and wondered whether I was missing something from contemporary music and working with lots of different artists including you guys, Healing Potpourri. 

I really did want to sort of expand the palette and brush up. I wanted to also make a record that was saying “thank you” to this generation of people from 20 to 35 who I think are making really, really important music in LA, London, New York, Chicago. Really important music. And it’s music that’s informed by—I know it’s kind of a strange thing to say—but I have this theory and I think that music changed in the ‘90s. The ‘90s was an important time insofar as the very musical side of hip hop really happened, and so we had all the records by artists like Questlove, A Tribe Called Quest, and MF Doom, and obviously the influence is J Dilla. I believe that there’s a before and after. And some people say, “Oh, no, it’s before and after punk or before and after psychedelia or whatever.” I like to think actually we should be talking about before and after that period. I think after that period, a new musician emerged. And that was a musician who wasn’t afraid to say, “I can play jazz, I’m a jazz player, but I also have really good knowledge of contemporary music, of R&B, of hip hop,” and let’s not forget that the ’90s change in hip hop actually opened up that kind of crate digging and that search.

AD: For older stuff.

Sean O’Hagan: For new ideas and new sounds.

AD: Breathing new life into music through sampling.

Sean O’Hagan: Absolutely, absolutely, and I know we’re talking [20+] years ago, but I’m trying to talk about this sort of time before and time after, and I think that generation, they’ve grown up with this. That’s why I think the R&B now is incredibly adventurous. There’re so many influences going on. You’ve got an R&B influence, a soul influence, you’ve got a lo-fi influence, you’ve got a bedroom indie pop influence, you’ve got full on jazz influence, all these things are going on, and it’s creating some really extraordinary music and there are people who are just in their bedrooms and said, “I’ve always fancied trying to do something,” and they make these incredible tunes, and they get them out there, and obviously you can get it out there because of Bandcamp and SoundCloud. The conversation about whether the music industry is an ethical place or not is a different conversation, but stuff is out there. I come from a different time, and I just wanted to say, I think we live in the best times now. I think I remember when we were making the music, making your album, I was saying, “I think we’re in the best times.”

AD: We’re celebrating 50 years of hip hop now in America. The genesis of that started about 50 years ago, and now it’s not even a separate genre from pop. It’s just completely intertwined, and now—not just with your new album, but in all of pop music—it seems we’re combining it with Latin American music, with music from Europe. It’s all intertwined into pop music and it’s not “world music,” jazz, hip hop. It’s all one thing. That’s something to celebrate. Students of music, we love it all, and why can’t we put it all into the same bag, mix it around and see what happens? Your collaboration with Fryars and Rae Morris—what in their production or songwriting inspired you? What did you want to add to High Llamas’ bag of tricks? 

Sean O’Hagan: I got interested in rhythms. I’ve always been interested in rhythms. I’ve always tried to push them, but I was always a little bit shy, and I always had a distance from them. I think the album Snowbug, rhythmically is a really interesting record, and even though it’s our record, I do think we were trying to do something there.

AD: My favorite album probably of all time. I love it and I’m so inspired by it every day.

Sean O’Hagan: Oh, bless you. I think since Snowbug, I got away from rhythms. I mean, Beet, Maize & Corn didn’t have any, it was without any, effectively, rhythm section. But once I got in that room with Fryars and I understood how young producers actually worked with beats…I guess it was a bit of a cliche. I came from an old cohort and I thought, “Okay, I love hip hop, but essentially, it doesn’t have the sophistication of great writing.” I’m sure that was in my head, which is a poor thing to say. 

When I was in that room, I was like, “No, wait a minute. This is extraordinary,” and I just experienced a transformational moment. I remember I went and just tore up the rulebook a little bit to a certain extent. I thought, “Well, I’ve always wanted to try to go there, but I never thought I had the language or the ability to, but I felt as though I had a little bit of ability to do it, and so I just tried. I tried to understand how to work with those rhythms and how to work with going back to synths, which I’d ignored for a long time because I was working with real orchestration for so long and really enjoyed it and loved it. I suddenly thought, “No, I could really enjoy working with synths again,” and to be quite honest I didn’t need to work with orchestration again. I’ve done it so much, I didn’t know what I had to say in that genre anymore, but I had a lot to say suddenly with synths again, and I completely just found it challenging. I didn’t work exclusively with synths. There’s lots of piano on Hey Panda. Loads of piano. There’s actually a lot of guitar.

AD: There’s strings as well.

Sean O’Hagan: Yeah. There’s a little bit of strings here, and even the guitar. I was doing that really nice thing that I learned from a lot of neo-soul records where you play a delicate R&B finger riff but then you bring in just a little bit of modulation to give it the tone. And I’m talking about harmonic modulation, the actual tuning wobbles. I just love that. I wouldn’t go near that. I thought, “Oh no, no, no. I can’t do that,” and now I’m going, “Oh, yes, you can.” It’s so beautiful where the tuning just kind of modulates. Whereas I was real, “Okay, you can have the root and the fifth. You have that always in a chord, so you get the feel of the chord,” and suddenly I wasn’t doing that. I was really playing counterpoint on the bass again, and it’s just such fun, just such fun. There’s still piano there, but it’s slightly broken up, modulated. Some of the old sounds are there but there’s plenty of new sounds, as well.

AD: Especially those really frenetic bass synth runs that are kind of disjointed but very staccato, I think of it as like ear candy, but in the lower frequencies you almost never have that. It’s such a fun way to use the bass, and I just love that, and it adds all that interest. It makes the chords more interesting, too, and you’re the master of chords.

Sean O’Hagan: The other thing I’ve never shied away from is that thing of “stop, start.” There’s two or three songs that start with a very sedate piano, and you’d think, “Where am I?” and it’s a sedate piano, and then suddenly beats come in. I found it fun, and I thought, “Well, I hope other people find that fun.”

AD: Oh, yeah, and that’s it, and that’s signature High Llamas to me where you never know where the next part of the song is gonna go. You never know where the chord changes are gonna take you, you never know what the middle section is gonna do, but it’s like, “Wow, so this is the song. This is the meat of the song,” and then it disappears and you’re into a beautiful coda that is completely different from the rest of the song but it’s all one tune. I’m curious about how you decide, and if you decide, why is this one song, why is the whole album not one song, all just little vignettes? How do you piece those together?

Sean O’Hagan: Well, they are literally are indiscriminate voice memos from over several years, over a little period. They’re three different writing experiences and might be 18 months apart, and then I just think something like, “They can coexist together if I just write this bridge between that chord and that chord. They’re gonna work,” and then you feel it, and then you try to play it, and you go, “Whoa that’s audacious.” You play again, you go, “It’s less audacious,” and then you play it the third time, and you say, “It’s natural.”

AD: It’s a little bit like how listening to your albums is, to be honest. The first time I heard Hey Panda, I was like, “Wow, there’s a lot of new stuff happening here. There’s autotuned vocals, there’s very R&B-feeling tracks, there’s soul gospel stuff, but you listen to it twice and it unfolds, but you listen to it three times and then you’re just in it, and that’s the experience that I’ve had with this album—with a lot of your albums—where on repeated listens they just unfurl in your brain, and they just make more and more sense. The most bizarre changes, the most daring and adventurous changes can become sort of normal. 

Sean O’Hagan: You know something? Some people don’t want to do that with me. Some people don’t want to leave questioning or challenged. I want to leave you with a glow. That’s the intention.

AD: A glow. That’s beautiful.

Sean O’Hagan: Lyrically…I’ve never thought of myself as a great word person. I had a lot of challenges as a kid. I didn’t do well at school. I had dyslexia. I was a slow reader. Three of the songs are actually about that. I left school with no exams. I worked in car factories.

AD: You’ve got lyrics about the words dancing on the pages.

Sean O’Hagan: Yeah, that’s about dyslexia. And “Toriafan,” basically again. “Show me again and again and again because to see is to do is the way that a few of us learn.” It’s about kinetic learning. Some people learn through visuals or kinetically, and we know that now in education. We know about the kids who can’t sit still, and before that would have been punished, and now it’s okay. If that’s how you’re gonna learn, if you’re just over there, that’s fine. If you need to walk while you’re learning that’s fine, and also obviously with Hey Panda the other thing is there’s animals and learning difficulties, shall we say. They’re two of the pillars of ideas.

AD: Go into that a little bit more. What are you talking about in reference to Hey Panda there?

Sean O’Hagan: The [specific] panda is a panda I saw on TikTok during the pandemic, who ate carrots every day—huge carrots. What I’m trying to say in a song is that the TikTok platform is attacked by, well, certainly by the US government [but] social media is the biggest change that’s happened. I mean, people say it’s the internet, and I would say yes, but I think social media is the massive change. There’s all sorts of debates, and they’re usually very negative debates. I just want to land on, “Well, what about the positive communities that come out of social media during the pandemic when people were at home?” People were lonely. They did reach out, they performed, they spoke. There was an awful lot of creating a gentle space where people could just chill and one of those spaces was animals, big time. People were at home with their pets, “What’s my pet doing?” and that wonderful panda eating carrots every day was very much part of that, and I felt like I was the friend of panda.

AD: Beautiful.

Sean O’Hagan: “Fall Off the Mountain” is another song, and that’s about hill walking. I know you go walking. I think you go walking? You go into the wilderness a bit, don’t you?

AD: Yeah, my girlfriend Ava [Rosen] and I take our dog out to the redwood forests close by here where I am in Oakland, California.

Sean O’Hagan: You come to the UK sometimes—do you know the north of England a little bit? 

AD: A little bit.

Sean O’Hagan: There’s a little bit of hill walking in Yorkshire, in the north of England, and one song which is just humor, it’s about the people. If you think about the walkers, they’re usually educated people, Democrats or liberal people, and they’ve got a certain—I don’t know—they’ve got views on life, and it’s a very particular type of person, and I like the fact that the animals are there watching these people, and they’ve got their all-weather gear and they’ve got good boots, but the animals are sort of laughing and just saying, “You don’t belong here. This is ours.” It’s humorous, so “Fall Off The Mountain” is about sheep watching hill walkers in the UK.

AD: While we’re talking about lyrics, I gotta ask you about what is my favorite song on the album so far, “Bade Amey” the third track. That song just really grabs my heartstrings and I don’t know why. What is that song about?

Sean O’Hagan: It’s about my piano. One day I was looking at the piano and I thought, “I’ve had this piano for so long and I should treat myself to a decent piano,” and then I went away. I thought about that, and then I thought about the piano as a personality, [imagined that] the piano heard me say that, and then the next time I went down it basically said, “I’m gonna set my case to you,” and said, “Save me. Listen to me, my echo and song. Though I’m not on my best, I can still fill the room with echo and song.” I just think the amount of times I’ve recorded that piano in this house, cables trailing down the stairs, taking the mics up and down, but then there’s a bit where Livvy sings, my daughter, sings. This is the funny part of it: So the piano is at the front of the house, and now because we live in an age where we don’t go to the shops as much, everything’s delivered to the front door, and the piano says “The other thing is, I always hear the front door when you don’t,” and “When the parcels are delivered I’m the one who hears the bell,” and there’s another parcel, so the piano is saying, “You can’t get rid of me because I’m part of the house and I hear the door.”

AD: Pianos are beautiful, spiritual instruments, and I adore them, and I’ve written songs about my piano, as well, and so I just love that. While you mentioned it, your daughter Livvy’s singing on this album is just so incredible. I love her voice so much. It’s so pure and gentle and it’s like melted butter in my ears, although that sounds horrible, but you know what I mean. It’s just the most gorgeous sound, and I love her voice.

Sean O’Hagan: She would be overjoyed to hear that.

AD: She’s a singular talent. I really love her voice and especially with your compositions. It’s a really awesome team.

Sean O’Hagan: She writes like me. She writes like I do. Same kind of way.

AD: I love Rae Morris’s voice, too, on the single “Sisters Friends.” I just love the pop vocal performance by her. It’s just spot-on gorgeous, and it’s so soaring and so singable, and I love your autotune counterpoint in between the passages.

Sean O’Hagan: She’s a serious talent.

AD: You mentioned social media. A lot of the sounds listening to this album strangely remind me of scrolling through TikTok. It’s like you hear like hyper pop, high-pitched vocals for a few seconds, and then it changes. It’s not necessarily music for a shorter attention span but…

Sean O’Hagan: It’s a different experience. You’re absolutely right. I think that’s a really, really good point. You go through and it’s almost like experimenting with music. You just hear a turn of a chorus and it’s, “Whoa, that’s beautiful,” and then suddenly you land on something else, and then there’s just this beat or some pianos and there is definitely a character, and I think there’s a joyfulness in it. I love it. I absolutely love it. That’s a really interesting observation. The record sounds like scrolling through TikTok. That’s amazing. 

AD: I hope that that came off as a compliment.

Sean O’Hagan: Oh, I’m gonna use that.

AD: It’s just pulling from so many different inspirations, but it’s your feed, so it is targeted to you, so it’s all cohesive in some way. I feel like a lot of the writing about this record so far has been, “Oh, this is something brand new. It’s completely unexpected,” but I feel like for the true heads, High Lamas fans, we’ve been hearing this a little bit in your music for the last several years, creeping in with a record like Radum Calls, under the Sean O’Hagan name. 

Sean O’Hagan: Yeah, it was the beginning there.

AD: And then The Wild Are Welcome was deeply psychedelic, deeply groovy, but adding in the autotuned vocals and more experimental production techniques. You’re kind of like, “Where does the songwriting end? And where does the production end?“ They’re just completely intermixed.

Sean O’Hagan: I mean, neo-soul is a good term. It’s a term that I didn’t use for ages, but actually I think it’s a lovely term. I think there are aspects of the Healing Potpourri that are neo-soul, and we’re both friends of Pearl & The Oysters, and I think there’s absolutely neo-soul vibes there. It’s a really good place to be, and of course we talked about John Carroll Kirby and Eddie Chacon’s record, the whole Stone’s Throw project. I think there’s definitely a movement there, and I love it. I absolutely think it’s gorgeous.

AD: I agree. We got to get you over here and collaborate with all these artists. You mentioned Pearl & The Oysters. I feel like Pearl & The Oysters, Peel Dream Magazine, me, we’re all High Llamas super fans and ambassadors in California for your music. We’re showing people what we’ve got, but also where our inspiration comes from, and whenever people ask, I’m like, it comes from you, comes from before you, the Beach Boys, the Burt Bacharach, all of that stuff, as well. High Llamas really speaks to me because it is taking that stuff that I love from the past and bringing it into the present. Strings with synths, electronic beats with gorgeous pianos, like when you put those together.

Sean O’Hagan: Melodies, at the end of the day. Melody’s still the thing. It’s still the thing at the end of the day. The great melody is the gift. I always think of melody as a gift or a rope. When you throw a rope to somebody, they catch it and it’s like a connection. You pull it, that’s the melody. Melody’s always so important.
