## Body

Patterson Hood writes ghost stories, figures of the past rendered into ephemeral phrases. The neighbors, the parties, the cars, and the late-night haunts all faded into dust; here one minute, gone the next. Withered notions of the South unfurl themselves under Hood’s pen, his songwriting pointed and jagged but nevertheless moving. With the Drive-By Truckers, Hood examines the region he’s called home for most of his life, adding the much-needed edge of his liberal politics to the Southern rock genre. When he moved to Oregon, though, Hood began looking back on his Southern adolescence in a new lens, writing his most personal album yet, Exploding Trees & Airplane Screams. Ahead of his third solo release (his first in 13 years), we sat down with Hood, discussing Muscle Shoals, fiction writing, and Vic Chestnutt. | b arthur

Aquarium Drunkard: I know you grew up in Muscle Shoals and your father, David Hood, started Muscle Shoals Sound Studio and played in the Swampers. Could you talk about your first experiences with music?

Patterson Hood: Obviously, my dad’s had quite a crazy, cool, long career. And people’s perceptions are probably that I grew up hanging out at the studio with rock stars and shit, but that couldn’t have been further from it. Dad kept work very separate, and as a kid, I was obsessed with music. It was kind of a sore spot for me growing up because, you know, I wanted to know about it and wanted to talk to him about it. He’d come home and would not talk about it—it was like a secret society —and as a grown-up, looking back on it, that makes perfect sense. For starters, he was raised during a different era. Parents weren’t supposed to bring work home. But also, it was a dry county, Bible-belt, super conservative place during the Civil Rights era, and Dad made his living playing on Soul records and R&B records with black artists. So they knew instinctively, I think, from an early time on that it just wasn’t talked about. 

Most locals had no idea that such a thing was going on there. And I think that was probably part of the appeal to artists coming in, too, is that they’d be left alone. You know, no one bothered the Rolling Stones when they came to town. So I kind of grew up separate from it, except for Dad’s record collection, which was pretty huge. I’d come home from school and tell Mom I was going to go downstairs and do homework, and I’d put headphones on and spend hours rating the record collection, learning about all these different artists and all these different kinds of music. So that was kind of my education — if a record had a cool album cover, I would dig in to see what it sounded like, which probably has a lot to do with how, you know, how I feel about packaging and album covers and things like that.

AD: Do you remember the first time you expanded out your dad’s collection and found stuff of your own?

Patterson Hood: I started buying records at about nine or 10. I got my own stereo and I spent every weekend out of my great uncle’s house, out on a farm. My parents were teenagers when I was born, and with my dad doing what he did for a living, I spent a lot of time with my grandmother and my great-uncle. My great-uncle bought me my stereo, and I kept it out at his place, where I spent every weekend. But my taste veered off from Dad’s around ‘77 with the advent of punk rock. That was the first thing that I fell in love with, and Dad didn’t like it. He thought it was terrible, a travesty: like “God, their guitars were out of tune,” you know. And because he’s a studio guy, of course, it was awful to him. 

Ironically, as he’s gotten older, he’s grown more accepting of that. I mean, he ended up playing on two or three Frank Black records, and he and Charles became pretty good friends. I can remember playing him [my dad] the Pixies for the first time and him hating it. But, you know, the Clash was a really big deal to me, you know, by high school, and, and then, of course, you know, in college, R.E.M. happened, and the Replacements, the Silos, they were a big deal to me.

AD: What led you back to solo work after 13 years?

Patterson Hood: My friendship with Chris Funk had a lot to do with it. I met him when I first started planning our move [to Oregon]. We moved to Portland 10 years ago this summer, and about a year and a half before that, we came out during the winter for six weeks and stayed in an Airbnb during the winter to see if we could deal with the rain and stuff. I met him during that time, and we instantly clicked. Whenever I’d play shows in the Northwest, he would sit in with me, and we’d play together. We just had this great chemistry that was also very different from what happens with my bandmates. So I thought it’d be fun to explore that. I started keeping a folder of songs that I thought would be cool to explore that way; that I felt like if I did them with the band — while the band would kill it because they can do anything — but knowing the venues we play and the way our show is paced, there’s always a couple of songs on every record that I really love that never get played. Either they’re slower or quieter or more introverted than what the band does and they tend to kinda get lost. I thought it’d be nice to do something with those types of songs, where they’d have their place to shine. During lockdown, when I was cooped up and couldn’t leave the house for a year and a half, I spent some time up in my attic room with demos and songs that were in that folder, and that kind of became the blueprint for this record.

AD: How do you write and view your material—is this solo work in line with the Truckers, or something completely different?

Patterson Hood: I mean, this is my fourth solo record, and each of them has their own crazy backstory. I mean, Killers and Stars came out of a time when the band was kind of fighting, and I needed to do something separate to keep from going crazy. In those early days, there was more of that type of thing, which doesn’t really exist anymore because we’re pretty harmonious as far as the band goes. And, you know, Murdering Oscar came from a lot of songs I’d written that predated the beginning of the band. I’d always wanted to do something with that group of songs, and then I wrote some new songs as a companion piece to them. The Heat Lightning record came through some personal stuff — I mentioned being raised by my grandmother and my great uncle and I spent a lot of time with that generation of my family who were Depression era World War Two kids. They were the last of that part of my family passing on and dying off. I wrote that record as my kind of way of dealing with that. In the years since then, I’ve just been so busy with the band, and honestly, really happy with the band, so I wasn’t looking to do anything else until this project came about. I wanted to make it as different as I could: more so than the other three of my records. That led to woodwinds and strings and flute, things like that. You know, I’ve always had a genuine love and fascination with old analog synthesizers and Chris Funk was collecting some of that type of stuff, too. Some of these songs I wrote on piano as a way of trying to expand myself as a writer and break it out of my comfort zone. And then Chris informed me, a few months before we cut the record, that he wanted me to play the parts on the record myself, which was kind of terrifying. But I’m glad he did it, because he made me do it. I would have gotten someone else to do it. [laughs] And I think forcing myself to do it probably made the record better. It just was further pushing me out of my comfort zone. 

You know, people who don’t play [the piano] play it differently. I didn’t want it to sound like shit, obviously. It also struck me how much my piano playing kind of reminds me of my guitar playing, even though I would hope I’m a better guitar player than I am a piano player. As far as my approach to the instrument and where it sits in the song, there’s a lot of commonality there. I hope to keep doing it and get a little better and better at it, because, if nothing else, it’s fun to write that way.

AD: I know the song “Exploding Trees” started as a short story you wrote. Could you talk a bit about the process of writing fiction and then sort of translating that into a song?

Patterson Hood: I still don’t know if it made a better short story or a better song. It’s hard to make a song out of something like that because there are so many little details that make the story. But at the same time, I didn’t want it to be too long or too drawn out, and I wanted it to make sense whether or not you knew the back story. I still don’t know if that song works or not, other than I thought it made a good entryway to the record because it’s sonically so weird and dense, and it sounds so different from what I do with my day job [with the Truckers]. But at the same time, it might also be the weak link on the record songwriting-wise, I’m not sure. I feel very confident about the songs on this record being some of the best work I’ve ever done as a writer. I think “Miss Coldiron’s Oldsmobile” and “Pinocchio” are some of the best songs I’ve ever written. I don’t quite know where “Exploding Trees” lands in all that, except, it makes a real interesting entry point to it all. So I figured it’s worth something.

I almost put the short story as the liner notes, and there was an earlier draft where I had that, but they were too long. Then, at some point, I flirted with just having the short story and not having liner notes. In the end, I took some quotes off of it and put them in there. I might post it if anybody’s interested in such things. I still haven’t given up on the idea of writing a book at some point. I’ve got a couple of different book projects I’ve worked on, off and on for a long time, but I never seem to get it to a point where I’m quite ready to do anything beyond just picking away at it. But that’s one of my New Year’s resolutions this year. I want to write more than I’ve written in the last five or 10 years because the time I get to spend on my writing has suffered the last few years. During lockdown, I didn’t do a lot of writing during that because I was kinda mentally shut down. I didn’t have a lot of new ideas during that time, because I was just really dealing with my own kind of mental state, but I really wanted to write a little bit every day, whether it’s a song or an essay or just bullshit. I think it would be a positive thing.

AD: When did you first start writing?

Patterson Hood: When I was younger, you know, I wrote all the time. I would sit in school and write instead of listening to the teacher, so my grades were terrible. But I wrote a lot of songs, and by the time I graduated from high school, I’d written over 1000 songs. I mean, literally over 1000 songs, because I wrote every day and as a young adult, my career was a dismal failure. My personal life was fucked up, but I wrote all the time because I didn’t have anything else that was as important to me as writing. But now, I’m super busy. I’ve got a family. I’ve got kids. I tour a lot. Writing music on the road is hard for me because there’s so much music and things happening on the bus or wherever I am, so it’s not conducive to writing a lot of songs. But I can do a lot of just other writing. I’m going to make a push to do more of that this year during my touring because it all helps each other. I’ve noticed when I’m writing a lot — even essays or short stories — when I do have an inspiration for a song, the motors are raised up and turning better.

AD: Does your fiction center around a similar cast of characters: the gritty, Southern gothic type of thing?

Patterson Hood: I think I was always drawn to that type of thing. So I think just seeing that someone can do that, the possibility of like, oh, that, crazy thing in my head that works. Vic Chesnutt was like that for me. There was a certain type of songwriting I gravitated towards, but I wasn’t really sure if it was possible. The first time I heard Vic Chestnut, and actually saw him live, it was like, “Oh my God, you can do that. That does exist.” I would say Vic would be a huge influence on a song like, like “Miss Coldiron’s Oldsmobile.” Even though it may not sound like anything he would do, just the possibilities he showed me from him doing what he did.

One of the book projects — the fiction book project I’m sort of frustratingly working on — is actually a story about musicians. It’s a story about artists and it’s kind of generational, it spans like three different generations. Being a second generation musician myself, some of the things that I’m drawing from might have to do with things that I’ve experienced, growing up with my dad and all that stuff. There’s a character who was a rock star in the 60s and 70s — it gets into some of his backstory in the 60s, but also, the punk rock era. That character’s a strange daughter who’s — for lack of a better way of describing it — a sort of a PJ Harvey type of artist, a really badass artist. I tend to hate fiction books about musicians, because they tend to have the same story arc — that Walk the Line story arc. So if I’m going to write something like that, my top priorities are to not have that in it. And even though, obviously that happens for a reason, because that is sort of the story art for so many musicians. I want to kind of write about artists that transcend that.

AD: A lot of the new album reflects on your adolescence, which you’ve done in the past, but not to this extent. I’m curious as to what sort of led you to reflect back on those moments?

Patterson Hood: I had no idea that this record had a story arc to it until we were almost finished making it. We were mixing it and it kind of dawned on me. It’s like, “God, all of these songs take place before I’m 30, and I’m 60 now.” I turned 60 while I was making this record and some of it takes place when I’m really a little boy. I guess Southern Rock Opera was a bit of a coming of age story — I’ve written a lot about being a teenager and getting fucked up and going to rock shows and how that helped me deal with the angst of my youth. But I hadn’t really written about my earlier childhood, which was really pretty wacky and weird because of the teenage parents, but also being raised by my grandmother and my great uncle and witnessing the generation gap playing out in front of me. It was the 60s and early 70s, you know, my dad was concerned with not wanting to go to Vietnam. The cultural differences between their generation and the other generation of people who were raising me. They weren’t necessarily politically conservative, but they were culturally conservative: they were Roosevelt, Kennedy liberals. They were raised in the church and had those old school values and stuff. It was kind of fun having this record sort of touch on that as deep as you can in a collection of songs. And, of course, “Pinocchio” in particular is an extremely personal song about my childhood and I guess the aspects of it that I still carry with me, even though I’m an old fart now.

AD: Yeah, now that you are in Oregon, do you think the way that you view the South or write about the South has changed?

Patterson Hood: I think so. I haven’t written about the South as much, but my relationship with the South has gotten way better. I had to get away in order to fall in love with it again. I got really just beat down by the politics of it and the bullshit of it and maybe just getting older and feeling like I’ve never been anywhere. I mean, I’ve obviously traveled and I’ve toured a lot, but I’ve never lived anywhere outside of Alabama and Georgia. So experiencing that definitely made me appreciate and love the things that I love about it even more. Of course, the differences between Athens, Georgia and Portland, Oregon have more to do with the size or just the physical location of it. I mean, culturally,  Athens is very similar to Portland. They’re both very artistically oriented, liberal, progressive, hip, cool towns: except Athens happens to be a small town in the South, and Portland’s a bigger city in the Northwest. I would say our elections probably have an almost identical percentage point spread — the liberal candidate tends to win with about 70% or so.

AD: I know you originally wrote “Airplane Screams” almost four decades ago. Can you tell me how that ended up on this record?

Patterson Hood: Well, that song never went away. I kept coming back to it for years and years: Adam’s House Cat worked it up, but never actually played it out live. It was actually the last song we worked up before we broke up. At one point in time, the Truckers worked it up, but we never played it out. It was just waiting for the right approach. Now long after I moved here, I got asked to contribute a song to an EP for the Southern Poverty Law Center. So Chris Funk and I went in, and he brought in Kyleen King to play on it, and we recorded a version of that song. I’d always kind of planned on putting that on this record — even putting that version — but it’s very similar to the version that’s on the record. I think the version that’s on the record is better, but it’s a very similar approach. And we even actually got Kyleen to just do what she had done before, because it was so perfect.

AD: I was wondering if you could talk about the other collaborations on this record — Waxahatchee, Wednesday, Kevin Morby, Lydia Loveless — what was it like working with them?

Patterson Hood: I mean, they’re artists that I love. I mean, I’m a huge Waxahatchee fan. During lockdown, her record St Cloud and Kevin Morby’s record from that year, Sundowner, helped pull me through that year. I played those so much and they just hit such a mood. Her songwriting is so evocative to me. And then Wednesday — actually, Justin from Aquarium Drunkard first turned me on to Wednesday. He sent me a link of them covering “Women Without Whiskey.” I was like, “Oh my god, that is the coolest thing ever.” I became a huge fan of theirs, and we took them on the road with us after the lockdown was over. They’re just sweethearts and I kind of knew I wanted them to play on “The Van Pelt Parties.” I’m in such a wonderful place because I get to play in the band I play in. I love my band and I love them as people and I love playing with them, too. But the downside is that it doesn’t allow me a lot of time to interact musically with other players and other musicians. So this was kind of my chance to get to do some of those things. You know, I didn’t even have a second choice for “Werewolf and a Girl” — it had to be Lydia. That’s what I heard in my head. I was so thankful that she liked the song.

AD: This is the first record you’ve done since Wes Freed’s death in 2022 — such a longstanding relationship between the Truckers and him. Could you talk about the legacy of his artwork and what his work’s mean to you over the years?

Patterson Hood: You know, that’s a heartbreak. I’ll probably never get over it totally. I guess you don’t really get over anybody you lose. We were so close as friends, plus our working relationship — I mean, we considered him part of the band. We would send him demos of songs before we’d record him  and he would be listening to that while we were making the record. We’d send him, send him the roughs and send him the mixes and by the time we would master the record, he would be done with the artwork. That was our working process, and it was so wonderful. I don’t know, when the Truckers make our next record, it’s going to be painful — not having him as part of that.
