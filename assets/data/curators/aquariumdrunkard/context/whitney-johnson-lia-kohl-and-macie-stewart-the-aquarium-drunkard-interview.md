## Body

It might take a decade before you’re ready to make a record. Lia Kohl, Whitney Johnson, and Macie Stewart, multi-instrumentalists and stalwarts of the Chicago experimental music scene, have been playing and recording together since the late 2010s, in duos and on each other’s respective solo LPs. But 2026 sees the trio finally release BODY SOUND, their improvised debut, for International Anthem. 

Assembled from sessions of open improvisation, featuring Kohl on cello, Johnson on viola, and Stewart on violin, with all three contributing vocals, BODY SOUND is a testament to intuition and the receptive listening that accumulates after hours and hours on- and off-stage together. The record stands as 11 tracks of gorgeous, prismatic chamber minimalism whether the listener realizes that nearly every sound is extemporaneous, but that detail provides a key to understanding much of what makes the record, and its three creators’ discographies, so special.

Aquarium Drunkard spoke with Kohl, Stewart, and Johnson about friendship, the sacred, Yoko Ono, tape loops, surprise, improvisation, and more. This conversation has been edited and condensed. | a eleanor

Aquarium Drunkard: How did the three of you meet and start making music together?

Macie Stewart: We’ve all been playing in the Chicago improvised and general music scenes here for a long time. I don’t know that there’s a pinpoint moment of the three of us meeting, but I do know that there’s a pinpoint moment of the three of us playing together, which was around 2017. We did a trio at Elastic Arts in Chicago. It was such a special experience. Since then, we’ve only really been playing like one gig a year. We love playing together, but we’re all really busy, we’re all making a lot of different kinds of music, on top of playing with each other in different permutations. Some point last year, or maybe even a couple of years ago, we decided that we waned to make something more concrete. We wanted to actually record something with this project and do something where we can all collaborate in a bigger way, because it was so lovely every time we would play together.

Lia Kohl: I remember doing a gig around that time at Sleeping Village in Chicago, which is usually a rock venue, and coming off the stage and thinking, “The feeling I had on stage just now with those two is the whole reason why I do everything.” That’s the best feeling ever, and I think it was a shared feeling. It’s so rare. Sometimes you have a great feeling about a gig and other people are like, “Eh, that was fine.” We’re still chasing that feeling. 

AD: Was it a serendipitous decision to play together at those first shows? How much was planned?

Macie Stewart: One of us got offered a gig, and we’d been wanting to play together more but didn’t have anything lined up. Someone suggested we do the trio and it was really fun. It was a bill where it didn’t make sense on paper to have us opening for this specific artist. But then it totally made sense. That’s also why it was exciting, because it felt like where our trio fits in was this really special, malleable place. We can exist in many worlds, which is something that I know is criteria for me with projects that I want to pursue. It just felt really exciting and invigorating.

AD: Did you approach making BODY SOUND similarly to how you have approached playing live over the years?

Whitney Johnson: I would say yes and no. Both live and recorded, it’s fully improvised between us. Three voices, three strings. We started incorporating some electronics, but the first shows were fully acoustic and most of our sessions are acoustic. What’s different [with the recording] is that there’s a heavy hand of production with tape. It’s all recorded to tape, but we’re allowing the tape to kind of break and become a little gremlin. That’s usually not as much part of the live process. Although, maybe in the future we’re gonna do some tape stuff. 

Macie Stewart: It was exciting to work with tape and introduce it to this practice of improvising that we have. All the things that we recorded were improvisations with three of us, but the way that we worked with tape and put things together in the studio also felt like improvising in a way. We had these elements and followed the flow of this thing. Or say, this tape machine’s making a weird noise so let’s put it in [the recording]. It felt like we had little extensions of ourselves with the equipment. It felt like a solid throughline. The improvisation process between the three of us is present in whatever medium we’re working within.

Lia Kohl: I think play is a really good word [for it], as if it feels like we are playing in every context. 

AD: I didn’t really realize how active [using tape] could be in a performative setting until I watched the live video of “Stone Piece.” Whereas when you’re just listening to the record, it does just feel like an additional improviser. With how important improvisation is to the three of you and to your respective creative practices, how important is it that listeners are aware that all the music is improvised? The music will still stand on its own, but I feel like knowing that it is improvised colors how one listens.

Lia Kohl: I think it depends on the audience. For some people, they don’t need that context or it’s not surprising. We did a run of shows opening for Andrew Bird in December and that audience was very much Andrew Bird’s audience—people who are used to songs, people who like things that are composed. So we started telling the audience that it was improvised. I remember hearing the audience gasp, because they were so surprised that people could do that. They were so surprised that this kind of music, which has a sort of composed sound that sounds consonant, was improvised. It’s fun to remind people of that when it’s a surprise. Because then people feel like they’re in on the process and they can follow along with what you’re making in real time, 

AD: Even just hearing after the set ends that it was totally improvised would retroactively make you reconsider everything you just heard. 

Macie Stewart: I think about that in context of recording. When listening to [BODY SOUND], at first it doesn’t feel as necessary to emphasize the improvisation beforehand, but I don’t know, maybe that’s not true. We’ve had a lot of conversations about how to contextualize what we’re doing for an audience, like what’s necessary to contextualize, what isn’t necessary to contextualize. Like what Lia said, it varies depending on the audience and on the moment. I want people to know that our record is improvised, but it takes on a different character when taken at face value.

AD: If someone listens and look into [the record] and find out, “Oh, this was improvised,” then they can listen again with that knowledge and it deepens the experience.

Lia Kohl: I think there’s also something about the instruments that we play that make people think of classical and highly-composed music. It’s fun to turn that on its head and show, yes, we are playing those instruments, but we’re making things up in the moment, 

AD: Lia, you’ve referred to improvisation as a conversation. What differences do you feel when you’re musically conversing with someone new compared to someone with whom you have a deeper musical understanding?

Macie Stewart: Some of my favorite improv moments have been where I sit in and play with someone for the first time, improvise onstage in front of people. You can have a surprise connection with them and you’re like, “Whoa, we’re on the same wavelength, that’s amazing.” I get to know a person so much more in a shorter time through that process. There’s something intimate about that. Playing with people over a longer period of time, I feel like there’s a deeper understanding in knowing what sounds someone is drawn to. You know what sounds you can occupy, you know where your Venn diagrams cross over. There’s always something that’s going to happen depending on the personal spaces everyone is playing in; that creates another instrument to play with. The beautiful thing about improvising with people you’re familiar with is having those comfort zones, but there’s still things that are going to surprise you depending on mood or day or space. There’s a deep comfort there that doesn’t happen when you’re improvising with someone new. 

Lia Kohl: And the depth of that surprise can be really profound. To be surprised by one of you is really wonderful. . 

Macie Stewart: And I feel like that did happen with the Andrew Bird shows. Like, show four, we’re in it and suddenly, “Holy shit, where did that come from?” It makes those moments even more special. Sometimes you think you know where it’s going to go and you just don’t. And that’s beautiful. 

AD: It’s beautiful to relish the unknown. Did each particular piece on BODY SOUND have a specific genesis, or was it more long form? What was that process of turning the improvisations into a record?

Whitney Johnson: These are called from different sessions and some different approaches within those sessions. We had time in a studio at Shirk Studios, we performed at Big Ears, and we had some time at International Anthem headquarters, too. Some of those were improvisations that might have been cut up from a longer session, but some of them were intentional. Let’s have a beginning and end point of the conversation. A couple were layered. We had two different pieces that were transposed onto one another. I think those turned out really nicely in recorded forms. I think of this trio as six elements, three instruments and three voices, then that became 12, so there was a lot to work with.

AD: An instrumentalist is a performer and the instruments are additional performers in the space itself. It’s more than just a trio, if you get a little abstract. The intent was to be open. Was “Stone Piece” the first piece to get on tape? There was a double single in late 2025 preceding the record’s announcement.

Lia Kohl: We had a moment in the studio with “Stone Piece,” putting it through the broken-ish tape machine that Whitney mentioned. We had explored some different versions of what the record could be like—maybe there would be synthesizers, maybe field recordings since we all work with them individually. There are so many directions we could have gone. That moment with the tape machine led us to doing everything with tape. It wasn’t the first thing we recorded, but it was the first thing that coalesced the album. It was like the key.

Macie Stewart: The key to unlock the record, that’s a good way to put it.

AD: Given that the track titles are all references to Yoko Ono’s Grapefruit, it helps put the album in conversation with the idea of a “musical score” writ large. When did the conversation with “abstract” scoring begin, particularly given the tendencies of classical music?

Macie Stewart: It connected all of those things together. When we did the sessions for this record, there were some moments where we were completely open with our improvising. We’re just going to play and record. Then at the end of one of our sessions at Shirk Studios, we each gave a prompt, like a one sentence story. It was really just a setting. 

Lia Kohl: Putting ourselves in a room of thought. 

Whitney Johnson: A sensorium.

Macie Stewart: We would improvise with those stories in mind. That was our “score” for a few pieces. The last piece on the record (“fog | mirror”) was one of those. As we were making the record, a copy of Grapefruit was on one of the tables. We flipped through and it felt connected [to what we were doing]. There wasn’t any intentional connection with it. That book just happened to be there as we were making this thing, as we were working in these processes that felt very similar to how those scores work. Along with “Stone Piece,” this also felt like an unlocking of new understanding, of how we were interacting with each other and with the material that we were making.

AD: With how important it is to the record, how did you attempt to capture the sound and feeling of the invisible vibrational space surrounding you? You can obviously mic the room, but there’s no perfect way to get space on record.

Lia Kohl: We are always capturing the space because we’re always really affected by the space. We all feel that in every musical context. This trio relies on resonance so much. We’re using our voices and we’re using these very resonant instruments. I always feel the space in our playing. If we’re playing in a really dead space, we just play differently. 

Macie Stewart: It’s so different.

Lia Kohl: In a way we’re sort of making an improvised picture of the place that we’re in. It’s less about recording the room tone and more that our improvisation actually changes in response to where we are in a really alive way.

AD: After improvising together for so many years, are there any consistent things that stand out to each of you about the others as sound makers and improvisers?

Whitney Johnson: Back to the idea of improvisation as conversation, a new improvisation can be really exciting. You feel a spark, like meeting someone new at a party. You have this first interaction they’re saying things you’ve never heard before, and it can be almost upsetting, but it could be really fun. There’s a level of intimacy and trust that comes with someone with whom you’ve built a language, almost a culture. It feels like a family. We have a certain degree of trust with one another; without knowing what they’re going to do, you know that something’s going to happen that will carry this thing forward. Sometimes that trust is not there with brand-new improvisations, but it’s always there with this trio. I always trust that something new is right around the corner.

Lia Kohl: You know you’ll be held in that surprise—if you make a new choice, the other two will hold you, saying, “Yes, let’s do that.”

AD: Were there particular feelings or ideas that you wanted to imbue each respective improvisation with?

Lia Kohl: There were a couple improvisations during our long studio day, after we recognized our tendencies towards a chordal space, long shifting harmonies like “Stone Piece,” we decided to change it up and do some more percussive things. But that could go in any direction, and it often did. It would take an immediate left turn.

Macie Stewart: We generally try to keep it open. We try to stay away from forcing something to be what it’s not. Sometimes we’ll set an intention while always knowing that we don’t have to remain there if it doesn’t feel right. If we set out to play a chordal piece or play a percussive piece or if we start with prepared cello or something… it doesn’t mean we have to be there the whole time and center ourselves around it if it wants to go somewhere else. We’re always following the instinct of the moment and really listening to where and when we are, trying to respond to that. 

AD: What is something that you love or appreciate about what you bring to your music and to this trio specifically? I asked Lia that question a couple of years ago, and she talked about always bringing a sense of play.

Whitney Johnson: I always try to bring a feeling of sacredness. When my solo work is at its best, it feels like it has the sacredness. I have a background in traditional sacred music and I don’t take part anymore, but this is sacred, right? It’s set apart from the rest of life. It has a feeling of elevated and energetic intensity. We usually get there during every performance. I feel like the recording has that sacredness to it as well. 

Macie Stewart: Those words, “play” and “sacredness,” remind me of what I love about playing with the both of you. Those things are also important to me and it’s nice to hear them. I try to bring intention to whatever I’m doing, no matter what type of music is being played, no matter if it’s like improvised or composed or songs or literally anything. I try to bring a direct intention into whatever it is, and that’s something that I think we all bring to this trio too. Whatever we’re doing, we’re committed and we’re in it.

Lia Kohl: The beautiful thing about those answers is that we feel as much play and intention and sacredness from each other as we do from ourselves. Those are three good words for what we all do.
