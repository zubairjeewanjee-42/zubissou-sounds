## Body

There seems to be something downright mystical about everything Anaïs Mitchell does. It’s in everything she touches, from her songwriting to her musical Hadestown, based on the Greek myth of Orpheus and Eurydice, which she has staged both as an indie/folk production (featuring Justin Vernon of Bon Iver and Ani DiFranco) and a Tony-award-winning Broadway show. 

Themes of family and community also course through Mitchell’s work, showing up in her collaborations with others, like the Aaron Dessner-led Big Red Machine and, of course, her folk rock supergroup Bonny Light Horseman. She and the other two members of Bonny Light’s core trio Eric D. Johnson (Fruit Bats) and Josh Kaufman have forged a unique yet timeless sound that evokes, at once, a spiritual gathering in the woods and a warm, homemade meal shared among friends, merging traditional melodies and motifs with more modern experiences drawn from the goings-on of their current lives. 

Their most recent effort Keep Me On Your Mind/See You Free, a double album and their third release to date, blends conversational songwriting and a cast of characters culled from our collective mythology to expand upon their already rich brand of storytelling and music-making. Anaïs joined us via Zoom from her family farm in Vermont. | m h coons

Aquarium Drunkard: Whereabouts are you now? You guys are touring, right?

Anaïs Mitchell: We’ve had a little smattering this summer. We just came back from two shows in Colorado, so I’m home now, in Vermont, on this family farm where I grew up. We don’t have anything else for our band until October, when we start to really tour the West Coast, and then we’re going to Europe after that. 

AD: How do you approach determining the set list for a tour with all of your new material, while also trying to incorporate some of the older stuff?

Anaïs Mitchell: It’s so fun to have this many songs, because when we first started touring, we had put out one record, and we really didn’t have any other material. We’d get to the end of the set, and it was like, “That’s all, folks!” But now there’s a lot more to choose from. Eric is kind of our setlist boy. Josh and I will give feedback on it, but Eric enjoys it, and Josh and I don’t enjoy it, so that’s one of the jobs that Eric has taken on. We’re playing a few songs from the first (self-titled) record that feel like mainstays of the set, and then from our second record, Rolling Golden Holy. And then, kind of like a rotating cast of characters from the new record, some that always show up, and then some that are kind of like, “Oh, this night feels special. Let’s try this one out.” And there are some that we have never yet played live. We’ve only done one two-week tour so far, and then these festivals, which tend to be shorter sets. We have a lot of range, you know. Constructing the arc of the set list, I think Eric thinks of it like the hero’s journey or something. He’s got a lot of theories about how to put it together.

AD: It’s so interesting, the vocabulary that you’re using: Eric puts the set list together like a “hero’s journey,” those songs create this “cast of characters.” You’ve been hard at work for many years on Hadestown. Do you as a band share an approach to thinking of songs in terms of stories and characters and mythology?

Anaïs Mitchell: That’s so interesting, right? Maybe my language has been influenced by my time in the theater world. At one level, Hadestown and Bonny Light Horseman are extreme opposites. When I was working on Hadestown, Bonny Light was this ray of light in my world, because [with] Hadestown, the task was very specific: we need to fill in this arc for this character, and it has to rhyme and only take one minute, because it’s going to affect the rest of this song or whatever. And [there were] a lot of people weighing in, so there was a level of scrutiny and tightness to that where Bonny Light was like “let’s get in open D tuning and just trance out.” Especially in the case of our first record of traditional music, it felt very relaxing and easy and flowy compared to the kind of rigor that was going into Hadestown. 

But when I pull back the lens, I also see that they’re kind of the same. I think I always am interested in the intersection of what’s “trad,” what is ancient and universal, and then what am I feeling right now? Or what are me and my friends feeling? We started out as a band reinterpreting or reimagining these ancient characters or stories, and then when we started to co-write, we were still sort of reaching back in the way that the Grateful Dead did, certain bands where they’re writing contemporary songs, but they’re grabbing this cast of characters, like Casey Jones shows up in a song. Or like in our song “Mountain Rain,” John Henry shows up. These touchstones, we feel them. We don’t even know why. They’re in our DNA, just like the Greek myths. You don’t even have to have read the Greek myths to know the scenarios and the characters that come out of them. This new record, because it’s so sprawling, there’s a lot of stories on there. Some of them are my story, some of them are Eric’s or Josh’s story, and then, more likely, they’re a kind of a patchwork of all of those things, including what comes from the ancient.

AD: You really do get a sense of that. You even trade off singers within the same song, There’s an interweaving of stories, almost a conversation between them.

Anaïs Mitchell: You just hit the nail on the head. A lot of times when we were thinking about the writing and the recording—what this record is—it felt like a conversation. We described it sometimes as a dinner, some kind of epic dinner party with your friends, where someone tells their experience, and then someone [else] is like, “Oh, hey, and then this,” and you sit among the wreckage of the of the plates and the leftover food and the wine, and you just kind of keep going. It felt like [that] was happening with this long, two disc record.

AD: I feel like the song that encapsulates that for me is “When I Was Younger,” where you sing, “I used to seek pleasure. I used to drink wine… Now I’m rocking a baby who’s always crying,” then Eric sings, “I used to dress fancy…”

Anaïs Mitchell: We really did reach back to the trad for that. There’s a whole genre of songs that are basically that song—“Single Girl, Married Girl,” is one of them—and we were like, “Oh, we want to write a song in that genre, but let’s make it a duet so you can hear from two people.” And I do have two kids, so I have logged some hours rocking the baby, but Eric doesn’t have kids, and he wanted his verse to feel like something that was true for him, that he could sing from his experience.

AD: It’s very conversational, that song. It feels like two people catching up.

Anaïs Mitchell: Yeah, it’s a conversation, and then it’s kind of like there are no more words, so Josh just takes the guitar. It’s his moment to speak in the conversation.

AD: I’m so interested in the role that collaboration has played throughout your career. You go from solo folk artist to playwright, creating this cast and staging a production. Then it seems like you’ve dipped into all these different collaborative spheres, from the residency that created Bonny Light Horseman [37d03d, pronounced “people”] to Big Red Machine and all of these different ways that you’ve come together with other musicians. I read that you used to host something every Tuesday night where you would have people gather and you would all play.

Anaïs Mitchell: Yeah, it’s been really important to me. I will say, I do really enjoy just going in a room and writing a song for myself. There is something very pure about that. It’s you and the Muse and you’re just trying to figure it out. But what I love is that [by collaborating] you gain access to something bigger than yourself, which I think is what we’re after anyway, the experience of losing yourself or blurring the edges of what is you and what is someone else. With Bonny Light, especially, I’m able to bring 100% of who I am and what my gifts are to the table. Then these other people are doing the same thing, and we’re writing songs as a band that I would never write myself, making records as a band that I would never make myself. Sometimes, when we’re playing, when it’s at its best, Josh takes a guitar solo, and I feel like it’s me taking the solo, because it’s us, and I’m feeling every part of what’s happening. I’m less in my head. It feels like an experience, like it’s easier to step out of myself and into a channel. I’m really grateful for that. 

I’m just inspired by other people, and I want to have a conversation with them. [With] the Big Red Machine stuff, that project is so mysterious, because Aaron kind of knew what was going on, but nobody else knew. Everyone else was working on one or two tracks, like, “here’s a lyric for a chorus,” but he had this whole tapestry going on. [For me] it was like, “Oh, I’m talking to my friends,” writing my part of this tapestry that they’re weaving. It was inspiring, definitely.

AD: Almost like you’re recording a voice memo to a friend.

Anaïs Mitchell: I love voice memos. I’m really on a voice memo kick right now—for my girlfriends, mostly. You do that?

AD: Yeah!

Anaïs Mitchell: We call it a podcast.

AD: Yeah, it is like a podcast. They get long.

Anaïs Mitchell: Also, as you said, I come from the Folk world, very wordy and heart-oriented. My chakras of influence are my heart and my head. And I love gaining access to playing with the band, playing with the rhythm section, and writing tracks that are coming from all the other parts of the body and the spirit. It’s fulfilling to plug into that.

AD: I totally understand what you mean. When I’ve done some somatic, bodywork stuff, I feel a lot of activation at the solar plexus and up, then really nothing at the root level—just blank, nothing going on there. I can see how connecting with the rhythm section would create an embodied experience, because that’s where we feel the drums.

Anaïs Mitchell: Oh, that was also really true of my collaboration on Hadestown, because I had these two incredible orchestrators, Michael Chorney and Todd Sickafoose, that worked on the music from very early on when I started writing the songs, and brought this kind of jazz/art-rock/New Orleans sound to the Music. It felt like it really became part of the character of that music.

AD: Yeah, it’s cool to hear each version of the Hadestown music, because each occupies a different sonic space.

Anaïs Mitchell: It’s so funny. Working on a theater project takes so long, especially when you start like we did in the music world. So there are so many versions of it. People watched us evolve in public and had lots of feelings about it. Every record that came out, people would be like, “What happened to the third verse of ‘Epic Three;’” “what have you done to ‘Wedding Song?’” But in order for it to become what it was trying to become, it had to go through this process of evolution.

AD: And you wrote a book about it to give people even more insight into that journey (Working on a Song: The Lyrics of Hadestown).

Anaïs Mitchell: It’s funny, one of my jobs now, which I totally accept and enjoy, is that musicians reach out to me all the time who are trying to write musicals. It’s like every couple weeks—someone called me yesterday. Having come through that experience, it really feels gratifying to be able to share what that was like, because they’re very different cultures. They’re after the same things, [to] move people with storytelling and music, but it’s just culturally super different. I wrote that book like, mostly as therapy, because I had spent so many years of my life working on Hadestown, and I came out of it, and was like, “What happened to my 30s?” But then I realized maybe it could be helpful for other people that are trying to do the same thing.

AD: You give back in that sort of way and shepherd other people through the process with your experience that you have now—talk about “the hero’s journey,” that’s really what that is!

Anaïs Mitchell: I love that.

AD: You’re passionate about people’s rights and feminism—these different activist parts of your persona. I’m just wondering, with that lens, do you perceive a difference in how it’s received when you collaborate with Bonny Light Horseman versus when you’re the one fronting the band?

Anaïs Mitchell: What do you mean?

AD: Not to diminish the fact that you are such an integral part of Bonny Light Horseman—it is very much the three of you, and you are a crucial part of that dynamic. I ask this mainly because I’ve been a part of my husband’s band, and I’ve also played solo acoustic, and I’ve definitely noticed a difference. I wonder if you notice a difference.

Anaïs Mitchell: Hey, that’s really interesting. Okay, so the first thing I’ll say is that it’s an intention of mine to become more focused and clear in terms of my activism and my speaking about things. God, this news cycle is so insane and it’s hard to keep up with how to actually make an impact in the forums that we have. And I do think it’s easier, obviously, for me to speak as Anaïs Mitchell than as the band, because you don’t want to speak for someone else. But when the legislation overturning Roe v. Wade passed, I was on tour with Bonny Light. We were opening a tour for Bon Iver, and we were, like, in Nashville or something. And it felt like I can’t not say something on a microphone. [If] I have a microphone, I have to say something on it. And I think actually, those guys were glad, because maybe they felt more comfortable to have me do it than one of them, but everyone was feeling it. It’s interesting in terms of gender with that band, because there’s ways in which I love being the woman in the band. I have a voice that’s different—and that’s literal and metaphorical—and then there’s also really a deep way in which I don’t feel different from them. I feel like we’re all friends and artists, and I don’t wear my identity as a woman, a feminist, at the front of that band. For me, it’s like we’re friends and artists, and we’re having an incredible time losing ourselves together by creating this music. And that feels so healing, to not have to be standing in your identity all the time.

AD: Thank you so much for going after that question. As I was asking it, I was like, “Oh, this is kind of an intense one,” so thank you for being brave.

Anaïs Mitchell: Thanks for asking.

AD: And I just want to say that the perspective that you do lend to Bonny Light as the woman in the band—or the one speaking for caregivers, let’s say, because Josh has kids.

Anaïs Mitchell: Josh has three, actually. Three kids.

AD: Oh, beautiful. So you’re coming together, speaking as caregivers, on the new record

Anaïs Mitchell: That’s almost, like, his role in terms of the storytelling. He has this beautiful, deep connection and love for his wife, his kids, his family life. Just to brag on Josh a little bit, he works so hard. He’s just making tons of records all the time, on very little sleep. And then he’ll walk into his house from his garage, and he’ll make, like, three meals, because his little twins won’t eat the same thing that his older daughter will eat, and then, for his wife and us. And he’s, like, cleaning the kitchen while he does it. He’s a really extraordinary homemaker, actually. 

AD: That’s beautiful. I love that. 

Anaïs Mitchell: Yeah, it’s very cool. He sings that song, “Rock the Cradle,” on the new record. It feels very Josh. It was funny, because the line “and keep the baby warm” is a trad line, and he was singing it, and by accident, he said, “babies,” plural. This was right when we were about to record it in Ireland. And we looked at each other and we were like, “Oh, yeah! It’s the babies, because he has two.” So it just felt like he was singing it entirely from his heart. There’s a lot of eggs on this record, also. I think the frying of eggs gets mentioned, like, three different times on this record. And it’s great, because Josh is a very excellent egg fryer.

AD: That’s very cool. Fertility symbolism as well.

Anaïs Mitchell: There you go. Yes. I wonder if you ever have talked to Carmel Holt. She has a podcast called Sheroes. She’s in the Hudson Valley, so she’s a good friend of Josh’s, and I’ve interviewed with her multiple times. She also is, like, really into astrology. Carmel would totally slot in with the chakra shit and Joseph Campbell shit and the connection between the frying of eggs and the fertility symbol.

AD: I think I’ve actually heard of her podcast.

Anaïs Mitchell: It’s really cool. I interviewed with her, and she was like, “So what does it mean that both of your bandmates are Geminis?” I was like, “Wow.” Like, she’s too deep in it.

AD: I’ve just started learning about astrology in a deep way recently.

Anaïs Mitchell: I kind of love all that stuff. And I’m also into Tarot and stuff, and I never was before. And I wouldn’t say that I am studied in it or anything, but I love that it’s just these archetypes. I think that astrology, at its best, is like that also. It’s like an invitation to the archetypes as they pertain to you and your experience.

AD: Yeah, for sure. I think of them as prompts, like a potential lens through which to experience your day. 

Anaïs Mitchell: Exactly. And since you just said that, I’m going to also say that I use that exact same language when people ask about the title of our record, the new record. They’re like, “is it two different albums, and one is called Keep Me On Your Mind and one is called See You Free?’ And I’m like, “no, they’re alternate lenses through which to view all the songs on the record.” Josh put it very simply one time: He was like, “you want to be held and you want to be let go,” you know? 

AD: Ooh! Yes!

Anaïs Mitchell: Yeah. That’s both in a romantic sense and also in the scope of your mortality—all kinds of ways that that can be understood.

AD: Oh, absolutely. I have to ask, now that you mentioned that you are into Tarot, is there a card that holds special meaning for you, that stands out, that you’re drawn to? 

Anaïs Mitchell: Can I say two of them? 

AD: Yeah.

Anaïs Mitchell: So, one of them is kind of maybe obvious. I’m going to say The Fool, the very first card. Eric and I thought about that card when we were working on, “I Know, You Know.” It has that line, “I’m a fool if I love you, and a fool if I let you go.” The Fool has this energy of total innocence and freedom—like Zen Mind, Beginner’s Mind, kind of—so I like that card for that reason. The other one that has come up for me a few times for some reason this year is the Eight of Swords. It’s got a picture of a woman, she’s blindfolded and bound, and she’s surrounded by a prison of these eight swords. But my understanding is that the energy of the card is that it’s a prison of her own making. She could choose to walk away if she wanted to. And I think that’s powerful.
