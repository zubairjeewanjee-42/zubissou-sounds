# Roedelius :: The Aquarium Drunkard Interview

## Body

When asked about the casual way that Hans-Joachim Roedelius, Dieter Moebius, and Michael Rother came together as the kosmische supergroup Harmonia in the German countryside of rural Forst in 1973, Rother described the group’s genesis as “easy action.” That term—action, but at ease—keeps coming as I listen to 90, from Rother’s one-time bandmate Roedelius. Released last last year, it was compiled by composer Tim Story, who painstakingly combed through Roedelius’ private archives to select 50 tracks featuring recordings of piano and Farifsa organ paired with an Echolette tape delay. These records are especially intimate: in them you hear the late Moebius in conversation, as well as the sounds of other media, and the sounds of his family, including his kids and wife, Christine Martha Roedelius.

Echoing the sound of his Selbstportrait—”self portrait”—LPs at times and leaning into more classical motifs at others, 90 isn’t a summation of Roedelius’ work at age 90 so much as an examination of the deep immersion over decades that allows his music such effortless grace. “Time is a fiction,” Roedelius tells me near the end of our Zoom call, Martha echoing in agreement. The idea is not escaping aging of course, but remaining in touch with the boundless timelessness we all have access to—that is if we allow ourselves the peace to experience it. | j woodbury

Aquarium Drunkard: 90 is a fantastic collection. You recorded this work privately. What has the process of sharing it with people felt like?

Hans-Joachim Roedelius: My best friend in the U.S. is Tim Story, who dared to try to find out how many works I’ve recorded. I rounded up a lot and he did a lot to save and digitize it. That’s the reason why the boxset exists. We spent a lot of time together to make it available for the public.

AD: The music was recorded on a reel to reel machine over the course of decades. Along the way, did you revisit the music or ever pull stuff out and listen to it over the years? Or did you mostly record it, and put it away?

Hans-Joachim Roedelius: All of it produced hundreds of thousands of kilometers of tape. It’s all still here, and there’s a lot yet unreleased. But what shall I do? I’m so fruitful. [Laughs] 

AD: Has it always been like that? Have you always been that productive?

Hans-Joachim Roedelius: Yeah, but not just by purpose. Just by the way it happened, it fell from heaven and came to me like a gift.

AD: In materials that Story wrote about this project he calls it an “audio diary.” Does that sound right to you? Does it feel like this is something of a diary for you?

Hans-Joachim Roedelius: Well, it is. I think every piece is a snapshot of my life, and of being together [with my family] in reality. All these tracks are snapshots done somewhere. And within the normal real work I had to do during the daytime. And also overnight, of course.

AD: There’s a kind of shared sensibility I hear in the early recordings, that overlaps a lot with the sound of Harmonia and your Selbstportrait albums; did you ever have a period of recording the same time each day?

Hans-Joachim Roedelius: It just came to me and had nothing to do with time. It came to me during the normal way I lived: any work I’m doing, walking the dog, bringing trash outside, doing something for Martha. The idea had to come. I couldn’t think about it before, it just arrived. Everything came to me as a gift of the moment.

AD: One thing I like is how non-hermetic these recordings sound. You hear radios, the kids, the sounds of life around you. It doesn’t feel isolated at all. 

Hans-Joachim Roedelius: Yeah, it was all integrated in my normal life; normal life is part of the game. The family noise, everything that happens, it’s the stuff of daily life. 

AD: When I spoke with you in 2015 about your band Harmonia, you told me much of the feel of that music you made with Dieter Moebius and Michael Rother came out of living your lives in rural Forst. Getting water and chopping wood—normal life gave way to experimental music.  

Hans-Joachim Roedelius: It was our profession, but we loved it. Harmonia, Cluster, solo, we were always into it. 

AD: You recorded to a 2-track Revox; you could only multitrack by bouncing down. So you end up with all these layers on top of layers baked in. I’m curious if you found in your experiments that you liked the sound of those things blended together? 

Hans-Joachim Roedelius: It was just fun. And I was adventurous because…I never had enough money to buy new tape. You have to reuse and reuse again.

AD: The early recordings feature your signature sounding Farfisa organ. Do you still have it? 

Hans-Joachim Roedelius: No, it’s at the Eboardmuseum in the south of Austria. I could rent it if I wanted to do something with it, I could get it. 2020’s Selbstportrait Wahre Liebe was the result of my label asking me to go back to those times and play with old gear. I didn’t use the Farfisa, but I did use old gear. 

AD: You had a very tumultuous youth. You were conscripted into the Stasi and forced to appear in propaganda films and spent time in jail. Eventually you are set free and Germany has to go about re-imagining itself. In the late ‘60s, you helped found the Zodiak Free Arts Lab. What was exciting to you about that moment? 

Hans-Joachim Roedelius: We had to find out how to do it. We became more aware of what was possible. To get tones and sound. It was like a university Zodiac Free University—where we could learn what we could do. To become aware of the importance of it, the relevance of it, because there was already a public—every night was crowded. Everybody participated. Everybody was invited to come bring themself into the game. 

AD: It was aggressive music—banging on metals, cooking pots, anything you could use. So much of your music since has been so peaceful and even healing. How does that shift start to happen? 

Hans-Joachim Roedelius: I’m a physiotherapist myself, that’s my first profession. I had to touch bodies and had to listen to people. More and more, I became aware of how important nature is, how important freedom is, how important peace and love is. It all came together and brought me to a different level of awareness. Which I really appreciate very much. I’m still on that level. I’m still learning. How to be still, to listen to what the inner voice is telling me in the moment. I work with resonances: strings on the grand piano, especially. I go by my own interest, by curiosity, just to create something which brings people to unity and love.

AD: You converted to Catholicism in the early ‘80s. Does music feel spiritual practice to you?

Hans-Joachim Roedelius: Yes of course, but it’s not the purpose of it. It just happens through it. I’m living the way I want to be treated by others. I think that’s the normal way: everybody should look to find unity, love, peace, understanding, and communication with others in the best way. That’s the spiritual thing .

AD: Does music feel like a form of prayer to you?

Hans-Joachim Roedelius: It’s like a prayer. If I’m really relaxed then I fall into a sort of trance. It’s as if I wouldn’t be there anymore. My ego is gone.
