## Body

Lifeguard emerged out of the doldrums of the pandemic period, when Chicago’s artistically-inclined young people found themselves forced to fall back on their own resources. Instead of sitting around, bored out of their minds, kids were forming bands, making zines, booking underground shows and connecting with each other outside the regular commercial channels. The scene became known as Hallogallo, a nod to Neu! but also a reference to the original German meaning of the term, “dance party.” It spawned a raft of scrappy young bands, Lifeguard, Horsegirl, Friko and Post Office Winter to name a few. 

Lifeguard — that’s Isaac Lowenstein, Kai Slater and Asher Case — started playing together in the early 2020s, and very soon after that, began recording and playing out. Their first full-length, Dive, was a DIY released cassette, their breakout Crowd Can Talk / Dressed in Trenches a mash-up of two self-released EPs packaged together by Matador. 

So, in some ways, the latest Ripped and Torn is Lifeguard’s first proper full-length, conceived and written and recorded as a cohesive stand-alone project. It is, perhaps, a bit less frenetic and angular than earlier material that earned comparisons to Fugazi and Unwound. It leans a little harder into the melodic, chiming guitar sound that was always there, but obscured by scree. 

We recently caught up with Lifeguard’s three principals, all quite young, all passionate and well-informed about music and music history. Here’s our conversation, lightly edited. | j kelly

Aquarium Drunkard: I know you guys got started during the COVID lockdown in what sounds like a pretty exciting scene in Chicago. And could you tell me a little bit about how that bubbled up and your connections to some of the other bands? 

Isaac Lowenstein: We actually started right before the lockdowns. We had like a few months of playing together right before it happened. COVID created this weird rift in local music. All the kids who were having shows, the high schoolers and even the college students, a lot of the house venues kind of got shut down. So, there was this absence for a moment of young music being made. Or maybe it was just like, you know, a moment of something new, coming up. 

We spent a lot of our lockdown, you know, in the months when we could start playing together there, just like playing over and over and over again, and writing and recording and putting stuff out. That’s what we and all of our friends dedicated most of that time to doing.

AD: No sourdough? 

Isaac Lowenstein: Yeah, exactly. Yeah. You know… I did start making electronic music too, so I, you know, I’m not completely short of a funny COVID hobby. Once we kind of came out of the lockdown, it felt like everyone was dying to hear live music again. So, it was us and Horsesgirl and Friko and Post Office Winter. There was a heightened excitement around live local music. 

AD: The scene is named after a Neu! song, “Hallogallo,” which is an interesting reference for guys your age. And I was wondering if you could talk a little bit about your musical taste and how it’s developed. 

Kai Slater: Yeah, I came up with the name for that. We had some bad working titles like some like astronomical references and stuff that would have been really cheesy. I just remember being in a forest preserve with my old band, and we were just like we were just humming the motorik beat the whole time we were walking. It just seemed the pulse of the group. The term “hallogallo” is an old German phrase for dance party.

It’s always been like a big thing for the music to be danceable. The whole scene is about having a space for young people to dance and have bodily autonomy in the absence of weird social norms. So having it dancing-centric in that way seemed important. 

AD: Okay, but you guys did come up pretty early on some fairly obscure music, I’m guessing. 

Kai Slater: There was kind of a big explosion for me around that time. Like, I had just started writing songs. But I had been going to the record store near where my dad was teaching as a professor for a long time as a kid. I would just pick CDs based on the album covers. And I remember, Krautrock was a big thing for me because there’s so many great symbols in those albums, like the can on the Can album and Neu!, all that stuff. I think that just happened to be a big influence purely based on going a record store and seeing the covers and being like, what is that? It’s so mysterious and iconic looking. 

AD: Do the other two of you have some weird out-there music that you were into early on? 

Isaac Lowenstein: My sister and I both kind of got really into music at the same time, so I feel like a lot of my taste discovery happened with her.

AD: She’s the one in Horsegirl. 

Isaac Lowenstein: Yeah. Two of us would bounce things off each other a lot. All the time that we would spend together just like one on one, like if we were waiting for the train to go somewhere, we would be talking about records. I discovered a lot really cool stuff like that, scouring the internet alongside my sister. Early things that I got really obsessed with? I got really into Cornelius. And growing up in Chicago, Tortoise has always been such a thing. I knew about that as a kid, but it wasn’t forced on me. Tortoise was a big introduction into the whole non-normal rock sort of thing, involving more electronic influence and interesting production. 

AD: I feel like they’re the American motorik band. 

Isaac Lowenstein: Yeah, yeah, yeah, for sure. It was elements of rock, but it felt like you could track so many other worlds coming from Tortoise. Like their contemporaries, the Stereolab records with the production. 

Asher Case: I was going to definitely bring up some of that Chicago music. I was raised in a Chicago musical household. 

AD: You’re the one whose dad is in FACs, right? 

Asher Case: Yeah. The way it manifested was that those were my parents’ friends. I was one of the first children to be born to those people who were making music at that time. And so, they were just the people I was always around. It’s always been a very intrinsic thing about being at home that there are weird records on and interesting music happening. That infiltrated my sensibilities, I guess. 

One of the first major obsessions I had that was not related to my parents or to what was always around me was, Bowie. Finding Bowie was really interesting for me because it was this type of music that was very removed from any type of, like, locality. It wasn’t anything that was close to me, but it was something that I related to so much. That influenced how I treat, like, a lot of the more experimental stuff I’m into, too. Arthur Russell was a big touch point for me. The connection was that it’s theatrical, but also very abstract. I really like the more electronic Bowie. And, that stuff set up my interest in instrumental music also. 

AD: My sense is you were just frantically putting music out at first, just EPs and singles and tapes and just all kinds of stuff. And I think at the time, I thought you were probably using the recording process to work out what you wanted to sound like. Do you think that’s true? 

Kai Slater: Yeah, a central part of how we grew as a band was that we kind of didn’t wait to record. We were very inspired, and everything that we made, we once we finished it, the logical next step was to record it. That was usually recording ourselves and self-funding, all of that. Instead of being like, let’s shop it to a bunch of labels and see who picks it up so we can record in LA for a bunch of money. It was important that we just did it and kept on doing it. It allowed us to evolve to have amazing musical chemistry as like a band. 

AD: Do you feel like you’re there now? 

Kai Slater: Yeah. I mean, you’re always growing. Who knows what will happen? But being able to record or almost being forced to record ourselves and self-produce, created a strong foundation. We always care about the production and how our records sound. That has crossed over into everything we’ve put out. 

Isaac Lowenstein: After our first EP, everything from there on out, we were very passionate about making it sound like exactly how we wanted to. Kai is giggling because he knows I think our first EP sounds bad. But it applies to everything now. Even doing Ripped and Torn alongside Randy Randall, we are crazy nitpickers, you know, about the production process. I’m sure at points it almost became annoying when we would try articulating this stuff and Randy would have to receive that. But production has been essential to us as a band for a long time.

AD: This is either the second or the third full length, depending on how you count the one that spliced the two EPs together. How was this one different? I assume you’re at a different level of success and have access to different tools. But how was it different doing this one from the ones before? 

Asher Case: I feel like in terms of writing, it was different because we went into it being like, this is going to be our record. And like before that, with the first record, Dive, it was just sort of like, these are all the songs we’ve written, and now we’re going to put them out. 

The EPs were written separately, like two different things. I don’t really think of those as a record because they’re pretty different and from different times. They’re from different headspaces completely. But with this record, it was like, okay, we have to write this whole record. And there was even a song that we put out a couple months ago called “Ministry,” and we were thinking about putting that on the record. Then it was like, no, that doesn’t make a lot of sense. It should just be all of these songs that we are writing with the intention of releasing together. That was something that we hadn’t done before. That led us to have a lot of additional ideas and like using more instruments and stuff, which is a pretty big part of the record, I would say. 

AD: Tell me about that. What were some of the other instruments? 

Asher Case: Well, for one of them, I mean, there’s a couple songs on the record with a baritone guitar instead of a bass. That’s a new addition. I don’t remember exactly how it happened. I had access to one and brought it to practice, and then we wrote a couple songs with it. The song “Under Your Reach” that we put out last week is the first song we wrote on that. 

It was like a pretty quick sort of like click thing. There’s also a synthesizer that Isaac plays on a record. 

Isaac Lowenstein: It’s an organ. 

AD: What kind of organ? 

Isaac Lowenstein: 1970s Italian combo organ called the Crumar. 

AD: Oh, cool. How did you find it? 

Isaac Lowenstein: Well… Funnily enough, I didn’t own one when we were making the record. There was just one in the studio. But a few months ago, I came across the most insane Facebook marketplace deal. $200. 

AD: Oh, wow. So now you have one. Now it’ll be on all the records. 

Kai Slater: Right. Yeah, it only has like 18 sticky keys. 

Isaac Lowenstein: Yeah, shut up. Shut up. It’s perfect. It’s beautiful. 

AD: When you were saying lots of instruments, I was thinking, I didn’t hear any strings or oboes or anything like that. So, I’m relieved to hear that I didn’t miss all that. 

Asher Case: Lots of instruments are like more than three, you know. 

Isaac Lowenstein: Also, auxiliary percussion became a thing for this, whereas that wasn’t really around in the previous recordings. So, there’s lots more arrangements on top in general. Also, there’s a lot of dub echo stuff being thrown about. That almost counts as an instrument in itself. 

AD: I felt like this record really brought forward the kind of fuzzy, chiming, melodic element of your songs, especially in “T.L.A. and “How to Say Deisar.” There’s still plenty of turmoil, but also kind of a rough-edge New Zealand tunefulness, almost a Bailterspace vibe. Would you say that’s true, or am I, you know, making shit up? 

Kai Slater: Oh, definitely. 

Isaac Lowenstein: Definitely, yeah. Absolutely. 

AD: Can you talk about why that appeals to you? 

Asher Case: I feel like all those like New Zealand bands are very like buried in my subconscious in terms of influence. Like I don’t really think about playing like them because it’s such a specific sounding genre or version of rock. But definitely with the way that the baritone sounds on “T.L.A.” and just playing the baritone in general, definitely, I feel like I’m tapping into that. With the pitching. There are two strings on that guitar that are pitched the same, and then they fall in and out of tune with each other, like, quickly. It makes this, like, really sort of chorus-y effect. 

Kai Slater: Yeah, I remember going for a Toy Love “Swimming pool” type of thing. As a guitarist, I have homed in on what I want in my guitar tone. When I started playing more 12-string guitar, I was really influenced by Robin Hitchcock and the Soft Boys, that amazing sound. It’s very cutting kind of razor blade punk guitar, but also has so much room for like jangly, chime-y guitar. 

The baritone really helps kind of create a sort of wall of sound with the instruments together. This album is essentially completely mono, so the wall of sound idea was kind of a big thing. 

AD: I think it’s in the vocals, too. 

Kai Slater: Yeah, for sure. And with the vocals, we were playing with fading into the guitars and harmonizing and stuff like that.. It’s been like a really awesome thing to grow with Asher and get a blend together. 

AD: You also continue to play with noise a good bit, for instance, in “Me and My Flashes” and that buzz that opens “Under Your Reach.” Can you tell me a little bit about how you like to use these harsher, more abstract sounds in your songs and why they’re important? 

Isaac Lowenstein: Initially, we had a plan to sort of make this long piece to sit somewhere in the center of this record. It’s supposed to be a 10-minute kind of improvised tape splice thing. We ended up abandoning that. We realized that the record was meant to have a pop at the center of it. If you look at the three pieces, that are the noisiest on Ripped and Torn, all of them are very short-lived and very immediate. 

There’s not really the like brooding, slow onset quality that music with those descriptors usually follows. 

Asher Case: They’re kind of blink and you’ll miss it sorts of things. They’re just there for as long as they take to get the point across. 

AD: Noise interludes. 

Isaac Lowenstein: Yeah, but although yes, they basically function as interludes, we tried to never call them interludes. To treat them as songs essentially. I mean, yeah, in every typical definition, they’re interludes. But in terms of actually constructing them, we were trying have as much intention with them as every regular song on the record. 

AD: Okay, that makes sense. 

Asher Case: Interlude kind of dumbs it down to stuff that you were doing that you have on the end of the tape that you throw in between the songs. But these took a long time to write and then record and then make as small as they are. 

AD: So, they were longer, and you boiled them down? 

Kai Slater: I just wanted to mention the Guided by Voices influence, just the tape-iness and found sounds. That was a big influence. I recently read the Robert Pollard book. It talks about Robert Pollard accidentally dubbed over some tapes with his own songs. He became obsessed with the idea of sound collage. I think that was a very big part of what we were trying to do. We recorded the album on tape and a lot of it on cassette, too. That collage-y element of it is super important to making a full-length album

AD: Where does that come through the strongest?

Kai Slater: It’s more just how it works as a whole I guess. The way “Charlie’s Vox” runs into “T.L.A.” doesn’t feel like a big, like, prog rock piece. It’s supposed to feel like an accident or something that just happened by chance, because a lot of it is improvised music. But it was very intentionally made to sound like that. 

AD: I really like “It Will Get Worse,” which is the first single. Can you tell me a little bit about how that song came about? 

Kai Slater: We started doing, like, “In the City” by the Jam and really getting back into classic Buzzcocks. We’ve always been into that stuff, but we were getting better at writing as a group and effectively making pop songs. We had this fast kind of poppy but not a pop punk song. Like a punk song with pop sensibilities. 

AD: I also like “Under Your Reach” a lot. 

Kai Slater: With both “It Will Get Worse” and ”Under Your Reach,” the vocal harmonies became a really big counterpoint to the abrasive punk music. 

AD: It’s really a neat thing, because you’ve got different parts going on, the bass and the drums and the guitar. They’re all sort of bouncing off each other, but also kind of cohering. And then this pretty complicated vocals crisscrossing over each other. Really, there’s a lot going on, but it kind of comes together. 

Isaac Lowenstein: Yeah. I would say actually those two songs are kind of the rallying cries. Both of those were written, and we were like, these are going to be the ones that the record is about. 

AD: I wanted to ask you, you’re doing a lot of things that hark back to this earlier age in music, writing print zines and sending out cassette tapes and, you know, all this DIY stuff. Do you feel a connection to that sort of 1990s lo-fi period in music, or is it just that’s what you do?

Kai Slater: I think it’s just what we do. During COVID, the computer was the last place you would want to be in terms of any creative pursuit or connection. It seemed antithetical to do anything on a computer. So, I was very drawn to making the zine and recording analog and stuff like that. And I think it’s carried over since then. It’s an amazing way to make art. It just feels stylistically and aesthetically super in tune to what we’re influenced by and what we listen to and what we want to do. And it’s just like the process is super important. 

Printmaking is just a very important revolutionary tool and it’s very important, in the day and age that we live in to remain connected. Forms of communication like that and playing shows, things that bypass the digital age. 

AD: What do you guys think makes a great song a great song? What’s the difference? 

Isaac Lowenstein: If it makes you excited to play… 

Asher Case: Yeah, if it makes you feel like you want to dance, if it makes you like, if it sticks in your head, if it’s something that you can see for the first time and know that it’s good or know that you have a body response to it. 

Isaac Lowenstein: Also, if it makes you be like, why didn’t I think of that, you know, when you hear it? 

Kai Slater: Or you think it’s a cover when the band plays it because it’s just an instant classic. 

AD: Yeah, yeah, there’s something about the way we recognize them immediately. 

Kai Slater: I find that to be the best compliment always. Like, what was that? Was that a cover? That’s like, oh, my God, I made it. I made a hit song. 

Isaac Lowenstein: But never a cover of a specific band. This is the thing. 

AD: Because you can’t figure out what it reminds you of. 

Isaac Lowenstein: Right. Mysteriously kind of timeless but clearly learning from all of pop.
