## Body

Since reviving the Pedro the Lion name, songwriter David Bazan has been relentlessly digging through his past, trying to understand the people, events, and evangelical Christian upbringing that shaped him as a man and an artist. In 2019, he released Phoenix, which began a planned five-album arc covering Bazan’s passage from age 13 to 21, a period in which he discovered girls, rock music, and a world beyond the church. Last month, he released the third album in the series: Santa Cruz.

In these songs, Bazan wrestles with a close-knit family that loved him but, nonetheless, left him lonely, and all enveloping community that blocked out the modern world. He describes the slow dawning realization that music could lead him out into a more authentic and satisfying life. First The Beatles, then The Cure, U2, Nirvana, and Fugazi—each provided him a glimpse of other ways of existing, still driven by purpose, but not hemmed in by authority and a high control religion. Conflicted feelings about the people he loved—a disciplinarian dad who imparted his love of music, a mother who believed in him and his band, even as she worried about his soul—knot up in these verses. Nothing is simple. Self-knowledge is won painfully, insight by insight, song by song, as Bazan tries to look at his past head on.

“Maybe it’s trauma or things that I had buried for so long because I thought I needed to. It’s a process of bringing all this forth out of myself,” says Bazan. He joins us to unpack the album and what fuels his creative drive. | j kelly 

Aquarium Drunkard: I’m really enjoying this new album. This is the third in a series of very autobiographical albums. You’re going from 13 to 21 here, a really formative period for you in terms of discovering the outside world and women and rock music. How far are you going to go with this? Are there going to be records about you in your 20s and 30s?

David Bazan: Yeah, I think the next one is going to go probably 20 years and there will be one after that. What drives me to do this isn’t that I want to present my whole life. I’m trying to search through it all and understand how I ended up the way that I did, at a series of very low points between 2016 and 2019. The self-interrogation process that the records are doing have helped already, enormously. That’s the arc of it. 

 AD: It must be personally wrenching to go through all of this stuff again. How are you doing with that? 

David Bazan: Each time I make my way through a record, it’s such a relief and so freeing. Maybe the process itself can be overwhelming. Maybe it’s trauma or things that I had buried for so long because I thought I needed to. It’s a process of bringing all this forth out of myself. With Santa Cruz, it was a struggle somewhat to make it, but just getting to the other side of it, it’s so freeing and such a huge relief. 

 AD: It seems like it was kind of a lonely life for you. You were already outside of things because of the religion, but also you were always moving. Did you feel like you were lonely?

David Bazan: I did. There’s a way that loneliness can work where there’s always people around. The experience that I was having was something I was experiencing by myself, because of a few tricks of the light and misunderstandings within family and not being able to put roots down in a certain way. Some of that had to do with moving and some of it had to do with extreme people pleasing. Or a series of self-betrayals because I was trying to do right by the authority in my life. It added up to a really crushing loneliness.

Part of it is really trying to understand the complexity of that. There were times when there was a lot of affection and attention coming my way from strangers or people that I didn’t know very well. But I couldn’t metabolize or hold onto that because of the basic isolation that I had lived in up until that point, in terms of a bad bounce in my family. 

I don’t know how it is for other people, but there are times that something that can happen that is disappointing or crushing and just coincidentally, maybe nobody knows about it except for one other person and then you never know that person again. There are all these little episodes that I would carry with me. I was thinking at some point that I would develop the kind of friendships where all our reminiscences and stories that we would tell each other over trips or drinks, that it would all get touched on. It just never quite worked that way.

 AD: You never really found your people?

David Bazan: In some ways, I did, but it was in touring. And touring is a really isolating life, too. You’re always moving from place to place and meeting new people. I think that the primary relationship I got into was through marriage, which was definitely a construct from purity culture. I mean, everybody gets married, or at least a lot of people do. But the way that I went about it was definitely by the book. The Christian purity culture. Also, it’s a “for life” thing the way that I grew up with it. There’s really no excuse to get out of it. Even cheating. You’ve got to forgive. I’m not implying anything like that. But just how permanent it was once you stepped into that relationship. I really was prevented from finding, more than anything, the “my people” of myself. 

I was always just falling short of what the people in my life needed so that that was my priority. I was never taking care of my own needs, but really going out of my way to take care of the people around me. 

That’s a part of this process, too. It’s me saying, you know, I need to do this for me. It might not work for anybody else, this record project. Every time I’m reminded that I’m making a five-record autobiography, I think, oh that sounds terrible! What a bad idea! But what it is for me personally is what I need, and what I want to make and express creatively. In and of itself, it’s me choosing what I need versus what the market might prefer. 

 AD: I think one of the themes in this record is you discovering music outside of Christian rock. There’s this wonderful image of you leafing through albums at a friend’s house and discovering The White Album. And you recognize it because you’ve been warned about it, that it’s got this demonic message in it. I’m wondering about the push and pull of that. Having heard all your life that rock and roll is dangerous and evil, and yet you felt such a powerful connection.

David Bazan: You know, I think that when we grabbed the record and started to do the backwards masking—playing it backwards to hear “Turn me on, dead man,” in “Revolution #9”—I was curious what it would feel like. Would there be an ominous spiritual feeling, some kind of powerful, beyond feeling, and when we heard it back? It just seemed kind of dumb. Like this isn’t anything real. This is just some kind of scaredy cat baloney. So then when we put it on and spun it forward and played the record, there was such a depth of playfulness and joy and creativity that I couldn’t experience as anything but what I experienced it as. It was so different from what I was told. I was aware that maybe there was something insidious that I couldn’t pick up on right then, but the joy that I had, I hadn’t really felt that quality of joy or being stirred. 

It took me years to unpack and maybe I haven’t done it all the way, but the difference in feeling that I got listening to the Christian music, that I did love. I don’t want to suggest that as a seven-to-eight-year-old kid, I didn’t love those records that I found and bought. I didn’t have anything to compare it to, but I really did connect. But then when I put on the White Album and heard, “Birthday” and “Your Blues” and “Mother Nature’s Son” and “Everybody’s Got Something to Hide,” it was just so much…something so totally different. I just wanted to hear it more and more. I immediately was obsessed. And it felt good, not bad. 

 AD: What were some of the first big records for you at that stage of your life, besides the White Album?

David Bazan: I did have to put the Beatles away a few months later because I was walking around the house, singing “Your Blues,” kind of absentmindedly. My dad, hearing his eighth grade kid singing, “I’m so lonely I want to die…” It beautiful for me. It made me feel connected. It didn’t make me want to die. It made me feel less lonely. You know the way it works? But what he heard was very troubling, and so he was like, “Hey, I’m sorry but we can’t have you immersed in this music anymore. You can appreciate it periodically for its musical merits but you can’t…” Because at that point, I had a cassette with “Birthday” through the end of that side of the cassette on one side and the 1967 through 1970 best of on the other “Strawberry Fields” through the end of the B side of that cassette. It was in my auto reverse Walkman, and really, all waking hours that’s what I was listening to. 

I had to put it away again and it wasn’t until sophomore year of high school that I got…I just finally petitioned my dad, “Can I please sign up for this Columbia House thing?” And when I signed up, it was in the jazz genre, maybe to appease him that it wasn’t all this lyrical rock and roll, but that it was a lot of instrumental jazz. But I did get Joshua Tree and Disintegration by The Cure. 

It was that year, in 1991, that Nirvana’s Nevermind came out, and I started listening to that. And The Cure and U2 and Depeche Mode and the Violent Femmes. So when I started listening to the music, it was basically current alternative hits in 1991 and 1992. 

Also Fugazi, which wasn’t alternative hits, but my friends who I had started playing in a band with when I moved to Seattle. I heard Fugazi for the first time in my friend Brian’s car, and I was like what is this? And he was like, “It’s Fugazi, you’ve never heard them?” It was Steady Diet, actually. So I got to hear In On the Kill Taker, having consumed the previous two or three full lengths, depending on how you calculate it, and so they were huge for me. And as a Christian kid, listening to very message driven music, it was a good steppingstone to music that wasn’t about a message. Fugazi was occasionally very message driven.

 AD: They were a super moral band.

David Bazan: Very much so.

AD: And so was U2 in its way. 

David Bazan: Very much so. 

 AD: You were looking for people who were serious about things.

David Bazan: Growing up the way that I did, there was this sense of fidelity that I was reaching for or trying to embody. I think that’s been something, a factor throughout my life, as my faith shifted in my late 20s. What is my fidelity to, if it’s not to this idealistic set of religious beliefs? I think there’s still a sense of trying to understand what I’m being faithful to as I go about my life. So Fugazi and U2, there’s a seriousness and a sense of fidelity to something that really spoke to me. The Cure was a great band to listen to that wasn’t especially driven by message. They were driven by love narratives. But those were stepping stones and pieces of my listening portfolio that I really needed to have to kind of anchor me as I was changing. 

 AD: You mentioned your father, and I think he’s such an interesting figure in this series, because he’s obviously very strict and in some ways he keeps you away from the music that you want to play and listen to. But he also sort of begrudgingly lets you play drums on “First Drumset” and now on this one, you’ve got him teaching you to play a couple of chords. How much did he support you in your music and how much did you have to rebel?

David Bazan: I mean, you’re really right about the…I don’t know if dichotomy is the right word, but nuances about how both my parents were. They were very strict. But they also were very supportive at intervals. My dad was a music pastor. He spent his life playing music and performing music. My mom also is a singer. You see them in the record Phoenix in the song “Piano Bench,” sitting side by side and my mom’s singing and my dad playing. They were immersed in music. They were very musical people who love music. 

On this record, it’s in the voice of my mom on the song “Spend Time” where I was ashamed to tell people that I was taking seriously the idea of being in a band for a living. It seemed unrealistic and outlandish but they both were supportive of the idea. My mom said, “You should tell them what you’re doing. There’s nothing to be ashamed of here.”

It was my dad who, after two years of college, he picked me up at the airport when I flew back to where they lived for Christmas break. And he was like, “Hey, have you ever considered taking a semester off school and just pursuing the band?” 

My grades were failing because I was having band practice twice a week and I was having shows twice a month. I was very motivated and very invested in the band, and I couldn’t care less about school. I was shocked that he suggested this. But it was also practical. I wasn’t doing well. I was spending money that didn’t need to be spent. I was failing. And so I did. And I think I understood that it was kind of coded. Like, why don’t you take a semester off, wink, wink. Let’s see how it goes. Everybody knowing full well that once I took off in that direction, there was no stopping me. 

 AD: There’s so much warmth toward your family in this album. It sounds like it was a difficult situation, but it’s so clear that there was a lot of love between your mom and your dad and your sister and you. I especially like “Parting” where you’re finally breaking free, but you’re describing your mother with such love and gentleness. Did it make it harder, the fact that you were so close to your family, to find your own way.

David Bazan: Yeah, very much so. I’m so glad that that comes through. I hope I’m including that. But writing lyrics and songs in this way, it’s hard to know what comes through. I’m really thrilled that it does. That is how I feel. 

But I think within high control religion, in this case evangelical Christianity, there’s such a strong value placed on the kids growing up to believe roughly the same things that the parents do. Maybe sociologically, or whatever, it’s really difficult for kids who grow up in evangelical Christianity to grow up different from their parents while still maintaining some kind of positive relationship. The parents feel like failures on a cosmic level if their kids fall away from the faith that they were supposed to raise them to be. 

I think I felt that pull without even knowing it the whole time. I really wanted to do right by them and please them. And some of that is because of the scarcity that can show up in families without anybody doing anything wrong or trying to …just wanting to be taken care of, so how do you do that? But to try to please them. I had a really…more than any of my friends, I had a really healthy respect for authority, even while I was being kind of crushed by it. So yeah, it took me longer than some of my peers to think my own thoughts about certain things. I think it was really hard to differentiate from them. Even when I was making records that were kind of horrifying to them. 

AD: Well, if you’re not horrifying your parents between the ages of 15 and 20, what are you doing? 

David Bazan: Yeah, exactly. 

AD: So the lyrics on this album, and really all your albums, are really wonderful, sort of rough and conversational, but super specific and very evocative. I’m wondering if the music part of this record also refers to this time in your life? There’s some sort of abstract guitar music when you discover The Beatles and then this crazy dance beat in “Don’t Cry Now,” and I’m wondering if those are nods to the music you were hearing about this time?

David Bazan: I think so. You kind of set directions and markers for a creative process, and then pretty early on, I just have to let go and play around. Kind of see what comes out based on those ideas. Part of it is that I want each of these records to sound and feel like the place and time as I experienced them, which is a very vague set of criteria that I’m just trying to feel out as I go. Also every one of these songs came from a little creative burst. Like “Teacher’s Pet,” I was staying at a friends’ house and we had spent a couple of days together. Me and this husband and wife who are my very good friends. My ride was going to come pick me up in about a half an hour and we were all packed up and just said our goodbyes. I said to my friend Charles, “Hey, can we just go into your studio and make something real quick? Like for 20 minutes or half an hour?” He was like, “Yeah.” 

So I went in and I laid down this bassline, a drum idea and a vocal, and then asked him to lay down a guitar lick, and then we made a mix. And then my [ride] showed up and I was gone. And this stayed in a folder for two years and over that two years with 30 or 40 or 45 other little snippets. Coincidentally, I had every piece of the song in that one minute in a series that I thought was a weird verse form. I just had to tease it out and stretch it out. 

And every song came from a little burst of creativity like that. Each of them are playful and somewhat aimless. I didn’t have the sense that “I’m writing a record right now.” I just was logging these little bursts of creativity. So when it came time to find the pieces that would link up with these themes, I just scanned through this list of very playful moments with myself. That has a different feeling, and I’m glad because I think it coincides with the teenage hopefulness that I had and that energy of meeting new people and getting crushes and just the wonderful pastime of just shooting the shit with your friends on the youth group trip or cutting class or just the joy of being a teenager, even amidst all this loneliness. I was very hopeful that it was going to land right eventually. 

I do think that the 1990s music experience found its way into this stuff.

 AD: It sounds like you, as it always does, but it just seems that there are these snippets that might be memories themselves. 

David Bazan: I think so.

AD: Do you have any favorite bits, like lines or sounds or moments on this album, things that really resonate for you?

David Bazan: We’ve been playing nine of the eleven songs every night. It’s kind of changing. 

I’m playing drums in the band on this tour. Playing drums live for the first time on this tour. So this song “Tall Pines,” which I like a lot in general, has that line “What am I going to do/follow in my father’s footsteps.” Which was a real thing. I was thinking of music ministry or just being a pastor in general. I was very immersed in that world, and those were paths that I saw other band members do. And then, “Or maybe music work I haven’t dreamt of yet/I hope it’s drum set” like I love that line. Because I was sitting in the pews at church when it occurred to me, before I ever played guitar. I want a life in music, and I’m a drummer, so I’m pretty sure I want to play drums for a living. That’s what I want to do. And so to be playing drums on tour in the band that is my livelihood, singing that line every night…the first few times I sang it, I really was affected emotionally in this way that I loved. It was like continuity. 

And then, you know, “Modesto” that was such a formative experience for me, differently than The Beatles. The Beatles was a general thing that pertained to the whole world. But in that moment of hearing that Grandaddy song “Taster” in 1994, it felt like such a personal summons. Not because I wanted to sound exactly like Lytle’s music, but because Lytle…it struck me that he sounded so much like himself. That was the summons. You can sound like yourself. He was writing about the losers. He was writing about people struggling. And I only heard that first song but then on all of the rest of the records, the first song on Western Freeway is about a loser. Maybe I don’t want to use that word, “loser.” But someone who was left out, having to leave the place that they loved and were not accepted. And then “He’s Dumb, He’s the Pilot,” “Did you love this world? Did this world not love you?” I didn’t know what was to come from his music, but I saw in the DNA of that one song, his deep sense of being present with broken hearted people. And so to be playing that song now, and to …I don’t know. That one hits really hard for me. It also was something that I was tentative about doing.

I had the idea for the song, and then I thought, oh, I can’t really do this because it’s too corny or it’s too on the nose or it implicates this band that’s a real band that people can know about. I feel embarrassed. But what really won out in the end was my intense love for that music. And the important role that it played in my development as a person. Just right place, right time. So to pay homage to that. Like the Beatles, hearing them for the first time, and then something so much more personally important. I don’t know. It was a risk that I had to work myself into taking, and it feels vulnerable in a way that, I’m just really glad I did it now. 

AD: I know you said that you make this music for yourself, and I get that, but I’m wondering if you’ve had any contact with fans and how they’re reacting to it, and what, if anything do you hope that people get out of the music? 

David Bazan: I think there’s a way that it’s worked for me several times. Maybe I have the perception of an audience in mind. Like the record Curse Your Branches, which was a David Bazan solo record. When I was in the process of making that record, the first solo full-length, which, in my mind it was an opportunity to, again, recast myself, maybe as somebody who’s not just singing about God and marriage and alcohol. But what was coming out, uncontrollably or what rhymed when it came out, was all these songs about that stuff. I called my manager, Bob, and I was like, “Buddy I am really sorry but I cannot stop doing this. I wanted to do something different, but this is all that comes out.” And he was supportive. He was like, “make what you make.” So it was me turning away from the audience in my mind and just tending to my own needs, thinking well, shit, this is my chance to connect in a different way, and I just can’t do it, so I’m just going to connect with myself. Maybe a third or a quarter or ten people will resonate with this. This weird middle space of not rejecting faith but also writing about it obsessively. 

It turned out that so many people were already in this space, and so we all got to share about it together. So I think I have that at the back of my head. I just put my head down and do what I need to do. It’s not going to be huge. But there will be other people that connect if I am true to what I need. And because of the hole that I was trying to dig my way out of, personally, mental-health-wise, emotionally, at the start of this process, I was really trying to connect with my subconscious and in a sense to get my subconscious to trust me again. So that we could be close.

When I was writing Phoenix, I would hit these dead ends, and I would turn to my subconscious and be like, look, if everybody loves this record but I haven’t satisfied your criteria for this, I will be unhappy. So please, just tell me what to do. I think there’s a process of being thoughtful and careful with these projects and guarding against self-sabotage or external sabotage to come in and steal or betray the process by people pleasing. Slowly I had built trust with my subconscious in a way that is palpable and healing and stabilizing and grounding. That’s such a strong motivator to get down with myself and try to make deeper inroads. Hoping, trusting that if I do that that people might connect.

I have had both of those experiences. I’m way more connected with myself than I was at the beginning of this process. And also I’ve gotten to talk to people about their own journeys and how they connect with the records. Even talking about it, I just feel really lucky and really grateful that it has landed that way.

AD: I know you’re touring now. Are you working on anything else that you want to talk about, side projects or collaborations.

David Bazan: I’m in a band called Overseas with Will Johnson and the Kadane brothers. 

 AD: Oh yeah, are you doing another record?

David Bazan: Yeah, it’s almost done. They’re waiting on me to finish some lyrics. 

AD: I really like the first one.

David Bazan: Thanks so much. I met Will later and knew his work once I started making records and stuff. But with Bedhead…Bedhead and the Kadane brothers and The New Year, they were early heroes in my late teens.

AD: Those New Year records were so good.

David Bazan: Oh my gosh, they were everything to me. So to meet them and become buddies and to be in a band with them…even for them to be irritated with me because I’m running behind on things, it’s a dream come true. 

AD: That’s funny. I look forward to that. You have kids now, right?

David Bazan: Yeah.

AD: So how does this whole digging through your past process affect the way you interact with them, or does it?

David Bazan: I think it’s just more understanding. In a way, this process is me re-parenting my kid self at various stages, just sitting with that kid and seeing him and offering love and support to that kid who, in a lot of ways, is still kind of stuck back then. So it gives me an understanding of how to interact with my kids more. Rarely do they need to be told what to do or how to think about a situation, but just to be given attention and care and support and listening and validating their feelings and being that support. I can see how it works now with myself and with them. A little bit more all the time.
