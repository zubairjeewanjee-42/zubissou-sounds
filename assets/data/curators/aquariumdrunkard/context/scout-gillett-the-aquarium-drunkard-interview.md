## Body

As an artist, Scout Gillett knows what she wants, and she has embraced vulnerability to enter full command mode with her second album Tough Touch. From the variety of sounds she fuses on this batch of songs to the musicians she has now incorporated into her band from her “build-a-band” project to the personalized details (even her own handwriting) that adorn the vinyl and its cover, Gillett has been hands on every step of the way, and she glows speaking about what she was able to accomplish in transmuting the pain of the last few years of her life into this latest musical offering, her second LP since 2022’s No Roof No Floor.

Growing up in Kansas City, Missouri, Gillett was steeped in the DIY, punk, and hardcore scenes that surrounded her, absorbing a multitude of influences that she still incorporates into her work. “I don’t want a record to sound like what everyone else is trying to sound like,” she says, and that means keeping her various inspirations close, allowing Neil Young, Lucinda Williams, and The Deftones to share space in her imagination. Gillett joined us virtually on a sunny Wednesday morning from her home in Los Angeles. | mh coons

Tough Touch by Scout Gillett

Aquarium Drunkard: How long have you been in LA?

Scout Gillett: I’ve been in LA for, like, a year and a few months. I moved here in October of 2024.

AD: And how long were you in New York before that?

Scout Gillett: Eight years.

AD: How is that feeling for you?

Scout Gillett: It’s feeling great. I was spending time over here for the last two years, subletting, and I really wanted to live here. It just felt natural, and I felt like I had already had a life here. I felt like there was more for me to explore. I love New York so much. It’s the best city in the world. I was just tired and burnt out, and I felt like the slower pace and openness [of LA] was gonna really align with me and help me.

AD: And you’re from Missouri originally. Do you feel like geographical change has influenced the sound of this latest record in any way?

Scout Gillett: This latest record was mostly recorded in Austin—one track was recorded in Vermont and one was done in LA—but the tracks all have different soundscapes to them. A lot of it was written in Brooklyn. This record in particular really pushes a lot of boundaries, but leans into my sound and what makes me unique: catharsis and having arcs to songs and exploding sounds. There’s definitely a few rockier tracks, but still a lot of emotional and vulnerable moments. 

AD: And you mentioned taking kind of a build-a-band approach to constructing the album.

Scout Gillett: Yeah, that was very interesting. I toured a lot after No Roof No Floor, and I was just trying to find ways to make it make sense to keep playing shows in Nashville, LA, New York, and Kansas City (where I’m from). So I had this kind of old school idea of how Elvis or Jerry Lee Lewis would build a band, and I built a band in each city. I had to trust and be open to hearing what people had to offer, but also being able to articulate what I was needing and wanting to hear. These songs were played out live quite a bit. I was really stoked about trying to get to know them on a deeper level. Some of these songs were recorded three times in studios. 

The first record was amazing. We all got together and did this record, and it just came together—and that was beautiful in its own way. But I really wanted it to be different and unique and to have a certain sound. When I finally found the band I wanted to play with for my European tour, I was like, “Well, I’ve got to record with this band.”

AD: So all the pieces had to kind of all together, and the right versions had to come about with the right locations.

Scout Gillett: Exactly. The guitarist for the record, who’s still playing with me now, Omar Schambacher played on the record, and I met him on the build-a-band tour. I was like, “Wow, you’re the best guitarist I’ve ever played with, and we have so much chemistry. Let’s do this.”

AD: Do you feel like the musicians that you were able to play with helped you to push boundaries in your sound?

Scout Gillett: I think so. It really took a lot of intense listening; voice memoing every single practice, every single thing; re-listening to it; going back to other versions of the songs, and being like, “Oh, I actually liked when this player played that part, so I’m going to try to incorporate that.” A lot of the hooks and leads I wrote myself, but I also liked to give people the freedom and trust to be able to express themselves. I felt really lucky to know people who helped connect me with people. It was just completely word of mouth, like, “Hey, I know this player in Nashville. Do you know anyone who would want to play the show?”

AD: What were some of the things that you wanted to really push yourself to do when making this record?

Scout Gillett: I wanted to push myself to trust myself more, to trust the fact that I am a writer, and these lyrics mean a lot to me. I discovered so much about myself and others through making the record. It’s really about, after you’ve had a lot of heartache or pain or grief, making that hard choice to still push through and be vulnerable—how challenging and rewarding that is.

AD: Anything in terms of the instrumentation or sounds that you were reaching for?

Scout Gillett: I definitely wanted it to be grungier and rockier than the first record. Sonically, I think I was influenced by a lot of people: Neil Young, Hole, Nirvana, Lucinda Williams, PJ Harvey, and even—-as crazy as it sounds—Alice Cooper. [Laughs] 

AD: You know what? I believe that, because, as I was listening back to your discography, I caught your Deftones cover of “Change (In The House Of Flies)” on your EP Imagination, MO. I was like, “Wait, why do these lyrics sound so familiar?”

Scout Gillett: [Laughs] I definitely grew up a little rocker, and still am. It’s a big part of my background and history, being in Kansas City growing up and doing DIY shows—hardcore and punk and all that. It’s definitely within me.

AD: Yeah, I had to ask, because I was absolutely enchanted with and kind of haunted by that song when it was really popular back in the day. Why did you choose to cover that one?

Scout Gillett: I think that song also really, really affected me. What’s cool about that EP is there’s a lot of covers on it. When I’m writing, I try to keep up with playing covers, and that was just one that came to me, knowing people who are suffering and knowing you have no control or ability to change or help them, and how hard that is. My dad sings with me on that track. It was so sweet. I covered it in my bedroom, and was like, “Oh, I really like it slowed down like this. I think this could be a ballad, but still be epic.”

AD: How do you interpret that song?

Scout Gillett: I mean, yeah, it’s like when you have an idea or perception of someone, um, and then then it changes. It’s a really hard thing to process and navigate from. Being a musician and a human being, I know a lot of people who suffer, or who have changed, or who have gone down the road of being an addict, and it’s a really hard thing to witness. Sonically, when it gets big, it’s all strings and balladry, but still really emotional. At the end, it almost just sounds like a hammer, or like you’re nailing the point down, like “You’ve changed.” I can’t do anything.

AD: Yeah, and I guess I’m wondering, too, if there was something meaningful about having your dad on that song in that context of what that song means?

Scout Gillett: Yeah, I think we both know someone who struggles, and it was very cathartic for us to work on it together. We didn’t have to explicitly say, “This is about this person in our life,” we both just knew, and I think he felt really emotional about it. We both cried.

AD: Wow, that’s amazing. That’s such a sweet thing to be able to share.

Scout Gillett: It was really special. I had never recorded with him, and it meant a lot.

AD: Going back to this record, you do so much to vary your sound track by track. On all of your releases, this has been something that’s been consistent, whether you’re throwing in a lower humming part on a chorus, or a weird synth sound to be kind of a character in a song. What compels you to add that kind of variation? What are you seeking from that?

Scout Gillett: So often, artists get pigeonholed into being like, “I’m an Americana artist,” or “I’m an indie rock artist,” or “I like this record, so I’m gonna make mine sound just like that record that I like,” and I really try to push myself to explore and to let each song have a world within it. No Roof No Floor definitely is a “safer” record. I just got the test pressings for this new record and listened to it, and after listening to it so many times through the mixing, recording, and writing process, I feel really sure that the record makes sense, even though each track has a little world [of its own]. I like that people can’t really identify my genre, because it’s not like it’s just all one sound or you can kind of daze out. My record is meant to be listened to; it’s not background music. If someone wants to do that, that’s great—you can play it in the background—but I think I wrote the lyrics, these songs, to be heard.

Tough Touch by Scout Gillett

AD: Do you have a certain kind of emotional vocabulary as a songwriter, in how you write the different sounds that you attach to the lyrical content? Do you feel like your melodies and the sounds that you choose have a consistent pattern in drawing out certain emotions? I notice you use a lot of pitch glides and interesting melodic jumps.

Scout Gillett: I think so. A lot of my tracks have a longing: a longing to live, to be seen, to be heard, to be loved, to be understood. Whatever I’m trying to write definitely displays in the sound or in the way I choose to sing. I also think that the kind of crooning choices that I make—or belting—is certainly attached to the lyrics. My favorite song on the record is “Tough Touch,” which is why I chose to name it that. It’s the first one I wrote for the record. That song basically covers all the other songs, in a sense. It’s about choosing vulnerability and learning that you can open yourself to being vulnerable in or out of a relationship, on your own path. That song has many dynamics, and it’s my favorite to play live, for sure. It’s the one that the crowd resonates with the most, because [of its intimate lyrical] moments. It’s about the challenge of trying to find your home in this world, your home within yourself and your relationships. 

AD: It seems like there’s a sense of victory at times.

Scout Gillett: Definitely. When I’m writing a song, usually, I’m working through whatever, and then I interpret it, change it, or mold it. But there is a sense of like, “Aha! I think I figured something out,” within my own journey. “Gonna Change,” the third track, says, “exist inside the gray/ where nothing stays the same.” It’s like you’re acknowledging that things are always going to change, but asking, “How can I continue to strive to be in that area that’s not black or white?”

AD: That’s a really interesting point, because you’re covering these difficult emotional journeys on this album: a couple of breakups, an injury—is there more?

Scout Gillett: Touring, family, leaving my record deal…there was a lot of change in my life, and I just kept writing.

AD: At some points, the songs do have kind of a more jubilant, upbeat, tenor to them. And then you do also let that little bit of new metal influence eke through.

Scout Gillett: Yeah, that “Cherry Blossoms” solo is so rock. [Laughs] But I appreciate it because, no shade to anyone doing their thing at all, but so many records or songwriters sound the same, I’m not gonna lie. There are so many great musicians, too, and I’m stoked about a lot of new music, but I don’t want a record to sound like what everyone else is trying to sound like. I just have to be authentic. Already, writing out the third record, I have a whole other idea. It’s definitely going to be less like genre bending, but that’s what I wanted this record to be, because it was about learning about boundaries and finding yourself.

AD: What are you excited about right now?

Scout Gillett: I just signed on with the new manager, and I’m feeling really good about it. I’m building my team out and going to Europe in March with the band, getting my third record together, and touring. I don’t have that many US tour dates yet, but I’m hoping that it will. And the vinyl, I’m excited about that.

AD:I s there anything that you want to share in particular that you’re really proud of concerning the vinyl release?

Scout Gillett: I’m really proud of how it sounds, it sounds like it’s meant to be heard on wax [while reading] the lyrics. I put together the album packaging, and used my handwriting, and I’m really proud of how that turned out. I took control over this record.
