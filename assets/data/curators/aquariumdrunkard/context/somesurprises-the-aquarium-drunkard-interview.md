## Body

somesurprises began modestly, with Natasha El-Sergany singing her songs into the Voice Memos app on her phone. With the band’s latest record, the excellent Perseids, they’ve moved into a positively widescreen space, delivering an immersive collection of songs that buzz and float around El-Sergany’s lush vocals. It’s a dreamy sound, occasionally reminiscent of such legends as Stereolab, Grouper, Mazzy Star or Yo La Tengo. But it’s not just dreamy. Beneath the gorgeous drones and sweet motorik pop-rock, there are plenty of sharp edges, both sonically and lyrically. With a US tour looming in May, we hopped on Zoom with El-Sergany recently to get the lowdown on Perseids (among other things). | t wilcox

Aquarium Drunkard: I saw on your Instagram that you caught Laetitia Sadier on her latest tour — what did you think?

Natasha El-Sergany: It was amazing. It took her a little while to get settled into the backing tracks she was working with. But then it got really, really weird and cool. She played trombone and did some lip-synching. It was actually inspiring because she did some guitar looping stuff, just layering on atmosphere — basically like producing a song live for us. I loved seeing her do all that alone and pull it off magnificently.

AD: Has Stereolab been a big influence for you over the years?

Natasha El-Sergany: Yeah, definitely. Stereolab is kind of the default artist my partner and I will listen to when we’re on a drive. I’ve heard so much of their music — of course, I couldn’t name more than five song titles [laughs]. I just put Stereolab on and I know it’s Stereolab. Some of the earlier stuff from the 1990s that was a little more kraut-y is my favorite.

AD: I’m always finding new things in their catalog — it feels kind of endless. But hey, somesurprises! It’s been a little while since your last release. Are you always working on the band, or does it come and go?

Natasha El-Sergany: We only stopped during lockdown. By mid-2021 we were back at it. During 2020, I had a hard time picking up a guitar for a while. But I started going to a practice space every week to work on things by myself. I was lucky to get a residency at a place called Crybaby Studios in Seattle. It was this beautiful room that I had all to myself. I just loaded in every single instrument that I own and would force myself to go multiple times a week. I ended up demoing some stuff that turned up on the new record. I really tried to get my bandmates to work with me remotely, but they were like, “No, dude, sorry!” [laughs] But once we got in a room together, we started working on new things and getting ready for shows. We’ve never taken a real break.

AD: You’re an attorney in addition to being a musician, right? What’s the balance there — can you separate those parts of your life pretty easily?

Natasha El-Sergany: I used to try to compartmentalize, but I’m working on trying to be a more integrated person. I work really hard at my job — it’s a nonprofit that helps people apply for citizenship. I used to do deportation defense, which is a lot more intense. It could be heartbreaking, whereas what I’m doing now is a little more technical, working with people with complicated immigration and criminal histories. Sometimes, we have to give folks the bad news that they’re not able to apply, or they need to wait a few years. But a lot of times, we’re getting a happy result. That’s awesome work to get to participate in. On the other hand, I use all my free time for practicing, writing and decompressing from all of it. I do feel like I have two careers in a way.

AD: So, what’s the capsule history of somesurprises as a musical project? I think I first heard you on your Eiderdown tape from a while back.

Natasha El-Sergany: The very first beginnings of somesurprises were back in Charlottesville, Virginia. I lived there for a year before I moved out to Seattle. I was having trouble finding a real law job. There was a music room in the place we were renting at the time, so I was able to start writing songs, record them using my phone and put them up on Soundcloud. When I came out to Seattle, I started meeting various noise musicians who were friendly and curious to know if I was working on anything. I started getting booked for things that way. Then, I met my partner Josh [Medina, somesurprises guitarist] and we jammed together. I had a few songs, and he had a four-track.

Andy from Eiderdown had heard this little voice memos tape I had put out with my friends Marcus and Dustin. Adam said, “Hey, you want to do something for my label?” That felt like I won the lottery, honestly. The best way for me to get inspired is someone saying, “You’re good at what you’re doing — you should do more of it!” Making that tape with Josh was my first time being able to harmonize with myself, add layered vocals and make the textures really rich.

The band itself really started in 2016. By the time that Eiderdown tape came out we played the release show as a band. There have been a couple lineup changes — it’s always been me and Josh. After Andrew moved to Chicago, we played with [bassist] Emma Danner, and we’ve had Laura Seniow in her place for awhile now. Perseids is the first record she’s played on, though. Nico Sophiea was our drummer for seven years; recently, he’s focusing more on Greek music. Now, we have Benjamin Thomas Kennedy playing drums with us.

AD: What do you like most about your bandmates? What do they bring to the table? 

Natasha El-Sergany: I feel really lucky — with Josh, it’s been over eight years of working together. It’s hard to get on the same page with anyone creatively, but that seems to be a natural fit for us. Josh is really good at picking out the thing that the song needs and expanding it. We joke about how he has to be the one to make the song sound cool [laughs]. “Just make it sound cool, Josh!” He’s like, “I can’t just always do that!” But he can!

With drummers, I’ve gotten lucky. Nico was the first drummer I played with long-term, and it was a very unspoken thing. He taught me to play guitar better. Within the rhythms that he was playing, I could get into these micro-rhythms. Dynamics, too — I’d hit the strings lighter when he hit the drums lighter. I just had a really intuitive thing with Nico. Ben’s been with us almost six months now, and he’s very sensitive. He does what works for the song instinctively, I think, and adds his own signature. It came together pretty fast.

Laura is a total badass. She’s the shredder of the band. Whenever I do a little introduction onstage, she always gets the loudest applause [laughs]. I’m often playing pretty simple things, but she’s all over the fretboard. But always keeping things going in the right direction.

But generally, I’m just looking for nice, honest people. Realistic people who also have dreams.

AD: That makes sense! And then you’ve got some special guests on the new record — including cellist Lori Goldston, who for someone my age is an iconic figure for playing on Nirvana’s Unplugged. How did you hook up with her?

Natasha El-Sergany: I think about the fact that we play with Lori, and it sometimes surprises me even now. It’s so cool. Part of why I came out to Seattle is probably that Unplugged session, you know? I believe I met her when I was volunteering at a community radio station. There were some meetings about how to create some more anti-oppression tactics within the station and make it more diverse and representative of the community. And she was present at a lot of those meetings, and it grew from there. She’s awesome — she’s just a really good friend and a special person. Actually, when my bandmates weren’t emailing me back during COVID, Lori sent me tracks of her playing along to my demos. Some of that ended up on the new record.

AD: Perseids starts with the lyrics: “Be reasonable / It’s not inevitable.” I wanted to ask if you felt like that was an optimistic statement? Or is it something else?

Natasha El-Sergany: I don’t want to create a meaning for it that stays a static thing. But at the time that I was writing that, it was a time of a lot of social unrest. My neighborhood here in Capitol Hill in Seattle was all over the news because there was this encampment set up called the Chop. And there were nightly protests for Black Lives Matter and people were routinely getting treated like shit by the cops. We could hear helicopters over our apartment at three in the morning. There were a lot of conversations about police abolition at that time and many people said, “No way, that isn’t practical!” But a lot of the saner responses have been to say let’s widen the scope. Let’s talk about the context of everything that’s going on.

For me, it’s about trying to be someone who’s open to change — whether that’s personally, politically or globally. That’s the most centered reality. Cynicism and just believing that things have to be done the way they’ve been done in the past, or that someone else has to get the short end of the stick for them to benefit — those are actually twisted versions of reality. So, that’s one of many things that could be attached to those lines you mentioned. But there are so many other layers … like the process of trying to be rational when you’re spinning out mentally. “Be reasonable” is a little bit tongue in cheek, maybe.

AD: It does seem like it’s extremely easy to just go along with these narratives we’ve been given — and to feel powerless in the face of these systems. “This is how it is and there’s nothing I can do about it.” But then you remember that these are all things that we just made up. The thing that we can do as humans is create our own reality. But that’s paralyzing, I guess? Anyway — the album’s title, Perseids. I was familiar with the Perseids meteor shower, but I wasn’t familiar with its place in the realm of Islamic spirituality. Can you talk about how that became an overarching theme of sorts for the record?

Natasha El-Sergany: Right now, it’s Ramadan — and I think it was Ramadan in April 2022 when Jessika Kenney, a friend of Lori’s, came to the studio when we were working on the title track. Lori said, “You should ask her to sing on the record.” I’d already played a show with her in LA, and she was really great and nice. But I was trying to talk to her about the meaning of meteors in Islam. When I had looked it up, there was this kind of cartoony image of demons being shot with arrows from the heavens. OK, I thought, that’s very on the nose [laughs]. Jessika is also scholar of Persian poetry, language and music; she told me that there was this Hafez poem that talks about slaying the demons of grief. That really was moving to me.

This poem by Hafez talks about “killing the demons of grief.” So, rather than by slow healing, it’s this instantaneous way of being able to feel whole, no matter what. I really appreciate that. Jessika and I were kind of laughing about it though — in the poem, Hafez is saying, essentially, “My poems can heal your grief.” It’s sort of arrogant, but it’s also sort of badass. I’m definitely not trying to say that about my record, but this form of storytelling and narrative can take whatever has caused you grief and make it a part of the whole of who you are. That’s something I’ve been trying to reflect on.

The other side of the Perseids meteor shower is that it’s named after Perseus, who in mythology is the one who killed Medusa. You can’t look at Medusa or you’ll be turned to stone; but he’s able to do it by looking through a mirror. In the same way, you can’t look directly at the trauma in your life. You have to form a narrative around it in order to heal it or resist the thing that’s oppressing you — whether that’s inside yourself or it’s the government or Zionism or anything. I really appreciated those things.

Of course, the title is also tied to a literal experience I had camping with my bandmate Laura on her birthday and watching a beautiful meteor shower. There are all these other themes that ended up nicely intertwining.

AD: Tell me about the process of recording the album — where did you make it?

Natasha El-Sergany: I started demoing at Crybaby and some of those parts made it onto the record. But we mostly recorded it with Paurl Walsh, who recorded our first album. We did almost all of the drums and vocals at ExEx, which is the studio space where we rehearse in Fremont here in Seattle. And then Paurl actually built a studio in his backyard, so we did overdubs there. The process was very long — I’ve been saying that I never want to spend this long making a record ever again. Even though I’m thrilled with how it came out, it was a lot. We had a few whole band days and then Josh and I did overdubs every Monday for like — it feels like forever, but it might’ve been six months.

AD: Obviously, you have deep roots in home recording — do you like working in the studio? Would you ever want to go back and do a 4-track record?

Natasha El-Sergany: Yes! Actually, Josh and I performed as a duo recently and we called it Serious Dreams, which is the name of that Eiderdown tape. We thought we should separate it from somesurprises because it started getting confusing. We put a set together that we might want to record, and we’d probably use a simpler approach.

AD: Understandable — but the new record sounds terrific, it’s got such a nice, big spacious thing happening. All those Mondays were worth it!

Natasha El-Sergany: Monday nights after work — that has to be the worst time to try to do anything [laughs]. But yeah, Paurl was great and very patient with us.

AD: Do you have any favorite songs on the record — or even just favorite moments?

Natasha El-Sergany: On “Black Field,” there’s a sample at the end of some birds that were flying near my grandmother’s nursing home when I went to visit her for what ended up being the last time. She passed away last February. She was not a very compliment-giving person, which is one of the things I loved about her [laughs]. But I played her some of that song and she said, “Oh wow, it’s like you’re in another world, isn’t it?” She was this 93-year-old British woman with a stiff upper lip. And that was such a loving thing to say.
