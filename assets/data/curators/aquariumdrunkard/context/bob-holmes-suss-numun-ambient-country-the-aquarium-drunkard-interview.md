## Body

“What fascinated me and still continues to fascinate me about Louis L’Amour is that he’s a real old Western genre hack, you know,” the late/great author/screenwriter Rudy Wurlitzer told Alan Licht in 2011. “But the first two or three pages of a Louis L’Amour book are just riding into open space. You don’t really know where the rider is going; there’s no particular destination. It’s really about its own rhythm, its own process. That’s what I loved about Louis L’Amour. And when the plot kicks in, that’s where I stopped. I was always trying to get back to that early sense of being in the moment.” 

Though unrelated to music, Wurlitzer’s description here strikes me as one of the best sum-ups of the so-called “ambient country” or “ambient Americana” movement, which has been picking up speed in recent years. Like any genre codification, it comes with its own issues and problems, but the still diversifying, still intoxicating sounds coming out of the scene speak for themselves. 

One of that scene’s biggest proponents for almost a decade now has been Bob Holmes, whose work with SUSS, numün and the Ambient Country podcast — among many other efforts — have spread the gospel far and wide. Holmes’ latest project is Across The Horizon, a collaboration with Northern Spy Records that brings onboard various like-minded artists drawn “from the wide landscape of instrumental music” (including Luke Schneider, Marisa Anderson, William Tyler and more) to curate a series of digital releases that will culminate next year in a double LP compilation of stellar sonic explorations. 

Holmes is a busy guy, though — in addition to Across The Horizon, SUSS released an excellent new LP this year and are readying an intriguing collab with Wire’s Colin Newman; numün’s third album of kosmische pastoral psych drops this month; and he’s readying an ambient country showcase for next year’s Big Ears extravaganza in Knoxville. Aquarium Drunkard caught up with Bob during a rare moment of free time. | t wilcox

Birds & Beasts by SUSS

Aquarium Drunkard: Since you’ve become an ambassador for this certain sound, how do you define the “ambient country” genre? 

Bob Holmes: Oh god, I knew you were gonna ask this question. [Laughs] You know, it started out as a concept that I had in my head, which was meant to be polarizing. I couldn’t think of any two more polarizing words than “ambient” and “country.” But I knew that there was like any good Venn diagram, that there was a middle ground. There was a big overlap. When I put it to the original members of SUSS, each of us had our own singular idea what that meant and the commonality of what our ideas were is what ended up being SUSS…and we called it ambient country. 

At first, people thought we had invented this out of thin air, but that wasn’t the case. Our point of view was that it had been hiding in plain sight for the last 50 years—whether it was Eno and Lanois’ Apollo or Ry Cooder’s Paris, Texas, or Bruce Langhorne’s Hired Hand….I could go on and on, but the point is that people have been making music in this vein for a while. They’ve been using Americana instrumentation with some combination of synthesizers, loops, atmospherics. 

AD: And then the Ambient Country podcast came along … 

Bob Holmes: I started the Ambient Country podcast to show that we weren’t a club of one. We were a club of many, consisting not only our peers and modern existing artists who are out there doing it, but the musicians who have had been doing it for years. I wanted to put it in context. 

After the podcast had been around for about six months or a year, I started asking other artists to be on the show and it quickly became apparent that the influences and backgrounds of the people making this type of music were wide and varied. It was bluegrass, it was jazz, it was gamelan, it was experimental noise, it was electronic, it was psychedelic, it was soundtracks. Sometimes a combination of all of that! Over the course of the last three years, the podcast was not about defining the genre. It was about breaking the boundaries of what the genre could actually be. With every show, it got wider and wider. 

Opening by numün

AD: It turned out that it wasn’t really just one specific sound. 

Bob Holmes: I mean, would you call Tortoise an ambient country band? I wouldn’t—yet almost every artist that I have on the show points to Tortoise as one of the ways they got into this in the first place. So maybe then what the Ambient Country podcast is all about is an exploration of what instrumental music can do. But then more and more of the artists started bringing in vocals…Mojave 3 is a perfect example. I mean they are just smack-dab in the middle of what I call ambient country. And they have vocals. But they have a vibe, right? The vibe is a big part of it—that big sky, open feeling, creating a musical experience that that is very open. The word inclusive shows up a lot. Inclusive of musical genres, yeah, but also men, women, transgender, different races. I always say that the thing about ambient country is that it’s a big country. There’s plenty of space. 

AD: That brings us around to the Across The Horizon project — how’d that get rolling? 

Bob Holmes: When Northern Spy came to me, they initially said, “Bob, are you interested in a job as kind of an A&R person for this type of music?” And I was like, whoa, I don’t think so. While I’m happy to call these people my friends and peers, I don’t want to choose some of them over others and all that. But I came back at them with this concept. I said, “What if I invited a series of artists to do one-offs and each one of those artists brings in their own artists to contribute?” Northern Spy loved the idea and then I put it out in front of the artists. And it’s really taken off. As I found out on the podcast, no one loves to talk about their own music, but they love to talk about everybody else. 

We got so much positive feedback. We thought at first that we’d be lucky if we got four artists to sign up as curators. We got 12. Then the hard part was to trim it back to eight. Eight curator artists who will bring on two more each. That means 24 tracks total—a double album in the first year. It started to feel like it might be a herding cats-type situation, but everyone has been supportive and into it. We have enough people interested to do at least three years’ worth of volumes. 

first bounce fly fly fly by Marisa Anderson & Luke Schneider

To be honest, none of the artists that I chose needed to release anything through me or through Northern Spy. They’re releasing new stuff all the time. But it’s fun to bring other artists into the fold who might not have that opportunity, or whose music might fall so far outside the confines of this concept that it might be a surprise. 

Also, when an artist chooses two other artists, it says a lot about them themselves. Without giving away too much away about what’s going to happen in the future, William Tyler is one of our curator artists, and he’s bringing on Claire Rousay. I love them both, but I never would have thought of that. But it’s like chocolate and peanut butter. You bring the two of them together and It’s like, ohhhhh my goodness, this is amazing. Again, it’s that that sense of inclusivity. All the boundaries just get knocked away.

AD: That’s awesome. Yeah, the first Across The Horizon “drop” that Pan American put together has some very diverse sounds, but it all hangs together in some way. 

Bob Holmes: Yeah, they fit together in some strange way. If you can figure it out as a writer, please tell me, because everyone says they fit together but I don’t know why [laughs]. The next round is Luke Schneider, the Kyle Hammett Duo and Marissa Anderson. And if you think you know what to expect from Luke Schneider, this is going to change your mind. And again, if you think you know what to expect from a Luke Schneider and Marissa Anderson duet, it’s going to change your mind. I thought I knew … but I didn’t! 

When I was interviewing Mark Nelson (Pan American, Labradford), he sort of headed us off in this direction. He said, “I really like the idea of bringing on two other artists. It makes it worth my time if you let me do something that doesn’t sound specifically like Pan American or Labradford or anything that people might be expecting.” So hopefully, this should be an opportunity for all the artists to step outside of their comfort zone, get outside of their normal wheelhouse and move — as the title would get me to believe — across the horizon. Do something that is a bit unexpected and makes people think of me and my music and my musical taste in a way that had not been obvious before. That’s the theme. 

Point Harbor by Pan•American

And we’re going to make sure that that the overall choice of artists is as wide and as inclusive as possible. It’s a very male oriented genre of music, right? But there are some amazing women who make this stuff. I was really happy that two of our first releases were from women and, and one of our next three releases is from a woman and so on. And the fact that we have trans artists on that we have artists of all ethnic persuasions — we really were trying to look for something to show people that there’s a diversity of perspectives here. 

AD: It’s a big tent. 

Bob Holmes: Genre’s a funny thing. I was in a one hit wonder new wave band in the 80s, right? And we were credited with coming up with the term “country punk.” We might have said it — I don’t remember saying it — but somebody said we said it: Ohh, you guys invented country punk, right? And what that meant is that there were bands like us and Rank and File, Lone Justice and the Long Ryders and Green on Red and Jason and the Scorchers and so forth. Was it a real genre? I don’t know. But William Tyler said to me in the early days of all of these shenanigans: “I’m kind of like Groucho Marx — I don’t want to belong to any club that would have me as a member.” A genre can be a double-edged sword. 

AD: Right, the “ambient country” concept isn’t supposed have a rulebook — it’s more of a though starter. 

Bob Holmes: What ambient country is really about is the polarizing dialogue between what is “ambient” and what is “country.” Would you call Prairiewolf ambient country? I would call it hillbilly Kraftwerk. [Laughs] You guys make it sound like a natural combination, but when you actually really look at the pieces, it seems like it should not work. That’s been SUSS’s recipe from the beginning. And maybe all of the bands we’ve talked about in this conversation, that would be a common element. If you actually talk about all the pieces that are put together to make the music, you’d say: “That should not work.” 

AD: Can you point to any reason why this music has gotten the positive reception is has in the past few years? 

Bob Holmes: You can’t ignore the impact of the pandemic. People needed background music to help them focus. But then they actually started paying more attention to the background music and started to do deep listening to it. Obviously, new age music has been in spas and massage centers and elevators forever. But all of a sudden, I think people started realizing that this music could be therapeutic and mindful in ways that they never thought of before. This is a common conversation that I have with all the artists that I’ve talked to: there’s a deep, almost reverential, appreciation for the music as a healing thing. You talk to somebody like Luke Schneider, and that’s the main reason he’s making his music—for its healing, meditative properties. Unabashedly. Coming out of the pandemic, I think that aspect is really appealing to listeners. I hate saying all of this because when you put it in print it sounds so hippie dippy! [Laughs] But it’s a real thing.

Also, I think as audiences begin to age like myself, maybe like yourself, they found that they didn’t always need lyrics for the music to be transformative and transcendent. Our capability of filling in the gaps and finding our own story within the music was bigger than you might think. When you really take it apart, except for the lyrics and vocal melodies, this stuff can sound like Pink Floyd or the Grateful Dead or Phish — some of the most popular music in the world. It’s not experimental downtown noise music or something, you know? It’s accessible.  

AD: Where do you think the music is headed in the future? 

Bob Holmes: I can only speak for what we’re doing, which is heading towards 360° experiences with music and visuals — things that you can’t get anywhere else, where the music is just one part of the whole thing. SUSS has experimented with it a bit and it’s a different thing altogether, and it engages all aspects of our creativity. One of the band’s original founders was Gary Lee, who I went to art school with way back. He was sort of the Terry Gilliam to our Monty Python, and when he passed away, we were a little at a loss. But we decided we wanted to go ahead and take audiences places that Gary would be proud of. It’s a big picture thing, multi-media. It might mean we’re a little less-prolific music-wise because of the amount of production time and all the moving pieces, but we’ll see how it goes. It’s exciting.
