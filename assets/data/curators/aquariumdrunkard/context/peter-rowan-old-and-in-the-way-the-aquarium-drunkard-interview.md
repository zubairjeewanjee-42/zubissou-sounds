# Peter Rowan (Old and In The Way) :: The Aquarium Drunkard Interview

## Body

Peter Rowan’s brand of bluegrass has always been hard to pin down. He’s always been near the genre but always pushing the form forward, past the forefathers of the genre and into something new. In 1964, he began his career in Nashville, working under the father of bluegrass Bill Monroe. Rowan played guitar and mandolin and sang, but after being a Bluegrass Boy for a couple of years, he spread his wings past traditional bluegrass. As Earth Opera, he played psychedelic rock with John Cale, often opening for The Doors. With members of the Blues Project, he created fusion music as Seatrain, making an album produced by George Martin.

In 1973, he moved out West, returning to his roots of straight bluegrass. Working with Jerry Garcia, David Grisman, John Kahn, and Vassar Clements, they created a short-lived but widely celebrated outlet: Old and In The Way. Morphing Rowan’s own compositions with traditional covers, the group merged Garcia’s improvisational mindset with the band’s experiences in bluegrass. The result is just as compelling now as it was fifty years ago: bluegrass birthed out of the foothills of California. | b arthur

Aquarium Drunkard: Talk to me about moving out West?

Peter Rowan: I decided to drive across the country and left the band Seatrain. I had written “Midnight Moonlight” in April of that year and then left the band a week later. I drove across the country and had these songs, “Panama Red” and “Midnight Moonlight,” that I had written, that Seatrain didn’t want to do because they were a little too country for their sound. They wanted more classically based music with the fiddler Richard Green. But at any rate, I had left the band and stayed on through the summer in Massachusetts, and that fall, I drove across the country and went down through Oklahoma, New Mexico, and kept on going out to California, where my brothers had been recording for Columbia Records with David Grisman producing them.

I was longing for that West Coast thing, not only the weather. I had already stretched my legs and gone out there when I played with Bill Monroe and the Bluegrass Boys in Nashville. I got out of there, and we drove into Mill Valley after, after I had written “Land of the Navajo” on the way, we had stopped at this place in the Southwest, near Monument Valley, and I had had this experience, and I wrote “Land of the Navajo” as the sort of allegory of what I’d been thinking and studying about the whole year. We drove on out to the West and kept going and kept going, and pulled into Mill Valley, where Mike Bloomfield was standing in the middle of the street, welcoming us with open arms. It was a fairly loose time. There wasn’t a whole lot of traffic, and Mill Valley wasn’t a place at that time. It was just a cool place with a little health food store. Anyway, I had a friend of mine out in Stinson Beach said that there was an apartment up there that we could take, I think it was $100 a month. And so we drove out Stinson and started to settle in to this old building. We took the upstairs apartment, and David Grisman was just down the way.

AD: How did you meet the rest of the band?

Peter Rowan: We just started picking one morning, and David said, “You know, Garcia lives up the road.” I had met Jerry on a trip out there before, just taking a break from the road, and went out there to see my brothers and David, and we hung out with Jerry a little bit in the living room. We hadn’t played any music at that time, but this time, we went up there, and David was all fired up because we had our little spark of bluegrass. We drove up to Jerry’s house at the top of the little beach town called Stinson. It was the house at the very top of the hill above all the other houses. There was a sign over the gate that said, Sans Souci, which in French means like, no problem, no hassle. So we walked through that gate, and there was Jerry already standing in the yard with his banjo strapped on. He knew we were going to pick, so we just started picking. It was a buzz, you know, it was just a chemistry between people, you know, a kind of alchemy. We didn’t have any trouble following each other, and with just three acoustic instruments, it was like, you know, you could hear everything, and was so inspiring.

Jerry called up his buddy John Kahn, and the next night, we went up after supper time. All his kids were in bed, and Mountain Girl was there. It was a peaceful time for Jerry, I think, because the Dead had finished 10 years of hard work, and this was their year off, and they were going to get back together in the spring. So we had that fall and that winter to just play bluegrass for our own pleasure. We took out all the old songbooks — the Stanley Brothers, the Osborne Brothers, Bill Clifton and Bill Monroe, and Flatt and Scruggs — and we reunited our passion for the songs, especially the singing.

Something special about the Old and In The Way was the development of harmony. Except for “Knocking on your Door,” I don’t think we ever did a song that just featured one voice. You know, they’re stacked in a certain way. The lead is the lowest voice, and that’s Jerry. Even if I was singing lead on the song “Midnight Moonlight,” Jerry was doing the chorus because it was deep, right in his range. David and I would stack our harmonies on top of that so that, so that it was almost all the songs are lead vocal with two parts on top, yeah, rather than a part underneath, then the baritone part underneath. And I realized, after years of just hearing these songs, that was really unique because Jerry sings lead on most of the choruses, and then David and I are just jumping up above him, yeah? And there’s always a high part that’s way up on top there, like in “Pig in the Pen” and stuff like that. We had to really stretch, you know.

AD What was it like playing with Old and In The Way after all those years with Seatrain?

Peter Rowan: It was great, you know, because everybody in the band wanted to play, and they wanted something a little different than the straight three-chord bluegrass songs. You know, “Panama Red,” “Land of the Navajo,” and “Lonesome L.A. Cowboy.” In the early days of October and November, the New Riders of the Purple Sage were rehearsing for a new album, and they were looking for material, so Jerry suggested I go down to their rehearsal space over in San Rafael and sing “Panama Red.” I think “Panama Red” and “L.A. Cowboy,” both Marmaduke [John Dawson] liked them, and Dave Nelson liked them, and the rest of the band were just, you know, they were all happy-go-lucky at the time, and very enthusiastic about their music. It was a very exciting thing for me to have been to have those songs rejected by the band that was in Seatrain, and yet appreciate it out on the West Coast, you know, where you know, minds seem to be a little more open.

We played almost every night at Jerry’s house. You know, even when he went on tour, he’d call from the road and then play his banjo over the phone. We’d set up a phone call with Mountain Girl that Jerry was gonna call at a certain time, and we’d go up to the house, and we’d be there for the call. We’d all talk to him and be like, “Yeah, we can’t wait for you to come back.” Eventually, we played up five nights at the Boarding House, and that became the basis of pretty much twelve records. 

AD: Talk to me about the fiddlers?

Peter Rowan: Yeah, the Dead had a gig at at a theater there with Rambin’ Jack Elliott opening for them. And then the next night, it was us at the Passaic Theater. That was a few months after we started to get together at Jerry’s house. But those gigs were booked, and we didn’t have a fiddle player. I called Richard Green, and he was too busy. We called John Harford, who had played with us. And John was busy doing his thing, and his record Steam Powered Aereo Plane had come out. He had been with Glen Campbell, and “Gentle on My Mind” was now a massive hit. So John was in great demand as a solo artist. 

And I don’t remember how I thought of it, but I just reached into my pocket, and I pulled up my wallet and found Vassar’s number, and called him. I said, “My name is Peter Rowan,” and he said, “I know who you are, Pete.” And it was like, oh, the bluegrass world is very small. And I said, “We’re going on tour and we’d love you to come play fiddle—the first gig is in Boston.” 

That was the only East Coast tour we did: I think three shows, sort of in-between Dead shows. But quite honestly, it was asking too much of Jerry, really. There’s just so much involved with logistics and personnel. You really have to stay focused, especially when touring, so that you have that open-minded freshness on stage, which is what people are coming to hear. That was especially Jerry’s approach, right—always spontaneous. 

AD: What were the audiences like? Deadheads? Bluegrass fans?

Peter Rowan: Yeah, a small handful of people were Deadheads following Jerry around. And you can hear that on the Boarding House tapes. People are falling out all over the place. It’s like they’ve never heard it before. Yeah, they’ve never heard bluegrass. And they’re just like all falling all over themselves. People were just freaking out. They were very surprised. You know, bluegrass was breaking barriers. Bluegrass was getting out there, and we were on the pop charts. We came in at ’69, but we didn’t follow up, you know. We didn’t tour, and we didn’t try and make a hit out of it.  But that wasn’t the attitude. The attitude was, Well, everybody’s doing something different. I was trying to work with my brothers. 

Going from Seatrain back into acoustic music with Jerry and David and John and then Vassar, it’s very fragile. On stage, I don’t remember ever being satisfied with the sound, although when Owsley did the sound at the Boarding House, that was pretty close because he’d been on the case for a while. He had been the Dead’s soundman—he had built that Wall of Sound. So Owsley was sort of in on it, you know. And with this acoustic music, he was very excited about it. He had two Swiss tape recorders—two stereo tape recorders, so that meant we had four microphones on stage, and it was mixed. It was mixed on the spot by Owsley. But we didn’t have a board. It was just two machines, and Owsley had earphones on. He was like that. He was deep into it. Deep, deep. 

AD: What is it like playing the music 50 years later with Sam Grisman?

Peter Rowan: It’s a joy. I’ll tell you, it’s a joy to work with the kids. I’ll call them kids because they call themselves kids, but I mean, they’re in their 30s. [Laughs]. They’re enthusiastic, and they’re good listeners. I mean, it’s uncanny to play with Sam because it’s just like playing with David. He’s got so much of David in him, you know, but this is Sam’s time to make his mark, you know. We’re doing this epic tour in Colorado that will also take us to Charleston, South Carolina. It’s great.
