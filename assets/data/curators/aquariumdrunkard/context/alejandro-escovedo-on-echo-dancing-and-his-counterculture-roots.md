## Body

On paper, journeyman songwriter Alejandro Escovedo’s latest album Echo Dancing is a career-spanning look back at his song catalog. In actuality, the record is something more radical—a reimagining that suggests Escovedo’s got plenty of fire in reserve. Working with Italian musos Don Antonio and Nicola Peruch, he injects these readings with punk energy, blues swagger, thick electronic scuzz, ambient washes, and dubby vocal effects. 

“If I should not see 49/Bury me with my last silver dime,” he sings on “Bury Me,” originally shared as a rootsy, jazzy sway on 1992’s Sway, but presented here over snarling electric guitars, Pops Staples-style tremolo, and a blown out skeletal bass drum beat. Escovedo has made it past 49. At 73-years-old he more than qualifies as an elder statesman. But Escovedo miraculously remains in touch with the spirit that fuels his songs. He joins us to discuss Echo Dancing, his history of collaboration, and which member of the Velvet Underground “scared the shit” out of him. | j woodbury 

Echo Dancing by Alejandro Escovedo

Aquarium Drunkard: Though these are songs from your back catalog, Echo Dancing is obviously such a dynamic and fascinating record. It reimagines them in such fascinating ways.  

Alejandro Escovedo: My intentions originally were to go to Italy and record with Don Antonio and Nicola Peruch. I knew that I just wanted to use the two of them. I knew I didn’t want a band, and I knew that we could create something special. Originally, the idea was to go and totally improvise the new record.

I had little sketches of lyrics and ideas for some chord changes and stuff, but nothing solid, not like a song, a complete song, really. I didn’t want to go about it that way. I wanted to go and really just try to create something from the ground up. But on the way there, I started to listen to a record called Por Viva, which was made for me to benefit me when I was very ill in 2003-2004. I started listening to this version of “Wave” by Calexico.

AD: The great band from Tucson.

Alejandro Escovedo: Great band and wonderful guys. They did this really gorgeous version of that song. They deconstructed the song in a way that it was much more. They added this atmosphere to it. It made me feel like there was more life in these songs than I even knew. I listened to John Cale’s version of “She Doesn’t Live Here Anymore” and once again, he totally tore that song apart and came up with something that I never would’ve dreamt of in my wildest imagination. 

So I thought, this looks like fun. I want to get in on the fun. And that’s kind of what I went with. The first two songs were “Wave” and “Sensitive Boys” and we just ran from there. All the three of us produced it. We were getting three or four songs a day. It was pretty amazing.

AD: There are moments that draw from roots, blues, post-punk, Suicide-style rock, Stooges vibes. Did you and the guys have conversations about what sort of soundscapes you wanted to explore? 

Alejandro Escovedo: I think that I really knew that once we began and I saw what the three of us could do sonically, totally. [In 1974] I was living in San Francisco and my record collection was pretty crazy. I was listening to Robert Wyatt records, Kevin Ayers, all that Island stuff. Fear by John Cale. All that stuff was really cool to me. Eno was on the turntable constantly. I was really into No Pussyfooting with Robert Fripp. Cluster was another one course—those collaborations were really great. And also, I think really, and my older brothers really instilled in me the sense that collaboration was a really beautiful thing to kind of embrace.

AD: Your older brothers Pete Escovedo played jazz; the other, Coke Escovedo, also worked with Santana and Malo. Your younger brothers both played in punk bands. How did they model that spirit of collaboration for you? 

Alejandro Escovedo: I think just the way they were and the way they worked with their musicians. They were really cool with their musicians and they loved collaboration. They loved just playing with other people. So I think that always rubbed off on me. “This is a beautiful way to make music.”

AD: In 2006, you actually collaborated with John Cale on The Boxing Mirror. How did you first contact him? What was it like working with him? 

Alejandro Escovedo: I first met Cale way back in the day. I was playing in the no wave scene in NYC with Judy Nylon. Judy was managed by Jane Friedman, who was managing John. Jane had been Patti Smith’s manager and Jimi Hendrix’s publicist. She was pretty heavy but she was really great and supportive. We would go over John’s all the time and hang out at his apartment sometimes and play a lot of gigs with him. So that’s when I first met him. But he was a very different person than he was when we worked together. When I first met him, he was still drinking and carousing. He was a very imposing figure. He scared the shit out of me.

When we eventually worked together, it was a tribute to Sterling Morrison after Sterling had passed away in 1995. We did it here in Austin during South by Southwest. I got a string section together, and we did this song, and then he came and played with us, and we did “Sister Ray” and we did “Paris 1919.” Wow. We did some cool songs. He played on that tribute record. And then he came to play for the kind of showcase for that record with Ian Hunter. I asked if he would be interested in working with me, and he said yes. I remember asking him if he had a studio because I’d heard he had a really cool studio in his house, and he says, “No, I got rid of the studio.” I asked him why, and he said, “Because I hate musicians.”  

AD: Off to a great start. [Laughs] 

Alejandro Escovedo: We had a meeting in Los Angeles and I remember him telling me that he wanted me to go deeper. He wanted me to sound stronger and to really look to the future, to sound like I was ready for the future. It was a beautiful experience. I would say that John was almost like an older brother or an uncle or something that just you really love and really takes care of you and just shows you the way. 

We sat in the studio together, just the two of us, and that’s how we wrote the record. He was playing piano, I was playing guitar and singing. So it was a special experience and one of my favorite records I’ve ever made,

AD: As a fan that must have been surreal. What was your first VU album? 

Alejandro Escovedo: The very first, the banana record. I was in Southern California and there were lots of kids that were into that record. It was weird. You could go to a party in Huntington Beach during that time, and you’d always hear that record throughout the night somewhere.

I was lucky that I grew up around kids that were, I don’t know…a lot of things were changing at the time. We weren’t really into the hippie thing, so the Velvets kind of made more sense to us. The MC5 made more sense, The Stooges, those kinds of bands really kind of attracted me more. Garage bands in California were great, though. There were a lot of great garage bands, but that Velvets record was really the one that I just fell in love with. And I got to tell you, it’s the only record of mine my mom ever broke.

AD: She broke it? 

Alejandro Escovedo: She hated that record. She hated “Heroin.” 

AD: You worked with the late Wayne Kramer and James Williamson on 2018’s The Crossing. Connecting with the heroes of your youth has been a really cool thing that’s happened throughout your life. 

Alejandro Escovedo: I think it’s one of the greatest things that’s happened in my life from all this, just picking up a guitar with no intention of ever coming this far with it. To have been treated so beautifully by guys like Cale. And Wayne was a saint, man. He was just fucking great. So cool. And James, same thing. Just great.

AD: I got to talk with Wayne Kramer once after he had written his book. I did an interview with him backstage about that book, and he was, there was a humility to his presence. It’s this bizarre mix. He had this complete humility but also a palpable heavy, heavy confidence too. He really struck me as somebody who had lived through a lot and was aware of his fortune. His fortune to still be around. And I feel like some of your best songs also share that, a survivalist mentality, but also a real deep sense of humanity too. 

Alejandro Escovedo: The examples I had when I was younger is how people handled themselves in this world and life. My father, my older brother, they were so cool and just so gracious and really sweet to people. And then guys, Iggy Pop…I would see him on the street in LA when I lived in Hollywood. We picked him up once on the way to a Stooges gig at the Whiskey. He got in the van, we smoked pot, and he tried to hit on our girlfriends. It was all good fun, but it was just so real, you know what I mean? 

It wasn’t like these rock stars up to that point who had been so distant and unattainable. It just seemed like something that had nothing to do with us. I wasn’t about to sit in a room and practice guitar for the rest of my life. I just wanted to do it, we just wanted to do it. It was kind of automatic. We just did it, but we learned so much from all those people, the love of records and rock & roll, and the history of it and the legacy of it. 

AD: I can’t imagine many people watching Iggy roll around in broken glass and peanut butter would have predicted that in 2024, he’s going to be seemingly as healthy as he’s ever been. When you think of heroes—Lou Reeds, David Bowies, Gill Scott-Herons, whoever—you’re hearing artists taking risks right up until the end. Or Leonard Cohen. Or Dylan. You don’t have to fall into the trap of playing the greatest hits. 

Alejandro Escovedo: You also have to not fall into the “youth trap.” We all age, we all mature, hopefully we get wiser. We learn what not to do or how to be. We learn to love ourselves first, hopefully, and then love other people.
