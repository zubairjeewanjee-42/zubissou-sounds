## Body

What does it mean to be a season ticket holder for the music of bass player Anna Butterss? Reserved, front row seats to diverse and experimental music, whether as a founding member of improvisational bands SML and the Jeff Parker ETA IVtet, their own experimental solo work—the latest being 2024’s innovative Mighty Vertebrate—touring band member for Jason Isbell, and first-call, bass-player-for hire. Obviously, there’s no actual subscription available for purchase, but, if possible, it would mean regular access to one of the most exciting musicians working today. 

The latest chance to hear Butterss perform is as part of the new, sophomore release from SML titled How You Been. Employing the same process as the band’s debut album, Butterss and co-conspirators Josh Johnson (saxophone), Gregory Uhlmann (guitar), Jeremiah Chiu (synthesizer, live-sampling), and Booker Stardrum (percussion) selected recordings of live, improvised performances to edit and build on in the studio. How You Been is an expansion of the band’s vision as they move through steampunk grooves, sexy rhythms, and a complex maze of sounds with confidence and even a little swagger. This stance is well deserved as SML demonstrate that they are playing the best neo-funk, neo-jazz, neo-anything on the planet. Throughout the shifting nature of the improvised pieces, Butterss is the glue that unites everything. 

On the eve of the release of How You Been, Aquarium Drunkard spoke with Butterss about their “foundational” role as a bass player, SML’s creative process, being a bandleader, the future of the ETAIVtet, and moving between electric and upright bass. | s bunn

Aquarium Drunkard: The different members of SML have worked together on various projects over the years. How did you all decide to get together as a unit? What’s the creation myth of SML?

Anna Butterss: It’s a little unclear even to me. It definitely was not forward thinking in that way. Someone —I’m not sure who, it definitely wasn’t me —booked a couple of gigs at ETA, the famous bar that we all played at [in Los Angeles]. I hadn’t played with Jeremiah or Booker before. I have played a lot with Josh and Greg over the past 15 years. And I think it just felt cool, and it felt like we were getting at some stuff that might be interesting to explore long term. Of course, we recorded those. I think we maybe only played two or maybe four shows and then turned them into a record. 

Honestly, the conversation, as I remember it, started along the lines of, “Well, if we have a band name, people will be more inclined to buy the record than if we just put it out under all of our names.” I think that’s turned out to be true. But certainly, for me at least, I wasn’t expecting the record to make such an impact. Then the band kind of sprang out of that, really organically. But when we put out that record, we’d only played a handful of shows together, so we’ve been creating the band sound as we go and as we learn more about each other and as we continue developing an understanding of how the five of us play together. Because, like I mentioned, some of us have played together for a really long time and know each other very well, and some of us, it’s something that we’re still exploring. So that makes it an interesting and exciting dynamic, I think. 

AD: For the first album, you improvised those first performances and then edited those recordings afterwards. Did you hold to the same process with How You Been?

Anna Butterss: Yeah, we did. Fairly early on, we decided that the live show was going to be a separate thing from the record. So, the live show is always improvised. It’s possible that that will change, because there are a couple of songs on the new record that I think would be fun to try to play live. But up until this point, that’s not something we’ve done. We’ve kept it very separate and not tried to replicate the vibe of the record live. So, all the shows are improvised. We try not to really talk about what we’re going to do. Then, yeah, second record, same thing. We had a lot more material to draw from, obviously, this time around. It was all edited by us, collectively and individually, and then that’s what you’re hearing. Some of it is very constructed, very edited, and then there are a couple of tracks that it’s mostly what we played. Maybe we cut a little bit down, so it’s kind of a mix.

AD: Do all of the members of SML contribute to the editing? And what’s the band’s internal decision-making process?

Anna Butterss: We’re still learning as we go. I wasn’t involved in editing the first record. Timing-wise, it was right at the same time I was making Mighty Vertebrate, and I just couldn’t do it. But we had such little material to work with that it was easier, in a way, for them to just be like, “Okay, this is what we have. Here’s the record.” This time around, we had to cut so much stuff that was really good, just because we’re talking about vinyl, you have a time limit. The way we did it is, we had all the recordings from maybe eight shows we’d done, and we all individually grabbed what we thought was interesting, made some edits, uploaded it to a shared Sampley, and then we would get into each other’s edits. Or someone would be like, “Oh, I think this would sound cool if, Greg, you overdubbed a guitar melody or, Booker, if you overdubbed some tambourine.” Or we’d take it on ourselves. We’d kind of mess with them all and it was fun! Some of us had more time or space. I did, I think, maybe three edits total. Some of the other guys did a lot more. There was a lot of flexibility for us all to choose how we were going to be involved. And then, at the end, maybe the hardest part, which was cutting all the music down to fit a record.

AD: There are a number of shorter songs on How You Been. They feel like they serve as interstitial moments on the album. I heard you say that the band tries not to recreate things live, but I was wondering if they have the potential to evolve into longer pieces. 

Anna Butterss: I’m not sure. I think we’re really hesitant to try to recreate anything. I think so much of the ethos of the band is — similarly to the ETA IVtet — really focused on being in the room with a live audience. We’ve never been in the studio. It’s alive. I think the thing we’re creating is a moment for everyone. At least for me, once I start thinking about imposing something, some kind of external idea, it takes me out of what’s actually happening in the moment. But I could see similar things evolving organically in a certain performance. We’ll have to see.

AD: You said that you don’t like to bring in outside ideas or use the record for inspiration, so then, how does an improvisation start when the band is playing live. Do you say, “All right, it’s your turn”?

Anna Butterss: [Laughs] Sometimes! A lot of them start with Jeremiah because he’s often making a rhythmic sequence. So, a lot of times it will start with that and then all the rest of us play off that, whether I impose something harmonically or Greg or whether Booker takes something rhythmically, and we go with that. I think it’s kind of how we’re feeling in the moment. We’re all very comfortable doing that. 

I’m thinking back to some of the shows we’ve done with the ETA IVtet, where there could be a really long pause between songs while we all sit in the moment, and someone waits for an idea to come. At the start that made me so uncomfortable sitting in that space and just thinking, “Who’s going to play? Should I play? What’s going to happen?” But then doing it enough times with Jeff [Parker] and trusting that someone will find something and that it’s okay to have that space of not knowing, I think, has really come over into SML, where I feel like we can just sit there, and something will happen, and we can trust that it will. 

AD: Have you been working on a follow-up to Mighty Vertebrate?

Anna Butterss: I haven’t. I’ve got a couple of side projects I’m focusing on. I’ve been working on a trio record with [drummer and producer] Ben Lumsdaine and trumpeter John Raymond. I like having Ben involved in everything I do. We have a special kind of synergy, I think. Special protection. And then Ben and I have a duo project that I’m really excited about, where he plays drums and some kind of electronics and prepared percussion. And, last year he played pump organ as well, which I think was cool. And I mostly just play upright bass. Again, it’s improvised music, but with a different set of aesthetics than the other improvised projects that I do [laughs]. I think it’s important for me to distinguish that, okay, yes, we’re playing a lot of improvised 

music, but SML has its sonic world. ETA IVtet has its sonic world. This duo project has its sonic world, you know? So those are the two things that I’ve been focusing on this year and what next year looks like. 

AD: In the press materials for Mighty Vertebrate, you talked about certain rules and restrictions that you put into place to write that material. I heard you say on a podcast that you try to “trick yourself” into writing songs. You haven’t started working on the next album, but have those exercises that you used, do you think they’ve influenced your playing, either with SML or the other collaborations that you’ve been talking about?

Anna Butterss: I think they have. I’ll say that the reason I had all those rules, and then, like you mentioned, kind of to trick myself into writing, is mostly just to get out of my head. Because I know I can create really well if I’m not being judgmental on myself, and that’s always been the hardest thing for me. I think through that process, I’ve learned to be able to focus in more on a small idea and get a little bit outside of that judgmental headspace. I’ve been feeling really excited about practicing both upright bass and electric bass. So I found that coming into a lot of the things that I’m practicing and being able to follow a small idea without thinking, “Oh, what are the other hundred things that are wrong with the way I play? Well, let me just explore this one for a little bit” [laughs]. And I think that comes into the improvised music as well. It’s more of a vibe thing. I couldn’t tell you directly like, “Oh, that’s the moment.”

AD: Reflecting back on Mighty Vertebrate, how was it being a band leader in the studio? These were players that you’ve known quite well, but you’re steering the ship as opposed to being part of collective decision-making. How did that band leader role suit you?

Anna Butterss: It’s not my favorite [laughs]. The part I liked about it was being able to have creative control over everything, over the compositions and the sounds, and being able to edit it as much as I wanted to in post and adding this and adding that. Also, having the time and space with Ben — because we produced it together — to be able to be like, “Oh, let’s spend an hour trying this thing. Okay, we didn’t like that. Let’s try something else.” I think that element of it, I really liked. The element where I feel responsible for whether or not other people are having a good time playing my music, not super into it [laughs]. Because these are people that I care about so much and because they’re so great at what they do, I wanted to be able to pay them really well. I wanted them to have a really good time. Those things stress me out a little bit. Being a band leader, there’s so many other things you have to think about. As a bass player, I have the privilege of taking that up when I want to and being able to leave it and have enough work to not have to hustle like some people do.

AD: As a member of the ETA IVtet, what have you learned from Jeff Parker about being a bandleader? 

Anna Butterss: I’ve taken so much from Jeff by playing with him, listening to him, and spending time with him, talking to him, understanding the way that he thinks about music and hears about music. Jeff really focuses on that. He will say no to gigs if it’s not going to be fun for all of us, if we’re not going to get taken care of, or if it’s a schlep. At this point, because we all have other projects going on, we can do the gigs that we really want to do. That’s been in the band for a long time because it’s been a “no subs” band. Jeff could have had subs in that band and played way more times. But I think the fact that he made a decision that it was going to be no subs, which interrupted our Monday residency [at ETA] a lot because we’re all touring all the time. I think that kind of ethos radiates out into the types of gigs that we’re doing. So, yeah, I’ve learned a lot from Jeff about how the vibes have got to be good. Everyone’s got to be happy, and then you play good music together, especially if you’re improvising. I know Duke Ellington liked to pit some of the horn players against each other, but we’re not really like that in our band [laughs].

AD: I spoke to Jeff for an interview for Aquarium Drunkard last year and he was at a bit of a loss as it seemed as if the rug had been pulled out from under him with the closing of ETA. Now, all four of you have such busy schedules. Will you continue to play occasional shows and short tours? What’s the future of the ETA IVtet? 

Anna Butterss: I think the rug absolutely was pulled from under us. We all knew it would happen at some point. Every single gig lasts for a while and then it ends. As sad as it was, that’s also part of the cycle. I think it’s an opportunity for us now to move into a different direction, which, like you said, is playing shows less frequently. We played recently at Lodge Room here in Los Angeles. We played in the round. We recorded it. It was beautiful. It was really special. It felt so meaningful, and I think that the band has so much history and the four of us have so much chemistry together that it’ll always exist in some shape or form. Maybe we’ll find another residency. Who knows? Maybe this is a lull and maybe next year we’ll be back somewhere else. Has to be on a Monday.

AD: This is more about your bass: do you always play an electric bass for SML and an upright bass for the ETA IVtet? 

Anna Butterss: I do. That’s been a really conscious decision on my part, kind of like I was saying before, to separate those two sonically because I don’t want every band to be every element of the way that I play music. I want to have some separation and some focus and that’s a big part of it

for me. Check back in in five years and see what’s going on, but that’s definitely a very conscious decision on my part at the moment.

AD: And then same with the other projects that you’ve been talking about, are you shifting between the two within any of those collaborations? 

Anna Butterss: For the duo project with Ben and for the trio project with Ben and John Raymond, both of those, I am exclusively playing upright bass. With my own music, I played both on the record. Also the other element to consider for me in a lot of these bands is that there are other people who are bringing a really wide range of electronics, huge sonic palette, in a way that’s really cool. There’s something that I like about just playing bass. It’s kind of the way I think about bass anyway as a foundational piece and then there’s all this stuff happening around. I like to focus in a little bit. They’re definitely all conscious decisions. Upright bass has been really interesting to me, especially in the last couple of years, and with the main touring gig I do, I don’t get to play it very much. So, in these other creative projects, I’m looking for opportunities to play the instrument more. 

AD: That’s interesting that you use the phrase “foundational piece” for the bass because when I saw you perform at Big Ears, playing electric with SML and upright for the ETA IVtet, the phrase that came to me about your role was a “binding agent.”

Anna Butterss: I’ve always seen that as the role of the bass. It’s interesting because, pretty much exclusively, you’re playing one note at a time and you’re in between, in a lot of cases, drums on one side and keyboards or guitar on the other side. I think it’s an accurate representation of what I try to do at my best, maybe not always achieve it, but try. 

AD: So, what’s next for you? Do you have ambitions that you’re working towards? 

Anna Butterss: Man, someone just asked me about dates in 2027 which I can’t even, I cannot [laughs]. I’m at the point where I’m going to start looking towards the second half of next year and seeing what time I want to carve out. I would like to eventually make a follow-up record to Mighty Vertebrate, so that might be a time to do it. But I’ve been touring so heavily the last five or so years, I’m thinking about a trajectory in my personal life as well. Do I want to be touring like this long term? How do I make this sustainable? Is LA even a good place to continue living? You know, all the normal questions that you ask yourself in your mid-30s [laughs]. I guess I’m thinking six months to a year ahead.
