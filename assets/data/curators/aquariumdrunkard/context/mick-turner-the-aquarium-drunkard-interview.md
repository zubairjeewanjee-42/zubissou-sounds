## Body

Mick Turner’s guitar playing is instantly recognizable. From his role as the binding agent in the seminal band Dirty Three to his numerous solo albums and now his latest group, the dreamy duo Mess Esque, there’s no other guitar player with Turner’s distinctive sense of rhythm and tone. His sound can be hesitant, composed, jagged, and ragged, yet consistently in command and always compelling. 

Turner’s unique and arresting musical voice is central once again on Jay Marie, Comfort Me, the latest release from Mess Esque. The band is a partnership between Turner and Helen Franzmann, who also records under the name McKisko. Jay Marie, Comfort Me is Mess Esque’s third release and the second on Drag City. It demonstrates the band’s facility with minimal or maximum instrumentation and confidence in their ability to seduce the listener. Mess Esque’s power is on display in the deep water plunges of “Light Showroom” and “That Chair,” the sexy groove of “Crow’s Ash Tree,” the beguiling come-on that somehow fits inside an old music box that is “Let Me Know You,” and the swirling, triumphant feel of “Armour Your Armor.”

Given the moderately high-profile nature of his bandmates in Dirty Three with Warren Ellis serving as Nick Cave’s musical consigliere in the Bad Seeds and Jim White seemingly playing on every record that’s released, interviewing Turner presents an opportunity to not only highlight Turner’s unique musical voice but also his capacity to adapt and work with many different kinds of musicians. 

AD caught up with Turner about the artistic blind date that started Mess Esque, how he collaborates with lyricists, his approach for painting versus music, and more. | s bunn

Aquarium Drunkard: You’ve told the origin story of Mess Esque a number of times: you were writing music that you felt needed a voice and lyrics. What attracted you to Helen Franzmann? What made you know that the two of you would work well together?  

Mick Turner: We were introduced remotely by a mutual friend, Nick Huggins, who is a recording engineer I’ve worked with on numerous projects over the past 15 years, including my last solo album, Dirty Three’s recent album, and the “Solemns” EP, which was a collaboration by me and Will Oldham. He had also recorded the last McKisko album, which is Helen’s solo project. I didn’t know the music would work out with Helen, but I trusted Nick’s call. And, as we (remotely) worked through making pieces, it all flowed well. Neither of us witnessed the creative processes of the other, just the finished chapter (there is a lot of back and forth of each piece in its creation). Although we talk about our angle a little, it is mostly each on our own, so we remain partly a mystery to each other creatively.

AD: Do you ever ask her about her lyrics or share your interpretations? Or do you maintain a separation of church and state? If you do discuss lyrical content, has that ever changed your playing or the arrangements?

Mick Turner: Hardly ever would I ask anyone about their lyrics unless I find something particularly curious. It’s not that I’m not interested, more that I’m of the school where any song you might like and come to know really becomes your own story. Or rather your interpretation is the story, not the author’s intended meaning. And it’s so much more than words like as compared to just written words. There is count and phrasing and melody that adds so much more to the meaning. If you were to ask me though, I have to admit I think most of Helen’s songs are written from her angle, they’re a dialog with her children. Or they’re dreams. Or both. 

AD: How do you usually begin the construction of a song? Do you always use a guitar, or do you also use a keyboard since organs are a prominent part of Mess Esque’s sound?

Mick Turner: Ha! That is my secret weapon. There is no actual organ played on any Mess Esque record so far, hardly any anyway. It’s a magic box that makes the guitar sound like an organ and it rules. I work a lot with loops and improvising.

AD: It feels as though the song “Take Me To Your Infinite Garden” is an evolution of Mess Esque’s approach with an aggressive guitar riff and a dynamic that feels outside of the dream-state songs of the first two albums. Did you make it a goal to try different sonic palates on this album? 

Mick Turner: There was no defined goal. We both have broad tastes and when you start a song idea you don’t know where it might go. It’s a creative process where you only see a couple of steps ahead. Robert Forster of The Go-Betweens says, “Take Me To Your Infinite Garden” is our “Jumpin’ Jack Flash.”  I don’t think you could see that coming.

AD: Knowing that you and Helen mostly compose songs remotely, do you adjust the music you’ve written once she has shared her contribution with you? For instance, do you add or subtract instrumentation or alter the arrangement? 

Mick Turner: Yes, almost always if either of us feel the piece is not there yet. We may use rearrangement or replacing instrumentation and vocals, even changing melodies and backing tracks and whatever. There are no rules except we both have to feel a piece is complete to be finished. And it is a collaboration with both of us giving input, feedback, and suggestions to the other on all ideas; although we are working geographically separate, we are communicating with every step.   

AD: You’ve worked with singers and lyricists before Mess Esque (Cat Power on Dirty Three’s “Great Waves,” Will Oldham on Marquis de Tren projects, Caroline Kennedy-McCracken on your solo album Don’t Tell The Driver, and more), but you also work with violin players (obviously Warren Ellis in Dirty Three and Jessica Billey in Bonnevill), whose playing could be described as voice-like. How is it different creating music knowing that lyrics will be implemented as opposed to instrumental songs?

Mick Turner: The approach is the same initially, once the vocals are being added they are just another instrument, they do demand to be the main focus and that is always understood. The music is there to carry the words.

AD: It’s great to hear you playing with Jim White on the new album. Do you need to give him instructions at this point? Do you anticipate what he’ll play?

Mick Turner: Jim appears on two tracks, Kishore Ryan on another, and I think the rest might be me. Me and Jim might talk about what I’m wanting the song to do but I would never give him instructions. God forbid! He’d probably floor me.

AD: When you’re writing music, do you hold certain song ideas or chord progressions in reserve for certain projects? For instance, do you have instincts about a piece that it would work better with Dirty Three or Mess Esque or in your solo work? 

Mick Turner: To some degree, and I wouldn’t really do it deliberately, but it has happened where I have accidentally brought the same raw idea to more than one project and they both have ended up on separate albums, but the processes with different collaborators have meant the resulting pieces aren’t comparable. I dare you to find them! Ha! You’d have to have a pretty extensive Mick Turner collection.

AD: Do you ever set conceptual goals for a song, whether in Mess Esque, Dirty Three, or your solo stuff? For instance, “Let’s do a deeply romantic song,” such as “Light Showroom”? 

Mick Turner: I didn’t think that “Light Showroom” was a romantic song at all! My interpretation is it’s very much one person’s journey mostly in their mind, having some self realisations and experiencing a certain life magic. They’re in a light showroom and their kids are looking for them just outside their daydream. Ha! No, I don’t ever set conceptual goals. I think Helen is saying something definitive here for herself, but I don’t know what that is and I don’t feel the need to ask. I like the mystery. But now you say this, maybe I’ll ask, maybe not.

AD: You are a visual artist in addition to a musician. Beyond your work sometimes being used for album artwork, does your painting inform your music and vice versa? 

Mick Turner: Yes, in a sort of partly abstract, and partly logistical way. In process and approach. For example, I’ll one way or another discover a working method applied successfully in one that I’ll take to the other such as this — modifying an existing painting by only keeping a figure and changing completely where it is. I’ll do this when a piece is not working, and I might be trying to fix up small areas or tones or whatever when I realise I need to go sideways hard and take it somewhere unsure. That worked really well for me in painting. Even when it didn’t succeed, I ended up learning which is a success in itself, and even more, it is exciting to really mess up something like that, destroy what you have laboured over in the name of not settling for “ok.” Anyway, this approach works sometimes and it’s a very enjoyable and liberating creative approach. So too I have taken this to music, keeping only one part, one voice of an elaborate piece and completely reinventing the landscape it sits in.
