## Body

Over the last few years, and now three records, Jana Horn has quietly asserted herself as a songwriter of great merit amongst a vast and seemingly bottomless sea of artists also in search of answers through song. Her most recent LP, a self-titled work written during a period of transition in her life—notably a relocation to New York City having graduated from a writing program in the University of Virginia—captures movement not just in the physical sense of moving from one physical place to another, but of progressing the flow of her interior life. 

These new songs find Horn in figuring things out in real time, and admitting to herself the difficulties of knowing whether or not you’re pursuing the right train of thought, let alone path in life. In this regard, the fragility in her lyrics, intensified by her unfussed performance, resonate deeply. The album even ends with Horn self-consciously critiquing a take in the studio, suggesting, “It was good until I messed up… should we do it again from where I messed up?” The verite snippet suggests more than uncertainty with a take. What could be perceived as a throwaway moment, a behind the scenes glimpse into the album-making process, actually presents us, in the final few seconds of this beautiful album, with the overall thesis: life is messy and confusing and we don’t always know when we’re doing it “right”.  

With her trusted bandmates Jade Guterman (bass/guitar) and Adam Jones (drums) and guests such as Adelyn Strei (clarinet/flute) and Miles Hewit (piano) working together to build a minimal world that sometimes evokes Bill Callahan’s Smog material while also bringing you into a dreamlike free-jazz style courtesy of the loose tumble of percussion. There’s a minimalism to these affecting songs that’s comforting and compelling and really invites you into the privacy of Horn’s thoughts. 

On a busy morning in the lead up to her latest record being released, Jana Horn spoke to Aquarium Drunkard about surrendering to the process of making this beautiful album, the looming presence of author Ben Lerner on her life while she write these songs and heading to her hometown of Texas to record with her band. | z hedderman

Aquarium Drunkard: How is your day going? 

Jana Horn: It’s been pretty stressful, honestly! Through what’s going on in my life at the moment with releasing a new album, I have to get work on a music video that’s releasing today done. I made it yesterday, and then I slept for four hours and am in the process of uploading it right now. It’s been one of those mornings where I’m just like, ‘Oh, my God!’ 

AD: Hopefully this chat will be a nice distraction! Apart from that stress, how are you feeling about the album coming out? I’ve absolutely loved spending time with it.

Jana Horn: I feel really good about it. I feel a lot of relief every single time something comes out. I’m like, ‘Thank God!’ because I don’t like holding onto stuff. It makes me feel kind of like less of a person or something because I’ve got this stuff inside. When I can just let it go, it feels good. This album’s been done for a long time. And so, I’ve been keeping this weight, and it just feels good to get it going and let it go. 

AD: Absolutely. I’m always fascinated when an artist or a band, a few albums into their career, goes for makes the decision to self-title an album. I often wonder if it’s because they feel that the work is truly reflective of themselves or of the period they’re in. This is album three for you, why did this feel like the body of work that you self-titled? 

Jana Horn: I think there’s something significant about the number three; the 33rd year of your life, it’s the Jesus moment where something is complete. It’s finished. That was definitely going on in my head. I wasn’t trying to make a statement about anything. I actually had other album titles that I was thinking about, but some sort of instinct was like, “Just self-title it.” Lucinda Williams self-titled her third album, she’s definitely a hero of mine, and that gave me some comfort about the decision. 

I also felt, more so than this album representing me more than any other album, that I had really surrendered to the making of it, in a very literal way. I really felt like the album sustained me in a way that I wanted to honor and signing your name off on something is the most, or, that’s all you can really do. That’s the biggest thing you can do for something. I guess I was trying to offer that to the work. 

AD: Hearing that from you is interesting because I read an interview you gave a couple of years ago, where you had said, “I used to be self-conscious, or something. There was a time in my life when I really was frustrated by the fact that my music didn’t sound like the music that I liked or that I listened to. Now, I came to this realization that the music I make isn’t for me. I make music and I seek out what makes sense to me, what comes naturally. But, I wouldn’t say I’ve found that thing that is particularly reflective of me. I make it in the way that feels most intuitive to me.” Did that change making this album?

Jana Horn: Yeah, that hasn’t changed. Or, I guess that hasn’t really changed. There are some songs that I happen to make that I like more than others or something like that. But in general, I have to accept that what I’m doing just isn’t really for me, sonically. It matters that it’s done to the best of my ability, and that it’s my instincts and my presence is in it. I can hear that and not hear it. That’s how I determine it. As opposed to like, “Do I like this song? Do I like the way it sounds when I play this song,” which is not always true. There are some songs on this album that I’m like, “That’s not for me, but I hope you all like it!” It’s not that I think it’s bad, it’s just not my preferred style to listen to. If I could cosplay as some other artist that I like that would be nice. I just do what is literally possible for me.

AD: What artists would you like to cosplay the most?

Jana Horn: Oh gosh, there are so many. If I could sound like Arthur Russell, or The Fall, or Broadcast is huge for me. That would be fun! 

AD: You moved to New York from Charlottesville, around the time that you started making this album. How did you find that change of scene shifted your approach to songwriting, or how meeting new people in the city maybe changed your perspectives on music, or inspired you in different ways? 

Jana Horn: I’m involved in a more instrumental scene here in New York, and so that was definitely a different thing for me than any other city I’ve lived in. There’s string instruments, wind instruments and ensembles and no singing, which is nice. I started playing with Jade [Guterman], who was a guitarist, but for me plays bass, but I think really plays guitar on her bass on this album just from how noodley and melodic the bass tends to be. 

If anything, writing in New York has been the greatest conflict. I’ve had the most conflict with it because there’s just so much going on that to do something slow and contemplative there’s almost like a violence about it. You have to almost violently be contemplative. Does that make sense? 

AD: Yeah, you really have to shut yourself away from the noise of the city and the distractions. 

Jana Horn: Yeah. And maybe violence is the wrong word, but it takes so much more force to write here, I feel. It takes so much more energy. Have you lived in New York or been to New York? 

AD: I’ve been a couple of times. I have a friend who lives there and my sister used to live there, so I’ve been a couple of different times, and it’s a great city, I love it, but I couldn’t imagine actually having the discipline to work there. I would just wanting to be out doing and seeing different things there. 

Jana Horn: Yeah,it’s amazing and distracting and inspiring. I think a lot came out of all this and it felt like struggle. There was a lot of growth happening and pain with that and whatnot. 

AD: You lived in Austin and Virginia, how would you say that each of those spaces informed your style of writing? Where have you felt most creatively inspired? 

Jana Horn: Yeah, I think I definitely had the most fluid experience in Austin. I was also living in a more exploratory state where when you’re younger everything is experiential. There’s this amazing almost one-to-one experience and reception ratio happening where you’re getting so much from what you give and I think it’s always been fluid, and it’s always been stagnant. There’s always something going on like that. 

Then, in Charlottesville, that was extremely inspiring but in a totally different way. It was all writers, all books, all literature, all reading and almost no music that I was engaging with. I had a couple CDs or something but the program was pretty intensive and I was also teaching and my mom was having a lot of very extreme health problems and so, I just had to basically read and make the album that way. When I hear it, I mean I haven’t heard it in a lot of years, but what I remember of my second album is it being pretty mental as opposed to bodily. That’s at least how I felt when I was making it. It was a lot of thinking as opposed to running around, and then New York is purely running around, it’s all body, it’s all city and movement and energy. 

Jana Horn by Jana Horn

AD: With the second album and being in Virginia, you were teaching and doing your course, being so immersed in reading all the time, what skills do you feel you really honed during that period or, were there particular writers or books that unlocked something in you as you approached writing these songs?

Jana Horn: Yeah, I read a book that really rocked my world, which is called The Lover by Marguerite Duras. It’s really amazing. I had this superstitious relationship with it for a while where I only read the first 50 pages and then I read the first 50 pages 50 times. I wouldn’t finish it because I was afraid that if I finished it I wouldn’t finish my own book. I don’t know where I developed the superstition, to be honest. I just had a real resonance with that book. I actually finished it. I gave up on that superstition because I was like, ‘The world’s ending, so I need to finish this book! Whether or not I write a book, I don’t want the world to end before I finish this one’. 

I finished it when I was working on this album. I also read a book called 10:04 by Ben Lerner. That was interesting because it’s set in between Marfa, West Texas and New York, which is where we recorded and made the album. I was definitely feeling a lot of kismet when I was reading that and loving it. I even wrote him a little email, and he responded to it, which is nice. I don’t know if you’re ever reading a book and it’s seeping into your life in little ways? That was happening to me. First it was the setting and then there were all these other little things about it. I was working this job and on the first day, it was in the neighborhood that the book opens, and the receptionist at my job was named Ben Lerner. I was just like, “All right, I guess I have to send him an email!” I told him how his book had weirdly infiltrated my life, and that felt meaningful.

AD: That’s so crazy, especially like the name being the exact same! You mentioned Texas, for this album you recorded it in Sonic Ranch. What drew you to go there to make the album? 

Jana Horn: I have recorded my past few albums in a kind of piecemeal way; whenever there was time and availability, and other logistical things. I was already feeling scattered here in the process of making it. I didn’t want the recording itself to feel scattered and so I had to negotiate that. Recording requires so much presence and you need to truly not be anywhere else. I don’t feel like I would have been able to achieve that in New York at the time. Not that I would never be able to do that, but I think I would be thinking about the time. I’d be thinking about the clock; I’d be thinking about work the next day and other people’s things, and shows that we needed to get to. The whole atmosphere of New York would be in the studio with me. That’s what I felt. And so, I decided to do it this way, which I’ve never done before, you know, spend 10 days just out of place. It also happened to be in the middle of nowhere and it happened to also be where I’m from. It checked all these boxes and felt like a dream. 

AD: Of course. What you’re saying about how the city of New York might have influenced the process or sound if you had of recorded it there is interesting because the album is so peaceful and calming on a musical level in with your vocal performances. When you listen to your lyrics, they’re very self-reflective and sometimes even it feels like you’re self-soothing. You mentioned earlier about how album two was  quite mental in its essence, whereas this one focuses a lot more on the interior and talking about breath and moving the body. I really enjoyed things like in “All In Bet” and then on “Unused” in which you grapple with uncertainty: “Don’t know what I’m thinking” and then, “Telling myself what I think so I don’t take you as you are or not”. I’d love to hear about your experience writing these songs because it feels like you learned a lot about yourself through them and that they unlocked personal growth? 

Jana Horn: That’s a good question. I was wrestling with these songs a lot, during the making of them. I told you earlier that I really surrendered to the making of this album. I meant that literally. I would sit in the same square of my bed for hours and hours and hours for days and days and days to get one line out of a song. I felt like Jacob wrestling the angel and now I walk with a limp! There was a lot of existential questioning like, ‘What is required of me to get the thing that is needed and that is essential.’ This sounds dramatic, but some of the songs were like that. Where it was like, “What’s required of me? Do I sit here every single day in the same place for the same amount of hours? When will what is necessary, what I need, when will I get it?” A lot of faith in the process, a lot of pain in the process and having to not fully rely on the higher self but actually having to meet the higher self with a lot of exercise. A lot of boredom and confusion and saying, ‘What the hell, I thought we were writing. I thought we were doing this.’  That was my experience a lot of the time. 

That’s not always how it’s been. I think I was just in a place of not knowing and sitting down, sometimes literally in bed saying, “I don’t know what I’m thinking,” until another word can appear. Also, several of the songs came from a songwriting group I had with a few friends, Helena Deland, Jonah Yanoh and Jade Guterman called the Dead Letters Office. I said that I was wrestling with the songwriting process, and that was true, but there was lightness, too, and community in this little club, which I didn’t have in the same way on other records. It was very supportive and generative, and helped me remember the point to it all, of sharing and kind of collectively willing.

AD: Not at all. Do you feel like you were putting you’re putting a lot of pressure on yourself to find the right words or phrases to articulate yourself in these songs? 

Jana Horn: Yes, in a way, but also I think I was seeking bigger answers to my life through song. There’s pressure, but I was also not feeling myself. One way that I feel better about myself is when I’m being creative, when I’m writing and trying to solve my problems through this process of being intentionally receptive. And so, yeah, there was pressure. There’s always pressure. There’s always needless pressure, but I was unsatisfied. I was unsatisfied with my life. I was trying to get through this process. If I’m creating, if I’m making work, then I feel like my life makes sense. However, when it’s not coming out and when there’s all this going on, things just don’t make as much sense. 

AD: That makes sense. That relates to one of my final question for you, which comes from the closing song, “Untitled (Cig)”. That feels like the linchpin of the album, even the way it closes with you saying, ‘It was good until I messed up,’ where you’re referring to your performance in the studio, but from listening to you talking about how you weren’t satisfied with your life in that moment, it’s almost like something that’s deep down within your consciousness and maybe you’re saying that to yourself about your life. It feels like a really multi-dimensional moment and parting message. Some people might be like, ‘That’s a fun little throwaway behind-the-scenes moment’ but it feels very integral to the overall feel of the album. For you, is there a song from the album that captures the essence of the album?

Jana Horn: My first instinct was “Come On” and I’m not sure if that’s just because I’ve been listening to it all morning long and all night, but I think that’s true. My second thought was “Go on, Move Your Body” for different reasons. I think “Come On”, lyrically has the most collage and the most gathering together of elements. It was made as a trio, which is the core of the album; me, Jade and Adam [Jones]. There are other wonderful players on the album, but me, Jade and Adam worked on the songs forever together. We went out to Sonic Ranch together and others joined us later. There’s something that encapsulates all the things that are essential to what we were doing with the album.

AD: For your upcoming tour, will it be that trio playing together? 

Jana Horn: It’ll actually be a different configuration. We’ll start with me, Jade, Adam and Adelynwho plays the flute in the clarinet. We’ll start that way and then Jade and Addy have to leave at a certain point and  it’ll just be Adam and I as a duo. I’m really excited about that. I’m picturing the video for “Cross Bones Style” where it’s Cat Power and the drummer. That’s exciting to me. We’ve done it before, but not on this album so I’m excited about that. Then in Europe, we’ll do the trio of me, Jade and Adam, and then Jade will also leave and then it’ll be Adam and I again. There are a few different venn diagrams going on. 

AD: That sounds super fun and like it’ll also keep the shows fresh and interesting. 

Jana Horn: Totally. Unfortunately, my life is very like seat of my pants, whether I want it to be or not. Here I am uploading a music video while speaking with you, one minute before the deadline! I don’t know what’s wrong with me, but it keeps things interesting.
