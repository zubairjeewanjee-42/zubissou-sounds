## Body

In the four years since the release of his debut record Due North, Liam Kazar hasn’t exactly been silent. Be it on stage or in the studio, his contributions to the sounds artists like Hannah Cohen, Sam Evian, Kevin Morby, and Jeff Tweedy are tasteful and distinct. You know you’ll be in safe hands when you see him on stage or in the credits. But this month saw the release of Pilot Light—a sophomore album that couldn’t be further from the stereotypical slump. 

It turns down the volume as his lyrics say more than ever before. But don’t be fooled by its comparatively laid back, stripped down sound. Liam’s thoughts and musings remain simple and timeless with a clear-eyed yearning to find and enjoy life’s most gratifying experiences. You’ll stop in your tracks mid-groove, begin to ponder, and drop back in.

Aquarium Drunkard caught up with Liam last month while he was on the road with Waxahatchee to discuss the life changes and music that inspired him to write this batch of songs soon to be taken out on his own headlining tour. Once we’ve gotten our fill of music talk, he offered up some other food for thought. | n calvino

Aquarium Drunkard: Most listeners are probably coming to your music with some prior knowledge of your work on the side of the stage or in the studio. First of all, how do you juggle all of that?

Liam Kazar: The last few years have been lucky with album cycles for different people that I play with lining up. But also, you just can’t juggle it at the same time. Since 2021, I’ve done about 200-plus days on the road every year. Because I’ve been bouncing between my music, Sam Evian’s, Kevin Morby’s for a long time, Jeff Tweedy, and now Waxahatchee. And then some Hannah Cohen gigs here and there.
That phase of my life is coming to an end, you know? “Perennial sideman breaks out on his own.” Tale as old as rock and roll, I guess. And hopefully not so much juggling anymore and playing my own tunes.

AD: Well, it worked for Glen Campbell pretty well.

Liam Kazar: Yeah. If I could get one percent of that Glen Campbell vibe going, that’d be a huge success.

AD: For folks not very invested in music, I do think that “side musicians” or “session musicians” don’t really get the credit that they deserve. I believe you’ve spoken of your love for Carlos Alomar.

Liam Kazar: Yes! Yes.

AD: He’s a great example of someone that really added their own flavor to the David Bowie catalogue. Could you speak to not just his work, but perhaps other musicians like that who might not get enough cred. People you look to when trying to best serve someone else’s project whether it’s live or in the studio.

Liam Kazar: I think Bowie is one of the great casting directors of all time when he [was] putting together a band. What he was so great at doing was hiring people to be themselves, you know? When Carlos Alomar is on the record, you know it’s him the whole time. He’s not cosplaying as Mick Ronson, or something like that. He’s there. He’s in that room to be himself. As for me playing with other people, it took me a long time to accept that people were hiring me for me. Only in the last couple of years, honestly, did I have the realization of, “Oh, they want me to be myself. They don’t want me to be them.” Or to think about what they would do. They want me to think about what I would do.

And I’ve been doing this for fifteen years now, and some people find this at a really early age. That confidence just right out of the gate. But it took me a long time to figure out who I was and figure out how to bring myself to the sessions or bands that I was a part of. And that I’m not just here to be a bastardized version of something that happened on a record or another person that used to be in the band. I’m here to be me. 

But in terms of people that have played on records, Sean Mullins who plays on my record. If you do some digging around he’s—in New York City—one of the top drummers. He’s playing on everybody’s record right now. Anyone who can get their hands on him for a couple of days is using him. I always joke about Sean that he’s a composer who is wearing a drummer’s costume. He’s really a composer first, and that’s why he’s so good and why people use him on their records. He’s thinking about the composition as a whole. And there’s a lot of people in New York doing that, but he comes to mind. 

AD: For those folks coming to you through this context, they’ve likely seen you playing bass. Is there anyone that you find particularly inspirational being that playing bass is a very different situation than, say, ripping a solo for yourself or someone else. The low end of things. 

Liam Kazar: That “low end.”

AD: A “Low End Theory,” I suppose.

Liam Kazar: Yeah, sure. I’ll say it because it’s true. Sam Evian is one of the best bass players. Not often do guitar players pick up the bass and put in the work to really know how to play the bass like its own instrument and not like a guitar player playing bass. But Sam did it. He started maybe five or six years ago, messing around with it and now he is an amazing bass player. 

In terms of inspiration, the sound that eludes me that I still hear and think “god, that is so thumpy but not too subby” is the Crazy Horse bass sound on “Everybody Knows This Is Nowhere.” That bass sound and that bass playing that’s really simple but has an amazing tone. That’s amazing rock bass playing that I love so much. And I don’t know quite how to get that sound but I’m reaching for it all the time.

Along with Carlos Alomar, George Murray—the bass player in that stretch of Bowie. For my money, that’s ’70s inventive, sort of crazy midrange bass playing that is totally serving the song. I’d probably listen to that sooner than I would to the Jaco [Pastorius] / Joni [Mitchell] records or something. Or I’d “steal” from that zone more.

AD: Really hard to steal from “a Jaco.”

Liam Kazar: Yeah, good luck.

AD: You’ve mentioned Sean and Sam, but how about the rest of the band on this record stands out to you?

Liam Kazar: It was a family affair. Most of the tracking was four people in a room. Which was myself, Sam, Sean, and then the keyboard player Michael Coleman. We all were on Sam’s record Plunge together, the four of us. And there were some other people on that record that hopped in and out, but the four of us were there the whole time. And we had a year or two of touring under our belt when we did that record. So when we did my record, we had a shorthand communication. I know it’s been awhile since I’ve put a record out, but this record was recorded really quickly. It was about two three-day sessions, which was the bulk of it. And then some vocal overdubs.

With the bulk of it recorded in six days, you don’t really get that without people that really know each other or that have played with each other. Because you don’t have enough time in the studio to spell it all out exactly how everyone should be doing it at all times when you’re on that kind of schedule. You have to have the right people where everyone knows what sort of thing we’re going for. 

Not that there’s not going to be weird turns. And believe me, we tried this weird krautrock thing on a song that did not work at all. We scrapped plenty of ideas, but there was almost an unspoken guiding way and I think you can hear that. Not a lot was said between anybody. Once you got the chords going, we were just pulling tapes. 

AD: Did the same shorthand apply when working with Sam as the producer? 

Liam Kazar: Absolutely. I trust Sam. There’s a song the record called “Next Time Around” that was recorded in 2021, I think, separate from the rest of the whole album. We knew what that sounded like and we knew that we liked that sound. And we used that as our slight sonic template, and we just did that with these songs. In that sense, it was kind of the blueprint and we went from there.

AD: I think a lot of the qualities of Due North are there. The lyrics are simple but are also profound without seeming like they’re trying to sound that way. It’s not an affectation, I mean. But the sound is much more laidback and comfortable. Like you said—it’s four people in a room with little augmentation. Was that very conscious or just an interest in trying something new?

Liam Kazar: When I was writing, when we were recording, even when we were putting together the art after it was done…I kept thinking “Liam, just trust yourself. Trust yourself, don’t ask for things by committee.” Polling the committee every step of the way. I’ve done that in the past. And I’ve been a part of bands where that’s how decisions are made. And sometimes that works. Sometimes it’s necessary. 

But for this record, I just wanted to go with my gut front-to-back. And that’s what I did. Because I knew this record was coming together as a slightly…breakup album. Everyone’s entitled to one and I made mine. It’s not entirely about that, but it’s certainly a big element. So I wanted to go with my instinct and be as honest as I possibly could. And that’s sort of where the dry, natural sound came from, from how straightforward and honest I was trying to be with the songs. 

It felt silly to dress them up in the same way that we dressed up some of the Due North songs when I felt like I was so clearly saying what I wanted to say. Sam and I were very aligned on preserving that clarity. And to me, one of the greatest songwriters ever—one of the most direct songwriters ever, in the best way—is Christine McVie from Fleetwood Mac. I would say Christine McVie and her songwriting and the way that she talks about love and gets right to the point of it was a huge inspiration for me and something that I was striving for when writing about love or the absence of it on this record. 

AD: Talk about people entitled to breakup albums. 

Liam Kazar: Yeah, seriously. 

AD: Was there a particular album or group of songs that you were enamored with? 

Liam Kazar: All of her songs on Tusk are masterful. Front-to-back. With “Over & Over” being an all-time top ten song for me. That song is super important. 

AD: Do you go through phases, whether you’ve listened to their work for quite some time or are just coming to it, where you are noticing or appreciating someone’s music in a much more fixated or intense way? Did you come to McVie’s work in a stronger way partially because of the circumstances of the breakup?

Liam Kazar: I was thinking about my approach to lyric writing as I was making this record. And I truly didn’t realize this until I started writing these songs. In particular, “Try Again” or “Listening.” My father is a writer, and he’s a really good writer. He’s really big on saying what you want to say as concisely and as simply as possible. Growing up, he would edit my homework. Getting rid of fluff left and right, and I’m double-spacing and adding all of these dumb words just to make the essay two pages because it had to be because it was homework. And then he’d slash it down and it would be like two paragraphs of information, you know? Just cutting straight through the bullshit. 

I’ve asked him for advice about writing things…kind of my whole life. And I realized how much of an influence that had on me as a lyric writer with this record. Because I wanted to be as direct as possible. There’s a song called “More Than This” where “if a word doesn’t need there, it doesn’t need to be there” was sort of my philosophy. I have never taken a hack to it harsher than on this record, I think because of the way we were recording it. Being dry and open, trying to have as little fluff as possible. 

I just think Christine McVie is very underrated as a songwriter. Because I think she does that. I think she says things very simply and very profoundly and beautifully. I think she’s one of the best to ever do it. She did it in a songwriting capacity that I found very inspiring. And people are pulling inspiration from art without realizing it all the time. Going back to that point about my dad saying things as simply as possible, his favorite writer is John Steinbeck. And you read a John Steinbeck novel and it’s just “Sentence. Sentence. Sentence.” There’s absolutely nothing in that novel that does not need to be there. And I love it. The shorter the better, honestly. I haven’t read East of Eden yet, but I want to. That’s actually like Sam Evian’s favorite book. But I just find that so much harder and puts trust the audience’s intelligence to fill in the gaps. 

AD: There’s a Chauncey Gardiner parallel here…

Liam Kazar: [Laughs] Totally. 

AD: This was recorded up in the Catskills, but you were previously based in Kansas City, right? 

Liam Kazar: Yeah. I was in Kansas City for three years. 

AD: What prompted the move to the “heartland of America” in Brooklyn? 

Liam Kazar: I was in a relationship and I moved there for my partner at the time to begin an organization. A great organization called KC Tenants, which is still going strong. I’ve done some work with KC Tenants in the years since we’ve ended our relationship.

It’s an amazing organization. They’re basically fighting bad landlords. As a tenants’ rights organization, they’re giving power to people that are being screwed over by bad landlords by organizing them and developing tenant unions. Whether that means locking down courthouses and preventing illegal evictions from happening, or building a tenant union in an individual building and going on a rent strike until certain upkeeps are made. They’re doing great stuff.  

AD: Some of the first gigs that might showcase these songs will be while you’re opening for Jeff Tweedy, yeah?

Liam Kazar: Yeah, I’m opening for Jeff Tweedy this fall. I’m also in his band. I’m opening a third of the shows and then two other people in the band—my sister [Sima Cunningham] and Macie Stewart—are opening up the other two-thirds. We have a rotation. And then in December, I’m playing some album release shows with a band in New York, Chicago, Los Angeles, and in Kingston, NY at Tubby’s. You probably know that spot. That’ll be my first headline show in L.A. I’m excited about that. 

AD: Do you think it’ll be a blend of the two albums’ songs? The sound is so different between the two.

Liam Kazar: I think it’ll be like the new record, while the old record will be interpreted in the way that we recorded the new record. Due North material performed in the way that we might perform Pilot Light material. It’s just a three piece band—guitar, bass, and drums. It’s a very natural-sounding band.

AD: With the title and title track of Pilot Light, it evokes an image of something that can strengthen or wane but is always present. Is there anything about this record—perhaps in the context of “trust” or in your relationship to music—that you’ve found goes along those lines? The notion of something that can be summoned when needed but that might diminish in strength over time despite the fact that it’s never quite gone. 

With this being your second record made with folks you’ve worked with before, and with yourself as a songwriter that’s standing more and more on their own—or at least determined to do so—is there anything that “came back” with this batch of songs? Confidence in writing, the recording process, etc. 

Liam Kazar: That’s interesting. You know, I feel like this is the first time that I’ve ever made a record that centers on my voice. I think that I thought of myself as a guitar player and a songwriter, but not as a singer. And I think that this is the first time in the studio that I’ve used my voice as the main thing to try to connect emotionally with the listener. 

Playing guitar or bass in bands, I think I mostly thought of music in a group context. And I think this is the first time that I not only realized but accepted that we’re all there to help me get my voice across to the listener. And that that kind of has to be the center of the whole thing. 

When I first started playing music, I think I was recognized by others as being able to sing or hold pitches. And I always thought of that as one more element within a band. But then I stepped back and realized as a listener of music and lover of music. Chords are a building block of music and the sonic space a record sits in is a building block of music, talking about songs here, and not instrumentals.

When trying to get people to relate to a song that has lyrics and a lead voice, the lead voice is going to be the center of the song. I know that more than ever watching Willie Nelson play every night [while touring with The Outlaw Music Festival]. It doesn’t matter what’s going on! When he says “Hey, how ya doin’?” it’s like “Oh my god! There’s that voice! That’s Willie Nelson! I know that’s Willie Nelson but that’s Willie Nelson’s voice.” And this feels like the first time that we’re ok to put my voice at the center of the recordings and not the guitar or the vibe. And everything around it is helping to make that happen. 

AD: Beyond the trust that you’d have to put into others around you in a session, it sounds like Pilot Light is a record where you had to trust that you were serving your own self as a singer and songwriter. 

Liam Kazar: There was a lot of trust in the room while we were making the record. But it’s funny. I feel like I’ve known this the whole time as a sideman, but it took me forever to figure that out when it was my turn to say what I wanted to say. I didn’t realize it when doing it for myself, but I’ve always known that when I’m doing it for other people. I’ve always known that when playing with Jeff. The center of every Jeff Tweedy song is an acoustic guitar and Jeff’s voice. That’s the core of every Jeff Tweedy song. And I’ve known that as a side musician playing with him for ten years but didn’t seem to know that when it was my own music until now. 

AD: Do you feel that’s all in an effort to be more “objective” about the process or to get even more “subjective” in the output? 

Liam Kazar: I think it was built off of the end of my relationship. In my personal life, trying to open up in general and become a more honest and open person. It was a natural progression to open myself up a bit more to the world in my life and then open myself up a bit more in the music. 

AD: I’d like to ask something about food. 

Liam Kazar: Sure. I got a lot of opinions. 

AD: I’m sure any fan of yours knows that you’re a chef. What would be the food that you prepare for someone else to present them with something as opposed to when you’re making something for yourself to suit your own needs? And would those line up? 

Liam Kazar: Absolutely not. I don’t love myself enough to make myself a nice meal. That’s the total truth. When I’m at home and it’s just me, food is a utility. I’m trying to eat healthy, clean, and fuel up my body in the best and simplest way that I can. I make myself a smoothie with weird crappy protein in it. I make a pretty good smoothie for myself, actually. Cooking—for me—is not something I’ve ever want to put out in the world to show what I’m capable of. But as soon as I get one other person that I’m cooking for, then my ego clocks in and I want to make them the best thing that they’ve ever had.

AD: So if you were cooking for someone else and trying to show care and consideration through a meal, what would that meal be and what music would you pair with that?

Liam Kazar: I’m going to roast a chicken. Because there’s no bigger sign of love in the gastronomical world than making a roast chicken for somebody. In my opinion. Because to really do roast chicken correctly, it takes time and it takes effort. To cook salmon correctly just takes skill. To cook a steak correctly just takes skill. It doesn’t take a lot of time. But a chicken—the humble chicken—requires love, patience, strategy, and planning. Whenever I go to a restaurant and try a chicken and it’s amazing, I know it’s a good restaurant. If the chicken is dry and not good, I only have so much love for that place. 

So I would cook a roast chicken with some perfumed rice. The rice can be perfumed with cardamom, saffron, or clove. Some sort of really perfumey, floral rice. And some sort of roasted vegetable on the side. Probably carrots. Carrots, when done right, are beautiful, wonderful, delicious. A thin white wine gravy. And while we’re cooking it, we have Ray Charles’s Modern Sounds in Country and Western Music playing.
