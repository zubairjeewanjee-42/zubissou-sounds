## Body

At this point, Alice Coltrane’s legacy feels secure; close to 20 years after her passing, she’s been elevated from a footnote in the John Coltrane story to a highly esteemed composer, dazzlingly innovative musician and spiritual leader in her own right. And while Alice certainly had devotees during her lifetime, her place in the pantheon hasn’t always been so lofty — a fact demonstrated throughout Andy Beta’s excellent new biography, Cosmic Music: The Life, Art and Transcendence of Alice Coltrane. Whether she was simply ignored or outright attacked, Alice’s own particular genius is only now being understood and accepted.

But in Beta’s introduction he writes of his reluctance to classify Alice Coltrane “as an example of a beloved artist going from obscurity to belated discovery. The stars are always above us, but the conditions on Earth must be right in order to fully glimpse their splendor. In that sense, Alice’s ‘cosmic music’ was always there, waiting for that moment when a new generation would be ready to hear its message, aware and openhearted to ‘the most beautiful music in the universe.’”

For a look into Beta’s own journey through the remarkable life of Alice Coltrane, Aquarium Drunkard hopped on Zoom with the NYC-based writer. The edited results of our conversation are below … | t wilcox

Aquarium Drunkard: What compelled you to write Cosmic Music?

Andy Beta: I’ve known this music for so many years. It was always one of those things that I would go back to and realize [I was] hearing more information in it, right? Even the things that I thought I knew completely. I think it was maybe the 100th time I heard like “Ptah” that I felt like I was hearing an entirely new level that I didn’t hear other times. That happens in a lot of her music.

Honestly, I was trying not to write this book, even though there have been many things along the way that led to it. One of the roots was in interviewing Flying Lotus. I did that three times, and the third time was a much longer span of time spent with him. Every time, we had talked about his Auntie’s music. This was probably in 2010 — it was still a rare kind of thing to discuss. One of his houses has this amazing nine-foot painting of her. He talked about Lord of Lords, this journey of John Coltrane’s spirit through ascension. He talked about going to India with Alice when he was a teenager. He had seen how beggars on the street would drop to their knees and bow to her, touch her feet. I’d gone to India by that point, so I remember just thinking this is so different than anything I’ve heard of before. You can’t just go to India and people recognize your spirit. [But] people recognized the avatar, so to speak, the spiritual presence.

But still, I didn’t think I was going to be the one to write the book. I was still thinking, “I can’t wait for [an Alice bio] to come out. I can’t wait to hear the full story.” I went to the Ashram, but it wasn’t even going to be part of the story. I just thought, “I should go, I’m really curious about this. I want to see what it’s like.” And so I went [but] I didn’t write anything more about it. And then a few years later when it burned down, it was like, “Wow, I really am glad that I went.” Then I was pitching a book that was about western music and eastern spirituality, why they keep coming together. No one wanted the book really, no one was interested in it. But one of the sample chapters was about John and Alice, and an editor asked if I’d be interested in writing an Alice bio. At first, I said no. I’m not the guy.

But after I had visited the Ashram, I had stayed in touch with some of the members, like when the Ashram Tapes concert happened. Part of my hesitancy had always been if the [Coltrane] family was going to do something authorized. But the people at the ashram knew they weren’t planning anything. And then I talked to [critic] Ben Ratliff, who had written a book about John Coltrane. I asked him, “You’re a jazz critic for the New York Times — how was it dealing with the family? And he said, “Well, I didn’t deal with the family.” So I asked myself, “Can I do this just with what’s in the public eye?” I talked to Carlos Niño early on, and he was supportive as well. But he said I should only talk to the people that knew her. At a certain point in the book, I thought I’m not going to have enough stuff. Maybe the last third of the book will just about what her influence is. But that seemed like a really bad idea, because I think it’s still so early in terms of the appreciation and the deepening understanding [of Alice]. It’s only just begun in a way.

AD: I think that’s the right approach. And talking to people who were there is so crucial.You mentioned the Ashram burning and everything, and that does bring up the fact that so many of the people that could have illuminated a lot of things have already passed on. You gotta do this now.

Andy Beta: There are a few folks who I just missed. Then some people like Jack DeJohnette and George Bohanon passed not long after we had done the interviews. I was going to write George Bohanon a follow up, but he was already gone.

AD: There’s a real sense of urgency, especially because in some cases there’s a real lack of information.

Andy Beta: What was also interesting is it’s all very compartmentalized. There was no one who had insight into everything. The Ashram people know a lot, but they’re starting from the mid- to late-seventies. None of the Detroit guys knew her beyond Detroit. Same with the jazz people. Tulsi Reynolds had great insight into Integral Yoga, and then the moment Alice moves to the west coast, that’s it. 

AD: It was great to read the firsthand reviews of Alice shows, eyewitness accounts. I think of the wild Berkeley 72 bootleg that came out a few years ago. I listen to that, and I think: How could you have seen that show and not be talking about it for the rest of your life? It’s another reminder that things just happen. And if they’re not written about or filmed or recorded, they’re just forgotten.

Andy Beta: The richest source of information about Alice’s early days was in the Detroit Tribune and the Black newspapers of Detroit. I was just amazed that I could parachute in to 80 years ago and find some of this information. Obviously, I didn’t use all of it, like her brother talking about what he likes doing in the summer. But I couldn’t have ever known that she won the spelling bee, or that she gave a recital at one of dozens and dozens of, Black churches in Detroit. And here’s the program of what she played!

AD: Yeah, the spelling bee bit was unbelievable.

Andy Beta: And she’s a great typist. And then, you know, she’s experiencing astral projection. “Yeah, this is an interesting childhood, for sure.” [laughs]

AD: Even the parts of her life that get sort of shadowy I thought were fascinating. Like that period in between where she’s had her first child and is going back and forth between Detroit and New York. You think, “What was going on here? What were her ambitions?” Obviously, music was just moving her forward.

Andy Beta: Which is remarkable, just as a woman player. I think about what would have been available to her a generation before. The full limit would have been to teach music at home, play in the church and that’s it. Even though she grew up in this conservative household, they were still supportive of her to go beyond that.

AD: Putting a book like this together seems like a monumental task. But I liked that there was space left for uncertainties.

Andy Beta: I took solace in the fact of, ultimately, she’s unknowable, right? I feel that way about the John Szwed book about Sun Ra, for example. Or his book about Harry Smith. How much are you going to know about Harry Smith, who has this peculiar shell.

AD: I liked the detail that she was always able to find a good parking spot. That’s cool. She was this spiritual leader who has cosmic visions, but there were details that made you remember you were dealing with a human being as well. She was down to earth, at least in some respects.

Andy Beta: She wasn’t out to lunch, really. She’s up there on those higher planes, but she’s also a businesswoman. She’s the reason the Coltrane name is what it is. She preserved John’s legacy, never letting it get co-opted. It was always in service to what their vision was.

AD: I did think of Szwed’s Sun Ra biography as well, because a biographer could tread in a sort of tricky path with both Ra and Alice. But you take her on her own terms, as opposed to bringing your own baggage.

Andy Beta: In some of the British reviews I’ve seen they have a skepticism about the new age-y aspects, which I get. I mean, you start watching Eternity’s Pillar, and it’s really wild. But one of the things I went with was … I believe her. I didn’t come across anything she said that wasn’t true or that was misleading, or whatever. So maybe that comes from the Ashram, talking to the people that lived that life with her, and knew her best in that way. I just believe her. Maybe she’s having this discussion with Stravinsky, and you’re like, “Yeah, but he’s dead.” But it wouldn’t do me much good to try to disprove it.

One of the things I like in the book is when she’s playing the celestial psaltery, an instrument you play with your mind.  And, you know, Bach and Handel have played it. Sri Aurobindo, he’s also played it. And Jesus Christ, too! And now she’s playing it. It’s wonderful, the possibility of this spiritual vision. Even if it was a dream you had, and you woke up and that was your dream … What good is it to try to deflate that?

AD: Every page of the book, I thought: “What a remarkable person.” You started out writing a biography of a jazz musician. But at some point, it’s like, “Oh, this is a biography of a saint!”

Andy Beta: She got more remarkable as I went along. You hear about these early instances, and you’re like, “Wow, she’s really talented, she’s really gifted. She can pick up stuff fast.” But then as it goes along, it becomes clear that this is no longer a music biography. It all kind of falls away. On the one hand, the book has to continue along with a certain structure, and it still has to kind of be oriented towards the music she’s making. But what seemed to be the important part became the peripheral part. Her focus very much shifted. I did really feel like I was writing the story of a saint in that regard. I felt inadequate for that, because I’m not a religious scholar. But you want to strike a balance between the two. The book has a dedication to my grandmothers, and I definitely thought of the books I saw as a kid about St Teresa, Our Lady of Guadalupe, or The Miracle at Fatima. I felt like all of that was coming to bear on it as well. I’m telling the story of a spiritual being.

AD: Who also happened to be an incredible musician. Coming from a more cynical position, you might look at cult leaders who have used spirituality as a means to acquire power over people. But there really wasn’t any of that when it came to Alice. Zero negative impact. You have to look at it like “She really walked the walk.”  

Andy Beta: That’s literally what Steve Tibbets told me. He said, “She walked the walk.” She had this mystical aspect to her, but she was still of the earth. She was one of the major forces to clean up and help after the LA riots. That’s amazing. The amount of help that she gave to people: cash in envelopes, paying for college, whatever she could do as a force for good.

AD: Why do you think she was so misunderstood back in the day? I mean, “ahead of her time” is sort of a cliché.

Andy Beta: Misogyny plays a huge role, right? As I read the reviews [throughout her life], I saw everything from outright ignoring her to “She’s not John.” I get that John is the lodestar. One of the other things was [the critical claim] that she didn’t have jazz bona fides. But you don’t get Jack DeJohnette and Charlie Haden and other world class jazz players to play for you if you can’t play right. Kenny Burrell’s not gonna make you his piano player if you can’t play behind him.

AD: Like you said, her influence is impossible to measure at this point. But why do you think that there’s been this ongoing groundswell of people getting into Alice Coltrane?

Andy Beta: Alice said this about John’s stuff: You’re drawn to it because you hear another message in it. Something is being conveyed to you. I think that’s what draws people to it. When you listen to a John Coltrane record, I think, “Write all the books you want, think all you want about him,” and then you put it back on and it’s still this immediate, direct thing. It’s unfiltered. I feel that same way about Alice’s music. You can skip over jazz history. If younger generations only trace it back to, say, Bjork and Radiohead and Four Tet, that’s relevant, too. You know, there is no jazz lineage without her in it. But then here’s Universal Consciousness … how does it relate to jazz history? It’s really not indebted to history. It’s another dimension coming in. The freedom of what she did, the places she traversed — that’s what will make her legacy lengthen.
