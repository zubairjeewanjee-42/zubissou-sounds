## Body

In 2015, nearly a decade ago, J Spaceman, John Coxon, Tony “Doggen” Foster, and drummer Rupert Clervaux of Spiritualized performed a new score at a special film screening of photographer William Eggleston’s 1974 experimental film, Stranded in Canton, at the Barbican Gallery in London. Described as “Like Gummo with real people” by Dangerous Minds, Stranded in Canton was shot on a consumer-grade Sony Porta-pak, documenting a feral era of Memphis music, its ghostly night vision optics rendering Eggleston’s black-and-white scenes all the more haunted. Though a recording of their score sat shelved for years, it’s finally seeing release this week as Music for William Eggleston’s Stranded in Canton. Featuring droning blues and fugue state Americana, the music matches the phantasmagoric atmosphere. Ahead of live stagings in London, New York, Los Angeles, and Eggleston’s hometown of Memphis, on the invitation of the photographer’s son Winston Eggleston, J Spaceman, aka Jason Pierce, joined us to discuss Eggleston, Harmony Korine, and his time in Memphis making Ladies and Gentlemen We Are Floating in Space with Jim Dickson and Dr. John. | j woodbury

Aquarium Drunkard: I briefly met you once in 2013 at Coachella. You were doing a meet and greet at the Zia Records record store tent, where I working. We had a quiet moment after a meet and greet. Fat Possum had reissued some Al Green records and I asked you how it felt to be labelmates with him. You got this really wistful look in your eye and mentioned something about how so much music from Memphis has a very specific power. This project is a great reflection of that admiration. When did you first visit Memphis? 

Jason Pierce: I’ve only been there twice. I went initially to meet Jim Dickinson, so sometime around the making of Ladies and Gentlemen We Are Floating In Space at the House of Blues studio. I spent a good week with Jim and his engineer. And then I visited a lot later as a tourist, and just kicked around and did all the things you need to do when you go to Memphis. It’s one of those places, isn’t it? And you never get to go there on tour. There never seems to be a show, so it just seems like you can get close enough but it just seems slightly out of reach for taking the band there. So, in a kind of amazing way, this is oddly the very first show we’ve ever done in Memphis when we get to play there in December this year.

AD: I’m sure that long before you ever went there, Memphis loomed large in your musical imagination.

Jason Pierce: Undoubtedly. It’s almost a Biblical connection. The birth of Elvis. It’s Jerusalem, isn’t it? It’s the start. It’s not only Sun Studios, it’s Stax, The Killer, Willie Mitchell, Hi Records, Jack Clement. It’s the whole thing. Jim Dickinson has this line about it. He says that when places are at sea level, the air’s thicker, so the molecules that you’re pushing around with the speaker cones are bigger. It’s where so much great music comes from. It genuinely feels like it’s in the water there. It’s in Mississippi. The movement of people and poverty; to some extent those always produce music where people say, “This is where I stand.” I come from a small town in the middle of England, but it’s always had this intense romance, that world.

AD: The music of the place is baked into Stranded in Canton; you get a glimpse of Elvis, but also people like Furry Lewis and Alex Chilton, this other lost link in the Memphis wild man tradition. Music is all throughout the film. 

Jason Pierce: It’s infected with that whole world. And Jerry McGill, he sings a song there. I know people are excited about the fact we’re playing these shows, but even showing in the film is so rare. I think the last time it was shown in the UK, when we played that soundtrack to her, that’s getting on 10 years. It’s an amazing document. It’s from the hands of a master photographer; it’s framed so beautifully. It’s like the most primitive of equipment, but in the hands of a genius. It’s not hard on the eye.

AD: It’s beautifully framed but there’s extremity—beer bottles, chickens getting their heads bit off. It reminds me of Werner Herzog saying in his book Every Man for Himself and God Against All, while talking about watching reality TV, “The poet must not avert his gaze.” There’s that, but there’s also the poetry of your soundscapes. I know you hate the term “jamming.” The process is more compositional than that to you?

Jason Pierce: There was a kind of fashion in the UK where promoters got silent movies to screen and bands that were currently hip to kind of do just that, jam. And it was ever so slightly disappointing. It was almost like, “How can we make another show? What can we do?” I guess people were just getting creative in their show ideas to put more shows together. So we were very careful not to fall into “just jam.” I don’t jam anyway. I hate that. I hate the term, I hate the kind of idea of everybody just kind of needlessly making music. Actually, that’s really unfair because it works in so many different ways, but it’s just not something I’ve ever been interested in. But we were also very cautious and incredibly respectful. I’ve never wanted to make music that just kind of is a facsimile of that, a sort of fakery. So we were very light in the approach, I guess.

AD: I feel like I know what you mean about not wanting to do an emulation in a hackneyed way. I almost hear it as a spiritual fugue state version of American music—The Stones connection is there in that, too. I feel like you’re in that same lineage of people who are able to hear the, I don’t know, to hear the ghosts in American music in a specific way.

Jason Pierce: Me and John Coxon, who co-wrote the soundtrack with me, were talking the other day. We were talking about the important things in music and he said, “Intention, that’s the most important.” It’s not just the song. Everybody says it’s the song but if it was, every version of “The Dark End of the Street” or any soul classic would be fantastic. It doesn’t lie in the song alone. It’s in the intention of the artists that performs. I feel like there’s a way of hitting a guitar string that makes sense. There’s a way of playing that makes sense. And if you could narrow it down, you could be a millionaire if you knew how to do these things. But it seems that it’s not even in the kind of stylistic things, the tube amp or the right guitar, it’s a kind of combination of that, but the intention seems to be the most important.

AD: You’ve worked with Harmony Korine, and Stranded in Canton is one of his favorite movies. I hear some parallels between this project and your soundtrack for his movie Mister Lonely. Do you feels like there’s a connection between those two projects? 

Jason Pierce: Between the two soundtracks? Definitely. And also there’s a huge liberation that comes with writing soundtracks. I don’t get nearly enough work like that. It seems like a lot of times with movies, they go for what they know, the sort of the trusted few. A lot of soundtracks are written after the movie has been shot. So there’s a time constraint. But both times I found it incredibly liberating because it’s working to order. I don’t have to front it. I don’t have to stand and say, “This is where I’m at, this is what, this is where I am musically.” And there’s something incredibly liberating about that. It allows you to go in from any angle and to maybe explore things that aren’t forefront in my mind at the moment, but suit the film.

AD: To go back to your formative Memphis experience: What record made you want to work with Jim Dickinson?

Jason Pierce: I think his own record, Dixie Fried, more than anything, but also obviously Big Star’s Third. It seemed like he had all the stories. It seemed like some part of me wasn’t that concerned about my own record. It was more just me and him and hearing him talk. It seems like he knew, a little like Dr. John, like Mac, all the styles. He had knowledge of all the players and all the different styles. They understood the styles of other pianists and other musicians. It seemed like that was lost when both of them passed. That knowledge. They held so much information about where the music came from.

AD: Have you met William Eggleston over the years? 

Jason Pierce: I met him very, very briefly at a gallery show. His gallery show in London, but many years ago and really just to say “Hi,” not much more than that. But we both felt really honored to get that invite to go down and play the soundtrack in Memphis. 

AD: He’s making his own records too.  

Jason Pierce: Yeah, correct. There’s a beautiful thing on YouTube if it’s still there as well of him playing piano. 

AD: One of the coolest things about engaging with this record was seeing his work in black and white. It really does bring out a side of his sensibility that maybe is a little obscured by the color stuff, which just tends to be so dazzling that I kind of maybe miss, you know what I mean?

Jason Pierce: Well the color stuff, that changed the dialogue with photography before that everything, it wasn’t art unless it was in high contrast, black and white or black and white film. So he changed that. The film’s extraordinary just because the framing is so beautiful. Everything about it is, I dunno what to say. And also the equipment isn’t particularly good. I know he changed the lens…

AD: To put on almost like a night vision kind of thing.

Jason Pierce: It gives the faces his kind of ghostliness, a kind of extraordinary kind of translucence. But the framing, the whole sort of thing is still his eye. And that’s a rare thing. Anybody who takes pictures on their phone must know that. You can cut something down to make it more appealing. But to be able to do that without cutting it down to do it in the camera is an amazing skill.
