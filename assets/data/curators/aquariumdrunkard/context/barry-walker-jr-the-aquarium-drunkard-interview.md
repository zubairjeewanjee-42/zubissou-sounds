## Body

Pedal steel adventurist Barry Walker, Jr. has been a trusted and prolific collaborator in recent years — most notably in Rose City Band, but also as a part of Mouth Painter (with his wife Valerie Osterberg) and North Americans (with Patrick McDermott). His sound — both raw and refined — is instantly recognizable amidst the current crop of modern-day steel innovators. Walker has also released a handful of solo albums, including the verifiably awesome Shoulda Zenith, released in 2020 on the Holy Mountain label. The Portland, OR-based musician’s latest bears his name, but it’s not exactly a solo record. 

The Barry Walker Unit’s At the 13th Moon Gravity Well is very much a band effort, with Rose City bandmates Ripley Johnson (guitar) and John Jeffrey (drums) and Mouth Painter bassist Jason Wilmon joining Walker for a loose-limbed, thoroughly exploratory collection of live recordings taped last year at a local pub. With four instrumental tracks clocking in at over 40 minutes, this is deep, transportive stuff, giving Walker and co. a chance to stretch out and get loose, regularly finding moments of collective ecstasy. There’s groove mixed with freedom, melody crashing into dissonance. A total thrill from start to finish. To get a little more background on this magnificent record, we chatted with Barry a few weeks back from his home in Portland. | t wilcox

Aquarium Drunkard: Let’s start by talking about volcanoes! What do you like about them — other than the fact that they’re kind of inherently awesome. 

Barry Walker, Jr.: I’m a geologist, so I’ve been drawn to volcanoes my whole life, just for the reasons that anybody finds them special. The idea of lava and magma, even as kid, has been something that’s very visceral to me. I read a lot, but half of my reading is scientific literature. I’m just immersed in that world. Beyond my family, the two things that I’m obsessed with are music and geology, so when it comes to naming songs, that’s usually where my head goes. Anytime you’re totally into the granular side of any topic, there are all these weird terms, these turns of phrase that can have an emotional resonance. That’s what the geological side of me does — it allows me to put text to things. Naming songs is hard! Especially when it’s instrumental.

But the volcano thing — we just happened to schedule these [Barry Walker Unit] shows on May 18, which is the anniversary of the eruption of Mount St. Helens. It was only after they were booked that I realized that. But I thought, “Oh we should do something about that!” I composed a piece that was just strictly a visual indicator of when to play. There were no notes. There was a 24-hour seismogram of the events of that day. I took the seismogram, and I scaled it down to 12 minutes — 24 hours into 12 minutes. And we all had a score and our clocks, and we played that, just on that one day. It’s not on the record but it was really fun to do. We didn’t rehearse it or anything, but the guys were really into it. We didn’t put it on the record because there was very little emotion in it. It was kind of academic exercise that didn’t quite fit with the other songs. A good third of it was a wall of noise, like the eruption. But the room was packed that night, and I think everyone had a good time. We’ll probably release it at some point!

AD: I was wondering if you have a different perspective on things as a geologist, since you deal with epochs, millions and billions of years. Is that comforting at all during times like this, when it seems like the world is spiraling? 

Barry Walker, Jr.: Yeah, it’s comforting, but it’s definitely escapist. But it is comforting. In the same way, I just went out to central Oregon for a week. We had no cell phone reception. A little internet here and there, but we just dipped out of the panopticon. It’s a cleanse. But then I come back and I’m getting all of this information leaking in again. It’s highly disconcerting and depressing. I don’t know. It is just comforting mentally to read about stuff that has taken place over these large time scales. And I read science fiction as well, as a way to cope, I guess.

AD: Absolutely. But let’s talk more about the record — I’m assuming the songs on the new record are improvisational in nature. Is that accurate? 

Barry Walker, Jr.: I had like three or four compositions that we rehearsed. But with “High In The Hummocks,” that bass line was all that I had. And there’s a basic chord progression. But it was a loose structure. What I play and what Ripley and John play are always changing. But Jason had to hold it down. And he knew that and accepted the challenge. The first song on the album, “Sweet Gum In Ash” is more of a jazz thing, where there’s a theme and then we diverge and come back to the theme. The other two are completely improvisational. 

I’ve played with Jason for a long time in Mouth Painter. And then me and John usually room together when we’re on tour with Rose City Band and we’re always having long talks about music. We’ve all got a synergy of sorts, and I wanted to take that and do something with it. And I knew I wanted to make a live record. They were all game. In the end, we had like five or six hours of stuff we played and we thought: “Hopefully we can get 35 minutes of usable material out of that!” [laughs]. But that was the idea. It was to take these players and turn ‘em loose within a context. I actually think I’m more comfortable getting loose than the others are, maybe. Ripley and John have been playing together so long and they’re so tight. Even listening back to some of it, I could hear, like OK, Ripley and John are doing their thing. That’s them playing together for 10 years. But that’s great too. That’s just one more element. 

AD: Honestly, the thing I kept thinking about when hearing this record was the classic John Coltrane Quartet records, where it’s this ensemble improvisation. 

Barry Walker, Jr.: I tried to get Ripley to solo a little more, but he wanted to play a more supporting role. It’s like the guitarists in Miles’ ’74 bands. They’re grooving so hard. Ripley was listening to that and that was one of his inspirations — Reggie Lucas on those mid-70s records. Not going all McLaughlin on it. 

AD: You mentioned that you knew you wanted to make a live record right from the start — why was that? 

Barry Walker, Jr.: Initially, I wanted to have a residency at this place and play every week for months. But logistically that was just too hard to make happen. I wanted to have it where if people came into the club and didn’t have any idea a show was going on, they could still sit in the back and have their drinks and not listen to the band. Kind of a jazz club situation. Everybody in the band, we’re all jazz fans. That was my vision — we could play, and people could listen … or not. I was really inspired by the Jeff Parker ETA IVtet recordings that have been coming out the last couple years. 

If we’d done it in the studio, I would’ve wanted it to be different, overdubs, all of that. I just didn’t want to deal with it. I just wanted to play. And then, if we had to massage it, splice things, cut it down afterwards, we would do that. But it turned out we barely had to do any of that at all. 

AD: What do you like about playing in this more improvisational mode? Does it come naturally to you? 

Barry Walker, Jr.: Sometimes it’s hard. I think I’m always searching for this feeling that I don’t get every performance. Sometimes it’ll be five seconds in a whole set where I feel completely in flow. I’m in the energetic nexus. It doesn’t come from an isolated place. There’s a connectivity between me and the other players. With Rose City Band in particular, those guys are all really good. A lot of times, I’m the limiting factor. The highs will be higher, but the lows will be low [laughs]. Playing things the same way every time is not what I want to do.

AD: I have a running theory that everyone should be in a band. Not that I want to hear every one of those bands! But it’s a good practice, collaborating with people on what can be very abstract concepts, trying to find solutions for problems, sometimes in real time. 

Barry Walker, Jr.: Everyone has a different idea of what they’re comfortable with or what they’re trying to do. And they don’t always align. And you can feel very strongly one way or another about very small things. You think about how you want to represent yourself to a listener. This band, the Barry Walker Unit, was pretty well-aligned with how we wanted to sound and come across. But there’s always that chance that you tee it up and nothing really happens. It’s not happening. You know, we played two, two-and-half hours each night and you can start to run out of ideas. It’s hard to generate ideas during a truly improvisational set. 

AD: It can be humbling. As a musician, I’ve often thought: “I’m completely out of ideas here, there are only three things I even know how to do!” [laughs] And that’s like two minutes in … 

Barry Walker, Jr.: When we were rehearsing, our meditations were always bass and drums centric. What kind of groove are we going to lay down. And those were the open-ended suggestions I’d make to Jason and John. Here’s a zone and you can figure it out. Sometimes, when listening back to this record, I would just solo the drums and bass and think, “Man, this is amazing.” John plays great on the whole thing. Particularly with Moon Duo but also Rose City Band, his playing is really consistent. That’s his meditative vehicle, that consistency. He’d say that allows other things to happen.

AD: Well, this feels like a good live record — it’s happening! There’s a feeling of danger in there. 

Barry Walker, Jr.: I’m perfectly fine with embarrassing myself [laughs]. I know the other guys are somewhat. I know Jason is, because we’ve done that before. He doesn’t embarrass himself, but I’ve put him in musical situations where we could’ve embarrassed ourselves. And whatever! 

AD: Take me back a little bit — this is a fantastic pedal steel record, really showing off the possibilities of the instrument. What drew you to the pedal steel? 

Barry Walker, Jr.: I grew up in Nashville. I would hear the pedal steel, but I never really paid much attention to it. My parents didn’t actually listen to much country music. You know, there’s a tendency in the south to maybe be a little bit embarrassed about the hokey parts of Southern culture. Like the show “Hee Haw” was really embarrassing for a lot of people. Even though it was it was on purpose. You know, they were in on the joke. These were extremely great musicians doing all the music and hilarious people performing.

Anyway, it wasn’t until I was an adult where it was like, “Ohh, I should try to do this.” I don’t know what it was. I’ve related it to the bagpipes in the past. When I was a kid, I was kind of obsessed with that sound. It was just electrifying to me. And then at a certain point, the pedal steel became the thing. It’s that moving harmony. I love singing and it’s kind of a similar thing where you get a vocal quality out of it. I’m a big fan of Hindustani classical music, the vocalists in particular. That’s a major inspiration. 

I’ve never been very good at copying people’s licks. If I start to do that I usually don’t want to play anymore. With some of the stuff on this record, I was purposely avoiding playing how I learned how to play. In terms of my pedal steel education, though, Red Rhodes is a huge influence. Norman Blake is not a pedal steel player, but I feel like his phrasing and rhythm and melodic sense had a real impact. When I listen to Norman play guitar, it hits me. That’s exactly perfect for me. The modern pedal steel guys — Luke Schneider is someone I love. Paul Franklin is another one I’ll watch YouTube videos of, he’s a Nashville session guy. 

AD: My impression of the pedal steel is that it’s very difficult to play. 

Barry Walker, Jr.: It is a difficult instrument. When you’re playing guitar, you can use your fingers and do these various things. There’s a linear element when you want to play a higher note, which you can do with a pedal steel, but it’s different. Those frets aren’t there, so there’s always this intonation issue. Unless you’re perfect! Pedal steel players use the rods and the pedals. And then you use different strings. You hop around on the strings. 

But the sort of poly-cosmic view you have to have of the fretboard and your equipment … it’s deep. You have to be able to see it. That just comes with years of practice and experience. I’m still on my journey. Every single time I play, I’m trying to play things, and I can’t do it. There are things I want to play in the moment that I can’t quite reach. Sometimes it works, sometimes I realize I just can’t get there. Crash and burn. But the pedal steel can be so beautiful.
