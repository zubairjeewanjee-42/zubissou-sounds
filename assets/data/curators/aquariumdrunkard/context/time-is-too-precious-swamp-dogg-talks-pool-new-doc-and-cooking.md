## Body

You can count on Swamp Dogg to always do the unexpected. Whether crafting album covers that have baffled listeners for decades, pairing autotuned vocals with sleek indie soul, or going country, songwriter, producer, and raconteur Jerry Williams blazes his own path. So tellingly his new documentary, Swamp Dogg Gets His Pool Painted finds directors Isaac Gale, Ryan Olson and David McMurry eschewing typical music doc tropes in favor of psychedelic animation, oddball shorts, poignant interviews, and yes, an overarching artistic project of painting Swamp Dogg’s signature rat image in the pool at his San Fernando Valley home. With appearances from Swamp’s family—including his late wife Yvonne—as well as friends like Johnny Knoxville, Guitar Shorty, John Prine, and more elucidating Swamp’s tales, both tall and short, the film equals a one-of-a-kind look at his life. Williams joined us via Zoom to discuss, reflecting on his lifelong passion for cooking along the way. | j woodbury

Aquarium Drunkard: Congrats on Swamp Dogg Gets His Pool Painted; I’ve never seen a music documentary quite like it. 

Swamp Dogg: I haven’t ever seen one like it either. It’s almost like a movie more so than a documentary.

AD: Did making it take some convincing on your part?

Swamp Dogg: I’d worked with [co-director] Ryan Olson; we’d done a music video. Anyway, he’s nosy. He was over and started looking through my stuff, looking at my memos and mementoes, all my crap. He was shooting some of that and they decided they just wanted to shoot a lot of stuff. It’s turned out as a documentary. I’ve had two or three directors approach me in my lifetime—well, whose other lifetime could it be, come to think of it—about making a movie, but I’d never hear back. So I usually blow it off. I didn’t know if this was going to happen either until it really started happening. 

AD: The title itself is captivating. You don’t know what you’re in for. Were you already planning on getting your pool painted, or was that something that came out of the film?

Swamp Dogg: I wanted it. I think that was one of the first things that pulled me in, that got me really interested and wanting to participate: the fact that I was going to finally get my white rat and so forth in the pool. It is a weird title, isn’t it? Wait a minute, Swamp Dog gets his pool painted? Who cares? Lemme go see what this is about. Yeah, gets them into the theater. 

AD: Your album covers are very distinct. The visual side of Swamp Dogg has always been accentuated. 

Swamp Dogg: I had tried to make my own music videos in the past before they really got popular. Working with other people, they wanted to make them look old timey and black and white and crackly, and I didn’t like that. Then I tried to make some myself, and they came out worse than the ones that I hated [from other directors]. But making this was something I dove into and they did a great job. Sometimes we stretch things out, we want to say yes or no, but we’ll wait around before we do it. I don’t do that. The time is too precious. So I just say: do you like it? No, I don’t like it. Or, yes, I’m crazy about it. Either way.

AD: This film features a lot of your friends and collaborators, including Moogstar, who set up our Zoom call, and the late Guitar Shorty and the late John Prine. I want to ask you about John; what drew you to his songs? 

Swamp Dogg: He was a storyteller. And I tell stories too. I tell stories that are a little more upbeat than John. [Laughs]  Lemme tell you, I do “Sam Stone” usually at the end of my set, which he wrote, and that’s when all of the dancing and screaming turns to tears.  I like that. Before he passed, we had planned to go to his house in Ireland for about a week or two and just on some songs. I’m sorry that couldn’t happen. He was very quiet, but meticulous and strived for perfection and creativity. 

AD: The movie documents your time working with World Class Wreckin’ Cru in the ‘80s. Did you have a sense that a young Dr. Dre was really going to go on to be be huge? Could you spot that ambition? 

Swamp Dogg: I knew he was going to be something. They all had ambitions. Some had ’em in other fields, like DJ Yella is producing [pornographic] movies and videos. I know what Lonzo [Alonzo Williams] is doing because we connect at least once every 60 days, I guess. We do things together.

AD: You can’t fit a whole life into a movie but…

Swamp Dogg: They’ve got enough footage on the floor to do two more of these. Not that that’s going to happen, but there’s enough footage on the floor for that. They couldn’t cover everything thing unless I was a Lady Gaga or something and people would sit through that. It would have to be done in two or three parts. 

AD: I want a doc like that Beatles doc. Swamp Dogg Gets Back. Eight hours or something.

Swamp Dogg: Boy, you are glutton for punishment, huh? 

AD: Maybe there can be some bonus stuff on the DVD. I hope they make a DVD.

Swamp Dogg: I do too. I miss CDs and DVDs. 

AD: Watching the movie back, where you struck by anything that surprised you about your own life? 

Swamp Dogg: There are a couple of things in the movie that were so stupid, things I had to decide I wouldn’t let happen again. Seeing it back reconfirmed what I had already decided. 

AD: Regarding money? Blowing cash on a fleet of cars? 

Swamp Dogg: I just went off, I went crazy with money. Two cars is all I needed. I had nine. That wasn’t necessary, but I didn’t know what to do. I didn’t understand business. When I graduated, I’d say 50% of graduates didn’t even know how to write a check. And this was in the Black schools. In the white schools, they were learning more about economics and how to read the stock tables. We didn’t get that. We didn’t get no further than Columbus and his shit. And being Catholic, I thought the Pope discovered America! 

AD: You’ve worked A&R, as a label guy, and in the background of the music industry for years. How different is the industry now? 

Swamp Dogg: Very different because in the ‘60s, ‘70s, and ‘80s, accountants got involved in the music. Their thoughts were always on the bottom line. “I don’t think this is going to sell. Now, this one sounds like the other one that just did two million. So let’s go with that.” Attorneys and accountants, they kind of ruined the business. As a matter of fact, in my cookbook, there’s an entire article that I wrote for a magazine out of San Francisco, but it showed up in a lot of magazines. The title of it is “How Many Accountants and Lawyers Does It Take To Fuck Up the Music Industry?” broke it down. 

AD: That makes me of the cover of your 1981 album, I’m Not Selling Out—I’m Buying In! You’re dancing on the boardroom table. 

Swamp Dogg: Exactly. I’m like “You don’t want what I want, let me give you what you want.” You get tired of starving, so if this is where the meat and potatoes are going to come from. I’m going to give it to you. 

AD: You mentioned your cookbook, If You Can Kill It I Can Cook It. Have you always enjoyed cooking? Who got you started in the kitchen? 

Swamp Dogg: That was my way of relaxing, and it still is. The women that raised me made such great foods. I learned them. All of the recipes aren’t 100% mine. Some I learned growing up. And they taught me. I used to spend more time in the kitchen than anywhere else, especially when they were cooking. I give them credit. I loved them to death and they loved me.

AD: I’m really interested in getting my hands on the book, if only to find out what “Baked Beans Bo Diddley” is. 

Swamp Dogg: Now, there’s a lot of emotions in some of those titles! Because Bo Diddley, when he talked to me, it was like I was something that had come out of his ass. I went to him to try and do an album. He had just performed and he was like, “I don’t know if I want to do nothing with [someone] named Swamp Dogg.” I said, “Damn, give me a shot.” I wasn’t asking him to do it for nothing. I had laid out a whole plan for an advance and his percentage and it was fair. 

I wasn’t trying to get him to do his little Bo Diddley thing. I always thought he could really sing romantic. You don’t think of Bo Diddley as romantic. But that song “Crackin’ Up,” you ever heard that? That’s a great song and his performance is fantastic. I wanted to shoot for that. But anyway, back to what you were saying, the recipe is the shortest recipe in the book. I haven’t played his albums since then. It’s hard for me to enjoy Bo Diddley. All of us guys have a way of being nice even when we don’t want to be. He could’ve done that. 

AD: There’s a huge reveal party for the pool painting at the end of the movie, and people are coming to wish you well and honor you for your birthday. If one gets invited over to Swamp Dogg’s house, what’s the best thing to bring as a gift? 

Swamp Dogg: One of my albums for me to autograph! [Laughs] 

AD: I’ll keep that in mind. 

Swamp Dogg: You’ve got a standing invitation from this moment on.
