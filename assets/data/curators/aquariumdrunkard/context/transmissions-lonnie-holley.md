## Body

Welcome back to Transmissions, your weekly conversational series from Aquarium Drunkard in partnership with the Talkhouse Podcast Network. This week on the show, a long awaited return visit from Lonnie Holley. The Atlanta artist joins us alongside his manager, Matt Arnett, son of William Arnett, the Southern art curator and collector who brought Holley to the attention of the art world in the 1980s. 

His art draws from what’s thrown out—a theme he returns to often—but also from personal tragedy: first artistic project was carving headstones for his sister’s two children, who died in a house fire in Alabama in 1979. Since then, his found-object assemblages, paintings, and collages have endeared him to the fine art world—they have even been displayed in the Smithsonian and the White House—in part due to the patronage and care of Arnett’s father. 

In those days, Holley often worked directly with trash—taking discarded materials to forge his sculptures. Philip K. Dick has said “the symbols of the divine show up in our world initially at the trash stratum,” and in Lonnie’s case, that truth is made evident. 

Transmissions :: Lonnie Holley

But the younger Arnett helped Holley get on the path he walks know, as an oracular sculpture of sound. Lonnie and Matt join us ahead of the March 21st release of Holley’s new album, Tonky. Crafted with Irish producer Jacknife Lee (R.E.M., U2, The Killers) and featuring guests like Isaac Brock of Modest Mouse, harpist Mary Lattimore, rappers Billy Woods and Open Mike Eagle, spoken word from Saul Williams, and others, Tonky rattles with blues-punk-industrial art folk anthems. We discussed the new album, Holley’s poetic metaphysics, and his work with kindred spirit Richard Swift.

AD: If anything, these last couple of years, we’ve seen this rolling back of so much progress. It’s a strong and sad reminder that it’s a continual struggle. It doesn’t stop. It doesn’t go away. What are you talking about when you sing about love?

Lonnie Holley: When I look at it, when I look at just the word “protest” and the word “fighting” for a reason or “fighting” for the reality of the reasons, if our ancestors helped establish the reasons for us to be who we are, they worked hard as hell. They were considered—some of them, especially my ancestors—were considered the slaves, the first ones on this nation. And then, those that had done so much, going to war… Grandpa went off to war. Daddy went off to World War II. Grandpa was in World War I. And the women left behind mourned them—not only crying about them while they were gone or missing them but then facing the challenge of reintegrating them when they returned, only to find they were no longer appreciated for what they sacrificed. All of these things really, really matter, and they should not be left out of history.

And the best way for us to tell about those ways now, since we have so much digital and globalization, is this: protest with love. Whatever we’re going to do, let’s do it on our computers. We don’t even have to leave home. This is what that song is all about—not having to leave home, but making your statement right there on your laptop. Let your children see you doing that. Share that with them, and then they’ll see that our grandchildren now…You know what? I did a lot of things about the elder people that were protesting in Birmingham, Alabama. I did a lot about the children who lost their lives. I did a lot about the bombing of not only their home but the church itself. And away from that, the aggravation that a lot of the ministers and pastors and whoever else were dealing with—anybody that put their handprint or put their identity in it got treated like, “Hey, this is what it’s going to be like.” And it looked like we’re going to have to go back through that again. And I don’t think we need to go backwards.

Matt Arnett: One of the things that continually amazes me, having worked and known Lonnie for so long, is all that he’s been through in his life. You mentioned the album titled Tonky, which Lonnie chose because that’s what he was called when he was young. That was his nickname—Tonky—because he lived in a honky tonk. And when Lonnie was 11, he spent time at the Alabama Industrial School for Negro Children, which he talks about in the podcast Unreformed. In one of his many interviews for that podcast, he was asked, “If these people who did all of these horrible things to you walked in the door right now, what would you do to them?” That question was asked to a handful of the survivors. And all of their answers were about violence and the things they wanted to do to exact revenge. And when Lonnie was asked the question, he said, “I would get up and give them a hug because clearly, those people who did this to small children were broken. And what they need is love, not more hatred.”

Lonnie Holley: But also, it was forced upon them because they were under command. You have to remember—they were under the command of the lead commanders. They were behind the scenes, mostly. And that’s who you’re protesting against—the lead commanders. They are the ones who supply the currency to make these things happen. They’ll supply the money; they’ll give or don’t give—one or the other, you see what I’m saying? And then they got you out there protecting what they want most out of the whole thing. And that’s either the properties or the money. Whatever else they can get out of it to feed their growth.

AD: Lonnie and Matt, to hear you guys talk about that, because obviously, you’re so right on the money in terms of saying systemic protest is sort of a different thing than the one-on-one, back-and-forth bickering—because that doesn’t really produce anything good. But being able to look at the way the system even puts people who are ostensibly on the better end of the deal in the position of destroying their own souls through their actions, right?

Lonnie Holley: Because that’s what they’re destroying now. It’s almost like what has been made to gain—the change is going to come. Yeah. Changes that have been made for us, like the insurance policy that had already been made for us—now it’s going to be cut away from us. All of this stuff is going to be taken away from us. So now, here it is, you’ve got to go back through the whole nine yards of trying to get back what you once had. And it’s almost like the sharecroppers of Alabama. I remember my grandfather was a sharecropper, and he ran up out of Dadeville, Alabama. And my grandmother, she was a sharecropper. She ran up out of a place called Goodwater, Alabama. Yeah. Where they were sharecropping, you know, going into the field, doing whatever it was. It was not only cotton that these people were picking—it was a lot of other fields. 

That’s why, in my music, I may do a song called “The Many Fields” or “The Many Rivers to Cross.” It was so much more than meets the eye. And I try to bring it to our attention. Let’s pay attention. Let’s be willing to historically study. Don’t study to make yourself a fool, or study to let what you study lead you into the quicksand fields of stupidity. Stupidity is just a place to be. But having pity upon yourself before you even get pitiful. You know what I’m saying? There are a lot of ways that the elders or the grandparents or great-grandparents used their words. And they didn’t even know how to read or write. But they used their words very wisely. Whatever they said, that’s how their children could grow—and grow prosperously.

AD: Matt, you talked about this moment where, Lonnie, you were on the podcast talking about how you would give these aggressors a hug. You know, to me, it’s really worth thinking about how violence is sort of like—that’s where imagination ends, right? Like when you can’t think of another thing to do, that’s where violence comes in. That’s where violence comes into play. But when you have imagination, it can go beyond those limits. All of a sudden, now, violence isn’t the only option. There are other options too, maybe.

I think of imagination as almost like a thing that we can tap into. And when I’ve seen you live, every time, it feels like that’s what you’re doing. You’re jumping into that pool of the subconscious, and you’re swimming around. And in that field of imagination, essentially, you pull up all this poetry. And it’s so beautiful. But I think about how people don’t always give themselves permission to imagine that way—or maybe they don’t even know how to imagine that way. What would you say to people who want to get deeper in touch with their own ability to imagine all sorts of possibilities, right? Because it feels like our options are a little bleak right now. It doesn’t feel like there are a lot of good options. But that’s because we’re not imagining, you know?

Lonnie Holley: I would have to say, think about it. Think about it really, really seriously. Think about it. Because right now, there are things that are happening on this earth—even if they have to be protested about. They’re going to have to be. We’re going to have to go in with the established people that are considered to be the leaders or the ones making whatever funds available for whatever needs to be taken care of, especially the children. The children—their education started off with what their talents or what their skill growth pointed out to be. So if you take the arts and the crafts, just a little necessary thing for us to say: we know how to be skillful. We know how to work this. We know how to take a bunch of weeds and take the seeds and plant the weeds with the flowers. And then you’ve got yourself a bouquet of something that belongs to nature and something that belongs in the bouquet of flowers.

My thing is, let’s deal with the bushes as well as deal with the flowers. This way, you can see the beauty, and you can see the roughness of Mother Nature. Mother Nature and Father Time are the most terrible thing to try to fight with. We can make war with anything else on the planet or in the universe, but don’t make war with Mother Nature and Father Time.

AD: [Laughs] And so, you know, when you talk about Father Time and Mother Nature—when you add those two together, is that sort of where you get Mother Universe in your cosmology? You know, because I’ve always loved…

Lonnie Holley: She holds it all. She’s the mother of them. You know, she gave them birth. And she knew what they were going to do even before they did it. She knows what every planet is going to do because, you know, the whole thing about if I were God or if I were the God of the universe. Now, maybe God’s a different reason. This one, these are the mixtures of God that man has created on the earth. And a lot of times, when we call on them because we have played so stupid when we did or didn’t. It’s almost like, was chicken little when the sky was falling? The child who cried wolf and it wasn’t no wolf when the wolf did come around, when he cried wolf, that nobody didn’t even want to come to his rescue. These things here, we’re not. We don’t want to play foolish with the game that we are learning from. Yeah. We don’t want to play foolish now. Playing foolish is really, really stupid.  And my mama had 27 children. And the only thing she said was, “Don’t be stupid.” 

AD: When you have 27 kids, I suppose you really have to figure out something to say that’s going to speak to all of them. 

Lonnie Holley: A powerful word. “I’m going to get up with a little whoop-ass today. I’m going to tear up some ass”. No, you don’t gotta always do that. Just tear up some minds. Just say one word to affect their mind. I may have a lot of haters. They say, “What’s this old man talking about? He needs to go sit his old ass down.” I may have a lot, a lot of haters. But in a sense, haven’t we faced enough? Haven’t we faced enough while acting stupid? We didn’t even make preparation. And we know that here’s a new season coming in. Have we made preparation? Have we given donations to where all that we need to be protected, protected? We got enough recycled material. We got enough to have the stuff built to put a fence. We say, again, is the piece in the book William Arnett created called “Will You Build a Fence All Around Me?” where I’m asking the spirit to build a fence all around me and protect me each and every day. I’m talking about Matt’s father, William Arnett. He was the one that was interested in not only my art, but he probably even knew that I was going to turn out to be a singer.

Matt is the one that actually took my work to the level of saying, “You need to go into the studio, you need to have an opportunity.” And I think everybody that’s poetic, that has talents and skills, they should be given a chance. And that’s what we’re working so hard for. We’re working hard to where these people can have these institutions. These institutions can supply them with what they need for them. What do you say? Make, make yourself worthy. Yeah, yeah, and have the means. Study to do what? Make yourself worthy. It doesn’t mean you’ve got to go to college or go to high school or elementary, but it does mean that you have to study. You have to study your parents. You have to look at their ways. And many parents don’t have any jobs like their ancestors had. Ancestors had a hard job, man, because they were either in slavery, right out of slavery, or protesting to get way away from slavery. That’s all that. That’s if you listen to that new piece of music, the other music. If you look at them, they are just chapters behind the new album. And I think that was what Matt is finding out. That’s what he’s doing, Matt Arnett. I think what he’s doing, he’s listening to the music and saying, “We’ve got the next album. Yeah, we got the next album already ready to come out.” But it takes people to start believing in the first album. 

Yeah, not this one, but the first album, because it had so much on it. I see all those albums behind you. I see the two guitars. I’m—and I wish that you had a little piece of mind that you can have hanging behind you. But again, got me coming through for their ears. And it’s going to be great. You take two people such as you say, “OK, I fell in love with this man’s work.” You say, “I fell in love with this man’s work. “Somebody else is going to say, “We fell in love with this man’s work.” But you had to present it to them. You see what I’m talking about? And you have to present me to the world. You see what I’m talking about?

AD: Well, this one gosh, it’s such a cool-sounding record. You’ve been working with Jacknife Lee for a little while, and it seems like you guys went to a new height on this one. Lonnie, there are so many sounds I hear, but on this one, I really do hear a sort of a blues thing. And then rhythmically, it’s like industrial-blues pop? It’s so cool.You’re really on, really on one on this one. What do you think when you hear this stuff played back?

Lonnie Holley: I think Jack—I call him Jack, Jacknife Lee—I think he was listening to me. Verbally, he was listening to my experiences. And he knew, out of those experiences, he was also listening to the music of the past. He knew that. He knew that I had been listening to the music of the past ever since I was a little bitty boy. So with us being the one that actually—and Matt—he had to go through not only what I’m going through, but he had to go through all of these other different musical journeys, too, in order to say, “Well, now let me see where I should place Lonnie on this chart of music.” This is 2025. This is called the new millennium—25 years into the new millennium. And Lonnie Holley almost been in every year of that, because Matt started working with this in 2009. Right, Matt? 2009. Really, you had it in your car. Really, he’s been working with it way before then, before the new millennium even came in. It’s almost a process of stuff. We got to listen and see how much it takes for anything to be processed. Yeah, it’s like our new rulership right now that we’ve let slip back. We backed up in it. Excuse me, we backed up in it, right? And then if we allow that to govern our future, we won’t have a future because it’s already starting anger. It’s already starting anger. I think this album is dead-on fucking time. 

Already creating anger to the point people don’t know what they…I don’t know what to cry about. Half of the women don’t… I don’t know what to cry about. The children are going to school, being shot at in school. They elders are scared to go outside of their homes and doors. You got institutions closing. You got institutions that’s going to help humanity. You know, Matt was telling me the other day, “Maybe your insurance for your health is going to be cut off.” I’m 75 years old. I don’t need to be hearing no news like that, man. We’re barely getting things sold. Yeah, because other things are in the way. We know some people don’t have the extras to sell. That carries me back to, “You can’t wear but one pair of shoes off in your grave,” and my grandmama taught me that. No matter how rich they are, how many pairs of shoes they can’t wear one pair of shoes in their grave. That is a heavy… that’s a heavy statement…

…To me, my grandmama. And my dad, [he said] “You can leave home with an empty wagon, but don’t come home with an empty wagon. You can leave home with an empty trunk…but you don’t put nothing in your trunk?” I told my son, “Go to the junkyard and find me an old Rolls Royce and rebuild it for me and let me show them what I’m going to do. I want to show them I can in ride an old Rolls Royce. Well, watch and see how much art I’m going to– art material stuff. Watch how much comes out of it.

Matt Arnet: You asked about Jacknife. Our relationship with him began sort of by accident. He was a fan of Lonnie’s music, and he was working on a record—an instrumental record, like a percussion record, with Lol Tolhurst and Budgie. He was working on that material, and he decided that that record needed more and it needed voices. And he reached out to us to see if Lonnie would be willing to lay down some vocals on one of the tracks. And we were going to be in L.A. not long after that, and so we set up like a four-hour window for Lonnie to come in and listen to the music and lay down vocals. And Jacknife started… he’s like, “I have a bunch of these tracks. Let me play them for you.” And the first one he played, he got about 30 seconds into it, and Lonnie was like, “That’s enough. Stop. I like it. I know what I want to do.” And he said, “Do you want to hear the whole song?” And he said, “No, no. I know exactly what I want to do.” So Lonnie laid down the vocal track on the whole piece, and it took like—not very long—because he’s Lonnie, and he had an idea.

And, so, we had scheduled to have four hours, and here we were, we’re done after half an hour. And so, we decided… Jacknife’s studio is filled with instruments, keyboards, and machines. And Lonnie kept walking around, you know, “What does this one do? What sound does this make?” And, so, they just kept making music together. And it was so enriching for Lonnie, and the sounds that he was getting, and the collaborative process between him and Jacknife. It was so apparent from the jump that we decided to come in the next day, which was Sunday, which we both had to open and record again. And it just so happens that the morning that we went on Sunday to Jacknife’s, we had breakfast with Camae [Ayewa], or Moor Mother, before we went to the studio. And Lonnie asked her if she wanted to come hang out at the studio. And of course, if you’re in the studio, and you’re talented, Lonnie’s not going to let you just sit there and watch. And so, he had Jacknife set up a keyboard and a mic, and they collaborated, and it was so amazing that the idea to expand and invite other collaborators to join—that’s where that idea started. And, and it was so much fun, and the collaborators loved the music so much and added so much to it without kind of stepping all over it, which is always a concern. Yeah, they were also tasteful in their additive process that we just decided that, you know, we go to L.A. a lot, that we would just keep working. And the next thing you know, we had a finished record, but we weren’t done. 

So, we just kept working, and out of that grew a second record, which… really, it’s interesting that it sounds so different, but at the same time, they’re rooted to each other in a way. But so much had changed in the world, and because Lonnie’s process is all about what’s happening now, the musical styles and the things Lonnie was moved by and influenced by had changed. So, the records, even though they were made in the same place, don’t really sound like it.

AD: Yeah, that’s really cool. Well, it sounds like an incredibly fruitful collaboration with Jacknife. One of the things that I’ve loved talking to you about or hearing you reflect on in the past was working with Richard Swift, who, you know, that’s another person who really seemed to listen to you and hear what you wanted to do in this interesting way. I would love to hear you reflect a little bit on what your time with Richard Swift was like, Lonnie. What impression did you get of him?

Lonnie Holley: I think Richard Swift’s kind of attitude about music is probably the same as Jacknife Lee, because if you look at it, both of them were interested what could allow a person to groove. What the music did for them, how the music is a soother… See, I’ve always listened to music for a lot of reasons, one for an educational reason, and two, for the reason that it made me shake my head and made me get into the groove. You don’t always have to get on the dance floor, but when your body started reeling and rocking and you just sitting in one place and patting your feet, stomping your feet, and I’m hollering, shouting “hallelujah,” and all of this other kind of stuff, you don’t have to jump up and get all over the church to do that; you can stay right in your seat.

I saw how mild-mannered my grandmother was in church, you know, and she was in her eighties, getting ready to be ninety-something years old. So how, you know, she didn’t have the stamina or the energy to jump up like she probably did when she was in a younger age.. But the whole thing is to say that Jack and Swift were a person that said, “Go ahead and try, that’s what it’s there for. You do you.” I never had the opportunity in my whole life to sit down and play a piano the way I wanted to play it that day. But he had one all gutted out where you could just see—you could see almost straight through it. And then to see the keys moving in front of me as I did this stuff, man, it just went, it shielded me. Just the piece of music that I did on it with Richard Swift, I think it’s going to always be one of an original, not only an original, but it’s going to be one that was collaborated on. The man put his heart and his soul into it. I mean, literally, he got up after being me and jumped on and tased and came right on out the next morning and worked it hard as hell with me to finish making this album that we was working on. So these kind of people that show,”Hey, man, I’m going to be on the front line with you.” I mean, both of us cried on each other’s shoulder. He was crying. I was crying. I did a piece of art with him. I mean, let him do some painting. I don’t know if you saw the album, man. It was just like, okay. This person, who is he? Who is on the edge of the world, on the edge of America—in the backwoods of Portland, Oregon? He’s out in the depths of the tall trees. He’s out in the depths of what he needed to be in order for us to celebrate the burning. They actually fried that burning for miles and miles and miles. Now, if you look at what that, what was allowed to be celebrated with that piece of music, man, I’m just simply saying, I wish that people would understand…[Laughs] Wow. And he’s told some… he’s told some… he told some musical interludes in there himself. He actually charged it. Jacknife Lee charged it with some of his own energy, too.

Matt Arnett: I have to say that we’ve been very lucky over the years to find, you know, chosen family in the people that we’ve worked with, from Jacknife Lee and Swift and Shahzad [Ismaily], who’s mainly a figure at Figure Eight in New York. Just you go into the studio, you never know how that, you know, it’s a relationship. You never… when you book studio time somewhere, you just never know if it’s going to work out, and I’veheard so many horror stories about people recording and nothing, you know, nothing happened. And it’s been so lucky that, you know, Shahzad, who you started recording with about 2016 or so, and Swift, who we recorded with starting in 2013, and Jacknife in 2020. Those are all like, and Davis Hart, our friend in Florida. We started working with about five years ago, and Davis is, you know, travels, does a lot of Lonnie’s live recording and engineering.

Lonnie Holley: But the first one named was Chris, wasn’t he?

Matt Arnett: Chris Griffin, Chris Griffin, the first engineer… 

Lonnie Holley: No, you were the first engineer, but..

Matt Arnett: Brando Marius at Gee’s Bend. The very first recordings Lonnie did professionally, sorta professionally. We built a studio to record gospel music and Lonnie went. That was his first time, sort of, in a studio that felt like a professional studio, but he’s been very lucky that all of those people are like family now. Yeah, like they’re all… I mean, Lonnie’s older than all of them considerably. So he thinks of them as, you know, like sons, you know, and that’s… And I think when you’re that comfortable in a place, Lonnie’s already going to be open and give a lot of himself. But when he can feel that there are open arms on the other side of the board saying, “Give me everything you’ve got.” Don’t be afraid to tell me things, to say things and go anywhere you want to go. And I’m here to receive it. It’s very empowering. And it really leads to the creation, in my opinion, of great art.

AD: I really like that. I like that. You know, in the little film that was made about your time with Swift, I think, Lonnie, you said something like, “Swift heard in you the wild buffalo that hadn’t been tamed yet. Hadn’t even  been touched by humans.”

Lonnie Holley: You saw that buffalo. 

AD:  Yeah. Which is in his studio.

Lonnie Holley: I did a profile like that. And he had the buffalo on his wall, and he got the other one. And because of my nature, I am Poarch Creek, Alabama Creek. Yeah. And so I’m Native American, in a sense, and African-American, but I’m on that wild side. When it comes down to knowledge and wisdom, when it comes down to still walking and still up and down the creeks and the ditches, still seeing that the materials are the trash, garbage, and the debris are the particles of it. You know, I’m concerned about… I’m concerned about my depths of concern. It is like out of this world.

Matt Arnett: Well, we knew too. Lonnie was talking about playing that piano for the first time.
