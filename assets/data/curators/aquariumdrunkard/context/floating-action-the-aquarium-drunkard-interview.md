## Body

In 2014, about a decade into Seth Kauffman’s music career, the Black Mountain, N.C. based artist who writes, records and occasionally performs as Floating Action experienced an accidental epiphany.

His first and only album on New West Records, Body Questions, had seemingly slipped through the cracks during a changing of A&R guards at the label. Without a proper promotional push, the record never left the runway. Deflated but not defeated, Kauffman decided to stop touring and focus almost exclusively on writing and recording.

“I stopped caring about trying to make the band be a thing,” Kauffman recalls. “Touring just wasn’t working. Success was no longer the goal.”

Relieved of the pressure to keep a band on the road or write “hit” songs, Kauffman began to make music without expectation, fueled by a newfound focus on “just receiving creative ideas and making them whatever they can be.”

“Hold Your Fire (2014) was the first album where I implemented that approach, and I’ve stuck with it everything since,” he adds. That includes the latest Floating Action release, A Hand Carved Garden Tool, out Aug. 29.

Time away from touring helped Kauffman build a reputation as an in-demand session player and sideman, recording and touring with the likes of Ray Lamontagne, Lana Del Rey, Jim James, Dan Auerbach and Angel Olsen. He’s also played the role of producer, albeit a bit reluctantly, to artists like Courtney Jaye.

Over the last 20 years, Kauffman has quietly released more than a dozen albums of languid, slightly exotic indie rock once clunkily described as “Appalachian Beck.” He plays and sings every note tracked in his basement studio with a DIY touch that renders the hallmark Floating Action sound characterized by slightly-off-kilter groove and woozy warmth. | j moye

Aquarium Drunkard: Can you walk me through your pre-Floating Action musical journey?

Seth Kauffman: I grew up in Greensboro, North Carolina. I was best friends with a guy named Bryan Cates. We shared the same musical tastes: Robert Johnson, Motown and the Stones. Nothing current could be good. He’s a really good singer who started writing songs and taught himself guitar in high school, and he had me play violin, dulcimer and mandolin. His dad was a pastor, and there was a nasty, unused wing in the basement of his church where we’d stay up all night recording. Bryan was a Christian songwriter, so we put together a little tour playing churches. The drummer was this guy we were in school with, Joey Barnes, who ended up playing with [Chris] Daughtry [of early American Idol fame]. Paul Barber was the bass player. I have this theory that every Christian band has a guy named Paul.

We got a record contract, but Bryan freaked out and bailed. We moved out to California to try to have a go at it, but never really did anything and moved back to North Carolina. I knew I needed to play music with somebody else, so I looked up Abe Reed, who was the lead singer of the Blue Rags. He’d just won a national blues contest and needed a drummer for his solo band. I did that for a couple years, and Bryan actually joined for a bit. Around that time, ’01 or ’02, Bryan and I recorded an album as the Choosy Beggars. We were kinda serious for a year or two and did some demos with Audley Freed and Steve Gorman from the Black Crowes. Bryan was our contact guy, and that was still pre-cell phone. I found out from a friend of a friend of a friend who was like, “Man, that’s too bad what happened with the Choosy Beggars.” And I was like, “What do you mean?” They’re like, “Bryan just wouldn’t return their calls.” The Black Crowes guys had finished our demos and were gonna share them with us. But Bryan, I guess, was kind of like, “This is real now… I just can’t.”

That was when I started making up stuff and recording on my own, just under my name on burned CDs. Everyone I handed [Ting, 2005] to were like, “This is so good!” At that time I was still too scared to stand in front of anyone and sing.

AD: After releasing Ting and Research [2006] under your name, you adopted the Floating Action moniker for your self-titled 2009 release. You’ve stuck with the blueprint of recording everything yourself. Is that more about wanting to control every sound, or is it just logistically easier with that setup?

Seth Kauffman: I enjoy working in a studio with other people, too, but I feel like whatever a Floating Action album is can only be just me. I’m just not very good at playing each instrument. So it’s kind of like its own weird thing.

AD: How would you describe your playing?

Seth Kauffman: Sloppy, behind the beat. But kind of there.

AD: The sound you create certainly has its own distinct character. When you hear a Floating Action record, you immediately know it’s a Floating Action record.

Seth Kauffman: In the past, I’d go to great lengths to run through amps and a crazy signal path. But when I started not caring, I started doing less. Now I just plug a mic into the interface. People message me and are like, “How do you get your sound working with tape? And I’m like, “It’s actually not tape.” And they’re disappointed [laughs]. I use a ribbon mic, which kind of makes it dark. But I’ve also just come to realize that the way I play just sounds tape-y.

I’ve never thought I could mix. I don’t know what I’m doing at all, but I do it and it ends up sounding good. It’s part of the Floating Action theory of trying to make it super janky. It’s not right, but it works. Like with “Diamond Store” on my new album, there’s no click track. It’s just a groove. I could’ve have gotten a way better take and matched up the pushes. It never quite locks in, but it works.

I have a friend, Leon Michels of Big Crown Records, who’s a producer. I played with him on some Dan Auerbach sessions. He was a Dap-King guy and is the best, most talented dude. At one point I asked if he’d mix a Floating Action record. And he was like, “If I do it, I’d rather have these guys play on it.” Which would be cool, but it would’ve already been done by the time I had something to show them. Once I’ve created a song or album, it’s fully finished and has that weird Floating Action thing.

AD: Are your songs finished before you press record, or do they come together in your studio?

Seth Kauffman: They never really exist until I’m finished recording them. It all starts with voice memos of ideas. Until the pandemic, I just did music, and we didn’t have a kid. My wife would go to work, and I could just sprawl out in the studio and create. When recording and touring stopped, I started working as a handyman for a property management company. Now, I’m always on call which creates a sense of urgency. I’m recording in like six-minute chunks. I’ll turn everything on down in my studio, then get pulled away for a job. I can never seem to get a full idea down, which keeps it raw and cool, even if it’s disjointed.

I used to be adamant that every album had to have a different focus. I was trying to make every single song vastly different, just the deepest cut you’ve never heard. Now I’m just fascinated by exploring what ideas are, in general. When something pops in my head, I just record it without tampering it or letting my brain touch it.

AD: That seems to be how you and Michael Nau, who lives in Maryland, approach your remote collaboration, Dream Sitch [the name is a tongue-in-cheek reference to two reclusive artists recording 10 songs in 10 days without saying a word to each other].

Seth Kauffman: Yeah. When we worked together on that, it was during the pandemic. We thought, “Let’s just do something fun because no one’s ever gonna play a gig again anyway.” Floating Action is a just-me thing. And not that I get lonely, but when it’s with a like-minded person, we’re not fighting, and there are no egos. We weren’t trying to make it be anything. He’d start something in his home studio with a bunch of synths, create a weird loop, sing over it, and send it to me. I’d get in and add the first thing that popped into my head. instinct is what ends up being on the track — that’s kind of the rule.

One rule was that nobody could give notes on anything at any point. The first song he sent me, “From Now On,” was a slow piano ballad I made into something different. And then it was a game where every night we’d make something up off the cuff. There wasn’t much editing. And after we got two or three songs, we realized we were getting close to an album.

AD: In the Seth Kaufman universe — “day job,” making Floating Action records, touring and recording with other artists — is there a sweet spot you’re after in terms of a balancing everything?  

Seth Kauffman: Pre-pandemic, I did more studio work, which I love. Especially union, major label gigs. The pay is so good, and you’re only gone for a week. But it’s not happening much now. I’m trying to be more proactive about touring less because it’s hard on my family, and I don’t love it after having done it for 20 years. Now I’ll do one Michael Nau tour and one Ray [Lamontagne] tour a year.

AD: You seem to be on a Floating Action album-a-year pace. Is that the goal?

Seth Kauffman: Not really. After every one, I think I shouldn’t do anything ever again. I’ve made like 15 albums and hundreds of songs, which is cool.

AD: What keeps you hooked?

Seth Kauffman: The obsession with new ideas and melodies. It’s fun to think you’re not gonna play music ever again until those ideas pop up. There’s just something about that creative moment where nothing becomes something.
