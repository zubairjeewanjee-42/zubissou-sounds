## Body

The latest Wet Tuna LP is called Vast — and you’d be hard-pressed to come up with a better title for this collection of strange and funky flights. It’s a wide-open, far-flung album, deeply textured and ridiculously detailed, but somehow spacious and inviting. A psychedelic micro-galaxy/macro-dose that teems with life and imagination. AD scribe Jarrod Annis called it “a deep space soundsystem unto itself, dolling out heavy doses of dubwise cow funk like a pirate radio transmission broadcasting from the floor of a forest moon on the outskirts of some distant interstellar region.” Not sure I can do better than that!

Close to a decade in, this is the fourth proper Wet Tuna offering (not counting an array of more “under-the-counter” situations), but the project is just another whistle-stop on the Matt Valentine express; MV has been making beautiful noise for over three decades now, from Tower Recordings to MV & EE (with his partner Erika Elder) to solo excursions and collabs. With Elder and bassist Jim Bliss now rounding out the trio, Wet Tuna is his primary preoccupation these days, and it feels as though he could go on forever with it, finding new, weird and wonderful corners to explore. The last time Aquarium Drunkard sat down with MV was way back in 2014 — a meeting of the minds with James Jackson Toth. So it was high time we got back with him to talk about Vast and vaster topics. | t wilcox

Aquarium Drunkard: Looking at the lyric sheet for the new one, “Lucid and reduced by the computer screen” from “Pixel Entities” leapt out at me. Where’s that line coming from for you?

Matt Valentine: It’s cool you mentioned that. I almost didn’t put it on the album because of how I do the rhymes. That line has been sitting with me for a bit, and it was one of the first ones that I finished, my norm de rigueur for me. I’ll start with something that’s the centerpiece, and I’ll go so far, and by the time I’m done, it’s older, primeval to me, so I’ll tend to cut that. That was one of these things. I was really unsure about the lyric phrasing, even though in a lot of ways that’s a synopsis of the record. I was inspired from way back; I’m a big fan of John Martyn, and he does a tune called “Spencer the Rover.”

AD: Oh right, “he’d been much reduc-ed …” 

Matt Valentine: I always thought he kind of “Martynized” songs. Just made up those weird rhymes to suit himself and this usual manic energy that he had. That song, his version of that song, inspired my rhyming “Lucid” and “Reduced.” I guess you can’t really get that on paper, can you? 

But that inspired me with the whole pixel concept of just an infinitely small thing … everything from my existence versus the cosmos, versus the whole singularity that we’re in now that’s Earth. I’ve been kicking around that lyric for a little bit. My daughter had a concept where there are these androgynous relationships with pixels. She inspired the song for me, and it kind of grew out of that. But it was this thing where this pixel was an actual living, breathing, sexually aroused thing that could reproduce. That’s that tune. That’s the record [laughs].

AD: That’s the explanation. The computer screen, though … are you much of a computer guy?

Matt Valentine: I’ve never really recorded to a computer; I still don’t really use a computer. I don’t use Pro Tools. When we record, we record to tape or a standalone device. There’s never a computer dock getting stuff. That’s been a constant for me forever. I think it’s because when I started to learn, I learned in a studio, and I had a mentor. He kind of let me apprentice, and that was back when we used sixteen-track tape and 24-track tape, and all that. I come out of that school of things. And I always find when I do stuff to the computer, whether it’s the light or the visual noise of it or whatever, [it] just seems to affect what happens. It’s this phenomenon that I think is very real. 

AD: I’ve been prepping for this interview by listening to a lot of Wet Tuna; it’s a totally fun time. The term I came back to was “senses scrambling.” “Psychedelic” I guess, is the word, though that might be a cliche by now. 

Matt Valentine: I’m a big fan of the concepts of psychedelics and of psychedelic music. To me, it’s not a cliche; it’s part of the American vernacular. It should be as much as the colors, or ROYGBIV. I mean, psychedelics are a very important and never a pejorative thing. It’s always a cool word in my house, in my kind of spirit. It’s always really a great arc for me to touch on. 

With the recording and the mixing, I try to keep a Jamaican approach as much as possible. A King Tubby/Keith Hudson approach, all hands on the board. I try to have that ’60s and ‘70s thing with active mix-ins…active mixing. I’ve always made records that way, even when we had a four-track cassette at the helm. We dumped the four-track stuff to sixteen, and dumped more stuff onto that. I’ve always loved the idea of multi-tracking; every time I’ll do a playback I think of it as poetry. It’s like my house — I’ve accepted that it’s never finished for me. Every time I do a task, or walk through, I’ll always do a little tweak. I guess I get restless. 

With Tuna, we work a lot more now with the big manifesto, which is more of a groove thing. With the groove, you have a lot of the skeletal nature [and] it gives you a lot more leeway to hang stuff up on the structure. I think it makes more sense now. People can maybe deal with it more, maybe the world is finally ready for it [laughs]. And maybe the stuff sounds a little better now because I’m always reinvesting money that comes in from it back into trying to make it sound cooler. 

AD: It’s so layered, such a three-dimensional sound. ​

Matt Valentine: My friend Werner [Nötzel] from Metabolismus told me he can’t listen to their music after he finishes. He’s sick of it, he’s so done and cooked on it. To me, I’m just beginning when that part comes, I feel like I’ve finally found it. When it’s released, I’m grateful I’m finally stuck with that. Then it’s going to be something else when we play it live. Anyone who’s following or into it, and sometimes they go back to the original and they’re like,” Whoa.” For me, it has to do with the full evolution around the work. It may be a chaotic organ sometimes, but it ultimately is going to give me something, so I’m not fried on it. 

​AD: That’s what I enjoy about Wet Tuna. Every time you put it on, there’s some kind of new layer or new thing to hone in on, whether it’s the vocals or the bass lines or whatever. That’s what I dig about it the most, the constant freshness.

Matt Valentine: Far out. The idea would be that when you get a record, hopefully it’s intriguing enough that you play it again. Those are all my favorite records, the ones that I tend to keep playing or revisit at these different points in my life. I’m always really thankful when people do that or play something at all, let alone go back to it, especially now. 

Jim Bliss, he’s such a monster bass player. He’s like a brother to me. We’ve known each other for a long time now. Every now and again, he reminds me of what I said to him. He was playing in all these bands, and some of them were really good. They could just never get out of the basement or something, but they had great ideas and would always be on the scene. I told him he would just elevate whatever band he was playing with. And ever since I said that to him, we’ve been playing together. He’s been in Tuna since the beginning, he’s always been the live bassist.

AD: I was listening to that early Root Cellar live recording from 2016 or 2017 and he’s on there.

Matt Valentine: Totally. Sometimes he wasn’t on the album recording, but his spirit was there. He was around the sessions, and when I was working on it, I remember talking to him about, ”Oh, I’m making this record with this project I’ve had in my mind.” It started right around when our daughter was born, and I needed something that was going to be different. Erika and I had done so much for so long. We were always having more fun than we should’ve and never really took it too seriously, but then things started to grow. That world was definitely not ready [laughs]. Tuna was kind of the answer to that. I wanted to really focus on something that was not only a little bit clearer in its presentation and harmony. But also I had been working so long playing freer and not having a metronomic thing. When Jim came in, he fit really well into that philosophy. Swinging with drum programming. He’s such a great improviser. He’s got such a sweet feel, and a really nice melodic sense. He has all the same influences; he loves Michael Henderson and Peter Hook. He’s got his own voice, which is rare. 

AD: You mentioned dub reggae as a big part of what you’re doing. Do you remember what first grabbed you about that kind of music, what it did to your musical sensibilities?

Matt Valentine: Dub hit me like a massive ray when I first got turned on to it. I grew up in the Hudson Valley of New York. We used to pick up WNYU, the college station and they had this thing they ran called the New Afternoon Show. They only played new music on that, like brand new. That would go until seven o’clock at night. I would always have it on in my earphones, bicycling, driving around with people, and going home. Then that show would end, and a reggae show would come on. That was when I was fifteen, sixteen years old. This was the height of Eek A Mouse, Scientist, Tubby, Black Ark. They would play a lot more obscure stuff too, like a Bronx label called Wackies, run by this guy Bullwackie, Lloyd Barnes. The records were all distributed by a van at the local store. You would see these things in the shop, and they definitely had a vibe, a Lion of Judah thing. I got very taken by that label in particular at that time. A lot of legends like Sugar Minott, Love Joys and all these people that I still love and are deep influences of mine were making records on the Wackies label. They’d sometimes do these weird gigs in the archipelago of the tri-state area. You would think they’d be a big gig. But you’d go, and there’d be twenty people.

So that’s how I got turned on to it in the beginning. I got a job at this local bar. I started doing the door, and then they let me DJ on Friday night. There was a little DJ booth in the back, and it was serviced by some folks [who] had some genius idea where labels would send in thirty copies of whatever to them. You would pay dues to them, and they would send you a box of records every month. There used to be these crazy white label records. Pressure Sounds, Ariwa, Orb Records, WAU! Mr. Modo out of Sheffield. These crazy dubbed out 80s things. Those things really hit me like a blinding light of this awesome DIY primitive thing. This was a logical step from Peter Tosh, the Wailers and all that.

Hearing that stuff, I could see it was somewhat within reach. I was also having a limited but enthusiastic approach to trying to do home recording, getting some apprentice work and studio things. I was really lucky to be living in a time where that stuff was very tactile. It was actually work to ask someone who knew about it – “How’d you get the echo to do that?” You’d have to talk to someone about it, and it was really cool to learn about the methodology. I still do that to this day. Even if I’m using these digital things, it’s not quite the same thing, but if you’re demoing or just sometimes being in the box, you can still get these things to work, if you’re adventurous. 

But the mixes — to circle back all the way around to the sounds on Vast … the approach there was the illusion of layering, sometimes it is [layered], sometimes it’s not. I like the mixes to be really active. I like to think of them as being these living and breathing things. I’m very particular about mastering at certain frequencies. I think it can have an actual effect on your brainwaves. Maybe it’s flattened out a little bit, but I try to bring it into the record with people’s listening habits now. People who really listen and get into it, I think they’re noticing that more now. That is something that is definitely happening a lot on this record. There might be a mistake or something, but the vibe of the way that certain things, the echoes, rhythmically happen. Sometimes it’s such a blatant mistake that would never get left [on another record]. But it would get left in my music. I think that things just get a bit more “neutered” in more pro situations. Even though we cleaned it up a bit on this one, I also feel like I’m doing all the old janitor things. To me, it’s the spirit that has to be in there. 

The dub concept is such a cool thing. I’m always so psyched at how it’s getting more and more appreciated now; it’s not such a gimmick. A lot more people are hip to it. I think those effects are within reach for the common guitar players, keyboardists, and just studio production people. It’s pretty much everywhere, to me, it’s a real equator of people. It’s so funny because you can really tell real heads versus part-time heads. There’s so much information, but it almost shows handicaps more with certain people.

AD: The way music is now, it’s almost a radical thing to leave in a moment that doesn’t feel like it’s necessarily locked in or perfectly aligned. 

Matt Valentine: Totally. I’m not always the best judge of my own stuff. I often fantasize sometimes about what it would be like if someone else was producing. But that’s either a luxury or a curse, I’m not sure. Maybe it isn’t right for me. I’ve been at it for such a long time. If it changed, it might go in a way that might really derail the whole thing. There’s a small-is-beautiful level, or an economy of scale. All the music I like tends to be in that zone. 

I feel grateful I got to see some of these amazing artists like Sandy Bull, like Rother and Moebius. These guys were as old I am now when I saw them. They still had it. Guys like William Parker were playing three times a week. These crazy things, like you give Lamonte Young a piece of gum at a Vision festival show. Weird shit like that. 

AD: Is music just something you’re always doing, or at least thinking about? Do you always have songs percolating?

Matt Valentine: What a question. It used to be something that was more regular in the sense that I would play every day. When Tuna really started to kick off, and my life changed, my focus and priorities changed a lot. I started to get away from doing things that maybe weren’t leading to any results, like hoarding sounds, recording too much, not being able to process things. I realized I had giant tubs of everything from cassettes to mini disks to hard drives. I was like,”Wow, this is getting insane.” 

So, I kind of stepped back. That was when I really got into the zone of working on rhythms a lot more, exploring polyrhythms and multiple time signatures at once, but getting them to groove, and things like that. Doing the dual drummer and highlife thing with at least two rhythm machines and no midi. Instead of playing guitar all the time or doing ear training, I started to really get into programming and rhythm, and playing different instruments. So I don’t play every day anymore, but I “practice.” When I play, I try to tap in. I always try to find something and work with it. Try to figure something out. Sometimes with a lot of the material, it just happens in this way that sometimes I don’t know where it came from. I know that sounds kind of cornpone. Sometimes I just sit down late at night or early in the morning. I try to play a lot in the morning, if possible. That’s my favorite time. It’s amazing, if I just pick it up and I don’t go into it with anything, and if I’m patient, I try to wait for those moments more now, rather than forcing myself to play. 

With Tuna, we try to get together at least two to three times a month to jam and review, and evolve the material. In Garcia parlance, I always try to keep it fun. That is probably the biggest thing. I try to look at it on a much broader canvas now. We never play anything the same way twice, and I never have. I’ve always been in that same philosophy. It’s been with me since the beginning. I’m incapable of it, actually as a musician. I’m not really a natural musician. I have to work at it. 

I mentioned working with the kids and doing these workshops where we write songs. I’ll also go to the elementary school and do a thing with the Pre-K kids where they write some stuff. That is now also a part of my practice. In the beginning, the kids who signed up for the class in the beginning were [already] musicians, but I also encouraged the non-musicians to sign up. They’re always the ones who do cooler stuff. I try to keep it to where it’s never a mechanical, muscle memory thing. Keep it as organic as possible. 

AD: That’s awesome you’re doing this with kids, in this world that we live in where all the arts are being cut from educational programs. What is better than teaching a kid to make music? As you’re saying, not in some sort of Berklee School of Music kind of thing. Just being able to have those creative tools available to you. 

Matt Valentine: Some of these kids might end up at Berklee; some of these kids are really gifted. If that’s the path, that’s a cool path, too. Hopefully, the inspiration will be with that spirit. It’s just the access. One of the big things we do in the workshop is listen to different formats. I’ll bring a boombox, and we’ll listen to a cassette. I’ll bring a 78 and we’ll listen to a tube player playing a 78. Then we’ll listen to that same 78 streamed and compare. It’s a really great thing. 

I thought it was going to get cut last year. This is my eighth year on the town payroll stuff, which is unbelievable. We thought because of the current administration and all this bullshit that they were going to cut it. A big part of the funding came from Vermont Humanities, a state-run thing that believes in all kinds of radical human growth. They’re pro-all-things I believe in. My contact with them was like,” Well, this might be it.” But you don’t need much to make these things happen. And amazing shit happens. They’re finding the same doorway into this cosmic storehouse of everything that any creative person is. It’s there, you just need to go get it.
