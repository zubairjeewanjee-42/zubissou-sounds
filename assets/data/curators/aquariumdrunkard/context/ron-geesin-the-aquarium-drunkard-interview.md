# Ron Geesin :: The Aquarium Drunkard Interview

## Body

Originally from Scotland and surrealist by vocation, Ron Geesin is a unique figure in the history of British music. He taught himself to play the harmonica, banjo, and piano, and initially worked as a multi-instrumentalist, forming his own one-man jug band, before transitioning to jazz. In the mid 1960s, Geesin moved to London and, in his home studio in Notting Hill, began creating soundtracks while experimenting with tape recorders. In 1967, he made his recording debut with A Raise of Eyebrows, a jazz infused sound collage with a Dadaist flair. Not content to confine himself to the world of experimental music, Geesin shared part of his journey with folk artists, producing Songs for the Gentle Man by Bridget St John.

Meanwhile, in 1970, he collaborated with Roger Waters on Music From the Body and with Pink Floyd on Atom Heart Mother. This moment of visibility, however, didn’t turn him into a star. Geesin pursued a dual path in his recording efforts, alternating between functional soundtracks like Electrosound and more imaginative albums such as As He Stands.

Having since relocated with his family near Heathfield to enjoy a quieter life, he’s continued his work as a musician and become an avid collector of adjustable spanners. But that’s another story, as the following interview focuses on the early years of Geesin’s career. | g checcucci

Aquarium Drunkard: Between the late 1950s and the early 1960s, you started playing jug band and trad jazz. During that same period, jug band and trad jazz paved the way for the birth of British rock. I am referring to the era of skiffle and dixieland, with Lonnie Donegan and Chris Barber at the forefront. However, unlike John Lennon or Brian Jones, you seem to have taken a backwards route to the music of the 1920s and 1930s. Have you ever wondered what drove you in the opposite direction from most of your generation?

Ron Geesin: I don’t think that jug band and trad jazz paved the way for British Rock, but some musicians and budding songwriters of the late 1950s period got interested in that earlier and comparatively rebellious music before discovering the more American origins of Rock stemming from Blues, Rhythm & Blues and Black Jazz. I didn’t take a backwards route but, as a surrealist, was much taken by the absurdity, humour even, of improvised musical instruments and the polyrhythms of Classic Black Jazz. Furthermore, I was discovering other pioneering levels from the 1920s such as Walton’s “Façade”, using Edith Sitwell’s poems, and Gamelan music from Indonesia.

AD: After briefly being a “one man jug band” you played banjo in Mahogany Wardrope and his Pinetop Stompers and piano in the Original Downtown Syncopators. While performing with a third jazz and cabaret band, Dr. Crock and His Crackpots, you met Gavin Bryars. I believe this happened between 1965 and 1966 at the Greasborough Working Men’s Club, also known as the Palladium of the North. Can you tell me something about your relationship at the time and your collaboration with filmmaker Stephen Dwoskin?

Ron Geesin: During the period when I had left the Original Downtown Syncopators for the good of my mind and the health of my musical development, I briefly joined the ‘funny band’ Dr. Crock and his Crackpots. This was mainly to survive financially until my next chance happening came along. In our set, I performed a wild improvised piano solo. The double bass player in the resident group that accompanied the visiting stars, usually singers, of the period was Gavin Bryars. He approached me after one of my solos and said, “That’s very interesting, elements of Prokofiev and Schönberg even!” So we talked a little about that world – well, the little that I had found out, but in which I was getting increasingly interested. I had also just added a profound interest in the creative possibilities of tape recording and electronic methods of composition, so even Edgard Varèse came in to the Bryars/Geesin conversation. By 1965 I and my future wife had found a damp basement flat in the Notting Hill area of London, one of its centres of pioneering creative activity. I was introduced to the artist Steve Dwoskin, recently arrived on a Fulbright Scholarship from New York. During that time he moved into a flat just across the road. He was a polio victim on crutches. At that time, he was oil painting and earning some extra money as a book-jacket designer. We got on well and I became his right-hand man, sometimes going ahead to check if he could get into a lift for publishers’ interviews, and other times building shelves and doing other practical work in his flat. Gavin Bryars needed to get out of that rather mindless club bass playing and move 185 miles south into London. We offered him the floor of our flat! Steve Dwoskin had made some 16mm short films in New York, but they had no sound, so I made all the sound for these, until about 1969 when I was getting very busy with live performances and properly paid commercial film music. Most of the Dwoskin music is on Trunk Records “Pot-Boilers”. I then handed over that side of things to Bryars.

AD: Between the late 1960s and early 1970s, you played in folk clubs with Ralph McTell and Roy Harper and collaborated on record with Wizz Jones and Bridget St John. What was it like for an unclassifiable artist like you to engage with the world of folksingers? In an interview, you referred to your solo debut “A Raise of Eyebrows” as “folk”, your piece “Two Fifteen String Guitars for Nice People” has appeared in several anthologies of acoustic guitar tracks and you remained attached to that genre for quite some time. Could you explain your relationship with folk and folksingers a bit better?

Ron Geesin: Since my one-man show was, on one level, a hilarious and Dadaistic improvised eruption, using available piano, banjos, guitars, harmonica, voice, percussion and any available props found backstage and around, my syncopated style on piano and banjos, projected in an extremely humorous manner, suited both the audiences in the more forward-looking folk clubs and most of the performers there. You can add John Martyn to them. I was good friends with all of them, but never “joined in”. When Bridget was planning her album Songs For The Gentle Man, she asked me to produce and play on it. My compositional side wanted to separate her lyrics, which were essentially fine stand-alone poems, from what I’ve called ‘trundling guitar accompaniment’. I partially succeeded with the track “The Lady And The Gentle Man” on which she is accompanied by four trombones only.

AD: A Raise of Eyebrows is an unclassifiable work, a sound collage made with tape and razor blades, mixing jazz with eccentricity. What strikes me most is the playful side, somewhere between jug band and music hall. Do you think this humorous aspect has negatively affected how your work was received by academics? Were you perceived more as an “outsider” or as an artist linked to the “popular” scene?

Ron Geesin: If you don’t have humour, you don’t have life! I was never a member of any “scene” or “cult”. I try to let my work emerge from the subconscious. On the financial side, I was only interested in earning enough money to make the next work. Yes, I have been tenuously linked to the ‘popular’ world for the obvious reasons below. In my book of collected writings “The Stapled Brain” there is my definition of Academia: “A fungal complaint spread by Platonics that gets under the skin to cause an insatiable itch, principally round the arse.”

AD: In one of the texts on your website, you write that you were influenced by a music hall band like the Alberts. Did you also know the Bonzo Dog Band? If so, what did you think of them?

Ron Geesin: Yes, but only slightly influenced by The Alberts, which was not really a music hall band but a fully anarchic and hilarious bunch of post-war Dadaists with The Brothers Gray as nucleus. The Bonzos were certainly influenced. They’re all part of that style of entertainment called “funny bands”, even though a lot of the humour was non-musical, just good theatre. I deputised for Neil Innes in the Bonzos when he couldn’t (be bothered to) turn up! Another band that was much more serious in reverent reproduction of music from the “Roaring ‘20s’” was The Temperance Seven. I knew all of them in the early days and became very good friends with their principal arranger and trombonist John R. T. Davies. He was known separately as the world’s finest restorer of original jazz 78s for modern media – and I’ve done a lot of that work too. 

AD: In a review of your debut, Mick Farren compared your humor to that of another eccentric Scot: Ivor Cutler. From my limited perspective as a unlucky Italian, this seems like a fitting comparison. What do you think? Did you know Ivor Cutler and are you familiar with his work?

Ron Geesin: Again, in my book of writings, on “Eccentric”: “If you can find me someone who admits to being concentric, I’ll admit to being eccentric.” I knew Ivor well and shared some performances, separately, with him. We both came from Glasgow, or near, with a similar sense of the surreal. When I left home, I frequently had The Secret Life of Salvador Dali under my arm.

AD: As everyone knows, you collaborated with Roger Waters on Music from the Body and with Pink Floyd on Atom Heart Mother. I’ve decided not to ask you anything about that, as ou’ve written a beautiful book on the subject, The Flaming Cow: The Making of Pink Floyd’s Atom Heart Mother. Yet people always seem to ask you about this aspect of your career. How much does that bother you?

Ron Geesin: Most members of the consuming public need to be associated with Fame in all its guises – something about belonging to what is perceived as ‘success’ in our materialistic society – anything that has survived above the waves of endeavour. That art that is not perceived ‘above the waves’ is usually thought of as ‘submerged’ and not valuable. It does bother me that the comparative success of Atom Heart Mother has tended to deem the other 99% of my work as “submerged”. I have always considered my work for Atom Heart Mother only as “a damn good bit of crafting”.  

AD: Two tracks from “As He Stands” (“Roll ’em Bowl ’em” and “Mr Peugeot’s Trot”) were created for the soundtrack of The Dawn of Motoring (for the series History of the Motor Car). What was your relationship with British Petroleum, the company that commissioned the documentary? How did you come into contact with this oil company?

Ron Geesin: No relationship. The director of the films just wanted me to make the music.

AD: You often refer to your music as “organised noise” a definition I really like. In my opinion, it’s particularly apt for describing your work “As He Stands”. Compared to the dadaist anarchy of “A Raise of Eyebrows” it seems that on this album all the eccentric ideas are well thought out and fit together perfectly. It’s also a good mix between the ideas of the first album and commissioned soundtracks. Do you agree with me? And what do you think of that album today?

Ron Geesin: The term “organised noise” is, I think, John Cage’s. As I’ve said before, I just let my work emerge. It’s all I could do! After one has made some original work/piece/composition, that work becomes like an Autumn leaf in the wind; it might get blown down a drain or caught, specially treasured and mounted in a frame on a wall.

AD: After releasing on Transatlantic, Harvest, and KPM, you moved on to self-production with “As He Stands”. Why did you make that choice? Was there something in particular that bothered you or made you feel less free in the official record industry?

Ron Geesin: No one else would do it! I was never considered commercial enough.

AD: Your music is not analyzed in the famous 1974 book Experimental Music: Cage and Beyond by Michael Nyman. Is your absence due to the fact that you were not a student of John Cage and did not belong to the “highbrow” music scene that grew around Cornelius Cardew? Regardless of your negative views on terms like “experimental” and “avant-garde”, don’t you think it’s limiting and snobbish to only value music produced by academics and not that made by self-taught musicians?

Ron Geesin: You’ve said it all!

AD: At least professionally, you are quite a solitary figure, to the point of being described as a “one man band”. On the other hand, you come from a peripheral area (Bothwell), moved to another provincial area (Crawley), and after living in London, you left the capital to live near a small town (Heathfield). What was your relationship with the rural world and the urban world at the time? And what is the relationship between your creativity and isolation versus collaboration?

Ron Geesin: I did not “move to Crawley” but “was moved to Crawley” simply because that was the Original Downtown Syncopators’ home town. My relationship with the rural world was originally through my grandfather who was an amateur entomologist, kept bees and taught me a lot about nature when we went out walking in Ayrshire. I and my family needed to get out of the machine of London in 1971 and found an affordable place near Heathfield. At first, I was very worried I would never get another film job, but it all worked out. I have always needed space and my own control of time. A lighter, more humorous, reason for moving out was that, as an independent human, I reserved the right to drill a hole in a wall at any time of the day or night without anyone complaining.

AD: Around 1966-1967, Desmond Briscoe invited you to visit the BBC Radiophonic Workshop and asked you to join the group. But you declined. Can you explain the reasons for your decision? What do you think were the advantages and disadvantages of working in that environment at that time?

Ron Geesin: Desmond Briscoe! Again, I needed my independence – an advantage. The disadvantage was that the Radiophonic Workshop members tended to get all the best electronic music jobs within the BBC which, in turn, advertised their availability outside of it.

AD: In various interviews, you’ve said things that seem connected, in my opinion. On one occasion, you claimed, “one of the reasons why I was good at film work was that I enjoyed the restriction of the framework.” In another, you said: “composing, in music or ‘organised noise,’ is about forming a structure: building modules by any means.” And finally, in a third interview, you declared: “I set up my own world – for better or worse.” If I understand correctly, on the one hand, you assert your freedom of expression against outside interference. On the other hand, you seek to create a world of your own, using material and production ‘limits’ and ‘constraints’ as sources of inspiration for creativity. In this sense, you don’t reject the concept of “structure” but rather organize your compositional cells as elements of a map or grid. Don’t you think this makes you different from many of your peers who try, at all costs, to overcome structures and codes, mistaking that emptiness for freedom?

Ron Geesin. Well expressed! The only expression I would change is your “On the other hand”. I would say “On another level”, one of the many subconscious levels operating in the human mind, levels that are continually alternating between conflict and unison – the enjoyment of the eventual balance of all things, the Yin/Yang principle.

AD: I believe that the “experimental” music of the late 1960s and early 1970s in the UK can be divided into four main groups: the BBC Radiophonic Workshop/White Noise (Delia Derbyshire, Brian Hodgson, John Baker, David Cain etc.), Scratch Orchestra/Portsmouth Sinfonia (Cornelius Cardew, Brian Eno, Gavin Bryars, Michael Nyman, etc.), Virgin (David Bedford, Lol Coxhill, Mike Oldfield, Steve Miller, etc.), and Incus (Evan Parker, Derek Bailey, Howard Riley, Kenny Wheeler, etc.). Then there are the mavericks: besides you, Trevor Wishart, Basil Kirchin, Alan Davie, and a few others. Do you think that not belonging to a “family” has worked against you and the other mavericks? And do you appreciate any of these other mavericks?

Ron Geesin: Some of these I’ve never heard, nor wanted to. I always got on well with Parker and Bailey, even recorded them back in that damp Notting Hill basement. I actually put Gavin Bryars into Portsmouth Polytechnic when I had to get on with more demanding composition. Paraphrasing Edgard Varèse, “Nothing I’ve ever done is experimental; when it leaves me, it has been fully worked out, complete.”

AD: Surely, your independence is tied to the pursuit of freedom and the rejection of institutionalization. But I’d like to focus on a third factor: the need to maintain a high level of originality. In short, the desire to escape conformity. In this sense, I believe you are the exact opposite of Brian Eno, who has always celebrated contamination and collaboration. For Eno, individual ‘genius’ doesn’t exist; instead, there is a network of contacts exchanging information and inspiring each other. Eno calls this sort of collective genius “scenius” and contrasts it with the romantic myth of the “genius”. Consequently, he works within a “creative ecosystem” populated by many colleagues and connected to countless contexts. It seems to me, however, that you’ve operated by “intersection”. You’ve collaborated from time to time with filmmakers or musicians, only to retreat to your “den”. And I believe this tendency to take a “step back” has ensured that your artistic “voice” remains different from the chorus. What do you think? And what do you think of someone like Eno, who has taken a path very different from yours?

Ron Geesin: Extremely well expressed. I do not think of Eno, but I’ve heard the name plenty. Extra to all this is the subject of “The Living of Artists”, that is the earning of a living while still maintaining individual expression; the survival level; the conflict of patronage and grants against The Market Place. I went for the latter, taking whatever job that came along, a procedure I call “chance careering”.

And now for a pint of beer!!!
