## Body

It’s been close to a decade since Ambsace, James Elkington and Nathan Salsburg’s last duo release. But — as the pair note in the chat below — they’ve worked together extensively in the intervening years, helping Joan Shelley make several masterpieces. The excellent All Gist, out this week on Paradise of Bachelors, sees James and Nathan taking their musical partnership back to its roots. It’s an album full of gorgeously interlocking guitars, bewitching melodies and a couple of curveball covers. More than anything, All Gist sounds like a conversation between two old friends — one that we’re all lucky we get to eavesdrop on. | t wilcox

Aquarium Drunkard: How’d All Gist come together — it’s been about a decade since the last Elkington/Salsburg release, right?

James Elkington: We actually never stopped working together. It’s just always been with Joan. But now I realize that in the last interview we did, we didn’t even mention that — it was like we haven’t done anything musically together for the last 10 years. “We just hang out” [laughs]. But that’s not the case, I suppose. We’ve made these other great records with Joan.

AD: Did you always have another duo record in mind, though?

Nathan Salsburg: We had been wanting to do it for a while. Our big inspiration for it was our partners. They said, “You should do it, it’d be really good for you. You love to be together. Just go! Do it!” There was a lot of encouragement from Joan [Shelley] and Jessica [Linker]. So, we did a thing with the Fretboard Journal, relearning some old songs. They were having a summit and they asked me and Jim to come play a few songs for a workshop. And afterwards, we said “That was so fun, let’s play together some more.”

James Elkington: And we wrote something new as we were rehearsing. It’s the first song on the new album. That was one of Nathan’s tunes, and it got pretty close to completely finished during the weekend that he was up here to play. That was kind of a green light for another album, because not only was it super fun to get together and do something that is unlike anything else we get do elsewhere — but the speed with which that new song came together, how quickly it all interlocked, was really encouraging.

AD: Is your writing process together always fairly spontaneous like that?

James Elkington: One person usually has the initial idea. And what’s really fun about this is the person with the idea encouraging the other to figure out how they can fit into it. And then vice-versa — that’s when it gets exciting.

Nathan Salsburg: We work best under a lot of pressure [laughs].

James Elkington: We don’t usually have very much time together. Our first priority whenever we’re together is to catch up and be in each other’s company. Even though playing music together is a big part of our relationship, it’s not at the top. I feel like I knew Nathan for about a year before I even knew that he played guitar.

Nathan Salsburg: When you met me, I wasn’t playing guitar in any meaningful capacity. I played guitar in my bedroom, that was pretty much it. I hadn’t made any records. The first thing was for a Tompkins Square compilation, but that was after we met.

James Elkington: I remember you saying, “Oh yeah, I made this at home.” And I couldn’t believe it. Well, I could believe that he hadn’t been announcing his talent to the world, because that’s the way he’s wired. But I couldn’t believe that he could do that. He played guitar the way I did, but much better [laughs]! It made him the perfect vehicle for me to advance my musical designs. There were so many crossovers with what I was interested in. But technically, I wasn’t really able to do a lot of that then. But we’d already been friends for a while. We talked a lot about music; he was keen to get me interested in traditional music and I was keen to talk about the Smiths, which was a big preoccupation for both of us when we were younger. We have a lot of things in common outside our own musical voices, things we like to listen to. But I knew when I heard Nathan play that it was going to work with what I do — maybe before Nathan did. 

AD: What about you, Nathan — was there a moment for you when you knew it was going to work?

James Elkington: He’s still waiting for that moment [laughs]!

Nathan Salsburg: We’re going to make 17 records before I get there [laughs]. But no, I’m not sure I remember. Jim’s wife, Jessica, is my oldest friend — I’ve known her since I was five. Jim and I met when I went to see him play at Joe’s Pub in New York in 2005 or so. So, it was a solid five years of becoming fast friends. I’d come and visit Chicago and go see music with him. I don’t remember when it became a musical partnership, in addition to our friendship. But it really has always been as easy as saying: “Let’s play guitar and see what happens.” There is nothing easier than this for me — certainly not making solo records, and certainly not making records with Joan now, because we have a kid.

AD: When you started playing, was there a specific concept you had? Jim, I can see a Bert Jansch LP behind you — were you thinking of doing something along the lines of the Bert and John record?

Nathan Salsburg: That was kind of it, right? We were listening to that record and — even though it’s awesome — it kind of sounds like shit, too? Warts and all. We thought, well, maybe we could do something like that?

James Elkington: Mysteriously, that record is less than the sum of its parts. And we thought, we could also make a record that’s worse than both of us [laughs]! I still love that record, but it’s not the first thing I would go to. We’re both enormous fans of the British folk revival and that really informs both of our guitar styles. But there weren’t that many guitar duo records like [Bert and John], with really complementary guitarists. Not that it’s uncharted territory, but there wasn’t much stuff that I’d heard along the lines of what I was imagining we could do. In the early 60s, a real vocabulary was built for arranging folk tunes; it surprised me that people didn’t apply that to things outside of the folk world. It didn’t spring out of it very much, except in strange ways — like the way that Johnny Marr plays guitar in the Smiths. You can hear Martin Carthy in that.

Nathan Salsburg: Not on Strangeways, but “in strange ways” [laughs].

James Elkington: No, he played mostly piano on Strangeways [laughs].

AD: Are there any other less obvious touchstones you guys have? I was listening to your records this week and wondering: “Are these guys secret prog heads?!”

James Elkington: It’s no secret in my case. I have an aggressive love of prog.

Nathan Salsburg: And not cool prog either! He has some extremely dodgy prog taste.

James Elkington: It can be pretty bad, it’s true.

Nathan Salsburg: We were talking earlier about how when you’re young and music is such a fundamental core ingredient of your identity and gives you pleasures that you might not want to telegraph to the outside world because it might mitigate or corrupt the identity you’re trying to present. And as you get older, you stop doing that. And I think that’s always the point — you’re not going to be a good writer if you’re not a good reader. And you’re not going to be good musician if you’re not a good listener. A lot of people don’t realize that, and that’s why there’s a lot of shitty music! But I find that as I’ve made more records and written more songs, I’ve become a much more sympathetic listener. My tastes have only broadened as I’ve gotten older. My early records — both with Jim and without — were reflections of more limited influences, or the desire to only have certain influences present. That’s definitely changed. In some ways, we don’t have anything to prove to anyone anymore — whoever’s listening, which is not many!

James Elkington: It’s very emancipating, the number of listeners we have, waiting expectantly to hear what we’re going to come up with next [laughs].

AD: When it comes to recording this stuff, are there rules? Or is it like, whatever comes out is cool?

Nathan Salsburg: The rules have gotten looser over time. And this record definitely has a much looser sensibility about what’s OK to put on a record. On our past records, we wanted to sound as good as possible. Now, it’s like that Bert and John record: “OK, there’s a little flub here, it’s not that big of a deal.”

James Elkington: And we agreed before we started working on All Gist that we could use different instruments and textures and make other things happen that we wouldn’t be able to play live, really. We didn’t want to be held back by that. We even had a conversation where we were like: “What if we made a record and didn’t play guitar on it?” What if we just found another way of doing this? We weren’t necessarily serious about it, but it was an idea. We are guitar players, but increasingly, maybe we’re more about writing. It just happens that the guitar is our tool for doing that. We could apply it to other things — and that’s still something I’d like to try. We could write something and then see how little we can the guitar be a part of it.

Nathan Salsburg: One song that didn’t make it on the record is a sort of Bruce Langhorne-y / Hired Hand ambient piece. It’s guitar-based, but there’s a lot of other stuff — all by Jim. He piled some stuff on it. But in a way that was what we were trying to do on this record from the get-go. It ended up being way more of a guitar record than we planned. But that song is great, and it took maybe all of 20 minutes to do it. The next record will be more of that. I think we could make something really good like that. It came so easy and was so rewarding.

James Elkington: We didn’t have any expectations about that song, I think. And sometimes you’re interested in making music where you don’t know what it’s gonna be and you don’t put any limitations on it. It can be very freeing. You can really surprise yourself. The stuff I’ve been working on — All Gist with Nathan and the Me Neither record on my own — are my favorite things I’ve done, because I didn’t have a preconceived notion about them. I’ve realized that I nearly always have a preconception about how I want things to sound. And in doing so, I’m always having to deal with the shortfall between what I imagined and what it actually is. But if you take what you imagine it to be out of the equation then you’re going to hit the target in the middle every time. That’s how I want to operate from now — at least as much as I can.

AD: Are there any favorites from the new album where you wouldn’t change a thing about it?

James Elkington: “Death Wishes To Kill” is the song we wrote in the kitchen when we were supposed to be working on that brief live performance. Nathan had this idea, and I wrote my part, and it seemed to come together pretty quick. And when we recorded it, it was pretty much just as we had done it originally. That was the energy boost that we needed. When I hear that song, I’m always psyched for it. But all the songs ended up being more structured and song-like than we were expecting, which was cool. “All Gist Could Be Yours” — there are certain that we wrote and deliberately tried to leave enough space in it for almost anything to happen. To see what we could get away with.

AD: That’s my favorite on the record — the last two minutes of it, I wouldn’t mind if they went on forever.

Nathan Salsburg: The problem that I have is that I don’t know what song that is [laughs].

James Elkington: When we’re writing these songs, they all have code names. You’ve got to call it something at first. The name of the song won’t be decided until the album is finished. Afterwards, the Etch-A-Sketch is scrambled, and no one can remember what it was originally called and then you have to look at the record to see what it ended up being called. Other friends who play in instrumental bands, one person will say “Let’s play this one.” And the rest of the band says, “Which one?” And they have to remind each other — “It goes C to F” or whatever.   

Nathan Salsburg: We had one song on this record where the code name was “Bunshiner” — like “Sunshiner.” And I still remember it as that.

James Elkington: If I asked you to play whatever it’s actually called, you’d have no idea. But “Bunshiner” … But also, the original “All Gist” didn’t make it on to the record. That was something we just free improvised. I don’t know if you remember this, Nathan, but when we were trying to do the Bretagne piece on this record, we really got in a mess getting through it. It was really hard. I don’t even like to listen to it, because I can hear how much effort I’m putting in to try to keep up with Nathan [laughs]. Swear words were used between takes. If one of us messed up, we’d have to start the whole thing over. It was as close as we ever get to hard work in this project. We finally got to the end of this two-minute-long piece and stuck with the 10th take. I hit “Stop” and said: “OK, we’re gonna leave the mics, up we’re going hit ‘Record’ and we’re just going to play something. That’s going to be the next song.” And we free-associated this piece and it worked out great. That was really what we had in mind for the whole record. But when we came to choose the songs to put on the record, Nathan felt like it was too much of an outlier. Everything else had ended up so structured and there was this one piece that just floats. To put it on the record might be a disservice to it because it might sound like filler. But we took its name and gave it to another song.

Nathan Salsburg: I’m glad you mentioned the Bretagne piece — we called it “Rule Bretagne” — because I actually love listening to it. I think when we were sending mixes and back and forth Jim had listed it as “Rule Bretagne (Not Good).”

James Elkington: When I hear it, it sounds like it’s 30 seconds before I throw my guitar out of the window [laughs].

Nathan Salsburg: I love how loose it is. I love that we made it through. I don’t remember the experience fondly, but I’m extremely happy with how it came out.

AD: This record has two covers on it — the first one I wasn’t familiar with. Tell me about “Well, Well Cornelius.”

James Elkington: That was my idea. Howard Skempton was an English composer who was sort of contemporary of Cornelius Cardew, who the song is named after. He’s not hugely known, but there’s a small enclave of British composers and performers who were real John Cage enthusiasts in the 1960s. But then he started making these very contemplative piano pieces later on, which have an amazing stillness to them. He’s not very appreciated. But when I heard “Well, Well Cornelius,” I thought it would lend itself really well to what we do — kind of like the Duke Ellington cover we had on the last record. It was a piano piece that just got broken into the two guitars.

AD: The other cover I was very familiar with — Neneh Cherry’s “Buffalo Stance.” When did that reveal itself as something you guys could make your own?

Nathan Salsburg: It was my challenge to Jim. Well, I didn’t exactly delegate it to him, but he took it on himself. I had played the original for my kid and I thought, “This is such a great song.” The melodic elements are so good. They’re so compelling. I said to Jim, “We should do this!” And Jim said, “Well, it’s a lot of rapping” [laughs]. Not that Jim dislikes rap, it was more like …

James Elkington: … how exactly are we going to do this?

Nathan Salsburg: But within a couple of hours, Jim had worked up a demo for one guitar. And I remember when it came in, I was leaping around the house. “Joan, listen to this! It’s so great!” I was so excited to dig into it. And once we got down to it, it took almost no time at all. When you make a composite of all of those really compelling melodic elements, it was a total thrill. So satisfying.

James Elkington: It’s us trying to fit as much of the original song’s arrangement into two guitars. Of course, it’s slower and has a bit of a swing to it. But we tried to include as many of those synth lines as we could. The arrangement of the original is really intricate. There are moments where she’s singing a really beautiful melody and then it’s spoken — but behind the speaking there’s all of this really great melodic stuff going on. We could chart a course through it and play some of that without leaving the song behind too much. But this was totally Nathan’s idea. I was like, “I don’t think so.” In fact, I think I suggested doing “Manchild” instead. I think it was the single that came out after “Buffalo Stance.” I remember that more from my childhood. It’s got a really weird chord progression. I remember reading about this at the time — she was playing a Bontempi organ that had all these chord buttons on it. It had all these major chords that weren’t necessarily related to each other — B major to G major or something. And she just wrote this song playing the buttons and singing over it. So, it has all these chords and it’s knotty and kind of confusing to play. Nathan was like, “‘Buffalo Stance’ is gonna work, don’t worry.” And that is one of the defining aspects of how we work. If one of us has a strong feeling about something one way or the other, then the other person will just go with it. If Nathan thinks it’s a good idea, it’s probably a good idea. We’ll figure it out.

Nathan Salsburg: Only Jim would go chasing after “Manchild.” The challenge that it presents is like red meat to him.

James Elkington: I did get pretty far down the line with it [laughs]. But maybe it was a fool’s errand.

AD: So, once the record is out, do you guys have plans to take the show on the road?

James Elkington: We’re gonna play a week of shows in June with a view to doing more if we can find the time to do it. That’s always the issue.

Nathan Salsburg: This is a terrible metaphor but — it’s like if you make a big pot of stew that requires saffron threads. And those are expensive! You go and buy saffron threads and you’re like, “This is awesome.” But then, you’re left with all these saffron threads, and you have to do something with them, right? If we do the work to relearn these songs for a couple shows, we should probably do more. Our music is the sonic equivalent of saffron threads, that’s what I’m trying to say [laughs].
