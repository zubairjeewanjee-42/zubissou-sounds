## Body

Even those devoted to music sometimes forget when talking about “the latest record” from a musician that the word “record” itself refers to more than the vinyl disc on which that music has been collected. Among its many definitions, a record provides evidence or captures official documentation that is supposed to last in perpetuity. There’s a reason why a phrase such as “let the record show” has permeated our culture’s language beyond the courtroom to become a cliché. 

My Days of 58 is the latest record from singer-songwriter Bill Callahan. What does this record show? It is not only a document, but, as the title states, also an accounting of Callahan at a certain age, portraying who he is as a writer, a musician, a father, a partner, and a human being at a specific moment in time. 

Starting with 2019’s Shepherd in a Sheepskin Vest—the first album released after becoming a parent—Callahan’s songwriting has become less metaphorical and more direct. He hides less in allegory or fantasy, making no secret of the fact that his songs are written by a man named Bill Callahan. 

My Days of 58 continues that artistic approach, front-loading it with the songs “Why Do Men Sing” and “The Man I’m Supposed To Be.” Both titles make plain Callahan’s cards-on-the-table reckoning within his writing as he considers his purpose as an artist and a man. Furthermore, “Pathol O.G.” opens with a spoken-word recitation in which Callahan muses on his function as a songwriter “for nigh on 30 years” and even reaches a few conclusions. Inevitably, this line of inquiry can’t help but lead Callahan to the ultimate form of life-questioning with many of these songs containing allusions to death, which can be attributed to a recent health scare.

Nonetheless, My Days of 58 is anything but dark. Throughout, Callahan contemplates his own mortality with a plain-spoken directness, still infused with his typical dry and sly humor. The songs are dotted with quick, catch-them-if-can jokes and asides. Likewise, the music on My Days of 58 never wallows in doom and gloom. Featuring the same mash-up of instrumentation as a Western swing band (i.e., horns, fiddle, and pedal steel guitar), the musicians Callahan has assembled are nimble in their responsiveness to his words, adding dimensions to the songs.

In the run-up to the release of My Days of 58, Aquarium Drunkard emailed with Callahan about this record. Over the course of the correspondence, he revealed a recent cancer scare as well as thoughts about his utilization of repetition, dreaming about heroes, his musical partnership with drummer Jim White, the nature of engaging with his fans, and much more.  |s bunn

Aquarium Drunkard: In the notes for My Days of 58, you describe how you wanted the musicians to adopt a “living room attitude” for their playing: “Not too loud, not otherworldly.” Does that concept hold true for the lyrics of the songs as well? Are these “living room” compositions? 

Bill Callahan: I wouldn’t say the lyrics are living room lyrics. Part of me wants to say they are journal-y lyrics because that would tie in nicely with the title. But they’re not all journal-y. But something like “West Texas,” which is partially my attempt at something proggy (after having seen Peter Gabriel in concert a year or so ago, which opened up that door for me)—having a quote from [Francis] Bacon in the middle is kind of how I’d keep a journal—dropping tangentially relative quotes in there. I’m always haunted by the Billy Joe Shaver maxim of writing for the dumbest person in the room. I applied that to some of the record. It’s a good touchstone.

At the time leading up to recording I was appreciating the types of recording techniques that make the musicians sound like they’re arranged in a tangibly physical space. Like a Michael Hurley record. There is close and there is “close.” Billie Eilish is “close”—her vocal and mouth are close to the mic/the speaker. That is one way of doing it that I like. But she is close to the mic, her lips are close to the mic, but you can’t hear her body. Michael Hurley—his whole body is close to the mic. His whole body is on mic! And the aura around him. Billie is mouth and lungs for the majority of time. I enjoy that sound too but not for this LP I made. That is the other worldly sound I refer to. The singer’s mouth is like a black hole you get sucked into. That type of recording is its own sealed universe that you have an entrance to. Or there’s the “whole body is on mic” feel, which I was going for this time. You can hear there’s a space on the couch open for you to sit.

AD: There are a number of references to death in the songs on My Days of 58, but it would never be described as a goth album. These death allusions are matter-of-fact and almost practical, rather than the more morbid fascination with death such as in your earlier song, “Permanent Smile.” Can that evolution be attributed to getting older or is it something in your songwriting? 

Bill Callahan: Well, you could say it comes from having a real life staring contest with death instead of just musing on it as a facet of life. I was diagnosed with cancer in Dec ‘24 and had surgery to remove the tumor in Jan ‘25. I tried to focus on something Willie Nelson said about not worrying about something without knowing if it’s true. I tried to hold that in my heart while going through the steps and the waiting. There were a couple months of darkness in my heart while it was all being examined. It was a total surprise, despite it running in my family. I always thought my mom had it because she held so much in. Maybe I’ve been holding stuff in, too, but not admitting it to myself. It made me look at everything differently. I mainly just wanted to hang out with my kids as much as possible. Soak them up, let them soak me up. Every minute is precious.

The death of a father or a husband are commonplace things—so I think I wrote about it in such a light. These are things everybody goes through and, you know, no matter how spiritual you want to get about it, you also can’t deny the material aspect. The person was a body in slippers and then they are gone. 

AD: In a few songs on My Days of 58, you repeat certain words or phrases over and over again such as “Gabriel, come blow your horn” in “Stepping Out for Air,” “Are you human” in “Computer” as well as “electricity” and “dream land” in “And Dream Land.” You’ve employed repetition in songs before, including “Rock Bottom Riser,” “From the Rivers to the Ocean,” and “Eid Ma Clack Shaw.” Do you plan repetition in advance when you are writing or does it come out when you are performing the song? Are you doing repetition as a point of emphasis, or is it simply you enjoying the sound of the words? 

Bill Callahan: Interesting question. I’d say for different reasons I may use repetition. The “electricity” in “And Dream Land,” was simply cos it felt good to sing it. I wanted to sing it twenty times but restrained myself. I’ll save that for the live versions when people can’t lift the needle up.

In “Stepping Out for Air”, the “Gabriels” are more an incantation. To bring Gabriel to this plane at this moment. Gabriel, the messenger of God.

And the repeated “Are you human”’s in “Computer” are more to drive a point home or to let it sink in how strange it is to be asked that question (by a computer, no less). A computer checking to make sure it’s not talking to another computer. That’s a self-hating computer casting aspersions. Like guilty until proven innocent, this is “you are a computer unless you can convince me otherwise.” 

I’m often torn between saying something only once or repeating it. I try to choose wisely. Ted Gioia has an interesting essay, or maybe it’s the last chapter of his book, about why people like to hear extended versions of songs in a live setting. The repetition thing is part of it.

AD: In “Why Do Men Sing,” a dream version of Lou Reed makes an appearance as a sort of spirit guide for you. Earlier this year, Jeff Tweedy also invoked him in the song “Lou Reed Was My Babysitter.” You and Tweedy are one year apart. What is it about Lou Reed for songwriters of a certain age? 

Bill Callahan: I feel like it partially had to do with the timing of those two compilations—Another View and VU in 1986, I think. At that time, I got a great deal of my music education from the free weeklies in DC and Baltimore. The City Pages. And the Washington Post and fanzines. I thought music journalists were like sages or gurus back then and that they held this esoteric knowledge of music that they would sometimes share. I took the articles about these records seriously. I believed every word. Bought those two records and the first VU record. And at the same time Lou was having that tremendous run of solo records—Blue Mask, Legendary Hearts, New Sensations. So, I was buying those too. It was a mass infusion of Lou and Lou- related music imprinting on me. Maybe the same for Jeff? I have to think of Lou as the cool big brother I never had. 

The song was a real dream I had. I really saw Lou in white, in an apartment. It was after my diagnosis. He brought me there to be safe. He had a new Malaysian bride. And he was a little nervous about a show he had to do that night. We sat in a big circle and then he went off to think about his show.

I’ve had some great dreams of artists in the afterlife. I met John Lee Hooker and begged him to touch my hand so I could play guitar like him. He said, “No, son, it doesn’t work that way.” I begged and begged some more ‘til he relented begrudgingly, touched my hand and walked away disgusted. 

Willie is still with us—he’s such a spirit he probably already has one foot in heaven—but I’ve had several dreams of just hanging out at his ranch. No one pays much attention to me like I’m almost a part of the family. It’s nice. It’s like I come around a lot, like Urkel. 

AD: You cited fellow singer-songwriter Jerry DeCicca as an inspiration for how to approach the recording of My Days of 58. At this stage in your working life, is that how you are still influenced by other musicians, by process rather than trying to emulate their work?

Bill Callahan: Process. Yeah, maybe. I probably do listen more to process than just results these days. Back when I started, I didn’t know what process was. Music was much more mysterious to me. At heart, it remains so. But I know a tiny little bit about technical stuff now. I know what a delay pedal is. I’m mostly impressed by music that gives me thoughts of, “Oh yeah, you CAN do THAT!” People who do unique or unexpected things with arrangements or lyrics or mixes. I’m late to the party but really blown away by Kendrick Lamar albums. The adventurousness of the arrangements and deliveries and lyrics. For mainstream music, it’s really out there.

Jerry was on tour opening for me and Jim [White]. He’s a close listener and he knows a lot more than me about processes. He noted how Jim and I play off of each other, goad each other. Jerry had the idea that to let this fully blossom on a record, we should first track the whole album as a duo. So neither of us has to worry about hogging space. When it came down to it, it wasn’t even about giving space for Jim to play something complex. It was about leaving the space for the full sound of his drums—even if it’s just kick/snare—leaving that air there for him to inhabit and not covering it up later with other sounds. I also left a few songs up to chance — not always knowing what chords I was going to play or when I was going to pause or speed up, etc. So, we were both listening very closely and reacting in the moment. I was reacting to myself in surprise! “And Dream Land” was written a couple days before the sessions started. I played half of it for Jim as that’s where it stood, and he played along and I thought I’m just going to finish it and the first time he is playing the whole song; the tape will be rolling. Most songs were done in one or two takes. Then I brought the overdubbers in one by one. There is still room for improv in this situation. Maybe more so because the player has the unchanging track to dance around within. With the horns, some of them were charted. Some songs were spur of the moment. And some were a mixture of both—adding improv on top of charted parts when the charted parts weren’t quite doing all that I wanted. Everybody involved was super stoked by the songs and excited to be contributing. 

AD: You’ve made music with Jim White dating back to your Smog days, but you brought him back to the fold for YTI⅃AƎЯ and the two subsequent live releases. He’s once again drumming on My Days of 58. As a player, what does he bring to your songs?

Bill Callahan: Jim loves me. That is part of what he brings. He wants me to look good. It’s symbiotic though. We want to make each other look good. I try to lay down something he can shine on. We listen to each other. Sometimes it gets to be telepathic seeming. We’ll make a dynamic change at the same time. He has a way of playing that is almost like a whole band. I think also he likes adventure like me. I think he trusts me enough to not be like, “What the fuck are you doing?!” if I take the song in a surprising direction. It’s that blindfolded tightrope shit that we both dig and he’s along for the ride. He’s a melodic drummer. I think that shows a lot on A River Ain’t Too Much to Love and on the Dirty Three records and on the Ed Kuepper After the Flood record. His drum parts are almost like piano parts, left hand right hand.

AD: What a Night! documented a performance of just you and Jim and featured a number of re-arrangements of your older and recent material. How much of those modified versions are determined in the moment? Is it purely improvisational or do you discuss or rehearse anything with him before going onstage? 

Bill Callahan: It’s an ongoing thing. Jim thinks deeply about the parts. He knows there’s always a really good one and sometimes it takes time to find it. This is what’s great. You don’t want someone coming in immediately saying, “I know what this needs.” That’s formulaic. We’re mixing up the formulas, trying different ones. I think it reaches a point where we get the best arrangement going and then it’s flexible within those parameters. Things will be adjusted at sound check, “I’m going to drop out at this verse tonight.” 

So, it’s a mix of improvisation and premeditation. Or I just drop out without announcing it but try to show via telepathy or body language what’s going to happen. I feel very body conscious at times when I am performing. My body tells me what to do. We got a lot of the YTI⅃AƎЯ songs to a certain place with all the touring with a full band but then going back to a duo, maybe the full band stuff doesn’t work so you have to change it. Or “Let’s take advantage of this newfound sparseness and take the song somewhere else.” 

AD: Last year, you put out The Holy Grail: Bill Callahan’s “Smog” Dec. 10, 2001 Peel Session. Do you expect you’ll release more archival material or was that a special circumstance? 

Bill Callahan: Drag City and I had wanted to put out a radio sessions LP for years. They gathered everything they could find together for me to listen to. About 40 songs. I just decided that that Peel Session was something that should stand on its own. It had a quality that I didn’t want to dilute in the midst of 30 other songs. Not the best financial move to just release an EP instead of a double LP, but I wanted it to be this way. I don’t think the rest of the material is worth releasing! It served its purpose at the time, I guess. In the same way that most live performances aren’t released, they just exist as an event that some people shared one time. We record the records live anyway, so a radio session is not that much different most of the time. But the Peel Session had two covers that I don’t think we had ever even played. 

AD: Some musicians such as Nick Cave liken songwriting to a job and physically go to a set aside space and keep office hours in order to write. Do you have a similar process with your songwriting? 

Bill Callahan: Sort of. Since my kids are old enough to be in school, I can have regular hours. It’s a real inspiration to get up early in the morning and get them out of the house. I only really consider it a job when I have to do things I don’t necessarily treasure—setting up tours, booking hotels etc.—the logistics. The creative part feels more to me like a spiritual exercise that keeps me sane. Keeps me grounded. And it is my job, and I don’t want to get fired by my boss (the human race). So, I work hard. As I get older, I realize this type of work is not all about the actual act of writing a song. 

The body needs to move, the brain needs to be fed, human contact needs to be made. So, my work day involves these things a lot of the time, too because there’s only about three hours of creativity in me per day.

AD: How do you know when you’re done writing a song and it’s ready to record?

Bill Callahan: Because it’s done! How do you know when the cookies you’re baking are done? They look like how you want them to look. They look edible. They aren’t over or under cooked. You say, “Yep, that’s a cookie.” Go into a studio with an under or overcooked song and you’ll know it immediately. There is also the body test. If you try to sing an unfinished song, there is a great weight in your body. Like you’re being forced to tell a lie.

AD: Your epistolary novel Letters to Emma Bowlcut came out in 2010. You’ve mentioned writers that you admire in interviews such as James Kelman. Do you ever think you’ll return to fiction writing again?

Bill Callahan: Yes. I’ve got something cooking now. How has it been so long between books? Songs suck me in. Music has many more stages than writing prose. You write a book and there it is. There might be some public readings at bookstores but that’s about it. With music there’s performances and within those performances there’s re-interpretations. There’s recording which takes the written thing to a different place. Still, writing prose is a quiet colossus of experience and I love it and want that to be a part of my life too. But music is always exposing its thigh to me.

AD: You took up Twitter a number of years ago but left it behind. For a short time last year, you experimented with answering questions from your followers on Bandcamp. Are you still trying to find a good way to directly engage with your fans? 

Bill Callahan: I’m still wondering if that’s the right move. Are we supposed to engage with each other? Do you hang out with your therapist between sessions? Maybe it’s better if there’s a separation between the creator and the listener and the song is the place where we both meet, not the street or the internet. We are meeting and convening in the sphere of the song already. What could be deeper than that? The Bandcamp thing was fun but there were too many questions! Too many for me to answer and leaving them unanswered felt odd and incomplete. Even though I would preface it with, “I’m going to answer X amount of questions, and the rest will not be answered,” people sent more and more. I don’t like the energy of unanswered questions in the universe. That is also my problem with recording music digitally and having 100 guitar solos: All those guitar solos preserved in the digital ether. It’s too much weight. Did I mention the record was recorded to tape and mixed to tape? I can smell a digital recording from a mile away. Or not smell it. I don’t think it has a smell like an analog recording does. Digital recording is odorless.
