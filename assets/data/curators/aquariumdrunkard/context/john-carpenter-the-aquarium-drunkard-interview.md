## Body

Music is integral to the bleak zones created by filmmaker and composer John Carpenter. Think of the unforgettable intro to Escape from New York, which sets the dystopian mood to follow. Or of his infamous Halloween theme, which has inspired dread for decades. Though he began soundtracking his own work very early on out of necessity—see his sci-fi comedy debut Dark Star—Carpenter has long placed music near the center of his creative life. His father, Howard Ralph Carpenter, was a music professor, and Carpenter played in an R&B cover band in college. 

A decade ago, in 2014, he joined with his son Cody Carpenter and godson Daniel Davies, to embark on a second career as a recording artist with the Lost Themes series for Sacred Bones, collections of imaginary film music that helped the trio establish the feel to start composing for actual movies, including David Gordon Green’s recent contributions to the Halloween franchise. Now they’ve returned with Lost Themes IV: Noir, a collection loosely inspired by classic film noir. Carpenter joins us to discuss the new album, his conception of film noir, and more. | j woodbury 

Aquarium Drunkard: I really enjoyed my time with Lost Themes IV: Noir. You’ve really been on a tear making these records, and I think this one might be one of the most musically adventurous of the bunch.

John Carpenter: Oh, thank you. That’s very kind.

AD: I was reading a 1972 essay by Paul Schrader where he says that film noir, at least in his conception, it’s not a genre, so much as a style or mood. Do you agree with that? 

John Carpenter: Well, I don’t know. It seems equivocating. But it’s hard to know what he’s talking about. Film noir is a French term. Of course, you gotta watch out for the French, but everybody’s doomed in these things. That’s what I find. As a matter of fact, I’m sitting here right now watching Out of the Past with Robert Mitchum and that’s one of the ultimate film noirs, and there’s a mood that hangs over it that we wanted to capture musically. But I will never disagree with Paul Schrader. Never.

AD: You allude to Out of the Past with “Beyond the Gallows,” from this new album with your son, Cody Carpenter, and your godson, Daniel Davies. That’s one of your favorites—was it one of the earlier noir films you saw?

John Carpenter: Oh, that’s from 1947, the year before I was born. Sure. But I saw it early on. Sunset Boulevard would be another one. That might me my favorite of all. Just such a terrific movie.

AD: Thinking about the way this record works, I think the whole thing is nicely summed up in the way that these song titles hint and allude to specific films, but they aren’t full-on homages to them. You didn’t name them directly after films. They’re all a little tweaked.

John Carpenter: Well, we’re a little tweaked. 

AD: [Laughs] Was it important for you guys to have some sort of wiggle room when it comes to the concept? Because so often noir soundtracks feature orchestral pieces or jazzy fare, and there are a few elements that lean towards that here, but you guys mostly stick to your riffs and the synths—your signature sound. It’s more like you bend a noir to your style than the other way around.

John Carpenter: Well, that’s right. We can’t do anything about what we can do. This is the music we make, but we are inspired by and informed by film noir.

AD: Noir casts such a long shadow over pop culture. Every generation adapts noir ideas in one way or another. I was digging around and found the intro you did for Gunship’s song “Tech Noir,” and it got me thinking about how film noir has influenced cyberpunk. How did you get hooked up with them? Did they reach out to you? 

John Carpenter: This was a while ago. Yeah, they did. They just reached out and said, “Would you do this narration?” And I said, “Sure, why not?” 

AD: Are you a fan of a lot of stuff in that sort of synthwave genre? Obviously you’ve had an influence on that style.

John Carpenter: I like it a lot. Yeah.

AD: It’s interesting to me that so many people in the minimal synth scene emulate your style, when you started making music for your films out of necessity. It’s interesting the way that’s worked. But it’s like you said about what you and Cody and Daniel make, you just do what you do.  

John Carpenter: That’s all I can do, so why worry about it? 

AD: William Gibson’s 1984 novel Neuromancer is another blend of cyberpunk and noir. He’s said that he was influenced by Escape From New York. Were aware of your influence on him when that book came out?

John Carpenter: No, not at all.

AD: Did you ever end up digging into any of his work or adaptations of it? 

John Carpenter: A little bit. I don’t quite understand it, but I realize there a lot of things I don’t understand.

AD: I think there’s a noir quality to Escape From New York. Was that something you were sort of thinking about when you were putting the visual feel of that film together?

John Carpenter: I don’t think so. I’m just a big science fiction fan. This was an idea that I had back, oh man, this is mid-seventies, probably inspired by a movie called Death Wish.

AD: Oh sure, the Bronson movie. 

John Carpenter: The Charles Bronson movie. I enjoyed that movie for a number of reasons. Hang on one second, OK? I’m going to leave you for a minute and grab some coffee and tea. And they’re a little distance away.

AD: No problem. 

John Carpenter: Okay, I’m back. 

AD: We were talking about the influence of Death Wish on Escape from New York. 

John Carpenter: The inspiration also came from a novel by Harry Harrison, Planet of the Damned. I don’t remember exactly, but the essence of that novel was there’s a mission where they had to send someone to the worst planet in the universe, so they send the worst guy. So that also inspired me, but I wasn’t thinking about [noir] when it comes to the darkness. In the movie, New York is a prison, and the best way to shoot it, if you don’t have a big budget, is shoot it at night. It hides a lot of sins. That’s what we did.

AD: Well it’s one of my favorite films, and I guess maybe I see in a noir influence just in terms of the lighting—and like you said about noirs, everyone is doomed in them, and Snake shares some of that too. 

John Carpenter: Yeah. Well, but I’m not sure it’s classic noir. I don’t think so. But I appreciate William Gibson. I appreciate what you just said. Yeah, that’s always nice. Always nice.

AD: I wanted to ask about your collaborators, Cody and Daniel, who you made these great albums with and new music for the Halloween films. As I understand it, they first started playing music together at your place as kids. I’m curious, do you remember when you first started to notice what they were were up to and think, “Hey, they’re getting pretty good?” Was it pretty early on?

John Carpenter: Oh yeah. They were always into it. And Daniel’s dad, I don’t know if you know who he is.

AD: Yeah, Dave Davies of The Kinks.

John Carpenter: Yeah. Okay. So he had a musical pedigree. Anyway, he and Cody just grew up around here and he loved music, and they formed a band and off they went. They played without me. I didn’t have anything to do with it, but they were great. Cody was a drummer at the time. 

AD: How did you get the sort of idea to start playing with them?

John Carpenter: Well, it didn’t happen quite like that. My wife got me a musical set up down the lower floor of our house, and it was a computer set up. I got Logic Pro, and so I began experimenting with it. And my son at the time, he had graduated from high school. It was 2014. And for fun, we would play on the computer program and start making music. What we started doing was making sort of score, and we did a lot of it. We play video games and go play music, just generally have a good time. And I got a new music attorney at the time, and she said to me, “Do you have any new music?” So I sent her this stuff that Cody and I had done, and then about, I don’t know, three or four months later, I had a record deal.

AD: Wow.

John Carpenter: That’s how it worked. We were off and running.

AD: Music has been a big part of your life. I understand. Is it true that you dropped out of school to play bass in a band in the late ‘60s? 

John Carpenter: No, I did not drop out of school. But I did play bass in a band. 

AD: Did you tour? 

John Carpenter: No. It was in a little local band, a cover band is what it was. We would play fraternity parties to make money.

AD: What kind of stuff did you play?

John Carpenter: We played a lot of R&B that was popular at parties. We learned a great deal about human nature, watching the fraternity cavort

AD: Was bass your first instrument? 

John Carpenter: No, no. My father was a music teacher, so I had to learn violin, and I had keyboards in my past and the regular guitar, so I was pretty conversant in a lot of instruments.

AD: Did you ever sit down your son Cody and make him play music? 

John Carpenter: Never. 

AD: He came to it naturally? 

John Carpenter: Absolutely. He was just drawn to it and I had nothing to do with it. I decided I’m not going to do that. My dad did that with me and I really didn’t appreciate it.

AD: Did you come to appreciate it later when the ability to make your own scores became a reality and the fact that you had the tools that you could do it? 

John Carpenter: No. I mean, I’m talking about when my father decided I should play the violin. I had no talent, so I never appreciated that. But I loved growing up in the house. I did, because there was always music. I grew up with it. I grew up with classical music and that I was a movie kid, and I grew up with film scores, all of it.

AD: The Beatles were big for you too? 

John Carpenter: I love The Beatles. Yeah.

AD: Were you into the shift as they turned toward  psychedelic rock? 

John Carpenter: All of it. All of it. Yeah.

AD: I know another band that’s important to you is Metallica. I know you’d hoped to use their song “Enter Sandman” in The Mouth of Madness, but when you couldn’t you almost made your own version of a Metallica song with Dave Davies and Jim Lang. 

John Carpenter: That’s right. 

AD: How did you first encounter Metallica? 

John Carpenter: Through my kids, yeah. They were listening first and I heard it. It was “Enter Sandman” that got me going. I thought, “Wow, they’re pretty good.” 

AD: The Lost Themes records make it clear that your style is very distinct, but when I consider all your music it seems to me you’ve always been drawn to “riffs” more or less. 

John Carpenter: I agree with you, sure. 

AD: Heavy metal was born in the ‘70s and ‘80s—were you into it then? 

John Carpenter: Yes, the answer is yes.

AD: Well it’s fascinating how, while you don’t necessarily make metal records, it’s clear that you’re drawn to that stuff. This new one is great, I’ve really appreciated getting the chance to chat with you a little bit about

John Carpenter: Absolutely, man. Yeah. Good fun.
