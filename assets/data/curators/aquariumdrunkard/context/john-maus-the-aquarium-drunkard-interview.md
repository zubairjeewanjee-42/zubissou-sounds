## Body

Since the mid-2000s, when he was just an outsider experimentalist in a then-incipient indie music scene, John Maus gained a reputation as an arcane conversationalist, whose public dialogues offered as much intricacy as they did enthusiasm, with a philosophical repertoire only matched by his own nervous body language. Rarely was Maus ever pushed on the conceptual basis of his sayings, however, which would then often be left to the side, as if they were just the nonsensical ramblings of a naturally esoteric musician who had maybe read too much French theory. For this interview, we went the other way instead: plunged into the specifics of those discourses to try to trace the strange continuities between the continental thought and mystical traditions Maus is enamored with and the postwar pop sound his work inevitably comes to represent.

I met John Maus at the Beggars Office in Soho the day before his concert at Warsaw, Brooklyn, alongside artist Julia Santoli, who took photographs. Maus seemed quiet at first but quickly opened up. His takes during our conversation drew from Kittler’s archaeology of media to Adorno’s negative dialectic and Deleuze’s control society, and addressed musical issues as broad as the dissolution of Western art music and as narrow as the false promises of DIY synth-building, besides seemingly non-musical issues around the dead end of academia and the demonic rise of tech-bro America. These scholarly commitments, offering little friction on the path between theology and technology, clearly echo through the new record, where fulgurations of Gregorian chant and baroque harmony collide with the debris of phonograph culture and the “imbecile use of oscillators that were designed to track submarines.” The very origins of Maus’ pop lie in the distortions of the cathedral’s acoustics, or in an attempt at giving an afterlife to the sacred, refracted as it can be through contemporary noisescapes: the tape hissing and the needle fluttering and the deep resonances of his own voice in the legendary, bombastically repressed stage performances are then vehicles for Something Greater, that erupts from the outside.

In his answers, Maus also addressed his political beliefs and misfortunes, the recent leap of faith and the experience of marriage, thinking against the irony and detachment often projected onto his persona—rather, if we take him on his word, sincerity itself has become a radical gesture of presence among the ruins of civilization. It is exactly in treating the glitches, failures, and absurdities of his musical project and persona as necessary materials for a transcendental truth procedure that, Maus maintains, art can still “imagine the people that are not yet,” and “see the world from the standpoint of its future redemption,” and “bear witness to the contradictions and the senseless suffering,” and “all that sort of thing.” | r moraes | photos j santoli

John Maus: Yeah! He talks about the scene of the two, about forcing the hypothesis of eternity. There’s something about it that transcends objectification, lust, you know what I mean? It forces the hypothesis of eternity. It involves a vow until death. So, yeah, it’s the scene of the two. I like that idea. AD: So you do believe that love and art are two different domains of truth?

John Maus: Yes, absolutely. Yeah, I still hold on to that. It’s love, art, mathematics, politics… They’re beyond the domain of knowledge or whatever, as Badiou would say. Absolutely.

AD: I understand that you’ve been somewhat of a satirical man, maybe because of your association with the Million Dollar Extreme crowd. And once you’re a very ironic person, it’s hard to be taken seriously ever again. I think of, for example, Kramer from Seinfeld apologizing for being racist on David Letterman, and everyone keeps laughing at him, and he is like: no, no, this is serious. Because no one wants to be the first to fall for it, in case it’s a joke. This is just a preamble for my question, which is: is your religious devotion ironic? Because a lot of your new songs are kind of immersed in this hymnal atmosphere (like in “Let the Time Fly” or in the record’s finale), while others are explicitly religious in their lyrics (there’s “I Hate Antichrist,” of course). You also write in the notes of the album that you’ve been impacted by the recent loss of family members. Are you sincerely a Catholic now? Do you go to church on Sundays? Or are you more of a mystical Christian, or, what’s your theology? Do you believe in presenting yourself to a God that punishes?

John Maus: Well, first of all, with the irony thing, I’ve never thought of it as ironic at all. The thing that I always say is that anything that’s carried out with earnestness or sincerity is bound to appear ridiculous or comical. It was never ironic in the sense that, you know, it wasn’t meant as that… But then irony is a whole thing. Like, what does that mean?

AD: I mean, you do like to have fun with public displays and on Instagram…

John Maus: Oh, yeah, yeah. Well, the Instagram stuff, I mean, it’s a little bit getting my hands dirty. It’s just par for the course, you know. With the TikToks and Instagram, I’m just trying to do my part to be a good sport and promote the album. And yeah, there, I’m absolutely… I’m taking the piss a little bit there. But that’s not the music or, you know what I mean… It’s just reminding people that the record is available for pre-sale, you know? And then with the religious thing… When I was coming up, all the philosophy I read, the German idealists, all that stuff… Even Badiou! His book on Saint Paul. And Zizek. There was that Paul moment… I don’t know, are you familiar with Simone Weil?

AD: Yeah, I like her very much.

John Maus: Yeah, yeah. We must love he who is absent. This sort of, like, almost Christian atheism.

AD: Like a negative theology. And very ascetic too.

John Maus: Yes, right, right, right. But not even apophatic. It was, it’s some kind of… The supreme moment is ‘why have you forsaken me?’ And then German idealism is sort of the supreme religious picture, you know what I mean? It’s the impasse where the infinite becomes finite and the finite becomes infinite. That’s how I always thought of it, up until a point, and that’s the point I’m coming to, where, yeah, I think what happened was the leap of faith. The supernatural faith was infused and rather than the religion being just a mere picture of the absolute philosophical truth, the revealed truth took priority over it. And the Simone Weil stuff went to the side. And then you have sacramental presence, you have liturgy, you have mysteries, you know? It became a reality, you know? So that, I think, makes a big difference.

AD: Besides these kinds of themes that influenced the record, the music itself is also shaped by religion. You’re into Classical music, Baroque music, even medieval music—there are some Gregorian chants in “Theotokos,” right? I’ve also heard you like Rameau a lot, and you’ve studied with some monks for a while? How do you connect this affinity for traditional Western art music with the sort of post-war pop and punk music that you are most clearly continuing in your work? Because, in a way, it’s like you’re profaning the sacred music, both in terms of collapsing the discourse levels and the categories they belong to and in terms of their sonic rules. To me, your music seems way closer to what Thomas Mann describes as the Devil’s work in his Faustus book, with the strange uses of this kind of modal scales through the modern synths, and some slightly dissonant intervals sometimes (tritones, I think?), and occasional modulations, and dynamic compressions, and harmonic distortions, and so on… So I guess the question is: John, are you trying to destroy Western civilization?

John Maus: (laughs) No, no… I’ve always said that… There’s actually a line in “Postscript on the Societies of Control” where Deleuze says it could be that earlier methods borrowed from the societies of sovereignty will come to the fore as a kind of resistance in the societies of control, so that’s a thread we could tease out. But, also, there’s a weird resonance or a weird correspondence between post-war pop and Renaissance and medieval music, in a way. In terms of the diatonic harmonies they use, or the fact that we’ve cast off a major-minor tonality in the 18th century, 19th century, 20th century sense, you know what I mean? The adventure of key, all that sort of stuff, Beethoven or Mozart or whatever. We just totally abandoned that, and it’s just kind of these diatonic spaces now. It’s without voice leading rules, but you have the rock quartets of the keyboard, players playing a monophonic line, and the bass players playing a monophonic line, and the singers singing a monophonic line, and there’s this just diatonic counterpoint happening. Some things are different, of course: there’s no cadence anymore, there’s no voice leading rules at all really. But there’s a correspondence there, harmonically, I think. And then, just in terms of the Western harmonic tradition in general, it all goes back to the Gregorian repertoire. Our whole harmonic world, that’s the germ of it. In the same way, maybe, that our art tradition goes back to the icons and the sacred art. It’s always there, you know, as the germ. Any 101 music history book you get is going to start with the plain chant. So I think it’s just boiling it down to its essence, in a way.

AD: This brings up another question I had, about the voice that you sing with, this deep voice that I think also brings up a certain character, a persona. It’s as if you become something else, or reach for something so entrenched in you that it seems to be coming from beyond, from before you. I always think of the implications of effects like reverb and delay and some specific timbres to our imagination of divinity in music. Like, when we think of a gleam of light or a siren’s call, it all crystallizes in particular kinds of sonic morphology. There’s a relationship between some timbres or grains of the voice and this synesthesic or inter-phenomenological schema, let’s say, that’s connecting them to a sense of transcendence, right? I don’t know if “Let Me Through” makes reference to that, or, like, in “Losing Your Mind,” when the glitches and noises break to the surface, it’s almost like something escapes the control of your own signature. I also think of… I know you like Kittler, I like Kittler a lot as well. At some point, Kittler writes about how the architecture of the church was made for a certain sound, that there’s a specific distance between the cross and the organ that needs to exist to create a certain resonance to favor the sacred…

John Maus: Right, right, and that ties into the thing we were just talking about, where it’s even arguable that the reason the West develops the triad is because of the resonance of the cathedrals teasing out those overtones that brought harmony out. Yeah, no, absolutely. But here’s the whole thing: this technology, in that case the acoustics of a cathedral, is now, today, a misuse of World War II equipment. I really like that image as a way of approaching what’s unique about post-war pop music. It’s just a bunch of imbeciles, a bunch of stupid imbeciles whose cultural reservoir is like Steven Spielberg movies and Coca-Cola and Domino’s Pizza and Nintendo, and they have these scramblers and hi-fi equipment and oscillators that were designed to track submarines. And then they just make oscillations with them. There’s a way where the media is always defacing the voice, it’s always obscuring the voice, it becomes the thing that you’re expressing… That whole way of approaching it, yeah, I try to consciously mobilize that for sure, the medial dimension of the artifact, bringing that to the fore as an expressive device… Of course, there’s probably a million ways to do it better, but I just mean rather than the tape capturing a one-to-one performance, you mobilize the distortion and the wow and the flutter and all the obscurities that the tape introduces.

AD: So, your thesis, you talked about it already. It’s from 2014, it’s about surveillance or, say, the passage from Foucault’s “discipline society” to Deleuze’s “control society,” right? But it’s been 10 years already. How do you think this has panned out since? Do you see your own ideas reflected in the current field of “politics,” this terribly constrictive field of imagination? I’m particularly interested in the Kittlerian apocalyptic takes on recording technologies and their unavoidable genealogy in war you just mentioned. I also think of your use of Jacques Attali and music becoming a commodity for, as he put it, the “stockpiling” of noises. How, for him, music paired with social development and even predicted it, somehow. How do you reconcile being a musician in this case, and a pop musician at that? Do you think your music plays a role in bringing forth this control society as well? Like, do you think your 2010s work or the hypnagogic pop movement it belonged to led to our current predicament, of a long era of reactionary weirdness? Or: how violent is your music towards music itself, towards history?

John Maus: I think Attali is too pessimistic, in the same way that Adorno is, or at least the caricature of Adorno in popular music. I mean, he had some throwaway lines about a possibility of phonographic music, and he liked Varese, but in general they’re both kind of pessimistic in the sense that it’s always already completely some sort of organization of the sensible to the end of a control and to the foreclosure of subjectivity, and all this sort of thing. And that’s always a danger and a dimension of… I mean, anytime you speak, anytime you use an idiom, you’re mirroring the structures of power. But, you know, I’ve always thought that, on the other hand, there is the Event, you know? That somehow, impossibly, whatever it is, whether it’s punk rock or Motown, these aren’t purely the manufacture of mechanistic subjects.

AD: There’s lines of flight.

John Maus: Yes! There’s lines of flight. Exactly. I mean, if I didn’t believe that, I wouldn’t do it. But you have to be, you know, an exponent of the historical tendencies and their sworn enemy at the same time. It’s this weird paradox. And I think that, yeah, on the one hand you’re mirroring the structures of power by making, you know, pop music, with a regular beat, in structured parts… But there’s a zebra stripe of stupidity, perhaps, of ugliness, of dissonance, of whatever you want to call it, of positive feedback, where something other than the place takes place. At least in art, you know, you imagine the people that are not yet. You see the world from the standpoint of its future redemption. You bear witness to the contradictions and the senseless suffering. And all that sort of thing. So yeah, it’s both/and. It’s always been that way, whether we’re talking about these guys that are making music for the church or, in the 18th century, for the princes, and then for the Revolution, and then… You know? Music, in any epoch, is always both/and.

AD: You coded music for a while, I heard you used TensorFlow and I think some GANs? Did any of those experiments get into the new album at all? And do you think music is bound to end soon, with the rise of actual AI? Could we be witnessing the end of this art form, of sonic self-expression, or self-expression in general? Vilém Flusser has a good book called Does Writing Have a Future?, so now I ask you: does music have a future?

John Maus: Yeah, I think it does. I mean, I just used it before all the DALL-E and ChatGPT and all that stuff dropped, to see what it does. It’s just a kind of synthesis. It’s just like any other technology. I mean, when the phonograph came out, people were horrified that a voice could come out of a box, you know?

AD: It was like a ghost, right?

John Maus: Yeah, it’s just… They’re just filters. They’re just deep nets trained on whatever you train them on and they can generate samples, essentially. I mean, who knows how it will go? You could imagine that once biofeedback gets involved, there’d be some headphones you put on that just kind of hit the spot automatically every time, so to speak, musically, just generating it in real time. Sounds that hit the spot. But would that be music?

AD: Almost like those Spotify playlists of algorithmic music.

John Maus: It would just be based on your… what your brain is doing, or something like that. Like, it’s supposed to activate the pleasure center in real time. Nets that could do that.

AD: But did your experiments end up in the record?

John Maus: Oh, no, no… I have these Rarities for the Road CDs that are on YouTube. I used a couple of the samples on some of those tracks. It was just samples, I could only get 30 second samples, you know. And then there’s the whole question of paradelia: are you just hallucinating what you heard in these samples? And then you tease out the music you heard in the samples that were generated… But yeah, it’s just like any kind of algorithmic composition. I’ve done it with integer sequences before, where you just put in some random integer sequences, like the Fibonacci or whatever. And then you make diatonic chords come out of it to see what it does. But whatever rules you put in place is what you finally hear sonified back to you, you know what I mean?

AD: I have this composer friend who was inspired by Alvin Lucier’s “I Am Sitting in a Room” to put a lot of reverb on a silent track until at some point something emerged out of it. I feel like that can be a metaphor for how AI works. But isn’t that also a demonic thing? Because it’s a bunch of anonymous voices coming out from the ether. Isn’t Lucifer the one that says “I am Legion”?

John Maus: (laughs) Yeah, yeah, yeah. I mean, I think the demonic thing there is the Silicon Valley people, you know, Peter Thiel and Elon Musk and all these guys, and Reddit… That sort of metaphysics that they have where they actually think it’s intelligent. I would throw AI in scare quotes. It’s software. It’s not… It just doesn’t… ‘Well, what’s the difference? It talks. You talk.’ No, it doesn’t. A calculator doesn’t do math. It’s just a bunch of transit… You know, it’s silicone components that do what they do and your mind is the one that looks and intellects it. Metaphysics is immaterial. It’s not… A machine can’t have intellect.

AD: When you stopped releasing music regularly, it was because of your PhD, but also because you started building some synths. How did you interact with the quite militant synth scene? (I think the modular synth people are maybe more militant than the Maoists.) And how did this kind of practical reasoning influence your music-making? I often try to think of art as a form of thinking itself. A professor I know says Da Vinci did not paint because he was a genius, but he was a genius because he painted—painting shaped his worldview in a way that allowed the genius to come out on other fronts. And there are these hidden affordances in the tools we use to make art, and knowing their capital-B Being to the minutia also helps render their depth. I’m thinking, maybe, of Heidegger and the ready-to-hand. How there’s this pre-objective quality in instruments, and building them might allow for more connections to open up under your control, to understand how the instruments make meaning. Can you comment on that turn to the material engineering of sounds? How different is it from the abstract engineering of music theory?

John Maus: You know Kittler built synths too?

AD: Oh, yeah?

John Maus: He was a synth builder, analog synth. But yeah, I mean, I think mine was a failed experiment. What I imagined would happen is that if I built the instruments myself it would somehow… that this would come across. You would hear that somehow. You would hear that. (laughs) But I don’t know what I was thinking.

AD: Wait, it doesn’t come across?

John Maus: No, because a saw wave is a saw wave, whether you build the oscillator or not. A sine wave is a sine wave, whether you soldered all the components yourself or not. In that sense, I think it was aberrant thinking. I don’t know what I imagined would happen, but I did it all, and you can’t hear that the saw wave you’re hearing was… that each little resistor and capacitor was soldered by me.

AD: It’s an artisanal sine wave.

John Maus: (laughs) Yeah, right. Exactly. Exactly. I thought it was… that somehow I was blurring the subjective and the objective. Like, really having the most complete command of the most advanced means of construction. But then it was just… a saw wave is a saw wave. So I write it off as a failure, you know?

AD: A lot of people, journalists, like to mention this PhD you’ve got as a sort of a curious anecdote about you. But as someone who also went into academia and who has a deep suspicion of it, and who has stumbled into para-academic environments myself… For context: I teach at The New Centre, where Hui and Bratton and Terranova have taught too…

John Maus: Oh, yeah. I sent Terranova my dissertation and she never wrote back.

AD: (laughs) Yeah, I’m curious to hear about your time at the European Graduate School and your takes on the state of academia right now. Was it really worth it? Is it all a scam? How was the experience of teaching, and teaching in Hawaii specifically, which I personally think is totally at odds with your music? Like, I think of your music as Southern Gothic, Flannery O’Connor, or if not that then 1980s erotic horror thriller, Brian DePalma kind of stuff, and not really… SpongeBob or beach music.

John Maus: (laughs) Well, no, I mean, Hawaii has a music…

AD: I mean, I know, Henry Flint explored that a little bit, didn’t he?

John Maus: Yeah! Or Gabby… Gabby Pahinui, maybe I’m mispronouncing it, but it’s really, really… it’s a music. It’s a real music.

AD: But the landscape within it seems not really your thing.

John Maus: That landscape is embodied in their music. It’s so… I mean, props to the Hawaiian nation, the Hawaiian people. It’s very… I was very fortunate to live there. It’s really a beautiful thing. But I would argue that in my own Flannery O’Connor Southern Gothic way, if you listen to some of the stuff on Pitiless that I recorded there and you listen to some of the outtakes from that, like “All Joy for All Eternity,” there is a little bit of a Waikiki sunset in there. I mean, it’s the sublime. It’s the atom bomb of every day. You touch the infinite there. I think a little bit of that did come through. But what was the other question…

AD: About your time at EGS?

John Maus: Oh, yeah, EGS, and academia in general. Now, ten years on, having set it aside for the most part, I think if my experience was par for the course… Just based on what I see on Twitter and stuff like that, I think it is not good to start us on French theory. It’s just not alright. You don’t start with that. In the best case scenario, Descartes and Kant and stuff like that. All those French theorists munching on speed and going to leather dungeons and dropping acid, they all cut their teeth on the Greeks and on…

AD: So you would start with the Greeks?

John Maus: Yeah, I mean, you have to. That’s what I’m trying to backfill now, all the medieval and the Greeks. There’s something about scholastic metaphysics, where you’re like: whoa, I should have at least known this before I was reading Lacan, you know what I mean? So we’ve got three generations, maybe four, that started with freaking Lacan. That can’t… That kind of myopia can’t be good. That’s kind of how I feel now.

AD: And then you read Plato and you’re like: this guy’s really influenced by Badiou.

John Maus: Yeah, that’s what I mean, that’s exactly what I mean. It’s great if you can situate it as a form of thought in relation to our moment, certainly that, especially 1968 and everything that was going on then, precisely along these axes I’m talking about, like, how they’re all high on speed, and they’re all scoundrels.

AD: On that note, though, you mentioned the leather dungeons. I was wondering about the physical requirements of you on the stage, because it seems like it’s a limit-experience in some kind of way?

John Maus: Yeah, I mean, that’s what I mean: this is the theoretical repertory that we’re drawing on, limit-experience, Bataille, Foucault. Yeah, I mean, that’s what I’m drawing on. If I was to articulate it theoretically. It’s like the hysterical body, it’s a Jean-Luc Nancy quote. And then, of course, there’s always this ambivalent relationship with Adorno, because he was the great thinker in the 20th century, of music. I mean, there’s this volume that is, like, 1500 pages on music. This critical philosopher, steeped in German Bildung, who forgot more about Hegel than most of us will ever learn, and he’s writing about the advent of post-war pop and canned music and jazz, or at least his little caricature of jazz…

AD: I don’t think he got to listen to more experimental jazz, really…

John Maus: Yeah, yeah, yeah. But all the things he’s saying, you can’t just throw out if you take the whole critical project seriously. So you can’t do what Greil Marcus did and just say: oh, no, it’s punk rock, you know?

AD: You disagree with Marcus?

John Maus: No… Well, with the way he mobilized Adorno briefly in Lipstick Traces… But the ugliness, the dissonance, I mean, that’s there in my performance. The ridiculousness, the irreconcilability… I’m just saying this is the jargon that I’m using to articulate it. It would make his skin crawl. He would say: no, no, man, you need… His big thing was thematic integrity. You needed that. You needed that from Beethoven to Webern. If the whole isn’t included in the part such that the part… meaning the motive, meaning the little theme that’s developed into the whole. If the part proves the whole untrue, then you’re not really making subjective music. I don’t… I just put to that this zebra stripe of stupidity I’m talking about, you know? Like, what was that saying on Warhol? That sort of thing. You have to reckon with that too. Singing how much is that doggy in the window with oscillators and your face is melting and you are just kind of screaming until you sound like a donkey. Certainly, in terms of a Weimar thing, that could be “Pierrot Lunaire,” that could be Dada, there’s some aesthetic merit there that those guys could recognize, I would hope.

AD: I was also wondering about the physical stamina that’s required from you on stage. Your concerts are legendary because they look like you’re forcing some kind of spirit out of yourself, like in a sweaty exorcism. One can really see the entanglement of spirit and flesh. You mention the French theological turn a lot, so maybe you know more about that than me, about Nancy and Michel Henry and people like that.. But to me it’s also all intuited in the moment. Yet those concerts were for smaller audiences, for the 2010s blog audience. It was basement stuff. Do you still command the stage in the same way for this tour, with that sort of stamina? Is it still more about more presence than meaning?

John Maus: Yeah, that’s the show I’m doing, still. It’s really difficult and I get really nervous about it, and there’ll be some houses on the next leg where it becomes a little bit more difficult because they’re larger. If you get some thousands of people in there…

AD: Tomorrow in Brooklyn, that’s probably a large crowd.

John Maus: I mean, we can try to use video cameras and stuff like that to translate it. But that’s what the show is. I mean, I’m not Led Zeppelin. There’s not a band. That’s not the event that’s happening. The event is precisely that, it’s the limit-experience, it’s me coming apart. Which very much in my mind is the whole punk rock thing. Just as a teenager, you know, that’s what it was… that’s what giving a show was.

AD: Slightly changing the subject now. I’m not into ‘gotcha’ journalism or persecutory drives in general, and I know you’ve already expanded on the January 6 polemic hundreds of times in this interview cycle, but I still have some adjacent questions about that, even just because it’s on your own notes to the album. First, not about the event, which is a lesser thing to me, but about friendship. Your career starts with Ariel Pink, and you two maintain a long, close relationship, even after the sort of setbacks of what he has described as a cancellation amidst other somewhat performative and possibly ironic occasions, like his participation on Fox News and other things of that caliber. There’s a Spanish Catholic philosopher, Julián Marías, who wrote a book called, if I were to freely translate, Treatise on Coexistence. It asks how can it be possible that we live with others in deep disagreement, on radically different moral bases sometimes. He says we ought to love each other despite our disagreements or even because of them. In light of your last few years, would you agree with that? Or is that some sort of liberal nonsense to your formerly Maoist ears?

John Maus: No, no, no, no… That’s not liberal nonsense. The greatest of eases is love. Absolutely. Yeah, you got to… You got to do that. Yeah, we came up. We came up. That’s the lows. Did you hear the new one, his new album? It just came out. It’s great. He’s still got it. I mean, it’s the same questions, it’s the same threads. Those are my suspicions as well, for the most part. There’s a lot of overlap in terms of musical concerns, but then, yeah, in daily life… But you don’t throw your friends under the bus.

AD: You’re still friends, right?

John Maus: Yeah.

AD: At the same time, I think this kind of political mystery about yourself, this esotericism, it sort of works in your favor, it seems to me.

John Maus: No, no, it doesn’t. I still get three or four comments that say that I’m a fascist anytime I do anything.

AD: But that happened before January 6 as well, didn’t it?

John Maus: I guess. No, I… The thing I go back to is when my brother died, and there’s comments making fun of that. That’s just how the internet is. So I guess, yeah, you’re right. There’s always more of that stuff.

AD: It can get confusing. I’ve read people saying you’re a left-accelerationist, and I’m like: no, he’s an Agamben guy! He’s an Invisible Committee guy…

John Maus: I’ve never even read Nick Land. I know about these kinds of things just from the ether. But no, I was never in my ‘Nick Land era.’ I was into the post-Marxists, Invisible Committee, Tiqqun, Negri, Hardt, Tronti… Uh, you know, Gramsci… So yeah. In that sense, I’m guilty. I’m guilty of thinking that the good-cop-bad-cop thing is just… That’s so much liberal nonsense. Whether it’s eight million from Obama or whatever million from Trump, the difference really is that one of them makes really despicable memes about it and shit.

AD: You mean in terms of financing Israel’s genocide.

John Maus: Yeah. With the genocide, it’s like: either we’re full in on it or we’re sad about it but we’re going to do it anyway. Good cop, bad cop. You know all this shit. Don’t we just laugh at that spectacle and say: no, the true, real politics is not any of that management and parliamentarianism and that sort of thing? So yeah, I’m guilty of that. My adult friends are all like: no, man, come on, be an adult. Speak.

AD: You do like the mystery though, right? Like a good Catholic would prefer the mystery to the revelation.

John Maus: Well, I mean, I don’t like… No, I don’t like being called a Trump supporter. I mean, I don’t. I want to… I respond every time I see it now, but I know… Like, come on, man, look at my shit. I’m not trying to do some shit-eating ‘I’m mysterious’ gist. Is he, is he not? No, I don’t…

AD: In this album, you have some tracks like “Because We Built It,” which are more explicitly political. You even said you made a continuation of “Cop Killer”…

John Maus: Yeah, that’s what I said about “I Hate Antichrist.” That it was a cop killer thing. The most direct one is “Tout Les Gens…”, that’s literally the organisation politique slogan in relation to the sans-papiers. But yeah… I mean, it’s not going to… It’s gotten away from me. Reddit’s going to call me a Nazi for the rest of my life no matter what I do. So I just have to live with it now. But it sucks. And I guess it’s my fault, I should have been clearer at the outset.

AD: I know you like Tarkovsky’s Stalker a lot, I’ve seen you quote it a bunch of times. At the same time you hate what you call the Mickey Mouse Industrial Complex, and Coca-Cola. I wonder though, wouldn’t it be nice if Tarkovsky had a theme park at Disneyland in Orlando, Florida? That’s kind of a way of describing your own work, I think.

John Maus: (laughs) There you go. That would be The Stalker Ride, where you go into The Zone and you encounter The Other. That would be… I don’t know what that would be. The robots, the Chuck E. Cheese robots, all just start shooting black rope, or something like that.
