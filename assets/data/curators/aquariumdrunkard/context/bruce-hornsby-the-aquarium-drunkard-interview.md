## Body

Near the end of his latest Indigo Park, Bruce Hornsby sings something of a conclusion—or perhaps a belated introduction: “I’ve been seeking magical thinking/I think I detect a trend/This could be the start of something/Or this could be an end.”

The lyric isn’t cited to suggest the 71-year-old songwriter is going anywhere. If anything, Indigo Park speaks to the hot streak Hornsby’s been on since 2019’s Absolute Zero, when he began mining his cues crafted for various Spike Lee films (the two are longstanding collaborators) to craft full songs. To his surprise, these late period albums caught the attention of fans of adventurous music, his reputation bolstered by artists like Bon Iver, Vampire Weekend, Haim, and others citing Hornsby as an elder statesman.

Recorded with co-producers Tony Berg and Will Maclellan at Sound City Studios in Los Angeles and The Large Clothed-Eared Collider in Williamsburg, Virginia, Indigo Park demonstrates just how unexpectedly Hornsby operates. From the chiming folk rock of the title track to the Bonnie Raitt-assisted art pop of “Ecstatic” to “Might As Well Be Me, Florinda,” a woozy duet with the late Bob Weir aided by the sparking guitar of Blake Mills and Pino Palladino’s liquid metal bass, the album showcases Hornsby’s vast, ahem, range. At once funny, poignant, and audacious, it’s a gem. Aquarium Drunkard caught up with him to discuss the album, basketball chants, his work with The Grateful Dead, literary fiction, and much more. | j woodbury

Aquarium Drunkard: You’ve been super prolific as of late. You had records out in 2019, ‘20, ‘22, and now ’26.

Bruce Hornsby: We also made another record that hasn’t been released.

AD: That’s so much generative work. Many songwriters would love for the inspiration bug to drop in as much as it seems to on you. But with this album, I read in your notes, you almost resisted it?

Bruce Hornsby: That’s exactly right. It’s been a fecund period, such a fertile time. It’s very simple to me. Affirmation is inspiration. Absolute Zero got so much attention—positive, sometimes rapturous attention—domestically and throughout Europe. When you’re getting the collective pat on the back, it’s inspiring and makes you feel like maybe you’re on the right track. These [albums were based on] Spike Lee cues. [I was] turning these Spike cues I’d written into songs.

But then, fast forwarding up to this time period: for the last couple of years, I was really trying to not write. I was so fried with the process. Not just writing songs, but the recording and producing of a record too. Once the COVID-era seemed to lift, I had all this positive feedback that made me want to keep doing it. I’m always looking around for new inspiration from disparate areas: from the most basic folk song tradition to the most avant-garde modern classical music. I was constantly inspired, also, by the fact that I had to write all this music for Spike. Every time I see his number come up, or his name comes up on my phone, I go, “Oh boy, if I answer this, it means the next two months. For the next two months, I have to write 40 to 50 pieces.” 

It was beautiful, but it really fried me too. I was really burnt out and I needed a break. That was going along fine, until this song idea popped into my head. I tried to hold it at bay. I tried to give it the Heisman, but it wouldn’t let up. And finally, after six months of giving it the stiff arm, I finally said, ”Okay, I’ll deal with it, I’ll deal with you.” I was tired of waking up in the middle of the night with this thing in my head. 

So that led me to write this song “Indigo Park.” That was the catalyst, because I took the deep dive and wrote this song and I recorded it. You could call half this record my “Bruce McGuinn” record, because I’m using the Rickenbacker 12-string. I wrote and recorded “Indigo Park” with just piano, ‘Rick 12, and vocals. I was getting chills the whole time I was doing it, and that’s when you know you might be onto something. You can’t force that. If that’s happening, that’s maybe a good sign. I started playing it around my coterie of like-minded geeks in town, who I think are very literate musically and knowledgeable, and they had the same reaction. They just would look at me and go, “Whoa. This is something special.” It made me say, “Okay, now I gotta write nine more, yes.”

AD: This record features some familiar names, people you’ve collaborated with in the past, including Bob Weir, Robert Hunter, and Bonnie Raitt. But you’ve also got people you’ve never worked with, like Pino Palladino.

Bruce Hornsby: Yeah, I’d worked with Blake Mills, but not Pino, and not Chris Dave. Ezra Koenig and I did a duet on my 2022 record, ‘Flicted. So Ezra and I had had history, but just that one song. I just called him up: “Hey, you want to sing on my record?” He said, “Yeah,” and it was as simple as that.

Indigo Park by Bruce Hornsby

AD: It’s cool how you interject that new blood; but even when you’re working with people you’ve worked with before, it’s totally different. Your song with Bonnie Raitt, “Ecstatic” couldn’t sound any more different than “I Can’t Make You Love Me.” It seems like being surprised is key for you staying invested in the music.

Bruce Hornsby: I’m always looking for new areas, new ideas, new subject matter on a lyrical level. I’m always pushing it. That’s why you’ve seen me write about cryogenics and echolocation.

AD: It almost turns into sci-fi sometimes. 

Bruce Hornsby: It’s just whatever inspires me. I get so much from the reading that I do. I’m a literary fiction devotee: Don DeLillo, Toni Morrison, David Foster Wallace. So many people. I love the writer Nana Kwame Adjei-Brenyah. He wrote Chain-Gang All-Stars a few years ago. Fantastic. He has this short story collection called Friday Black, and a lot of  the stories took place in the “mall” world. I wrote a song straight out of one of those stories called “The Rat King,” referring to mallrats, and that came out as a key player on the 2020 record Non- Secure Connection. I could go on and name very specific references or examples of all this. 

But “Ecstatic,” that it came from something else. A lot of this record came from just my experiences. I guess you could call this my aging record, because a whole lot of it deals with where I am now in my life at age freaking 71. 

My son Keith Hornsby is my oldest son—by one minute, beating his twin brother Russell, who was a great runner at University of Oregon, a real fast-ass white boy. Keith is also a fast-ass white boy, but he’s a serious baller. He played at the highest level of LSU basketball, where he was the only white dude on the team. So we’re the only white parents in the stands, and we’re hanging out with the beautiful fellow parents who had had these amazing chants: ”You fouled, you did it/Raise your hand admit it,” or, “That’s right/You walked/You traveled and got caught.” 

They were so clever and fun, so rhythmic, and so I always said back then, “I hope one day I can find a way to put those into a song.” I had done that before with Robbie Robertson; we wrote a song for his second solo record called “Go Back to Your Woods.” Now, that was a chant from my old hooping days in high school, when we’d go down into the same hood of Hampton, Newport News, Allen Iverson, and Michael Vick country, where they thought we were the country=ass bumpkins. They were so urbane and they would chant, derisively at us, “Go back/Go back/Go back to your woods.” That’s silly. So I’d already mined that area once with the great one, and I went back to it, I finally got around to writing that song, and got my big sister in music, Bonnie Raitt to come in with me on it. 

AD: There’s a reflective quality to this album. Nostalgia is tricky. In part, nostalgia is how we remember the past and keep it alive. But, without also looking to the present and the future, it starts to get toxic. We can see how that backward-looking only nostalgia is destroying us.

Bruce Hornsby: I’ve never been one to look back, certainly not musically. Some of my old fans wish I could make those same early records again. That kind of fan is not so into this new record. I mean, you can’t paint it with one color: some of my old fans are mad for [new material], but a lot of them are mostly around for a nostalgic night out if they come to your concert. Let’s face it, most people who go to concerts are not necessarily the most adventurous listeners. They’re there because they like more than a few songs that you’ve made. I get it. They want to hear those songs with a faithful replication, but I’m not very good at that either. 

But yeah, looking back. The song “Memory Palace” is about that. Someone at my station, in my stage of life, is looking at retaining memory in the face of so much information and cognitive impairment. In that song, I talk about reciting prime numbers and Fibonacci numbers. And in terms of looking at the future, there’s “Sliver of Time”, which is basically a song about…I guess it’s my eschatological song? It’s an end of times rumination about the end of our end of life as we know it. That’s certainly not nostalgic; that’s looking ahead using the language of physics. 

AD: How do you listen to music these days? Are you streaming music? Do you hang on to physical media?

Bruce Hornsby: I’m a physical guy. I’ve got CDs and vinyl albums. I’m not a streamer at all. I’m not on Pandora or Spotify. Those are actually the only two streaming services I’m aware of. I’m mostly a reader. I’ll read Pitchfork. It illuminates a world of music that’s not mainstream, for the most part. They’ll write about the pop stars too, but, but a lot of what they shed a light on is semi-adventurous music. I wish the indie world was a little more harmonically adventurous. It’s actually very conservative, like most popular music is. I wish they’d throw in some dissonance, throw some chromaticism into the mix. You know, push the envelope in that way. But there are a lot of other areas where the indie world is very adventurous. But also, I’m a YouTube guy! I’m mining YouTube for modern classical music, for instance, I’ve found some great performances of the Ligeti etudes that I’m a deep fan of.

AD: Do you still tend to think of your records in terms of the LP format? I mean, this one, I don’t know if it’s necessarily a concept album, but there is a narrative flow.

Bruce Hornsby: For the most part, this is a concept record. I don’t need to make a concept record, but I’m not against it either. [Laughs] I like the idea. If there’s a common thread, I’m happy about that, because, again, I’m a book guy. 

AD: Indigo Park doesn’t sound like you’re gunning for the radio. And generally, the radio is not going to play most artists’ new material. Do you think to yourself, “Who gives a shit? I’m going to do what I want to do?” Does it embolden that tendency for you?

Bruce Hornsby: Well, no. Here’s why: on my first album, some songs were big hits. But a song about racism with two improvised piano solos? That’s not following the [hit song] formula. A bluegrass-y song about rain that sounds like a mandolin with David Mansfield on fiddle and mandolin and I’m playing accordion? That’s also not the formula. Here’s a song about a girl who gets knocked up by some country boy set to the sound of a guy playing McCoy Tyner chordal harmony over a rock beat? That’s “Valley Road”, you know? So I got away with that craziness a few times, but clearly I was not trying to do that. I was trying to write a good song. That’s always the aim. I had great models: Bob Dylan, Robbie Robertson, and Neil Young. But I was never trying to have hits. A BBC Radio One a guy started playing “The Way It Is”, and [the audience] just reacted. It was a great fluke, that record.

AD: The Way It Is celebrates its 40th anniversary this year.

Bruce Hornsby: I find it unlistable. I have a hard time with it. I’m not really a fan of that singer.

AD: I’m sure you also hold space for this notion that a lot of people have great memories associated with those songs and love that music.

Bruce Hornsby: I really like the song; I do. I’m proud of the fact that all that piano playing was allowed. [Laughs] I love Tupac’s version and all the other hip-hop versions. I love Polo G’s version from 2021. Don’t get me wrong. I feel very fortunate. I feel so lucky that that was the song that kind of brought me into the light.

I just try to move myself. I’m always in search of the chills, goosebumps. Sometimes I’m just looking to make myself laugh. I don’t really like repeating myself. Now that said, the last song on this new record, “Take a Light Strain,” is an attempt [at what] I would consider to be an old time BH song, something that’s very traditional. White note, diatonic piano music. I wrote that song inspired by a phrase that my old country father used to say when everyone was getting stressed out. He’d go, “Hey, take a light strain.”

Indigo Park by Bruce Hornsby

That’s an example of a pointed decision to do something completely retro from my own world. There’s a certain harmonic content to what I play. People say to me, “We can always tell it’s you playing. Why is that? How would you describe your style?” So I had to come up with a facile description: Bill Evans meets the hymnal. Bill Evans, with his gorgeous chord voicings, basically influenced by the great French impressionist composer Ravel Maurice. I love Debussy, but mostly I’m a Ravel guy, as was Bill Evans. So I deeply grokked all the Bill Evans things when I was coming along, but I also loved a certain harmonic movement from the hymnal, from church music. And so that combination is what I use. I can always give myself chills if I play that way.

AD: There’s a spiritual quality to your sounds. You grew up in the Christian Science church, right?

Bruce: Christian Science, exactly. We didn’t have our own hymnal, like, say the shakers had with the shaped note singing and all that stuff. Our hymnal was very traditional.

Indigo Park by Bruce Hornsby & Bob Weir

AD:  Your history with the Grateful Dead is evidenced on this album too, featuring collaborations with the late Bob Weir and Robert Hunter. You actually have done quite a few songs with Hunter, right?

Bruce Hornsby: We wrote five in total before he passed, starting with “Cyclone”, which may be the best of them all. “Tropical Cashmere Sweater,” is a good one too. That’s from our dulcimer record. There’s a song from Absolute Zero called “Take You There”. It’s a very powerful song. And then these two, yeah, which are probably the wackiest of the bunch. I mean, save the wackest for last. 

AD: After Bob Weir passed earlier this year, Lee Ranaldo of Sonic Youth wrote something for his Substack, about how the music of The Grateful Dead is going to live on. It’s a vernacular now; there’s a community that will continue playing this music and take it forward, even as the principles have passed on. I wanted to ask you to reflect on the Dead as somebody who has contributed to that body of song.

Bruce Hornsby: I attribute it to the greatness of their songwriting. The casual fan thinks about the long jams and all that, and that’s all. What sets them apart, to me, from everyone else in that world? Not that there aren’t very good songwriters and other bands and very, very good songs. Phish has loads of them, for instance, for instance. I like Rick Mitarotonda from Goose. I think he’s a very good songwriter.

But the Grateful Dead corpus is singular in that way. The 50 greatest Grateful Dead songs may line up with anybody’s 50 greatest, whether it’s the Stones or The Beatles or Dylan, and that’s saying a lot. I know people will go, ”Oh, no way”. But that’s just how I feel on an emotional level. Because all I know is, when I used to play with them—talk about the chills. I get chills just thinking about playing “Wharf Rat” for instance, right? That’s just a deep modal feeling, like the oldest folk songs. I think that those songs are the hymns of the Deadheads’ lives.

Talk about the word “spiritual.” I think that music is spiritual for the people who love it and that’s a whole lot of people. So you’re talking about songs like “Black Muddy River,” “Brokedown Palace,” “Row Jimmy,” “Standing on the Moon, “Jack Straw,” “Mississippi Half-Step Uptown Toodeloo,” you could just keep naming them.

That’s why I think what you’re saying is absolutely correct, because those songs are for the ages. A lot of them sound like they could have been written 100 years ago and that, and that’s the one of the highest compliments I could pay, because I’m an old time folkie. The first song I ever learned, at the age of ten, was “Like a Rolling Stone.” I played it so much because I loved it so much—I could phrase along with every line of the record. At ten, I was really a Dylan-phile. Later, I got into The Dead because of my older brother. I played in his cover bands. Grateful Dead cover bands abound and for good reason.

AD: Do you have an opinion on whether or not Dead and Company should keep doing shows? 

Bruce Hornsby: I don’t. They should do whatever they want to do. If they do it, I think they’ll have a great following. They created their own and it seemed to grow. I think Dead and Co. drew more people than the Dead ever did, and the Dead was playing stadiums. 

I’m a little uninformed, to be honest. I’m kind of spouting off from having half a clue but that’s my impression. My impression is that they should do whatever the hell they want to do, because they’re all great. Will we miss the original guys? Yeah, but look, you’ve got Oteil Burbridge and Jeff Chimenti. Not to mention John [Mayer]. And of course, if somebody leaves, there are 1,001 young punks who are standing in line to replace every chair, at every instrument. So, yeah, they could go from now until rapture. And they just may.
