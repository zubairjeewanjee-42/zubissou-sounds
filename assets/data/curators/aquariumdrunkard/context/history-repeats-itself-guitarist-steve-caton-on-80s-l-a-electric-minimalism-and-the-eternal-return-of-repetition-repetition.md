# History Repeats Itself: Guitarist Steve Caton on ‘80s L.A., Electric Minimalism and the Eternal Return of Repetition Repetition

## Body

Steve Caton has led one of those unbelievably charmed lives that could only have happened in the ecosystem of late-20th-century California. He came from a musical family – his father, Roy Caton, played trumpet with the legendary session musicians known as the Wrecking Crew, appearing on such albums as Forever Changes, Pet Sounds and The Birds, the Bees & the Monkees, and his home was constantly filled with musicians working things out and arrangers writing charts. He went to elementary school with a future Toto guitarist (and played music with him in his parents’ garage). He took a couple years out of school to pursue semi-professional surfing. (He still goes on long surfing trips regularly.) Unsurprisingly, this upbringing bloomed into an eclectic and rewarding career – Caton played guitar for Tori Amos on several albums and concert tours, and ‘80s pop-metal titans Ratt recorded a song he wrote, which was included on the Point Break soundtrack. He’s now a luthier, making custom guitars for discerning clients.

Amid all these accomplishments, his work with a minimalism-obsessed pianist in the 1980s, which resulted in three self-released cassettes under the apt name Repetition Repetition, could be a footnote at best. And, for decades, it was. “I didn’t hear anything about it for many years,” Caton told Aquarium Drunkard via phone from California. “Really, it wasn’t until Facebook came around.” After the arrival of social media, Caton would get occasional inquiries from people inquiring about the project or asking to put the music out. But it wasn’t until the Freedom to Spend label, which specializes in rescuing obscure avant-garde and experimental music, came calling that things got rolling. And now, Fit for Consequences: Original Recordings, 1984-1987, a selection of work drawn from the band’s slim but potent discography (or tapeography, really) has arrived. Like the best kind of archival collection, it showcases the adventurous work of a forgotten band – “music that has an air of the inward that sounds as personal as any lost private press record and is a document of rare and uncommon beauty,” as Ilyas Ahmed aptly put it –while shedding light on an unlikely hidden underground, in this case the ambient/electronic scene of Reagan-era L.A.

Caton found the other half of Repetiton Repetition, pianist Ruben Garcia, through the introduction of a friend, Ted Quinn (a former child actor, musician and co-founder of the Joshua Tree studio that hosted The Desert Sessions), who thought Garcia’s intense interest in the minimalism of Philip Glass, Steve Reich Terry Riley, et al would mesh well with Caton’s more diverse outlook. “When Ruben and I met, I was very, very familiar with the world [Garcia] was living in,” Caton says. “I had a bunch of friends that were CalArts students … this was in the late-70s to early ‘80s” Caton recalls, referring to the Santa Clarita school whose faculty included Ravi Shankar and Morton Subotnik. “This was how I could just walk into [Garcia’s} place and I had a feel for it, an appreciation for that style of music.”

Garcia’s situation couldn’t have been more different than Caton’s affluent-bohemian background. “He lived with a woman and her two kids — he referred to her as his stepmom, but it wasn’t a legal thing. He had a little back room, and we would play in there,” Caton remembers. Whatever their lifestyle differences, the musical chemistry between them can still be heard four decades later. On the surface, Repetition Repetition’s music owes much to the mid-70s work of Brian Eno and Robert Fripp, particularly Evening Star. But whereas Eno mostly worked as an idea man and sound designer in those collaborations, Garcia was very much a player, crafting limpid yet sturdy ostinatos and rolling chordal rhythms. Caton, for his part, mostly avoids Frippean contortions, contributing carefully placed accents and constructing astute harmonic layers. Later, as the group shifted their approach from playing and recording together in a single room to composing separately (“Ruben would fill a tape and bring it to me, and I’d add my stuff,” Caton says), the material becomes denser and more interlocked, as on the 15-minute epic “The Machinist.”

Even the more programmatic material possesses the immersive sense of space that was common in New Age music of the time and has plenty of ambient’s cloudy drift. But it also displays a palpable sense of motion. It never veers into the austere or theoretical, as so much of the more academic compositions that inspired it often did. This music isn’t gathering like a mist or relentlessly interrogating a concept; it’s intuitive and on the move. What results is something as close to classic Tangerine Dream as Einstein on the Beach (the original vinyl box set of which Caton bought for himself and Garcia). The nearest comparison might be to Manuel Gottsching’s monumental E2-E4 from 1981, but looser and more stubbornly introspective. The reverb-heavy keyboard-driven title track of Lakeland, meanwhile, sounds like Mogwai covering Satie.

Repetition Repetition’s fusion of ‘70s avant-garde, ‘80s chill and forward-thinking modality must not have been an easy sell. Garcia self-released the duo’s three albums – the self-titled debut in 1985 and Lakeland and The Machinist, both from ’87, on cassette. “It was in the hundreds, not the thousands,” Caton says of each album’s run. “Maybe 200 copies. … I don’t know that they were in stores.” Garcia sent cassettes to radio stations and magazines and handed them out to musicians and other scene insiders. Fittingly, what few tapes turn up for sale now go for more than $100. “I wish I would’ve kept a box of them,” Caton says with a slight chuckle.

Garcia’s efforts did net the group some fans, including Brent Wilcox and Dean Suzuki, DJs at KCRW, a community college station out of Santa Monica and one of the charter members of NPR, and Barry Craig, better known as A Produce, the producer and composer who included Repetition Repetition songs on two compilations on his Trance Port Tapes label. Their most prominent supporter arrived when, after some persistent epistolary pestering by Garcia, pioneering pianist and Eno collaborator Harold Budd agreed to meet with them, which led to a long jam session at Garcia’s house that, as far as Caton knows, went lamentably unrecorded. Two tracks featuring Budd on electronic “treatments” (shades of Eno’s work with Budd) were later recorded at a studio and appear on Repetition Repetition’s debut album. Curiously, neither appears on the new compilation, which takes its name from one of these songs, though some Budd material is included on a very limited supplemental cassette that accompanies the comp.

The Budd association also resulted in another tantalizing almost-connection. “When [Budd] got married, we went to his reception,” Caton says. “And Jon Hassell was there.” As Caton remembers it, the boundary-breaking trumpeter approached Garcia and Caton and said, “Harold told me I should make a record with you, and when Harold tells me to do things, I usually do it.” Sadly, Hassell did not follow through. “It never happened,” Caton says wistfully. “But wouldn’t it have been incredible?”

Instead, Repetition Repetition more or less wound down. “The project never officially ended,” Caton says. “We were just kind of doing our own things.” A former backup singer in one of Caton’s bands named Tori Amos hit the big time, and Caton went with her for most of the ‘90s, while also writing music for Hollywood. Garcia also struck paydirt, in a way, winning the California state lottery and moving out of state. Throughout the ‘90s he released a series of meditative solo piano albums (some of which featured Caton on a track or two), on what appears to have been his own label, Close Tolerance Music, but otherwise kept a low profile. He passed away in 2013. “It almost makes me cry,” Caton says. I really wish that he’d been here to see this happening. He would have been over the moon.” But just as the power of his and Garcia’s music was kept alive by a small cult following for years before finally being unearthed for a new, wider audience now ready for it, Caton can hear echoes of the Repetition Repetition sound throughout his career. Listening to the live disc of Amos’ 1999 double album To Venus and Back, he noticed “how much of this thing that Ruben and I did is in those live performances. … I was surprised at how far out we took it. If you hear the stuff with Ruben and you hear the stuff with Tori, it’ll be familiar.” | r jackson
