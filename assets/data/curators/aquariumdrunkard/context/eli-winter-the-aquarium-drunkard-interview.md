## Body

Appearing here at Aquarium Drunkard in 2022 for a Lagniappe Session, Chicago guitarist Eli Winter described one of his cover selections as “Arabian Nightingale” as “arresting, cool, and strange.” The three words come to mind regarding his latest LP, A Trick of the Light. Another full-band outing following his self-titled 20202 LP, the recording drifts even deeper into jazz rock territory, pairing Winter’s snarling electric guitar lines with drifting pedal steel and sax. That first Lagniappe Session found him covering Don Cherry and Ed Blackwell’s “Arabian Nightingale, from 1982 ECM album El Corazón. The new record finds him returning to the composition, expanding it out into a longform tangle of intertwined melodies and a locked in rhythm section of upright bass and shuffling drums. On paper, it might defy traditional logic to open your album with a nearly 17-minute epic, but it doesn’t just work—it soars—kicking off a set of Winter originals and a dazzling cover of Carla Bley’s “Ida Lupino,” which dials back the fervor in favor of a languid tropical feel. Aquarium Drunkard caught up with Winter to discuss the record’s genesis and what inspired him to spoof Hot Ones in a music video. | j woodbury

Aquarium Drunkard: A Trick of the Light is a great record, one of my favorites of the year. It feels like a high-water maker. 

Eli Winter: Thank you so much. I mean, honestly, I kind of feel that way too. Hope it’s not insane to say.

AD: It’s not an insane thing to say. Does the fact that it’s a band record with a dedicated group of collaborators make it easier for you to praise it? It’s not just you on the record, does that give you the freedom to hear it more warmly?

Eli Winter: I think a little bit, yeah. I think part of it too is just kind of coming into my own as a bandleader, especially as somebody who plays with bandmates who are a decade or two decades older than me. Sometimes I still feel like just a boy. But also, the songs are just scaffolding. I tour with Sam [Wagster, pedal steel] and Tyler [Damon, drums] in the trio, and that influenced it to, in terms of really building everything around the songs I’m bringing. 

AD: Could you tell me a little bit about the dog on the cover? Who is this dog?

Eli Winter: I have no idea. I really wish I did. That’s a photo by a guy named Steven Perlin. He also took the photo for a record that came out a few years ago called Anticipation [with Cameron Knowler]. I thought that this photo was going to be used for a different record that’s probably never going to come out, and so I asked him if I could use it for this one. 

The visual element is something I struggle with a lot of the time but generally, in the past, I’ve known when I’ve seen a certain image—that that’s the one for a given release. That picture certainly feels like it encapsulates the record. You can judge the book by its cover, so to speak. It was really just a lucky break. 

AD: So with this record, in addition to a fleshed out full band, you’re really approaching jazz territory. You’re covering Don Cherry and Carla Bley. I’m sure your albums of have been lumped into solo guitar, American, Primitive, maybe even just “Americana.” 

Eli Winter: Definitely. 

AD: What has embracing a jazz-style approach been like for you?

Eli Winter: I don’t know if it’s so much about embracing that approach as much as taking an interest in that music. And it’s always more a means to an end as far as a narrative goes, I suppose. Given the nature of my music, which is instrumental, the narrative is almost always going to be emotional at its core, at least if it’s not a literal academic exercise. So it’s less thinking of it in terms of how does I end this sentence; I’m looking for the start of the sentence. It’s about figuring how the songs can be vehicles for narrative or emotion as effectively as we can. It’s about finding out what the music wants. That might sound loosey goosey, but that’s always the most important thing.

AD: Maybe that sounds loosey goosey to some folks, but not me. That idea of what the song wants is fascinating to me. Creating music starts with listening—it’s a co-creative process, you as the songwriter and you as the song’s actual first listener. You originally covered “Arabian Nightingale” for Aquarium Drunkard’s Lagniappe Sessions. What inspired you to return to that one? 

Eli Winter: It felt pretty organic, but I’m not sure if I remember why. I know that I wanted to arrange it differently from the original session. The original’s in E minor, but when we started playing it live,  we played it in A minor. I never really think of chords in terms of the positions I’m using, but the positions and chords playing out of A minor, I found those more flexible than the original arrangement I came up. This record is mostly in standard tuning, but the B-string is dropped to facilitate some of that. It’s similar to what happened with “Ida Lupino,” where my dear friend and bandmate Tyler suggested we play that song and then I fell in love with it, in much the same way I’d fallen in love with “Arabian Nightingale” several years before. That song just fit like a glove, it just happened to fit in a completely different way. 

AD: I really love the “For a Fallen Rocket” video, your Hot Ones parody. It’s surreal and strange. I feel like experimental music, music with any avant-garde element, tends toward seriousness. But it’s nice to see some humor. Are you a Hot Ones viewer generally?

Eli Winter: Oh, no. I only really learned about it a few months ago. I have a good friend who’s managing me, after doing more or less everything on my own for a long time, apart from the label side. But I asked him, “What if I could get on that show?” And he was like, very honestly and kind of correcting my crazy ask, “That’s not going to happen.” But he said, “What if you made a parody?” 

AD: Are you really eating spicy chicken? 

Eli Winter: Yeah. I’m lucky there’s a fried chicken place around the corner in my neighborhood, and they have an “angry hot” tier of spice, the highest on their menu. But I got the second highest tier and a mild tier. The highest tier, you have to sign a waiver before you can eat the food and you have to eat it there. And I didn’t realize that. But still, the spice and my comical inability to manage how much of the sauce I’m putting on, that’s all real, none of it is faked. 

AD: You’re making some spicy choices guitar-wise too. I almost hear some Billy Gibbons of ZZ Top feel at moments? Was that someone in your head with this record? 

Eli Winter: I don’t think so but it’s funny you mentioned Billy Gibbons though, because there was one show where we played a song of mine and then went straight into a totally fucked up cover of “Sharp Dressed Man,” which is in the same tuning as the other song. It was a clear choice to do once and…probably never again. [Laughs] 

AD: Speaking of guys who approach things like jazz while not being a traditional jazz guy, Mike Watt is on this record. What was playing with him on the title track like? Had you already met him? 

Eli Winter: Yeah, we met a few times previously and recorded a couple times for totally different situations. It’s funny because he is actually not playing what it might sound like on the record. He’s credited as “lead bass” because that’s what what the file he sent me was labeled. But it was actually for a different song. I just didn’t know what to do with it. So I processed it heavily and included on that song.

AD: Is it that fuzzed out sound? 

Eli Winter: There are two single note drones. Watt is the lower one, and David Grubbs is the higher one. I chopped and screwed it. Well, I shouldn’t put it that way as that’s a little too specific, but as a Houston dude I can’t help it. 

AD: I want to jump back to your Lagniappe Session. You also covered Karen Dalton and Judee Sill, two of my absolute favorite artists. Can I ask you to reflect on Judee’s songs? What draws you to her? 

Eli Winter: I mean, it’s music that makes a nonbeliever feel like they could believe in God, at least if I’m any indication. One thing I connect with, on the  instrumental level, is how readily she translates parts for the same song between guitar and piano. That’s part of the challenge I wanted to take on when I arranged “Dreams Come True” the session. 

I can speak to some of the experiences I’ve had listening to her music. The first time I listened to her, it’s about as excited as I’ve ever been listening to music. After hearing her, I would lay awake in bed for hours just thinking about her music worked, just letting it run through my head, just trying to understand it, trying to think my way to understanding. When I listen to “The Kiss,” I mean, I just cry thinking about that song. I was going through a good amount of pressure internally when I was discovering her music, and when that happened, it was such a gift, to have a serendipitous discovery of something that alleviates that pressure. It’s just a specific example of one artist’s music serving the same function as so much other music has for so many people.

AD: This record has moments that do that for me. I love when it happens for us as listeners. It’s like a bolt of lightning. 

Eli Winter: Yeah, absolutely.
