## Body

Lagniappe (la ·gniappe) noun ‘lan-ˌyap,’ — 1. An extra or unexpected gift or benefit. 2. Something given or obtained as a gratuity or bonus.

On their 2024 album Last Circle in a Slowdown, Ohio’s Big Bend took concert recordings and reconfigured them, bending that live energy into weird, spacious and intricate constructions. Leader Nathan Phillips does something similar for his project’s first Lagniappe Session, turning these covers into loose, free and focused interpretations of the source material. Phillips has a way with intimate abstraction, often knitting his songs together using little more than empty space, an ambiguous groove, a little friction and a few repeated notes. Slowdown bore the light but pervasive imprint of Talk Talk, especially Mark Hollis’ tentative yet determined phrasing and breathy, plein air timbre. Phillips’ first two Lagniappe selections tend toward a somewhat more traditional folk-rock direction (both involving guys named Alasdair), while his final cut pays homage to one of America’s greatest and most eccentric street performers/composers. Big Bend unravels and respools each song, changing nearly everything but holding on to the elusive spirit that gives it life. Phillips on his selections, below. 

Big Bend :: More Than Ever (The Clientele)

My first encounter with The Clientele was only in the last year. The era they emerged from was unclear to me due to their often retro aesthetic, so I was surprised to find they were formed and hitting their stride in the 90s/00s. In my mind they were sharing a stage with The Zombies or Sonny & Cher – a fun fiction while it lasted. But this song has been a recent mainstay for me. I dig the wistful and yearning sound, although I don’t often feel like I can give myself over to it. It’s a pleasure to participate in the love song tradition with this one.  

Big Bend :: There Will Come Soft Rains (Sara Teasdale)

A friend turned me on to Alasdair Roberts’ rendition of this Sara Teasdale poem and I was totally mesmerized. The poem has been set to choral arrangements by composers throughout the 20th century and inspired Ray Bradbury’s short story of the same name. Alasdair is known for putting his spin on old folk songs, blurring the line between his own contemporary songwriting and traditional tunes. To contribute to the blurring, I incorporated a related Teasdale poem, Spring in War-time, to the mid section. These poems find solace in the persistence of nature in spite of human transgressions.

The instrumentation came from a session in Youngstown, OH at legendary polka studio, Peppermint Productions. It includes performances by Anthony LaMarca, Dean Anshutz and Eli Winter, with Gary Rhamy at the board. Best studio in Ohio, let it be known! 

Big Bend :: Do Your Thing (Moondog)

I find the wisdom coming from viking-garbed street performer, composer and poet, Moondog, undeniable. This song was written in his later career while living in Germany in the 1970s, after decades performing between 52nd and 55th Ave in Manhattan. It’s an affirmational song of advice, clear eyed and straightforward. I return to it time and again.

While on tour in 2024, the band got to open for Wendy Eisenberg in Chicago and earlier in the day stopped over at Electrical Audio. It was the highlight of the tour; gearing up for the big gig while looking out at my friends, jammin’ at the storied studio. That improvisation led to this rendition of Do Your Thing with an alternate melody and progression.. “be fancy-free to call the tune you sing.” We shall! Shout out to Jordan Perry, Maggie Cox, Sam Wagster and Troy Kunkler.
