## Body

Last year, I found myself wondering where we would be if heads in the early nineties had gotten into Gábor Szabó instead of John Fahey. Obviously, it never would have happened. Folks at the end of history were looking for rootedness and authenticity, and Blind Joe Death unveiled a strange and haunted American past yawning open beneath our feet like a grave. Just three years Fahey’s senior, Szabó, by contrast, was a mod emblem of jet-setting grooviness. A prodigious talent on guitar, he applied his craft to gypsy jazz and lightweight psychedelia, bossa nova and pop raga. At the turn of the millennium, that kind of hip, beaded cosmopolitanism was a punchline. But now, I am not so sure. Guitar soli primitivism has been running on fumes for a good while now; and suddenly there are flutes and vibes and Fender Rhodes everywhere. Elegant funkiness is on the rise again. And they’re making jazz nowadays (or something like jazz) with amplified horns and Danelectro sitar solos and banks of vintage synthesizers, all redolent a lost era of mid-century sonic excess. Maybe we’re finally sick to death of austerity.

I called it pulp jazz a couple of times, not because it was cheap or disposable, but because it was so immediately gratifying. It draws on long-traduced, sometimes crassly commercial, musical forms—jazz-funk, exotica, new age, sci-fi schlock, lounge music and library—and channels it all into deeply funky, low-key psychedelic groove music. More than that, like the best pulp, it somehow comes out sexy as hell, slinky and dangerous. Aquarium Drunkard has been here for it. The world could stand to be a shade groovier. And when we were asked for a mixtape of the primo stuff, we were more than happy to oblige. It’s what we do.

Let’s hope this fresh wave of fusion doesn’t reignite the jazz wars of old. But we’re down to fight if it comes to that. | b sirota
