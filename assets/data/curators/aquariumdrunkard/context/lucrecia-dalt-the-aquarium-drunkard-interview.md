## Body

Lucrecia Dalt has spent the better part of the last decade crafting some of the most quietly ecstatic sounds in experimental music. The new album by the Colombian-born, Berlin-spun, US-based artist finds her the most content with her own creative process, weaving together the intimate and the vast, the conceptual and the personal, the intellectual and the sensual, with imperative freedom. Recorded in the high desert of New Mexico, A Danger to Ourselves breathes with the expansiveness of the surrounding landscape and her own avant-garde influences while remaining tethered to pop song forms and to the self-centrifugal experiences of love, eroticism, romance, and loss.

In conversation, Dalt expanded on compositional methods, the limits of language and discovery, the construction of inner topologies and soundscapes, and the quasi-mystical navigation between abstraction and biography, from invoking Bataille’s headless figures to finding the deconstructed rancheras, dembows, and boleros hidden within carefully-positioned layers of sound design. Dalt follows the coherence of curiosity wherever it may lead, whether towards the ASMR-like intimacy of her close-mic’d vocals, the boundary-stretching collaborations with David Sylvian, Camille Madoki and Juana Molina, or the blissful cinematic arrangements that have made her an increasingly sought-after film composer. What appears from this interview, then, is a portrait of an artist who has found power in trusting that her organic expressions contain productive dichotomies, like the sacred wound of desire, which opens as it heals and heals as it opens. | r moraes

Aquarium Drunkard: I first wanted to ask you about changes to your life since the last record, in 2022, because that one was kind of explosively well-received. I mean, your work is usually well-received, but this one was very, very, very well-received, and perhaps more popular as well. So how have your own expectations changed, or how did that affect the new record, if it did? You also mentioned in your notes to the album that this was the happiest you’ve ever been while recording. Why do you think that is?

Lucrecia Dalt: I could say that because I always had the desire to work on a record with more detail, attention, time… Like, really surrendering to the act of composing in a way that wasn’t pressured, that felt true. Not that it wasn’t true before, but I guess in previous albums, because of the situation, financial or whatever, I needed to be doing more stuff at the same time. For this one, I needed to find a space to heal after a very intense time. Parallel to that, I was making the album. And I feel like it was the first time I could really synchronize all my daily thoughts and motions and everything else towards a concrete project, instead of other times when I’m all over the place, doing many other things at the same time.

AD: You’re still based in Berlin, right?

Lucrecia Dalt: Not anymore, somewhere else, but the record was recorded in New Mexico. 

AD: I’m not sure if you’ve read or know Agustin Fernandez Mallo, who wrote the Nocilla Trilogy books. I’m thinking especially of the first one, Nocilla Dream, which is this series of short stories about a tree that’s growing in the desert in New Mexico. And your album kind of evokes that for me. That book. Because it’s random little small stories around this life that is growing in the middle of an emptiness. I feel like your records in general have a lot of breathing space in them. There’s, like, a silence all around, a distance between the elements… So I want to ask you how you do that from a sound design point of view, as well as the integration of that with your background. Because I know you have an engineering background and you worked in a geography or geology firm, and there’s a sort of, let’s say, topological construction of resonances in the record. I feel like it’s a very palpable thing that requires some sort of excavation from the listener. Do you feel like there’s a point to that, do you feel like this desert of Mallo makes any sense as an evocation from your record, especially as you recorded it in New Mexico? And how do you design the record so that it feels so ample?

Lucrecia Dalt: Well, that’s three very, very vast questions for vast answers, but I guess, yeah… I’ve been asked many times about the idea of the desert affecting my record. I don’t feel it in that way… All the geological nostalgia wasn’t that present in this record. But obviously you are inevitably going to be affected by the environment and context. I was composing in front of a window looking at a garden and a bright blue sky. How the clouds are shaped, the vastness of the views, and so on. This vastness has informed certain songs more than others, like “scelliformia” or “cosa rara,” I can feel the heat in them. Or even “hasta al final.” But I guess, in that sense, I would like to say this record is a rendition of trying to be as connected as possible with the feeling… and to convey from what is coming through you. In a way, it is less programmatic and conceptual than previous albums. In the past, a good premise would be to explore what I can do with vocoders and subjectivity, and see how I intertwine them. Not to say that this record is not conceptual, because I feel that is inevitable for me as an artist, even if the core center of the concept is just existence and desire and love and stuff like that. It’s just that it is definitely more centered around the experience of self, you know, and how to convey that. Being less preoccupied with references. In the lyrics, I explore themes and things that I like that cross that boundary, because it’s all just part of your thinking process, of everyday life as an artist.

AD: And I guess music is a type of thinking too. Or thinking is a type of music, I don’t know. But you do… I mean, I’m not sure if you think it’s relevant to discuss your notes to the record, or if it’s even something that you consider at all versus just a guideline for PR releases, but in the notes you do mention some references, like Bataille, Ballard, Cocteau… So there is some sort of inevitable conceptual edge to them all, I’d imagine. You also provide this little insightful story of each song and what it means to you. I like this idea, which I think comes out in the sound as well, of… You’re just doing the things that you like doing with the sound and seeing the results. Like, there’s an emptiness in there, sure, and then you start putting objects in it as you feel like they fit, right? But I was curious about those relations to the authors. It’s obviously not a textual or lyrical influence, although sometimes it is direct, like with “acephale”… But how do you tie the manifestation of those musical and personal interests of yours to those more overtly intellectual ones? They’re clearly condensed in the same kind of lifeworld, but how do you feel like those references inspire you musically?

Lucrecia Dalt: I guess this is all the random pieces of information that you end up reading or knowing about or… There are films or sounds or books that could be accompanying you in the process of making the album and inevitably they help you work poetically, to add more depths of meaning. The lyrics stemmed from texts developed in the beginning of a relationship, as ways to communicate appreciation, consciousness, love… Even frustration. “Acephale”, for example, is used as a metaphor for telling your lover, hey, you are thinking too much, and you just need to connect to your heart. And I just thought about that idea of the “acephale,” kind of like [gesticulates a decapitation], cutting the head so that the person can finally feel at ease in their own existence. It’s obviously a metaphor for that. And that beautiful Bataille image helps you realize ways to explain what you’re after, and something that you feel compelled to explore.

AD: How do you tie your interest in the Latin pop tradition with the interest in more experimental forms and textures? I realize that by now this must be sort of a cliche way of analyzing your music, that maybe you are tired of it, but you do cite the second song of the new record as a ranchera, right? Although kind of a deconstructed ranchera, I suppose. And there are also some dembow and Cuban son inflections in there as well. I mean, I was there on your DJ set, and you played some Jorge Ben and some indie flamenco and what sounded to me at some point like a non-teleological marimba, but also, like, Tindersticks and obscure, dark folk from Hungary or whatever, and then just pure droning or noise music. I guess my question is: are you more interested in collapsing those genre distinctions or in their very superposition?

Lucrecia Dalt: I don’t know, I mean, I would like to say something as concrete as putting everything together through the coherence that you find in your life. I think that after many years of making music I started to feel… I’m not a musician that goes about the idea of being genre-oriented, of doing just one thing. I like to be free, I like to explore different things. If I feel like I want to explore boleros or whatever that means, like I did in the past album, I can do it, because… How wonderful art is, in that sense, to allow you to just be free. While I’m doing “amorcito caradura” I’m not thinking, oh, I’m gonna make a ranchera. It’s just that I’m flowing with the voice and at the end it’s like, oh, I think I just made a ranchera. Even with “caes,” as we were exploring the percussion with Alex, it’s like, oh, it has almost a hint of dembow, but it’s not, because it’s not repeating the kick right after the snare, which is kind of necessary for that to be a dembow. And I like that. When I thought about a remix I thought of Nick León because he could bring that song to that world, someone who is just well-versed in that universe could do it instead of me. It’s zero important to say that the song is a dembow. It’s more important to talk about the meaning, to talk about the depth.

AD: Although when you say like, oh, this is a dembow, even if it’s kind of not, there is a sort of overcodification that’s interesting too. It’s almost like you’re opening the field of what that term could mean. So I think it’s an interesting gesture too.

Lucrecia Dalt: Even to say no, you know? People could say, what the hell are you talking about, this has nothing to do with dembow. And I’m fine with that, you know? Like, okay, yeah, it’s just how I hear it. I’m more interested in how the music is helping the message to be conveyed. It’s a very hardcore song about embracing the act of falling, whatever that means. Allowing your life to end, allowing a change, allowing surprise, allowing something else to be in your life. Maybe that proverb that says “when you decide to jump you’re already falling” is in a way the whole idea behind it. And I was exploring it through the lenses of Ana Mendieta and Evelyn McHale… Just, like, how do we conceive that braveness, or those accidents, or those happenings of life, into something that becomes something else. There is a process of transcendence there. And whatever they thought was gonna be, it ended up being just totally different. Like, in Ana Mendieta’s case, it becomes almost her own final work, as crazy as that is, and as intense…

AD: You mean her life story? Her death?

Lucrecia Dalt: How she died, yeah. Especially looking at it in relation to her silhouettes pieces, it’s inevitable not to feel chilled about that idea. So I’m talking about beauty, the act of falling as a beautiful act, as an act of letting go. That’s why I thought my dear friend Camille Mandoki was perfect for that, because she carries a depth in her voice that is so different and so fundamental. You rarely hear that depth in voices nowadays. I feel like someone like Scott Walker had it. David Sylvian obviously has it. Voices that are definitely channeling something deeper than their own selves in that moment in time.

AD: I was gonna mention those collaborations. I was interested specifically in the David Sylvian one and the Juana Molina one as well. I feel like they add… You called it a Scott Walker-like… timbre or something in the voice that you’re interested in, and there’s this kind of cinematic arrangement to them as well. Some songs in the record have this kind of ultra-romantic feel. The sequence from “hasta el final” to “divina,” for example. How does that relate to your recent ventures into film scoring, which kind of requires a bit of this flow? Or doesn’t it? Where is that coming from, this kind of romantic… Although there was a seed of that in the last record, I guess…

Lucrecia Dalt: Yeah, I mean… I feel like scoring is a parallel life. That, yes, it does inform my solo work  and it helps me expand my creative brain. Scoring means ultimately to be in the mind and the vision of someone else. And that alone helps me expand my vocabulary. In regards to composing romantic songs, I guess it just comes from the pleasure of listening to music like that. And finally feeling brave to try it. I just thought, why not? I always wanted to make a sort of ballad like “divina,” with those types of vocals, but placing it in the present. And so, in a way, if I play that song on guitar it could be a very simple song. Because of how it’s composed and structured, with just a few recognizable elements: backing vocals, drums, the bass, and effects… Something else is missing, you know? There should have been a guitar, or a more insisting piano, to make it a traditional ballad. So I like those tensions of: I’m almost there in that space but I hesitate, maybe I don’t want to be, I just want to keep it containing my own preoccupations with sound while being on songform.

AD: Yeah, it’s almost like you’re stealing or extracting a feel from those types of songs without doing those types of songs exactly.

Lucrecia Dalt: I don’t know if “extracting” is the word… I guess it’s just the… I know these songs exist, I know their effectiveness, and I could try to do something in that line with my own vocabulary. I’m thinking of Donnie Hathaway, for example.

AD: Yeah, I feel like when I talk to experimental musicians they’re usually kind of afraid of doing a simple melody. Like that’s corny or something. And you mentioned, I think, Paul McCartney in your notes to the record.

Lucrecia Dalt: The Ram record.

AD: Yeah. I think Paul is the main guy that did that, that was his thing, those little melodies, those little themes. And not being… I feel like not being afraid of doing those more conventional themes is also freeing as a musician. I think experimental artists need to do that more, you know?

Lucrecia Dalt: That’s the thing, I don’t consider myself this or that. I’m a musician. I love making music. I just love, like I said before, being able to be free. I find it so strange that someone will just stay in one corner. I mean, if they feel happy there, by all means… And if there is a reason they can continue digging and exploring, because they found a path that connects directly to their core, fantastic. But I feel like I am not that kind of person. I love exploring. I love going back and forth. I don’t see why I wouldn’t do a super abstract album in the future, because I’m fascinated with sound anyway. So I’m not trying to impose anything, just trying to be honest. And being able to convince someone to release it and support me in the process is just incredible, you know? It’s the lucky part.

AD: Yet there’s this kind of erased history of a female avant-garde, specifically in Latin America, with people like Beatriz Ferreyra, Jocy de Oliveira, Jacqueline Nova, Alicia Urreta, and a bunch of others who contributed extensively to electroacoustics, and… Do you not see yourself in the same lineage? Or aren’t you in a way reversing that tendency to obscurity a little bit? Or is there any, let’s say, Colombian or, I don’t know, any specific experimental Latin American musician that you think we should know about or that inspires you very deeply to do what you?

Lucrecia Dalt: I’ve been a big, big fan of Beatriz Ferreyra. I listen to her pieces extensively. I feel her sensibility to treating… I don’t even know how she works, samples, voices… I feel, yeah, she has allowed me to learn about detail, about working with the source of things and thinking about the environment differently, and about moving from A to B without remorse. Mabe Fratti is also one of them. I admire the way she explores what feels kind of like a song but is embedded into something a little bit more abstract and raw. Just like I love Meridian Brothers because how they are givingg continuity to a genre that I didn’t know exactly how it could be continued, perhaps because Eblis’s brain is very attuned to finding the core of things. So I don’t know.

AD: I was also gonna compare your records—and I hope you don’t take this badly—to IKEA manuals. Because I feel like IKEA manuals, when you get them, the first time you look at them, they’re very alien and strange and you don’t know where or how things go together. Like, what even is this? Where do I even start? It’s in another language. But once the object is built and you look back at the manual it’s like, this is children’s stuff. This is so easy and simple. How did I not see it? Do you feel like this is a fair metaphor on my part?

Lucrecia Dalt: I mean, it’s impossible for me to know, because I have never experienced the album as you did. I experienced the album as a seed and then as a thing that slowly developed into these pieces. But I am also convinced that you are able to listen to your own music as a thing in itself only years and years after. Because only with time I stop thinking about all the process that went into it. But I’m very curious about what you’re saying, because I do definitely think about how people are gonna make that construct and if it’s clear at all at the beginning. Is it easier to digest and see it for what it is? It depends on your context and history, I guess, and also if your first listen is dedicated or not.

AD: The one thing about this metaphor that I think is useful for you, though, is that once I get it, once you look at it and you’ve done it, you feel like there’s no way this could be easier actually. If I was gonna draw this manual myself now, having built the thing, I can’t think of a way of doing it that is easier. But it’s just that it takes the process of doing it to see how easy it is, right?

Lucrecia Dalt: You mean that happens by listening repeatedly?

AD: Yes.

Lucrecia Dalt: Interesting. I mean, I love that. Definitely. Because I do remember when I listened to the first album of Panda Bear, Person Pitch… an album that appeared also on everyone’s end of the year  favorites. And I was like, I don’t feel I have the capacity to enter this record. Until I did. It was like, okay, I’m gonna surrender to this completely. And once I “decoded it,” I found the extreme beauty and coherence that everybody was talking about. So it became a fascination for me as a composer, how to create layers of meaning, by exploring gestures and exploring sound design and textures. I feel so much pleasure in that. And I always hope that if I find myself attuned to these frequencies, perhaps someone out there will too. Maybe, as you said, it is an experience that slowly forms, or perhaps for some it’s fully formed and for some others it will never form and it will just be a random collection of sounds, and that’s okay too.

AD: I don’t feel like it’s made difficult by design or anything like that, and the process of discovering it is really pleasurable. I think that what is interesting about it is finding out the pop song within it. And I feel like, with this record, you’re morphing into more pop form songs. I don’t know if I’m right about that or if it’s just that I know more about your music as time goes by so that I can experience it as more pop forms, you can tell me if that’s the case. But I feel like in the previous records it became less and less just about the voice and the sound effects, and you started to free yourself into doing these instrumentals, and using the strings more intentionally, and, you know, a bass line here and there, maybe even a drum pattern somewhere… What do you think? I also feel like your records have this kind of ASMR quality to them, because of the close mic and the sort of crispness of the timbres, there’s this sort of very careful sculpture of the timbres that I’m interested in. But more and more you’re shaping it all into the form of just pop, or this kind of… It tends to have somewhat of a more universal appeal, I guess. And, yeah, these romantic aspirations. Is that a correct perception? Or how do you feel like your work has been changing?

Lucrecia Dalt: It’s also related to the artists that I admire and that I feel do a good job in putting all their preoccupations and worlds in one place. Juana Molina is an excellent example of that. I was listening recently to her record Halo and I was mesmerized by the world she is creating. It’s even very sci-fi at times, and I’ve never heard that from her. She’s a good example of someone that can work within song form but also be very unique in her craft. Or Beak, the band Beak, which I really, really admire. Geoff Barrow is a good example of that, from Portishead to his recent projects, every song is so uniquely crafted and thought out, and produced… And there is always one melody that is gonna stay with you. I really love that. I’d die for those little melodies that you remember throughout the day. That not only you remember them as a melody but you will remember the effect it had on you as a listener, because you know that the person behind it was very careful about how to conceive that sound, how it was placed, how it was mixed, the type of processing, and so on. I am obsessed with musicians that think of all these details. But there’s also bands that I love, like Tindersticks, that use simplicity to convey something deep and meaningful… Or, like, yesterday I was writing about Saya Gray. She did this song called “Pick Up a Flower,” and I am simply obsessed with the first twelve-second loop at the beginning. How everything is filtered together and then how the song opens up after that. 

AD: You mentioned a lot of different collaborations you’ve made and how those changed the record too. Like, the features and the people who were around you, and how Alex provided some of the ideas, the germs of the percussion. You are mentioning a lot of people that inspire you. Who would you still like to work with, that you think could modulate your music in interesting ways?

Lucrecia Dalt: In retrospect, when I heard David’s music, I thought, woah, it would be insane to one day collaborate with him… And it happened. So I would like to think that collaborations will happen organically, and stem from mutual admiration.. It’s always hard to mention people I admire because it someone feels like an imposition, but let’s see… At this point I’d like to be able to collaborate with filmmakers that I admire and that have also made me reshape the way I think about my work, like Bonello or Lanthimos.

AD: How have films reshaped the way you think of sound? Like, Lanthimos, for example.

Lucrecia Dalt: For example, with Poor Things…

AD: That soundtrack is great.

Lucrecia Dalt: That soundtrack is insane! And I really admire when a composer is able to reduce the storyline into one sound gesture, I find that with the Poor Things score, it’s incredible, as well as Mica Levi’s score for Under the Skin. I also admired how brave they mixed the scores into the films, loud, you know? And the fact that they are not there to manipulate you, they are not there as a subtle effect, they exist as an extra layer that adds meaning and contrast to the film, and I love that.

AD: More like a character.

Lucrecia Dalt: Yeah, exactly. So it’s things that kind of, yeah, take you, but also give you an opportunity to expand upon the concept of the film. Those are the things that I’m, like, if ever I could… Bonello is one of my favorite filmmakers currently. The way he thinks of rhythm, space, and to be challenged by a brain like that, it’d be phenomenal.

AD: I have one last question which is about the Spanish language. A lot of people from multiple countries tend to just end up singing in English, either because they want to appeal to some broad English speaking audience or because they feel like there’s an aesthetic potential to singing in English. Like, English is a language that’s easy to sing in, I feel like. But you still are singing Spanish. Is there any specific power to the Spanish language itself that you’re drawn to? Would you just rather sing in your mother tongue sometimes, or do you think it favors specific moods or atmospheres? Because I’m Brazilian and I feel like that happens with Portuguese very much. If I sing in Portuguese or I write in Portuguese, there is a certain fate, it tends to create a specific ambience that, you know, if I do it in English it will be very different. It’s not only about the meaning of the translation, it’s also about the affect that it inputs into the final result. How do you feel about the Spanish language as a means for singing?

Lucrecia Dalt: Language is a blessing and a curse. It’s a tool, and I love exploring nuance and possibility with the ones I can speak. But in the realm of songwriting, it is just an exercise in what comes first, and what serves the song. There are songs in English and in Spanish because I was writing in both when I was making the album. Sometimes, in the process of improvisation, there’s something that resembles Spanish words and then it naturally becomes Spanish words. Like, “aguita con sal,” I don’t know what that even means, but it just came, it just came like that, and it sounded good to me. And why not, you know? And you try to kind of think, what is this? What is it? And you find ways to give a lot of meaning in the end by letting that just be…

AD: In English, you’d never be able to do “aguita,” like another word would not fit?

Lucrecia Dalt: When it becomes one thing I’m not thinking about doing it in another language. I don’t have that impulse. I even kind of… Who knows, maybe I will do it one day, but I honestly find it funny that some artists decide to translate their album to another language. Especially when they don’t speak it. Like, why? The song exists because words scan in the song. Why change that? If one song came out in English, I just allowed it to be, and that’s it. Or songs that have two parts because the chorus felt better in English, or in Spanish. It also corresponds to the way I live my current life. My partner only speaks English, but I live in a state that is mixed, my internal voice speaks both English and Spanish. My best friends are from Spain, from Mexico, my parents and sister from Colombia… So I feel like the record is honest in that constellation.

AD: Yeah, I feel that all the time. I feel like I have also started to forget a little bit of the vocabulary I have in Portuguese because of the amount of English I speak, which is something I resent sometimes. I feel like I got dumber in Portuguese, I don’t know.

Lucrecia Dalt: It’s okay, it will come back and it will surprise you.

AD: At the same time, I think of Nabokov, who just gave up on Russian and started to write in English and became one of the best prose writers in the history of the English language. I would never be able to surrender my language like that, I don’t think, because it requires such an ability to discover a new world from scratch. And even, you know… Language has its own humor. I feel like I’m funnier in Portuguese, and in English I cannot be, because it requires a different context, and the timing is different…

Lucrecia Dalt: Yeah, I absolutely connect with what you’re saying. And I have to say that finally, in my forties, I kind of understood that it’s fine. I’m aware that I can be funnier in Spanish, that I have much more resources But I have my body to help me in other languages, and I hope that people who are attuned to your presence, they’re gonna appreciate that. I was a little bit more fearful of the mistakes, of failing, of saying something wrong in English, of sounding a little bit too naive, because I don’t have enough vocabulary or coherence to make elaborate sentences that maybe I could do in Spanish. But, hey, it’s what I have and I do my best and, you know, you try to work with what you have. I really try not to allow those things to take too much space, because they work on my fear and I don’t want my fear to win at all.
