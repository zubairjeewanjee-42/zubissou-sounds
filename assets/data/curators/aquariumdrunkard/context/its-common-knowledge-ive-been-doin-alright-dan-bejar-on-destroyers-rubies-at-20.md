## Body

Easy-listening, middle-of-the-road, simplified: these are the terms Dan Bejar used, on an afternoon in early December, to describe Destroyer’s Rubies, one of the most exquisite rock records released in the 21st century. They’re certainly not the phrases I would use to describe the album, but hell, I’m not the one who made it.

Rubies was released twenty years ago next month, the band name knit directly into the title of the record, as if its ten songs might be mistaken for the work of some other artist without explicit credit. These weren’t just anyone’s Rubies, they were Destroyer’s Rubies—as if there could be any question. The voice alone was signature enough, to say nothing of the first song’s first lines: “Duelling cyclones jackknife / They got eyes for your wife / And the blood that lives in her heart.” Who else but Bejar?

An immediate, obvious masterpiece, the album capped a remarkable decade of toil, daring, and restlessness for the man behind Destroyer. Across six increasingly impressive records, Bejar had managed to chart a crooked course from lo-fi bedroom oddball to sure-handed glam rocker to neurotic synthesizer fetishist. Along the way, he also found time to lend his talents to Canadian supergroup The New Pornographers, contributing a much-needed note of acid to indie pop classics like Electric Version and Twin Cinema. Though Bejar would continue working with the Pornos for some number of years, Rubies made it clear that his artistic destiny was his and his alone. 

A synthesis of all Destroyer had ever been up until that point, Rubies is a bounty, a record bursting at the seams with undeniable tunes. The first side is one of the strongest sequences ever put to tape, each track building on what came before, pacing further along toward an uncertain catharsis: the magisterial title track, an epic poetic vision that stands alongside “Tangled Up In Blue” and “Changing of the Guards” as one of the all-time great album openers; the rollicking “Your Blues” and the fist-pumping “European Oils,” a doubleshot of hard, edgy folk-rock; the mesmerizing “Painter In Your Pocket,” a magic trick of a track. All perfect rock songs—and all prelude, too.

Catharsis arrives, finally, with “Looters’ Follies,” the lush, drunken waltz at the album’s heart. Extraordinary phrases tumble haphazardly out of Bejar’s mouth, colliding with one another, stacking, steadily, into a pyre ready to be set ablaze. Eventually the band obliges, exploding into a thicket of guitars as Bejar snarls the song’s shockingly triumphant conclusion: “Now step out of the darkness and into the light / Yeah it’s common knowledge I’ve been doin’ alright.” I’m still not sure exactly what he’s getting at with that line, but I don’t need to be. The truth of the song is right there in my ears, burning, swooning, braying with all the gusto of a man who knows he’s at the top of his game.

Which he undoubtedly was. Rubies is not always my favorite Destroyer record—that, like favorite Dylan, changes with the day; there is too much brilliance across too many records to firmly settle on one eternal favorite. It is, however, the best Destroyer record: consistent, nuanced, equal parts enervating and energizing. The record sounds effortless, as if these songs were always there, hovering unseen, waiting to be plucked out of the air and given form by Bejar and his murderous band of Vancouverites. Even after twenty years, I would not change a second. This is as close to perfect as rock records get.

Even more remarkable is that Bejar was just getting started. Five years after Rubies, he exploded the concept of what Destroyer could be with Kaputt, another one of this century’s finest records. The next in a long series of stylistic shifts for Bejar, Kaputt’s smooth studio rock accidentally intersected with the indie rock zeitgeist circa 2011. The album became a touchstone of the era, launching Bejar to levels of (relative) stardom he had never anticipated and didn’t necessarily want. Naturally, he followed his big break with Five Spanish Songs, a brief EP that hit the brakes on Destroyer’s industry momentum by delivering exactly what its title promised. “The English language seemed spent, despicable, not easily singable,” Bejar announced in the accompanying press release. “It felt over for English. Good for business transactions, but that’s about it.”

Fortunately for us all, Bejar and the English language soon made up. For the last decade, he has turned out immaculate, adventurous Destroyer LPs with all the regularity of a production line: the bravado Poison Season, the spare, sublime ken, the icy pandemic-era bookends Have We Met and Labyrinthitis. Last year, Bejar inaugurated a whole new era with Dan’s Boogie, a tidy record that managed to reconcile the many previous iterations of Destroyer while simultaneously pointing towards undiscovered territory. 

There has never been a better time to be in the business of Dan Bejar, a statement as true today as it was twenty years ago, the day Rubies was released. As you’ll read in the following conversation, which has been edited for length and clarity, the album was a hinge point in the history of Destroyer. Things were one way before, and they were another way after—simple as. 

A disclaimer: this piece was initially envisioned as a personal retrospective of Rubies on the occasion of its 20th anniversary, supplemented by a few choice thoughts from Bejar. I shot him an interview request, he graciously accepted, and we caught up shortly after the conclusion of Destroyer’s most recent European tour. I had a few nebulous concepts going in, and I drafted a sequence of questions designed to prod the conversation in those general directions. With any luck, Dan’s thoughts would align with mine, and I would get a couple solid quotes to deploy throughout my write up. As we started speaking, it quickly became apparent that the interview wasn’t a supplement to the piece. It was the piece. 

Making great art and discussing great art are distinct skills. To the extent that a person is talented at one, they are typically untalented at the other—that’s why artists are artists and critics are critics. Bejar is the rarest of breeds: someone equally adept at both. When dealing with such a man, it’s best to simply get out of his way. Anything I might add would only be a distraction. 

Even as humble interlocutor, I’m proud to be a part of this number. | i grant

Aquarium Drunkard: What do you remember about your mindset going into Rubies? Obviously, it was a pretty big departure from Your Blues.

Dan Bejar: I remember a lot about the actual making. I lived in this big old house in an infamous neighborhood in Vancouver—it’s kind of an infamous neighborhood in Canada.

AD: What neighborhood?

Dan Bejar: It’s called the Downtown Eastside. Not a lot of houses, just a lot of street-level poverty. A crazy neighborhood. We lived there in this big old house, and I could make all the noise I wanted. Sydney [Hermant, my wife,] had a studio, and the band just practiced in a big room off the kitchen.

The record came together really easily, and it sounded really good right away. It was quiet. I’m not used to playing in quiet groups, even still. So I could just sing and float over the songs. And I knew the songs had good bones, for the most part. At least the four key songs.

AD: The four key songs?

Dan Bejar: Yeah, in my mind there are four key songs on the record.

AD: Would you mind me asking what those songs are?

Dan Bejar: Going in, I thought “A Dangerous Woman Up To a Point,” “Looter’s Follies,” “European Oils,” and “Your Blood”—those came together really easily. They summed up this kind of writing that I started doing, which I swore to never do. But then I just fell into it. Typical rock poetry: imagistic, Beatnik writing.

AD: I saw an interview with you from ten years ago or so where the interviewer was asking you about Rubies, and you specifically called out Bob Dylan and Van Morrison as influences on your writing during that era. Is that where you were looking in terms of an artistic North Star?

Dan Bejar: Yeah. I was probably exhausted from Your Blues, which was a very severe record, an arch record. And then touring it, instead of singing along to an orchestral DOT tape, it was a really loud, frenzied punk rock version of [the record]. So at the time I thought Rubies was very easy-listening. I thought it was gonna be like a mid-70s Van Morrison record—not that that’s the definition of easy-listening. Since then, the world has embraced dad rock. But at the time I was just like, “this doesn’t sound like The Rapture. This is my exit. I’m going out with this one.”

I was about 32 at the time, which in rock years is old; at least, it was then. And I was like, “I don’t know if this is working.” I’m making these strange, dark records like This Night and Your Blues—they’re so different, they don’t speak to each other. But the writing of the songs on Rubies came really fast, really easy. I seemed to just be landing them. 

There’s a lot of words. When you scan them, it doesn’t seem like it would be the easiest thing in the world to sing. But it felt like I couldn’t miss, y’know? Maybe every singer-songwriter has that phase where they think they can’t miss. That was probably the one for me. I was writing what seemed like really ambitious texts, and then somehow just slamming them into these songs—these melodious folk-rock songs—in a way that felt effortless. Which to me was a good sign.

I really enjoyed singing [the Rubies songs]. Usually, doing a Destroyer record, there’s a lot of pulling my hair out and fretting, and there was very little of that making that album.

AD: What do you credit that to?

Dan Bejar: I don’t really know. Sometimes I go in with a concept, and the concept waivers, the songs don’t speak to the concept, but you muscle through anyways, and you come out with some aberration that you hope you fall in love with. Rubies was just like, “Let’s play these songs in this room, and then let’s try and go record them the way we played them.”

I still think the best versions of those songs were in my living room off the kitchen. We never quite nailed it at JC/DC [Studios] in the same way.

AD: That’s crazy to me.

Dan Bejar: I know that for a fact.

AD: I fully believe you, but I can’t imagine even greater versions of some of those songs.

Dan Bejar: Oh no, as far as performances go, it’s all in the living room. I don’t know if we quite nailed it when we toured.

We didn’t tour that record that much. We did four shows down the West Coast, and then we did maybe twelve shows on this co-headlining tour with Magnolia [Electric Co.] The record did much better than any previous Destroyer record, and the shows did well, and we were supposed to go out and do some kind of victory lap. And instead, I just moved to Spain. All the best templates for the songs live in that house still.

That house and neighborhood were so fucked up that I think that’s why I got put on the cover of Fader magazine—aside from that someone else must have dropped out. They came to do a feature, and they found the house and the neighborhood and my life so insane that they put me on the cover with this ridiculous title saying “Rock’s Exiled King.”

AD: [Laughs] Rock’s Exiled King!

Dan Bejar: That’s what it said. Because who would choose to live like that? I must have been in some form of exile.

AD: What a world 2006 was. Feels like a long time ago.

Dan Bejar: People from New York are very impressionable.

AD: Even if you say you didn’t nail the Rubies material in the studio quite as much as you did in the room, it was still mostly the whole band playing live on a lot of these songs, right?

Dan Bejar: Yeah. I think the ones where it wasn’t so much like that, you can kind of tell. 

I did a lot of work with a studio called JC/DC over the years—“JC” is John Collins, and “DC” is David Carswell. Rubies and Poison Season are more like DC records, and then anything that’s super cooked is more like a John record. And [John] was pretty fried from working on [the New Pornographers’] Twin Cinema, so he didn’t have very much to do with Rubies at all, except for one song called “3000 Flowers.” Sonically, that song doesn’t really fit in with the rest of the record. We never really tried to even practice that song, because it seemed like the idea would be to thrash it out and make it sound fucked up and then just walk away. But it doesn’t have the same sense of space, or the breeziness, of most of the other songs on the record.

AD: If memory serves, I think the process of recording Destroyer songs now is more like start/stop, distant, trading files over email. Do I have that semi-correct?

Dan Bejar: Yeah, we haven’t gone in the studio to do it like a band does it, where you lay down bed tracks—we haven’t done that since Poison Season. So not since 2014.

AD: Why? Maybe the answer is just like, practicality, but I wonder…

Dan Bejar: No, I don’t think that’s it. I think it’s more like even though I always end up making rock records, that’s not the kind of song I’m writing. In my head, I still am not in the place where I feel like documenting the band in a room, and me singing with the band. Even though I think the current version of the band is the best band that Destroyer’s ever been. I don’t even think there’s any competition.

But [as far as going into the studio together], it’s not totally where my head is at. Even writing, it’s not what I write towards. It’s just part of the schizophrenia of Destroyer on stage versus Destroyer as a record-listening experience.

AD: When you say that’s not where your head is at—is that something you want to try to work your way back to? Or are you more come-and-go with it? Like you are where you are, and you’ll be where you’ll be in the future?

Dan Bejar: I don’t know. I’d like to write a song and then stare at it and see what it wants. I’m not against it. I’m just not listening to a lot of music that sounds like that, so that’s part of what guides me. I get a thrill out of it when I go on stage to do it. That’s a different version of myself, maybe.

I will say that Rubies was like the tail-end of me just making records with friends. Doing it any other way seemed preposterous to me. I know that’s not how all bands operate, but in the 2000s, I was in that headspace. Rubies was the climax of that. And then Trouble In Dreams was the wreckage of me still trying to do it that way, even though it didn’t work. And then Kaputt was the complete about-face.

AD: Right—Destroyer goes big, corporate, sexy. Big money.

Dan Bejar: I mean …

AD: Obviously not intentionally so! But Kaputt, y’know—it seemed like it shocked you most of all that that record was received the way it was received.

Dan Bejar: Yeah, I wasn’t in tune with the zeitgeist enough to know that people would eat that up. I knew that I was singing a lot less and people would like that, and I knew that a lot of the melodies reminded me of children’s lullabies. People are like babies, they like that. So in that sense it worked.

AD: (Laughs)

Dan Bejar: I’ve said this before, but amongst the small cult of Destroyer followers that were amassed with Streethawk and This Night and Your Blues, none of them really agreed with each other. Lots of people who liked Streethawk hated This Night, and then there’s people who liked This Night who wouldn’t give Your Blues the time of day. So a lot of the success for Rubies—and it was, as far as Destroyer World goes, a successful record—was just everybody agreeing not to disagree, for the first time ever. 

That’s why it’s not that popular of a record with the diehard, psycho Destroyer fans. They see it as middle-of-the-road, I think. It’s just more like, “Oh, everyone can agree this one’s good.” You’re not alienating any one sector of your fanbase with your psychotic Scott Walker MIDI wannabe record, or with this really dissonant, dark, fucked up This Night record. And it’s not as tuneful and full-on glammy as Streethawk, it’s not as songwritten as that. Things are starting to get simplified and spread out, and I’m starting to explore talk-singing a bit more than I ever did on Streethawk. 

There’s kind of something for everyone on [Rubies]. And I think if you’re into lyrics, then people like it. The people who are not into lyrics but still are into Destroyer, which is a strange caste of music fan…

AD: The misfits in the Destroyer fanbase.

Dan Bejar: Those are the misfits in the Destroyer world, but in the actual world they’re the majority. I don’t know… there’s lots of like “ba-ba-ba,” these chanted choruses. I started doing that a lot. There’s little things for people to hang onto. 

When the band plays songs off that record, the arrangements work really well still. You can just blast through them. They don’t need much tinkering.

AD: Yeah, I’m in San Francisco, so I caught you at the August Hall date back in September, and I remember “European Oils” and in particular “Looters’ Follies”—they were just unbelievable. It was the loudest show I feel like I’d been to in months.

Dan Bejar: We just got louder from there. I’m not sure what happened.

AD: The Rubies material is still a big part of Destroyer live shows, even the stripped-down ones. I remember I saw you do the two-piece thing at The Chapel a year or two ago, and I think you played three or four Rubies songs. What is it about that material that makes it a stalwart, a standby for Destroyer live?

Dan Bejar: I can sing all of it. Going back much farther than that, things start to get dicey for me. I’m not gonna talk down the songs that I wrote in my twenties, I think there are a lot of good ones. But it doesn’t always square with where I’m at right now. Rubies is this spot where I can just inhabit all of it. As opposed to picking some random song from Streethawk or Thief or Your Blues or This Night, where I’m into them, but there’s always little things that I find to be stumbling blocks.

AD: Stumbling blocks mentally? Or like literally your voice can’t do what it did 25 years ago?

Dan Bejar: No, not melodically. I mean, that part is true, but I would do that anyways and just let the world see my broken old voice. It’s more just stuff where words stick in my craw, y’know? Things that seem slightly awkward to me.

I’m still trying to make really unfamiliar, ungainly language seem singable. That was a big part of the Destroyer project starting in the 90s, and I lost interest in that. A lot of it is very rhetorical-sounding, it sounds like it comes out of pamphlets. And for some reason, when I gave myself over to blasting out images just to make myself feel good, which is kind of what happened in 2005, I never really looked back from that. That’s the beginning of me really finding my voice. So I can still sing those songs because I’m there, y’know?

AD: On the note of lyrics, I tend to read some songs on Rubies as more… “confessional” isn’t the right word, I would never describe you as a confessional songwriter, but maybe a little more personal than other Destroyer material. Certainly more than the stuff you’ve done since Poison Season, let’s say. Does that seem on-base? Off-base? Somewhere in between?

Dan Bejar: It’s probably on-base. I don’t know if it’s confessional, or if it’s just that it’s the first time where I really started to immerse myself in what you would call “classic singer-songwriter” mode. Or if I was listening to Blood on the Tracks a lot, or even Planet Waves. Or if I started to really, for the first time in my life, get seriously into Joni Mitchell, and listen to Hejira a lot. And the way that she just ran with language in this freeform but very melodic way. Or y’know, get really into 70s Van Morrison for the first time…

There’s a personal element to that style of writing, and there’s no way that it didn’t rub off on me. I probably started trying to include elements that were part of my personal landscape, y’know? Just like, fix the songs to something besides my imagination.

AD: What do you think about the way that Rubies has grown over time in terms of reception and reputation? It strikes as maybe not the Destroyer record, which is probably always gonna be Kaputt, but it’s certainly one of the Destroyer records—maybe the first of those. It was well-received at the time obviously, and you’ve spoken to the fact that the record catapulted you to a new level. Do you have a sense of how the record has grown in terms of legacy over two decades?

Dan Bejar: I have zero sense, really. The way that people are discussing the 2000s, which they’re doing more and more right now, it’s like some other universe to me. So I don’t really know. In my mind, [Rubies] seems completely out of whack with what’s fashionable. It’s still Destroyer that way.

Were someone to be “into” the band, it would be the record I would start at—that or Poison Season, maybe. Just because I don’t think Kaputt is really that indicative of my writing and singing, in a lot of ways. It’s kind of the most Destroyer-y of the Destroyer records.

AD: Rubies is?

Dan Bejar: I’d say so, yeah.

AD: Interesting.

Dan Bejar: And it’s where a certain kind of writing that I was doing probably crested, y’know? I can’t write like that anymore. There’s a density to it that is, in my mind, effortless, and I can’t imagine getting to that place… Writing like that is not even a part of me anymore. And I think [Rubies] is the apex of that style. 

I mean, I think the lyric sheet to Trouble In Dreams supersedes it. I think that’s probably the best writing I’ve ever done—which is interesting, because it’s maybe my least-favorite record. So it was a good lesson to show [someone like] me, who writes words first, how pointless words are. But as far as nailing it goes, I think Rubies was the peak of this one version of writing that was the whole point of the band. It’s a strong example, and it’s pretty consistent from beginning to end.

AD: I’ll say.

Dan Bejar: But yeah, I don’t know what people think about it. I don’t turn on the radio and hear a 22 year-old band and go like, “Oh shit, they sound like they’re really into Rubies” [laughs]. That doesn’t happen.

AD: I wanted to ask about that, actually. You look at some of the hot names in indie rock: Geese, for instance, gets Strokes and Radiohead comparisons, MJ Lenderman gets David Berman comparisons. Destroyer has been around for a while at this point. Do you detect Destroyer DNA anywhere in whatever passes for “indie rock” these days?

Dan Bejar: Not really, no.

I mean, there’s a lot of 90s feelings out there right now when I listen to music—late 90s, early 2000s. And I always felt on the outside then. That decade, that time period… I was like a member of the French resistance. I was in hiding. There was a war out there, and it was all bad guys.

AD: [Laughs] What did Fader call you? “Rock’s Exiled King” or something?

Dan Bejar: I didn’t like any of that shit. Instead of dusting off really hard-to-find examples of things in the 90s that didn’t get a light shone on them, people are dusting off all the shit I already knew about and hated. 

It’s interesting how history works, y’know? In the end, you think that history will sort things out—like eventually what you’re into will surface once all the bullshit fades away. But that’s not really how it works. History gets written in a certain way for a reason.

AD: Bullshit has a way of not fading away, in my experience. When we talked about Dan’s Boogie earlier in the year [on Never Ending Stories], we talked about you as a singer, and I think you said something about how satisfied you are with your development. How do you feel about yourself as a singer on Rubies?

Dan Bejar: I think it’s good. It’s not my “thing” totally, but I think it’s probably the best singing I’d done up until that point. It was a lot of material to wrap your voice around. I probably wouldn’t write that many words period now, just because I don’t want to sing that many words.

Yeah… it’s ok. [Laughs] I’m gonna say it’s ok.

AD: A ringing endorsement.

Dan Bejar: The singing is ok. It’s the only thing I really listen to as far as being critical. Like, the band sounds good, even though I know the band sounded better in the living room. The recording is good, the words are good…. the singing, for the most part, is pretty good. But it demanded a certain kind of singing that, the minute that record was done, I lost interest in, even if I didn’t know that I had.

AD: On the note of Trouble In Dreams, I wanted to circle back to that before I forget. You called it one of the best sets of lyrics that you’ve written, but maybe your least-favorite—

Dan Bejar: I think I called it the best.

AD: I think you did! I was hedging a little bit there, I wanted to give you some room.

Dan Bejar: No no no, I’ll go to bat for the lyric sheet. I’d call it the best lyric sheet I’ve written.

AD: But your least favorite record. Where’s that disconnect? Why?

Dan Bejar: Well, lyric sheets are like one one-hundredth of an album. So there’s lots of room for other things to go wrong. 

I couldn’t figure out how to sing those songs. I think they were written in the same spirit as the Rubies songs but more heightened, as far as the words go. But I didn’t know what the band should sound like. There was trouble in the band…

I didn’t know how to sing [the songs]. I thought we could just figure it out in the studio and have that be a glorious mess of a document, and that didn’t really pan out. So y’know… you need more than really cool words in the end. That’s why there are so many records that are actually quite good where the words just don’t matter. But it’s never the opposite. There’s never a record that’s quite good even though the music doesn’t come off. It’s not a two-way street.

AD: With Trouble In Dreams, is it the actual finished product that dissatisfies? Is it the experience of making it?

Dan Bejar: The whole thing. We went back into the living room and we were playing at twice the volume, and it definitely did not work. And I didn’t have my head together enough to try to figure out some other way of doing the record. I just went blindly towards our booked studio time. And it didn’t really get much better once we were in the studio.

I wrote the songs in Spain on a Spanish guitar—really quiet. I had in my mind the idea of just recording them with my own stuff in an apartment in Spain while I lived there. And then I chickened out of that idea, and I made a rock record, and it didn’t work.

AD: Well, you’re allowed to be critical of your own work. For my part, “Foam Hands,” “My Favorite Year”— those are tough to beat.

Dan Bejar: There’s songs on [the record] I like a lot. When I play solo shows, I pull from that record, and I enjoy singing those songs. Some of them, anyways.

AD: Back to Rubies: do you have tapes from the living room, the house sessions? The real stuff?

Dan Bejar: That’s funny, I wonder… It seems more industrious than Destroyer ever would be to actually bother to record a rehearsal and listen to it. That would have involved finding a tape and putting it in a tape machine and pressing ‘record.’

AD: Hitting the button [laughs].

Dan Bejar: Hitting the button, yeah. I can’t remember if we ever recorded anything. If we did, I certainly have no idea where it is.

AD: This question occurs naturally to me, this interview being in preparation for a 20th anniversary piece, but like, you’ve been around for going on 30 years…

Dan Bejar: 30 years is about right.

AD: There are plenty of other contemporaries—even people who have much shorter and less illustrious careers—who are doing like, the 10th anniversary re-release, the 20th anniversary re-release with a remix and demos and cutting room floor material. My memory might be failing me, but I don’t think I’ve ever really seen any of that stuff from Destroyer. Is it just not something you’re interested in?

Dan Bejar: I think it’s for bands who have managers [laughs]. 

It’s too much for me to think about, and I’d rather put my energies elsewhere, even though it seems to be what people buy, and the kind of concerts that people go to. So that’s frustrating, but yeah, I can’t… I can’t do it. I can’t do like, a 20th anniversary Rubies tour. It’s fine for people to do that, but for me, that would shorten my lifespan.

AD: I wouldn’t want you to do one, for what it’s worth. I would love to hear these tapes and anything else…

Dan Bejar: Yeah, me too. We’re just a pretty undocumented band. There’s never been a ton of stuff kicking around. I’ve never gone into the studio with a ton of songs and then rejected half of them. It’s always just like, “Here are the songs, let’s go make them.”

AD: Pretty intentional.

Dan Bejar: Yeah, like, “Let’s go make these songs, and then let the chips fall where they may.”

I think I said [a moment ago], “Oh, even though everything felt fucked up, we could just go into the studio and make Trouble In Dreams and have it be a document of the moment, and revel in that.” Because that’s kind of how I approached all the Destroyer records before that. The idea of turning back because it’s not going well seemed ridiculous. And I still go in with that frame of mind a little bit. The songs are written, they’re etched in stone—what I do, the way I sing them. The rest is just about what the world throws at them. And if they’re strong enough they won’t be derailed by the world, y’know? That’s still my mindframe. 

I’d like to become a bit more conscientious with the songs maybe, but if I’m not at this point… Like, maybe when I’m in my 60s? But it already feels ridiculous.

AD: Hard to teach an old dog new tricks.

Dan Bejar: Yeah. I want to learn to write in new ways, and record in new ways, and I’ll still try and focus on that, but there’s a certain cavalier quality to making the [Destroyer] records which I like. Because it means it’s an adventure. For that reason, the records are flawed, but I think they’re interesting for that, as well.

AD: That’s why they’re called a “record,” right? It’s literally a record of this moment in time. That’s so much a part of the record as a format.

Dan Bejar: Yeah, I mean, I can’t live in denial of the fact that musically, I’m just not astute enough to have it all mapped out beautifully in my mind and know which way things are supposed to go. I can come up with some melodies and string some chords together… I have a kind of goosebump system of knowing when something sweeps me off my feet, but I’m not a mastermind when it comes to the music part of it. So I just gotta throw myself into it.

AD: Makes sense. One last question: what’s your favorite song on Rubies? 

Dan Bejar: Hmm…

AD: I’ll also accept least-favorite song, but I don’t wanna end on a negative note [laughs].

Dan Bejar: I won’t harp on least-favorite. When I sing the song “Looter’s Follies,” it’s still, 20 years later, really emotional for me in ways that I can’t compare to too many other Destroyer songs, of the few hundred that I’ve written. There’s something about it—something about the way the current band does it, where I kind of gut myself, which is rare. I catch myself by surprise. And it has a vibe to it, which I feel like we really nailed.

Like you said, it feels maybe oddly personal compared to a lot of other Destroyer songs. When you say that some of the songs on Rubies have a personal sheen, more so than the rest of the catalog, I would say “Looter’s Follies” is a pretty good example of that.

AD: Good pick. That’s my favorite, too.

Dan Bejar: Well there you go.
