## Body

People forget that Francis Fukuyama ended his famous summer 1989 rumination on the “end of history” on a note of dejection. The communist Eastern Bloc was falling apart and the Berlin Wall would be down before the calendar year was up. Fukuyama, a political scientist and analyst at the State Department, surmised in the manifest end of the Cold War what he described as “the total exhaustion of viable systematic alternatives to Western liberalism” and wondered whether it could be said that history had, in some sense, reached its terminal point. He did not mean, of course, that nothing would ever happen again. Rather, he meant that with the fiery destruction of fascism in the first half of the twentieth century and the apparent wreck of communism happening all around him, that humanity had at last found its final social and political form in the liberal democratic capitalism of the Western world. The only option left was a society characterized by, in his words, “liberal democracy in the political sphere combined with easy access to VCRs and stereos in the economic.” And over this he could not help but lament. “The end of history will be a very sad time,” Fukuyama wrote. A century of global ideological struggle, of idealism and heroism and utopian aspiration, would give way to an age of technical problem-solving, environmental concerns and the endless satisfaction of consumer demand.

More than any other musical form, post-rock was what the end of history actually sounded like. The ascendancy of the punk, noise and sludge sounds that eventually got tagged as grunge was really the harvest of college rock seeds planted at the tail-end of the Reagan-Bush era. Its studied disaffections and alienations made sense as a repudiation of 1980s conservatism and materialist excess, but they persisted in the 1990s as more of a hangover (and later, an affectation) than anything else. The same might be said, mutatis mutandis, of rave in the UK, whose liberatory imperatives seem inseparable from Thatcherism. But post-rock belonged fully to what we might call the high 90s, the Clinton era; and even while emerging from the interstices of alternative and electronica, it was emotionally an altogether different species. Post-rock permitted neither wallowing nor ecstatic release, content instead to burble along at a bemused medium-cool.

In post-rock, the anhedonia of the end of history found its ideal form. Indeed, post-rock was often conventionally inexpressive by design, studiously uninterested in trafficking in the kinds of human feelings that make up the traditional material of pop music. Love songs, road songs, revolutions, memories, teenage symphonies to God—all of them were reduced to the technical problem-solving that Fukuyama had projected as the ethos of the coming era. When the track starts skipping around minute fourteen of Chicago band Tortoise’s genre-defining epic, “Djed,” it isn’t a comment on the artificiality of recorded sound; it is just another sonic impasse to work around. The skipping simply serves as a new click track, and the groove is painstakingly reassembled atop the malfunction. Art became, in some sense, a matter of logistics.

Literary theorist Fredric Jameson once argued that postmodern art was characterized, above all, by what he described as “depthlessness,” by which he didn’t mean shallowness in a pejorative sense, but rather superficiality in a perfectly literal sense: a culture consumed with the play of surfaces—gleaming, reflective, transparent, ornate. This accounts, in some measure, for post-rock’s origami-like sonic constructions: surfaces folded meticulously into form. Even at its most portentous—say, in the so-called ‘crescendo-core’ music that evolved out of Mogwai and Godspeed You! Black Emperor—post-rock was never really transcendental ‘head music’ in the sense of psychedelia or spiritual jazz. It was a music of filigree and figuration. Like any good postmodernist, it didn’t want to be accused of selling anybody a bill of goods.

The basic building-blocks of the form were krautrock, dub, IDM, shoegaze and lounge. But post-rock’s true signature was repetition—and this it had gleaned above all from the American minimalists. Indeed, one could argue that post-rock aimed at the integration of Reich and Glass into a rock idiom the way their German forebears in Can had attempted to integrate Stockhausen and Ligeti a generation earlier. Post-rock was often, as Philip Glass describes his own work, “music with repetitive structures”—only now set to a backbeat. As much as its postmodern eclecticism, this consuming interest in repetition and reiteration is what made the genre so appropriate to its historical moment. Rock musicians in the 1960s wrote songs about traveling, about the romance of the open road. Post-rockers, by contrast, opted for the steady state: the music moved, but it seldom progressed. There was no place to go, because one place was pretty much the same as the next. Surely it was no accident that Glenn Jones named his crucial Boston post-rock outfit Cul de Sac.

Post-rock has been on people’s minds lately. In March, genre godfathers Tortoise released their first new music in over a decade. Stereolab recently announced that it will be touring North America in the fall. Archival collections of founding Tortoise guitarist Dave Pajo’s Aerial M project and David Grubbs and Jim O’Rourke’s formative Gastr del Sol have invited fresh reappraisals of the importance of their work. The recent reissue of early UK post-rockers Seefeel’s gleaming classic Quique has reminded listeners of the allure of this type of steady-state groove. And, of course, Tortoise and Isotope 217 guitarist Jeff Parker’s decamping for the west coast some years back has turned the adventurous Los Angeles jazz scene into a veritable colony of the late twentieth-century Chicago sound. In the outstanding work of Parker’s ETA IVtet bandmates bassist Anna Butters and saxophonist Josh Johnson, you can hear the legacy of post-rock being worked out in real time.

Of course, the polycrisis of American life in the 2020s surely plays no small part in nostalgia for the 1990s. The vaunted ‘end of history’ proved no greater than a brief parenthesis, stretching from the end of the Cold War to the events of September 11, 2001, and our culture has hardly ceased being throttled since its resumption. Seems as good an opportunity as any for a time-capsule of that fleeting moment in music history. Upon revisiting the sound of 90s post-rock, it seems strange to envy it its detachment and aimlessness, the almost-complacency with which it was content to stand still. But when the present alternatives are terror and dread, one can hardly gainsay the appeal. | b sirota
