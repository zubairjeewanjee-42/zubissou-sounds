## Body

Part two of our of our interview with legendary Downtown musician Peter Gordon, we pick up where we left off, discussing his rise through West Coast academia at Mills College in the early-to-mid-1970s after leaving UC San Diego and studying under Robert Ashley and Terry Riley, before eventually moving to New York City to become a part of the dynamic East Village arts and music scene—alongside collaborators like Arthur Russell and Rhys Chatham—and forming the great Love of Life Orchestra in 1977. From there we trace his confoundingly original body of work through the 1980s and 1990s, into a hiatus from the New York scene to start a family and work on theater pieces with his wife Kit Fitzgerald, and his triumphant return and rediscovery through James Murphy and the DFA crew starting in the 2000s. All of which would lay the foundation for his current status as a profoundly influential, if largely unheralded, pioneer of modern sound and interdisciplinary output both here in America and abroad. But even then we’ve just scratched the surface of his extraordinary life and career. 

Here’s what he had to say to us about all of it. | l shook 

Aquarium Drunkard: I was gonna ask you, you transferred after some time at UC San Diego to Mills College, another energy center for new music and exploration and academia. And I know you got to study with Robert Ashley there, but also Terry Riley, who you just mentioned. What was that like? Especially as a young person absorbing ideas from such radical thinkers and people who were at the cutting edge of new music and composition. How did your Mills College experience differ from UC San Diego?

Peter Gordon: Well, I think in many ways, it was almost like 180-degree difference. Because at UCSD— at least from my teachers—music did not have a literal context. Like, it  wasn’t a story. It was more like a mathematical structure. And therefore it was apolitical and non-referential. I remember my composition teacher, Kenneth Gaburo, making a comment to me about how photography wasn’t a real art, it dealt with real images. And that was sort of philosophy there. And after a while I began bristling against this.

AD: It was too academic? 

Peter Gordon: I’m not sure whether academic was the word, but it seemed like too removed from important things that were happening in the world. Things were happening in language— like language was being broken down into syntactical structure— but meaning was being ignored. And so, then Bob Ashley, he came down and he did a performance [of his opera In Memoriam…Kit Carson]. Number one, he put the whole performance together over a weekend with people of various range of ability. It had interesting sounds, it allowed for improvisation. Politically, it was specifically addressing a certain political financial structure that was happening at the time. There was a scandal going on with I.T.T. [the International Telephone and Telegraph Corporation from Watergate], and basically he took every article that appeared in the paper regarding this particular case and it became sort of the narrative of this. So, Bob bringing storytelling into a more avant-garde environment. So it wasn’t just like ‘let’s just let it all happen,’ but more like saying, ‘let’s let it happen, but these are things we should be paying attention to.’

AD: Right. And what about with Terry? What were your interactions with Terry like and what did you learn from him? 

Peter Gordon: Terry was amazing. I mean, a composition lesson with Terry is that you just start playing music the minute you enter the room. It’s like the complete antithesis of UCSD. We wouldn’t talk about it. We would be trading off musical ideas. So if you can imagine, basically every week we got to jam with Terry Riley for two hours and develop music ideas. And just seeing that, you learn so much because you see an idea develop.

AD: In real time. 

Peter Gordon: In real time. And you feel it too. And you don’t have to explain anything.

AD: So sort of removing that academic approach while still in the academic environment.  

Peter Gordon: Right. It was really experiential. I think, you know, as I look back, certainly informed by his own studies with [Hindu singer and Riley’s teacher, Pandit] Pran Nath and Indian music. It was so much about listening and being in the moment. 

AD: So a lot of improvisation.  

Peter Gordon: Yeah, it was improvisation, but I think of it more as real-time composition.  

AD: Spontaneous composition, as they say. 

Peter Gordon: Yeah. Because it wasn’t improvisation where you’d be trading off for soloing. It was improvisation, and it was melodic, tonal. Very often it would be modal. But there could also be gestural or clusters as well.  

AD: You also met “Blue” Gene Tyranny at Mills. Was he a part of that group when you were first there?

Peter Gordon: Yeah, very much so. And “Blue” Gene, he was both on the faculty— he was teaching theory and harmony to the undergraduates— and also ran the recording studio. He was the main engineer at the recording studio there and they had an 8-track. It was a one-inch Ampex 8-track recorder.  And Blue and I, we ended up playing together a lot. That’s when we started playing together. And sometimes he’d be playing with me and Paul Dresher and Gene Reffkin, a drummer. So, I started doing these multi-track studio pieces as well.

AD: You’re really starting to collect a lot of people who, between Ned and Robert and “Blue” Gene, would form the basis of this sort of cabal of people that you would work with for years and years.

Peter Gordon: Yeah. Jill Kroesen was there. That’s when I first met Jill.

AD: She was a student?

Peter Gordon: Yes. She was a graduate student, but she also went there as an undergraduate, and she was from the Bay Area. 

AD: So, you get all these amazing experiences with these very prominent and forward-thinking composers and musicians in your collegiate career. And then you moved to New York— back to New York, I should say, because you were born in New York— in ’75, and where you quickly became part of the burgeoning underground music scene in the East Village. Which I know Robert was a very active part of, and where you met and networked with a community of like-minded musicians, including Arthur Russell, David Van Tieghem, Rhys Chatham, and Laurie Anderson. And in places around The Kitchen and St. Mark’s Poetry Project. You’ve talked about the importance of community on that scene and supporting and participating in each other’s art and projects while also developing your own personal styles. Did you feel like you’d finally found your tribe? 

Peter Gordon: Oh, I knew it right away. You know, I could feel it. Initially I was living in San Francisco. I was on the road where I was playing with a rock band at the time. I was playing in this band called Butch Whacks & the Glass Packs. It was sort of a band and also kind of theatrical review. And it was like fifties rock and roll, so I was playing basic rock tenor. Which I love playing. So anyways, we were just coming off of a month gig in Chicago at the Playboy Club— this was like three weeks in Chicago in January. So, I got to playing rock and roll, the same thing every night. And so I got to New York, it felt balmy even though it was February compared to Chicago. And I initially began crashing around the East Village and met Rhys and Arthur the first week I was here. And everyone just really had this conviction and wanted to make music. And there were these composers and performers, and you couldn’t get any other ensembles to play your music, so we’d have to do it ourselves. So we began playing in each other’s pieces. It’s like two weeks after I was in New York, I moved to New York. Arthur says, “I’m working on this piece. Can you help me notate this? Could you copy the scores? Copy out parts?” And this was Instrumentals. So I helped Arthur as a copyist. So, it was all sort of very organic. And people were working, playing music, making art together. But at the same time working on construction shifts or hiring each other in restaurants as well. If you looked at it from the outside, it all seemed pretty bleak. I mean, it looked like a bombed out area, but at the same time, we could get an apartment in the East Village for $150 a month, you know? 

AD: And it seems like you all were just sort of like bouncing ideas off of each other. 

Peter Gordon: Oh yeah, it was an ongoing conversation. Like with Arthur, we’d have these long conversations over the phone. I don’t have phone conversations like we did back then— you know, there wasn’t the internet, there wasn’t Chat, or any of that. And people would talk on the phone. So, we had conversations and then, a day later, call back saying, “I wanted to correct something that you said.” You know, sort of like this ongoing thing. And Rhys and I would meet and have breakfast together on Second Avenue. Then you find places like, “Oh, there’s a bar, nothing’s happening there.” And they figure, well, if you can play music and get people in, then sure. I remember when CBGBs was literally a pool hall with country music on the jukebox. Then they would do poetry readings. It began by doing poetry readings, and then things began having a momentum going. 

AD: You once described what was happening in that scene as “grassroots music,” which I think some might find odd given how progressive and radical and futuristic so much of it was. But it was very organic in that way, and it was just groups of people coming together to create, and was very much just a natural progression of relationships in that community.  

Peter Gordon: Yeah, because there was no reason to do it if you didn’t want to do it. Because no one was making any money from doing it. And if you make 50 bucks, you’ll end up paying it to someone else on your gig. It all felt very radical in terms of it had almost this utopian idea that, you might not be able to change the world directly, but somehow, if you could model a change within the art, that this could contribute to some larger change.

AD: Which I’d say it absolutely did. And maybe it was something that paid those sort of dividends later as it was absorbed into the culture, and into the world of music and art and performance, as it was later disseminated outside of New York. But also in New York as well. 

Peter Gordon: Yeah, I think so, but for better and for worse. Things changed a lot in the 80s. But in the 70s, it was very much the breaking down of hierarchies like between high art and low art. Even if you weren’t the most highly skilled on a particular instrument, it doesn’t mean that you can’t contribute in a constructive way. Just in that sense of seemingly incongruous parts coexisting to create a workable whole. 

AD: I was gonna say, Love of Life Orchestra is very much a product of that aesthetic and mentality. I mean, it was punk and No Wave adjacent, but it’s also very much its own thing, and incorporated elements of serialism, jazz, rock, disco, and just dance music in general. And is a really heady amalgam of high, low, cerebral and funky, and a kind of populist avant-garde sensibility, I would say. I was wondering if you could talk a little bit about the genesis of the band and your original vision for it. 

Peter Gordon: Well, I hesitate to use the word populism now because it’s become this other thing, politically. 

AD: The old version.  

Peter Gordon: Yeah, the old version. It’s socialism without the national up front. But I wanted to create an ensemble in which people coming from different cultures, different musics, could coexist within the same ensemble. So, we had some musicians who came more from rock. Other musicians came more from classical music. Some were amazing sight readers, some couldn’t read music at all. But to create a music into which different individuals could find their way into it, and find different ways to contribute to the music, to be a piece of it. 

AD: And it really worked. It was a very interesting experiment in that way, like with the Extended Niceties EP, to have people like Arto Lindsay doing his skronky, scratchy, convulsive guitar, and then you also have this like 4-4 disco bass drum going. It really was mixing things up and all about cross-pollination. 

Peter Gordon: Well, yeah, it’s about cross-pollination, and also I think it’s about coexistence and working together. Just socially we were coming from all different places. And by creating a world in which people could bring their various talents and perspectives and get respected from wherever they’re coming from and be allowed to contribute, I mean, really that’s the only way we can survive.

AD: I was wondering how your first solo album, Star Jaws, fit into that world. I know it was recorded at Mills in ’77, but shares many of the sensibilities and touchstones with Love of Life Orchestra, and is even dedicated to the Love of Life musical family. How did that factor into that whole time period for you musically?  

Peter Gordon: Well, I guess I had just begun working with LOLO [Love of Life Orchestra]. But I still had close contacts from Mills. So I was kind of going back and forth somewhat to San Francisco. And at that time my folks still lived in LA. And that’s where I could get studio time and was able to record a record.  

AD: It seems like there’s some similar musical ideas that were happening on that record that you’d hear on LOLO stuff.

Peter Gordon: Yeah, very much. I mean, it was all part of things I was thinking of. It had a slightly different edge to it, of course. It was California as opposed to New York, but at the same time, for example, Blue “Gene” is on it, as well as Steve Bartek, who I went to high school with. He was a friend of mine and he played guitar and bass on it.

AD: Was it West Coast LOLO?  

Peter Gordon: Yeah, West Coast LOLO. 

AD: We have to mention Kathy Acker, who was a very important part of your life during that time period as well. I know you all were involved in a relationship together, but also performing together, and she was involved with LOLO as well. I was just curious if you could talk a little bit about her importance to that scene and your life at the time. 

Peter Gordon: We were living together and did get married. In one sense she allowed me to bring a certain irreverence to existing structures, and also in terms of appreciating vernacular speech, as opposed to sort of a more affected type of language, I guess.  

AD: And spoken word in general.

Peter Gordon: Yeah. I mean, it exists on a lot of different levels. I think the way she works with identity within her writing, I think allowed me to think in terms of adopting a different persona. Which in particular I think pertained when recording multi-track. I try to bring a different point of view, almost a different character, each time I put a different layer on when I’m recording in a multi-track sense. In LOLO we had Kathy as well as the poet Ed Friedman, so poetry and spoken word was part of the band.  

AD: So part of your sort of multilingual and multifaceted presentation. 

Peter Gordon: Exactly. As well in terms of some of the songs, and working with some of her words at the time, we created these sort of song suites. I worked a lot with her lyrics and did a lot of setting of her lyrics. But it’s not like she was really a back and forth collaborator. It’s sort of like, “Here, these are the words. Use them.” And I would use them or not, or, you know, appropriate them. But that was sort of in the very early incarnation LOLO. 

AD: Right. Well, let me ask you, despite working with some of the most prominent musicians from that scene— you have these guest appearances by Arto Lindsay, David Byrne, and Bill Laswell on your albums— it seems like Love of Life Orchestra never quite got the same recognition as some of your peers at the time. Did some of it fly over people’s heads at the time? I’ve always been sort of curious as to why you don’t see LOLO’s name mentioned as much in some of the histories of that time period, even though it seems like you were very much at the center of it. Is that a fair assessment? 

Peter Gordon: Yeah. I think because I didn’t establish the sound as a brand. I mean, now I think people have begun to understand it. I think some of my contemporaries, they would sort of find this one thing that would click and would stick with that. 

AD: It almost felt like it was maybe too broad for people because it didn’t have an easy entry point. It was so expansive in so many ways that I think even to today’s ears it can be hard to wrap your mind around what it is.

Peter Gordon: Oh my god, it’s hard for even me to deal with. Honestly it’s just beginning to make sense to me. And that’s sort of the process of discovering now. Now new sets of ears are discovering different things. Sometimes it can be more transparent. Also, a lot of it is instrumental music, which is harder to break through. My music is committed to that space in-between genres, but the market is ruled by genres. 

AD: Right. When you become sort of genre-less, and you’re not defining yourself, how do other people define you? It’s almost even harder. 

Peter Gordon: Yeah. I don’t know, maybe it hits the zeitgeist in a different way. There was sort of this confluence going on of punk and No Wave. Downtown was being defined as sort of this dark thing, and I’m the Love of Life Orchestra, you know? [Laughs]

AD: Even the name sort of stood in stark contrast to some of the prevailing vibes of the time.

Peter Gordon: Yeah. I once had someone come up and say, “Is this a Christian band?” [Laughs] You know? So that’s confusing. There isn’t sort of a redemption story associated with it or something. And my pieces aren’t short pieces. 

AD: You have to be a listener and engage with it.  

Peter Gordon: Yeah. I’ve been doing this thing lately, this monthly thing at a club [Nightmoves in Brooklyn], where basically I DJ my own tracks. And it’s invaluable in terms of like, there’s some people who know of my contemporaries, but mostly it’s a club and many of the people have never heard this music before. So it’s really nice to sort of work with it and play with things out of context or decontextualized. And it’s great because it actually connects, but it also is very informative to me.

AD: You get to decontextualize yourself.

Peter Gordon: Yeah, it’s both. I get to contextualize it myself. Plus, it’s kind of like Motown records. They used to have their quality control parties. You know, they would have these Saturday sock hops where they’d play the new records each week and see which ones worked. You know, the kids would come over, they just put new records out, and they know right away which ones would be the hits. So in this sense, it’s a way of sharing recorded music the same way DJs do in clubs, but I get to do it with my own music.

AD: I was going to say, you also worked on several of what are now considered like classic dance tracks from the era, featuring that same group of early collaborators, including Loose Joints and Dinosaur L. I was wondering how important groove was to you as a creative component, with both LOLO and other outfits at the time.

Peter Gordon: I think groove is still the most essential thing to me. I like music that has a groove. There are different types of grooves, but I still go into that. I like funky music. Working on those and working on my own recording things. And, I tell you, this is something which I really first realized when I first heard the Giorgio Moroder/Donna Summer records. It’s sort of like, man, if you’ve got a good groove going, you can put anything on top of it in any structure that you want, as long as you keep the groove going. James Brown knew that, certainly. You know, playing in funk bands, I certainly knew. So, if you can have a good groove, you can get away with anything. I mean, it could be the weirdest shit, you know? 

AD: True! And I think a lot of that LOLO stuff is a great example of that, and really incorporating the groove while allowing a lot of experimentation over the top and finding ways— intersections— for those things to work together. Which is really quite remarkable. I was going to ask you, your second solo LP, Innocent, came out in 1986 on CBS Masterworks and features several pieces from a collaborative performance called Secret Pastures, as well as new songs that saw you work with some of your inner circle musicians. But also people like Tony Levin and the Blind Boys of Alabama. It was also co-produced by fellow Beefheart alum, Gary Lucas. It’s a really interesting record for the time, and combines so many strands of music. Can you talk a little bit about it and how it landed on a major label? 

Peter Gordon: At the time, I’d done the score for Secret Pastures, which was with the dance company commissioned by Bill T. Jones/Arnie Zane Dance Company. And Keith Harring did the sets, and Willi Smith did the costumes, and it was kind of the hit of the dance scene at that time. So that got a lot of attention. And at the time Gary Lucas was working at Columbia Records, and he was working promo and doing some sort of writing for them there. But he got know people on different labels and he got interested in my music. And he got this A&R person, Christine Reed, at CBS Masterworks and they were interested. This is around the time New Age hadn’t quite broken yet and they were trying to find a different crossover thing to do. And he brought her to a club that we played at in the Lower East Side and got offered a record deal. So, Gary was the co-producer of it, and as well played beautiful steel guitar on it.

AD: Was the Beefheart connection something you all discussed? 

Peter Gordon: Oh, we definitely discussed it. Yeah. It was sort of like, we didn’t know that about each other until after, you know? Then we did. But we got some money to make the record Innocent. I never understood why they didn’t want to release Secret Pastures. We’ll have the piece on my new label, Adjacent. I’ll finally get to release that whole score, which is pretty amazing. But there are a couple of songs from that, as well as some newer songs done for the record. It was a combination of my team of musicians, as well as Gary also, who brought in some other folks like Elliot Easton— a great guitar player— and I think he was the one who brought Tony Levin in. One of the songs is with Laurie Anderson, and I guess she had just done some work with the Blind Boys, and she said, “Why don’t we give them a call?” So Laurie brought the Blind Boys in.  

AD: So another real fusion— I mean, I hate to use the word “fusion” in the context of your music— but there’s a lot of fusion going on there with these disparate elements. Thinking about the Blind Boys being on the same record as Arthur Russell is kind of wild. 

Peter Gordon: Yeah, well you got Blind Boys, Arthur Russell, and David Johansen there. 

AD: Right! Exactly. A real mishmash of different voices from throughout the 20th century. It’s a really incredible record and it’s a very interesting collection of songs. Then came Brooklyn in 1987. You were always keeping up with modern sounds and sensibilities and modes of production, and once said that “music should speak to your peers and your generation.” 

Peter Gordon: I think I try to do that. I mean, my peers are older now. But when you talk about a generation, I like to think of a generation as was once explained to me by my dear friend, the late artist, Lawrence Weiner. And he said, “You know, Peter, this is what a generation is: You look at a street corner at any moment in time and there are people there who are in their teens, there are people there in their twenties, and of all ages, people are there. But they’re all there at that moment in time. And that is a generation.” So in that sense, yeah, I think a generation is very much that of the present. And actually, I was thinking about this just yesterday— what I love about the new technology is that I love the app Shazam. Because I hear something, even coming out of a car, and I can now Shazam it. I know what that is. I can go back and listen to it. 

AD: Yeah, it’s wild. 

Peter Gordon: And I think that informs what I’m hearing now as much as going out to concerts or hearing what people have to say. Even just things that I hear in the neighborhood. 

AD: And you can figure out what it is immediately with a touch of a button.

Peter Gordon: Yeah, it’s like, back when I was doing the Brooklyn album, I had just moved to Brooklyn and in downtown Brooklyn there was the record store, The Wiz. It had a great collection of 12-inches and house music had just been breaking through. And since they were 12-inches they were relatively cheap. I was having a great time between that and listening to like WBLS at the time, as well as the new technology that was happening with sampling. Although, I was kind of doing sampling before there were samplers. So that wasn’t a new thing. Did you ever hear TheYellow Box record I did with David Cunningham by any chance? 

AD: Yeah, I was getting ready to ask you about that. I’ve heard it. 

Peter Gordon: That began with a session— it was actually the eve of the wedding of Charles and Diana, that whole thing— with me, David, John Greaves and Anton Fier. And we rented a 16-track and did a session at the Workers Music Association, and we just recorded some basic 16-track rhythm tracks. And then, over a period of almost 10 years, every time I would pass through London, David and I would get together and do a little more work on the recording. And eventually David did this amazing mix and sequencing that’s the record, which has a lot of sampling. But really, a lot of it was done and recorded like a year before samplers came out.

AD: Well, you’ve always been someone who’s sort of at the cutting edge of all that in terms of technology. I was going to actually ask you about that and the 90s and working with David, and then also the Balanescu String Quartet, and then new iterations of LOLO. But it seemed like the 90s were a little bit of a slowing down period for you, at least in terms of like recorded output. Was that a time of recalibration after such an explosive period of creativity in the 70s and 80s? Or was there just not as much being put out to the public? 

Peter Gordon: Well, okay, a few things: it was a time of musical regrouping. In 1993, I became a father— parenthood. In 1995 I moved to Santa Fe, New Mexico. And so I was living in Santa Fe from like ’95 to 2003.

AD: So just had removed yourself from the scene you’d been a part of for so long. 

Peter Gordon: Yeah, and then I began working more on theater pieces. My wife— Kit Fitzgerald, video artist— we did these video operas together, as well as I did music theater works with Talking Band. I was in Tokyo for a while. So, I was sort of focusing— especially with Kit— more on the performances with video, as well as thinking in terms of, at that time, longer phrases. And I sort of felt less connection with the pop music that was going on in the 90s and the recorded music. And maybe just because other things going on in my life. And at that point, I sort of began focusing more on longer, less rhythmic, less groove-oriented compositions. The Leningrad Xpress record came out in ’91, and then there was the LOLO Quartet— I think that came out maybe in ’95— and those were both on Italian labels. But there wasn’t so much interest among American labels. So that was— I won’t say it’s introspection— but it was sort of a matter of retooling in a certain sense.

AD: The 2000’s seemed like a bit of a reentry point for you, especially after getting picked up and promoted by James Murphy and the LCD/DFA crew, and re-releasing some LOLO stuff. How important was their acknowledgement of the influence of you and your work, particularly in and around New York, during that time period? It seems like they sort of helped introduce you, or reintroduce you, to a new generation of fans and younger acts who really embraced you. Was that a jumping off point to getting back involved in the New York scene?

Peter Gordon: Well, I mean, I did. I moved back to New York in 2005. I was in Santa Fe and then in California in LA for a couple of years. When they first released their Fabric anthology, which opened and closed with my track [“Beginning of the Heartbreak” from 1979’s Extended Niceties EP], I was  surprised. I didn’t know anyone had even remembered any of that. And the Arthur [Russell] revival was just kicking in as well. So, I heard that was happening, and then I met James and he said, “Why don’t you do something with DFA?” And then I worked with James and putting out the anthology. And I think that, not only did it introduce my music to a whole other generation, a whole other cohort of listeners, but it really allowed me to sort of reexamine those recordings and the music I had been making in the 80s, before I started focusing on other types of music. So, it really helped me re-embrace my dance music. And also, I found a real kinship with what DFA were doing musically. It’s sort of like I heard LCD Soundsystem and I said, “Okay, I get it. I hear where that’s coming from.” And then plus, I started going out to clubs again listening to James and the DFA parties. And it really led to like a rediscovery and new appreciation of dance music and club music. 

AD: Was it taking you back at all? Even though it was this sort of new modern era of underground dance music and DJ culture in New York? Was it giving you any sort of flashbacks to the late-70s and what was happening at Danceteria and Mudd Club and CBGBs in the early-80s as well?

Peter Gordon: Yes, but I wasn’t taking it as nostalgia. It seemed like a picking up of that energy. AIDS was a kick in the gut to the whole club scene and everything. And then the 90’s New York club scene, I wasn’t around for it so much. So, it definitely brought me back musically, and in terms of grooves that were working. And at the same time it gave me a way to look forward. It wasn’t like going back. It was more like, this opens up a new door. Also, by this time, I didn’t need to go into an expensive studio to record. With ProTools or Ableton, or whatever, I have the resources. That’s always been my tool anyways, and now I could do that working out of my own studio. 

AD: It seems like since then you’ve been on something of a tear: releasing symphonies, one-off collaborations, keeping the LOLO flame alive. Do you consider the past 15 years or so to be something of a renaissance for you personally, in that looking forward? Or have you always just been doing the work regardless of whether people were paying attention or not? 

Peter Gordon: Well, it’s been very productive in terms of public releases. It’s definitely felt like a renaissance. 

AD: Working with people like Factory Floor and some of these younger groups that I’m sure look up to you and admire your work— and then to be able to collaborate with them, and create new work it’s certainly got to be rewarding in itself. 

Peter Gordon: Oh, it’s a privilege. Just to be able to create new work with Factory Floor, with Tim Burgess— it’s rejuvenating. And you just keep doing it. And lately I’ve been doing more stuff playing out with LOLO live, which has been thrilling. And with some of the members of the original LOLO and my son, Max. So it’s like bringing it into the new generation of the music, and lately even going back to playing at a club on the Bowery, which was kind of thrilling.  

AD: It’s all come full circle. 

Peter Gordon: Yeah. It’s not like killing arenas or anything like that, but it’s an ongoing process. And the thing is, music still becomes more and more of a mystery to me. So I feel like I have to make more of it to try to figure it out. 

AD: You’ve just started your own independent record label with Adjacent Records, and it’s given you an opportunity to put out stuff from your archives and really dig back through your own catalog. I was curious what your plans were for that. 

Peter Gordon: Well, the next batch of releases which will be announced will include both some archival stuff, which hasn’t been heard, as well as new material, which I’m just in the process of mixing right now. 

AD: LOLO stuff?

Peter Gordon: LOLO stuff, yeah. It’s the umbrella I work under. I need to re-release the Geneva album, which hasn’t been out for a long time.  

AD: Oh, that’s exciting. That’s one of my favorites. 

Peter Gordon: I do have some earlier, more classical pieces. Some of which are even from the San Diego and San Francisco era— a piece for six flutes and three keyboards that I composed in 1971. Which, it’s scary for me to, at this point, just say these dates, you know? 

AD: I have to say, it’s an impressive career. It’s so much incredible music, and so many amazing ideas and confluences of people and places and things. It’s just a really remarkable legacy you’ve left, and are continuing to leave, with these new endeavors. Any other fun things coming up for you besides Adjacent?  

Peter Gordon: Well, there’s Adjacent, there’s the Nightmoves sessions that I’ve been doing at the clubs, as well as hopefully some other collaborations that I’m going to be looking forward to. And I found a great recording, which I had and didn’t even realize existed, of some duo recordings of myself with a collaboration that Kit did with Max Roach. She had like a 10-year collaboration with Max and her doing video.

AD: Oh, wow! 

Peter Gordon: And I just realized, there’s one little bit where as part of it, there’s Kit, but I’m doing a duet with Max, which is just thrilling. So hopefully this Kit Fitzgerald video with Max Roach and myself will be making an appearance soon as well.
