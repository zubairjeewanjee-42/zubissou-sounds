## Body

In her vividly descriptive lyricism, which comes alive across all her albums, but especially on her latest release, Pentimento, Carson McHone is a natural artist. Written on paintings and postcards, McHone deftly utilizes color, texture and movement in these exceptionally compelling and immersive arrangements. “Tell me if you want to, what colours I should use?” McHone posits on “Winter Breaking”, an immediate highlight that bustles with a Beatles-like swing and delicately embellished with birdsong carried over from the intro featuring a contemplative spoken delivery of an extract from a letter 1840 letter from Ralph Waldo Emerson to Margaret Fuller. Wrapped in those two opening tracks of Pentimento, McHone, demonstrates how the door hads been left ajar for collaboration between audience and artist, and, crucially, art with art. There may be almost 200 years separating Emerson and McHone’s poetic phrasing, and the worlds in which they were penned, but the works are connected through a sense of humanity and the importance of trying to remain hopeful in dark times. 

Residing in Ontario, Canada, McHone has spent a significant period of time, in the midst of writing and recording her fourth record, touring with Daniel Romano’s The Outfit, who contributes to the LP. The brilliantly captivating energy of McHone’s previous material paired with the vivacious personality of The Outfit’s output propel the Texan artist’s songwriting into a really exciting realm. McHone, assured in every turn as a bandleader across these songs, has so eloquently melded 1970s prog inflected rock with artful sound collage to produce an affecting and inviting body of work which captures the present moment without limiting its scope to resonate with listeners decades, a century even, later. 

Aquarium Drunkard spoke to Carson McHone about expanding Pentimento’s potential by transforming it into a multi-disciplinary project, dismantling her dream spaces and the influence of La Chimera and Nick Drake on her beautiful new record. | z hedderman

Aquarium Drunkard: Hey, how are you doing? How does it feel to have Pentimento out in the world and to be able to share these beautiful songs with audiences, and for them to take on a new life and meaning?

Carson McHone: It’s been a thrill. The release party was out of this world. My friend Coco, who played on the album and whose house we recorded in, came out and played a set of her own music, accompanied by her partner Will, to open the show, and some local farmers from Lincoln Farmacy donated some amazing goods for the event that we had out for refreshments and for folks to take home with them. There were canned beets and jalapeños, fresh herbs and flowers; it was beautiful to see everyone in the audience walking around with stalks of garlic! 

That night, we debuted this multi-disciplinary project along with the recording of the album that my co-directors, Allie Blumas and Deanna Jones and I worked lights and had a movement and dance routine that threw their shadows to a scrim, so it was really this wildly immersive way to take in the album; it wasn’t just a listening party. 

For me, it was like attending my own funeral to drop in on something sort of loosely associated with me, but that was really beyond me! To be in the audience and take in this incredible performance by my friends was a unique experience, and I was so proud just to be there, really. It was inspiring, too. The black drapes that framed the scrim and dance inspired images of the band set up there in front. I hope to be able to do a show where we can play the album live with the band in some slightly veiled or shadowy way and still have that scrim routine centred multi-disciplinary dream show! 

It’s nice to finally have the album out in full. I’ve received a handful of amazing messages from friends and strangers alike about putting the record on and listening to it twice in one sitting. I love that. I think the songs are in conversation with each other, so having the record in hand with the ability to sit down with it and let it play out is how I hoped it could be heard.

AD: Pentimento truly is such a stunning body of work; it’s electrifying, intimate, contemplative and incredibly alive, both in terms of the musicality and the imagery incorporated. The lyrics initially took shape as poems, and you’ve kept that structure by punctuating the album with gorgeous interludes like “In The Summer The Streets Burned” and also opening the album with the letter from Ralph Waldo Emerson to Margaret Fuller. Can you tell me about the early stages of building the world of the record and striking a balance between quiet contemplation and vivacious arrangements?

Carson McHone: I think ultimately the music dictated, being an album and all, but I’d like to think there’s a bit of a cinematic feel to it all. I love movies that combine something like magical realism and voice-over in a kind of tactile way that’s almost more like theatre. The words for this album came together on paintings and postcards and ended up taped to the walls around me, so I wanted to keep that feel to the album, so the spoken interludes are kind of a literal thread through the album. It’s funny because for me those things are tied to very real artifacts, but I think of a stranger listening to this album, the spoken word can kind of have the opposite effect and give it a bit of an ethereal feel. To any extent, I hope the whole thing sounds tangible still, organic and conversational, and I think that’s maybe the thread, actually, whatever form the words or meaning take, the sounds are all kind of “basic”, but in their combination they elevate the whole thing, take you elsewhere.

AD: Dreams and the act of dreaming are recurring in some of the songs. There’s a lyric in “September Song” where you sing, “To dream a song you have never ever heard.” Do you find ideas or visuals that you want to translate into your songs come to you when you’re in that unconscious state? There’s also a reading of dreams, particularly in art, where they can be places we go to escape or create our own utopias away from the hellish nightmare of the way the world is now. I’m interested to hear your thoughts on how, as a songwriter, you approach writing and structuring songs to be both a space to express real emotions and concerns about the world and society, while also providing solace in the painterly imagery and language and pretty arrangements.

Carson McHone: I am someone who has very vivid dreams, but they tend to be more nightmares. Often, too, there’s no language in them; they’re more visual, or just feeling.  I think when I reference dreams in songs, it’s more a reference to creativity, like to dream something up, to imagine. I think this speaks to the second half of your question, too, maybe. In my art, I am often scrutinizing the state of things, but just the act of doing that through music or whatever medium, that’s a part of resting the nihilism, or challenging and confronting the systems of oppression. I don’t think it has to sound beautiful to be beautiful, but isn’t it nice sometimes when it also is.

AD: Is there a particular lyric on the album that you feel encapsulates Pentimento’s essence and how it engages with the world that inspired it?

Carson McHone: “Oh, I know that which I seek / Is what I must come to be.”

AD: You’ve toured extensively over the last while, both performing your own solo material and as part of Daniel Romano’s outfit. Did you find that this period of performing and being so immersed in the energy of playing, singing and feeding off the live experience and audience reaction, made you more confident to push the breadth and melodic characteristics of the songs you wanted to write?

Carson McHone: I think more than anything, this time with the Outfit has inspired this sense of collective community effort and involvement, and I mean this down to the moment, like this instant we’re all contributing to, and I’m feeling that. Through Daniel and the Outfit, I’ve met so many wonderful artists, and a handful of those joined us to make this new album. It was one of those things that, of course, had to do with the music, but maybe even more so had to do with the energy that those people brought. I had no idea what Michael might play, outside of, generally, “things with keys”, but I knew after touring with him and Steve, hearing him accompany Steve’s poem songs, I wanted to be around that. And Coco, whom I met at a Julie Doiron show, I just immediately felt at ease around her. I felt calm but also challenged in this way that I appreciated. Surround yourself with people who inspire you and do your best to rise to the occasion.

AD: There’s a great depth in the details across your lyrics. I think of the bloodied smile in the opening of ‘Idiom’ and “The rolling sea of leaves” on ‘Fruits of My Tending.’ Your music videos, too, are also so visually striking and captivating. I loved the detail in the press materials about how you would write lyrics on postcards and on watercolour paintings, also. I’d love to hear about the importance of visuals in your writing and how your engagement and appreciation for visual art has shaped your particular style and voice, and continues to inspire you.

Carson McHone: I think maybe it’s just a part of my makeup. I so appreciate a good story, but I’ve never felt like I was a strong storyteller. I wonder if this is kind of obvious in how I was describing the way I dream, too; without narrative language but often really obvious visual metaphor with really visceral feelings. I think it’s why I like collage. I mentioned before feeling really inspired by certain films, the ones I love are kind of like this, and I think I try to take after them. Films like Varda’s Documenteur, Wim Wenders’ Wings of Desire, and La Chimera by Alice Rohrwacher feel like poems against visuals that have more than one narrative thread running through them, and always this sense of play that feels a bit theatrical. 

AD: The album was recorded over 6 days; within that time, there were clearly a lot of extremely fruitful jamming sessions with collaborators and a wonderful spontaneity captured in the energy of the tracks. With this being your fourth album, how did you find that you, as an artist and bandleader, had grown within this body of work compared to Still Life, Carousel and Goodluck Man?

Carson McHone: For this album, I knew I wanted it to be more collaborative, and that took me having to be open to spontaneity. This took lots of thinking and preparing, and arranging things on my end, by myself, in order to be able to step into this communal environment and have the confidence to go with the flow. So I think it’s just striking a balance. I know that I trust my methodical process, but I wanted to step out of my comfort zone, so I leaned into one to allow myself the freedom to open to the other.

AD: There’s such an inviting warmth in these songs, both in your vocal performance and in the many textural layers. I think of the interplay of guitar and accordion on “Vision in the Verse” or the bright 1960s and ‘70s sheen with the full band sensibility and luminous vocal harmonies on “The Canvas”. While you were starting to imagine the sonic character of these songs to pair with the themes and imagery, what were some new and different sonic or tonal textures that you were interested in exploring that you hadn’t before on previous LPs? Were there any albums or artists that you’d been listening to while you were making the LP that inspired you?

Carson McHone: Absolutely. I think it took breaking down songs that I loved and really listening to what made them striking to me. Often, it was the idiosyncratic things. For example, this Elyse Weinburg album, Elyse, is mixed totally wild; it’s like, ‘Who allowed this to happen?’ But then, realizing there was so much character in that record. I love hearing a ghost vocal or a tambourine come in for only one passage, really loud! It’s the oddities that give the sound that special character. Something that might be considered “bad” is actually quite interesting to me. The juxtaposition is nice, too, when you have a beautiful melody being played super crunched out, things like that. Sonically, I was trying to make something that sounded somewhere between the Velvet Underground and Nick Drake.

AD: One song I was particularly interested to hear about on the record is “Wake You Well”. It has such a captivating atmosphere, conjured from the wonderfully sinister string motifs that are so effectively paired with a subtle rumble of percussion and sustained notes as your vocals soar on top of the arrangement. In its production, it feels like a song that could have been written and recorded by Karen Dalton.I’d love to hear about the process of creating the world of that song and its role within the overall context of the album because it has such a contrasting air.

Carson McHone: “Wake You Well” was a song I wrote a long time ago that I never got around to recording, you could maybe say it haunted me. I wrote it after reading the poem “Suicide’s Note” by Langston Hughes, I think it was in 2012. Originally, I had written it to someone, but in the summer before I finally recorded it for this record, I changed the verses to tell more of a story, more of a personal account that could hopefully be related to, instead of talking at someone specific. I felt like how I wanted to deliver it called for more of a traditional story. I’ve always been drawn to those dark and lilting tunes, and the fiddle refrain takes after the song “Cruel Sister”, which has an incredible story, and is a song that I think should be made into a movie. Though it’s more narrative now than it was before, “Wake You Well” really embodies a long moment, and I think when we recorded, everything aligned. 

It was at night, beside the sea, Michael played essentially a single reed, Daniel the wonky violin, and Coco down the hallway on the drum. Steve got this bass drone going and we just did it one time and that was it. After the session, I took the songs and added my harmonies and it was interesting because that original vocal is still in there, and I like it like that. It’s very ghostly and also thematically worked with sort of exposing this “mirror self”.

AD: You collaborated with Allie Blumas and Deanna Jones to create a performance in three acts as a response to the album. How did that come about, and how did their engagement and interpretations of your songs for this work perhaps deepen or broaden your relationship with these songs?

Carson McHone: It’s just been such an incredible practice, meeting and working with Allie and Deanna. They’re both totally pro, but it’s amazing how adept they both are at falling right into this beautiful kind of play. It was so much fun, so interesting to be present while people sort of tell you what your songs mean to them with their bodies, with the casting of light, or shadow. It was really such an honor to have them both embrace this project, so generous with their time, and it gave me confidence, too. Before the album was released, that this record was resonating with people, outside the immediate contributors. It took me out of the songs, so I could enjoy it from the outside too, what a gift!

AD:  Finally, in ‘September Song’, a lyric that stood out to me was “Some persistent curiosity”. What continues to drives your curiosity?

Carson McHone: The world is full of mystery. I think curiosity is key to embracing that. Curiosity breeds creativity, and I think that’s necessary for living. When I am curious, I want to live; when I create, I feel like I become somewhat conscious of my small contribution to the greater fabric.
