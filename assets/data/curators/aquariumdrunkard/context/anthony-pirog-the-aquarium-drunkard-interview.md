# Anthony Pirog :: The Aquarium Drunkard Interview

## Body

Always fearless in his pursuit of sonic fusion, the wizardry of Anthony Pirog continues to invade new melodic landscapes. Hard noise. Ambient. Jazz. Punk. Experimental noise funk. There is a serious argument to be made for Pirog as the heir apparent to the Jazzmaster throne, as one of its leading proponents continuing to stretch the vocabulary of electric guitar. 

2024 finds Priog in the midst of an especially prolific period, releasing new albums with his duo with wife Janel, collaborating with everyone from Nels Cline to Andy Summers on his ambient solo effort, The Nepenthe Series Vol. 1, and a new full-length record from The Messthetics. For their third effort, Pirog and bandmates, Fugazi alumni Joe Lally and Brendan Canty, are joined by jazz composer and saxophonist James Brandon Lewis. Aptly named The Messthetics and James Brandon Lewis and released on Impulse! Records, the album features nine tracks, ranging from the melodic fury indicative of the group’s self-titled debut to an expanding frontier of textures made accessible through their transformation into a quartet. 

On a quiet afternoon, Anthony and I connected for a two hour deep dive into the roots of fusion guitar, the noise of the 90s, fear and the art of collaboration, and the power of creating without control. | n lekas

Aquarium Drunkard: What was starting a band with half of Fugazi like? 

Anthony Pirog: I feel good about the way that it started. I was excited to get to play with them. I was very nervous when they called, but I found out that Brendan [Canty] had come out to see me play. I was constantly playing around in D.C, doing whatever I could. I was doing solo noise sets. I had a surf band I know Brendan liked, I was playing jazzy gigs… Brendan told me he came out to see a Danny Gatton tribute I was taking part in, and I was like, “You like Danny Gatton?” He was like, “I love Danny Gatton.” 

When they asked me to play, I just went in and they started playing a groove, and it sounded so locked and so heavy. I knew I could do anything on top of it and it was going to be OK. They were really welcoming to me. I never felt like it was awkward for me to be there. I never felt uncomfortable. The only time I did feel uncomfortable was when we booked the first show. We had been rehearsing for months just trying to get ideas together and I was trying to figure out how far I could take the guitar playing and soloing in this context. Once I realized that we had to play, and people were going to have judgments, I got a little bit nervous. But they are both so cool. There was a point where I remember specifically that I told them I don’t want to solo in this band, and they were like, “Well then why are we doing this?” It’s funny. They’ve encouraged me to push myself on the guitar and push the boundaries of what people would expect. 

AD: Was it just a “let’s jam” situation or was there a project in mind from the get go? 

Anthony Pirog: Initially, they asked me to come play because Joe [Lally] wanted to start a trio, he had just moved back from Italy, there was a potential tour he was going to take part in, and we had one rehearsal just playing Joe’s music. The tour didn’t end up happening, so I waited a few months to try to build up the courage to ask them to be the rhythm section for a record I wanted to record, and they just said, “Why don’t we just start a band?” That was that. We started rehearsing two times a week. 

AD: That’s about as seamless as it gets. 

Anthony Pirog: Everyone likes so much music. Joe and I bonded over Ask the Ages, the Sonny Sharrock record and Brendan talks about going to Blues Alley in D.C. and seeing all these jazz greats. Everyone is into all music. 

AD: As a kid getting into punk, it took me a while to realize how deep Fugazi’s rhythm section really was. I think it was actually a bit overlooked. But it’s iconic. In the Messthetics Joe and Brendan sound like themselves, but it is wholly unique.   

Anthony Pirog: I do feel the weight of the situation sometimes and I try not to go there. At this point we’ve been playing for like seven years, so we’re friends now. I am very proud to be from D.C. I went away for school, but I wanted to stay here because this is my home. I love the musical tradition from here. I know I’ve said this a lot. Roy Buchanan, Danny Gatton, and Dischord Records are very important to me. I’ve had the opportunity to play with people who worked with Roy Buchanan and Danny Gatton. It’s a major thing for me to honor the regional tradition. 

AD: Gatton and Buchanan, the tele thing is so deep in that area–Bill Kirchen too. What were some of the first albums that got you excited about experimental guitar playing? 

Anthony Pirog: Early on, guitar was all over MTV. My dad was also in a surf band in the ’60s so I was always hearing instrumental music because of my parents. I really liked Robbie Krieger and The Doors when I was a little kid, and I really like Jimi Hendrix. Sonic Youth was on MTV and there was an element of noise in the popular music. So when I heard Fred Frith for the first time it wasn’t completely crazy to me. The first thing I heard was Taboo & Exile by John Zorn. There is a track with Zorn, Frith, Bill Laswell and Dave Lombardo. That was the track. 

Another thing was Pat Metheny Song X with Ornette. Some friends had that in the car and I was like I love this. Then hearing Derek Bailey. When I was in school studying music I could walk to Tonic in the Lower East Side, I would just hang out there as much as I could because it was like $10. I saw Frith, I saw Derek Bailey, Elliott Sharp, all those people. I would go to Tonic and see those people, and then go look at their section in the record store Downtown Music Gallery and see who they were playing with. It was pre-internet, so I could look through the sections and kind of piece it together. I had access to it, which was the main thing. Being able to be in the room with these people was exciting. Those are the earliest recordings, and then I was just around it from being a student in New York. 

AD: Were you specifically studying jazz guitar? 

Anthony Pirog: In high school, I went to the Berklee Summer Sessions for a week. I was into blues and I couldn’t use my pinky on my left hand. I had limited technique. I ended up getting a scholarship to attend the school and so I just decided I would go there. I went for two years studying composition and arranging. I was nervous about immersing myself fully in guitar studies. I was afraid I would lose some sort of edge. But after two years of Berklee I decided I should probably go to school in New York and try to see the people I wanted to see performing. In Boston I was under 21 so it was really hard to go see any live music. I would go see Rockabilly shows at T.T. the Bear’s Place a lot. There was a pretty cool rockabilly scene. Cliff Gallup is my dude. 

AD: Rockabilly was my gateway into thinking virtuoso guitar playing could still have an edge, or be cool. Gallup and players like that.  

Anthony Pirog: An accessible way to find technical guitar playing is something I struggled with into my 20s. It’s ultimately why I left Berklee. Like I said, I wasn’t using my pinky. The music that was the vehicle for technical playing wasn’t interesting to me at that time. I wasn’t into the musical aspects of the technical guitar players, and the technique wasn’t enough to draw me into that realm. I found a few players that spoke to me, that wrote really cool guitar instrumental music, and it kind of opened up for me. It all goes back to Charlie Christian for me. Even Cliff Gallup was very similar to Christian with a different tone. 

AD: Him and Lonnie Johnson. It definitely took me a while to connect to jazz music from punk, noise rock, and all of that kind of stuff. Did fusion follow pretty quickly? 

Anthony Pirog: My mom took me to see Nirvana on the In Utero tour. That was the first show I saw. The Breeders opened and that was the moment where I was like, “I want to play guitar.” In the early ’90s when the solo from In Bloom was blowing my mind, jazz fusion sounded terrible to me. My teacher gave me an Allan Holdsworth cassette, and I remember feeling almost offended. But now I think about Holdsworth everyday–one of the most incredible people who ever manipulated the notes. My tastes have evolved over time, but back then the tone of jazz guitar wasn’t interesting to me. If the sound wasn’t exciting I wasn’t going to dig in. That dry, rolled off guitar sound just wasn’t something I was drawn to in my teens. 

AD: The Nepenthe Series Vol. 1 is very cinematic. Did you work with a narrative in mind, or did it unfold after the fact as you were organizing the music?

Anthony Pirog: There are two kinds of things going on here. My wife and I have had a duo for 20 years, Janel and Anthony, we did two records, we have a third coming out on Cuneiform Records. But I have two trio records with Michael Formanek and Ches Smith on Cuneiform that don’t stream. Those are all my most important pre-Messthetics works. We mix everything up without making it the point of the music. It’s just an intuitive thing that we do. I bring that up because that’s largely been my approach. 

TM was the first time I was like, I really want to immerse myself in this one approach. So, it’s harsh noise. After that record I was sitting at my kitchen table with the laptop and I made Bernal Heights, and I was listening to it and was like, maybe I should do an ambient record? It would be at the opposite end of the spectrum from TM. I’ve met all these really cool people that I admire, what if I asked people to collaborate? I thought it would be an interesting approach. It would be hard for me to sit and gauge an ambient record alone like this. So the challenge was, if I ask people to send me a track I have to figure out what to do on top of it. 

I bring all this up because as far as the narrative of a record, I’m not thinking about that while I’m composing, but when it’s all said and done and I chose the pieces that I would like, I listen to it for a long time and try to get the right arch. The one I’m probably most proud of is Pocket Poem because the material is so varied but I almost hear it as one big composition. 

AD: What is the collaborative process like for you, did you give your collaborators complete control over what they do?

Anthony Pirog: Yes, that has always been essential with the way I work. I brought up those trio records with Michael Formanek and Ches Smith, when I asked them to collaborate I never told them what to play or how. I want your voice and I’m not trying to control the situation. That’s been my approach always. At least that’s what I’m trying to do. When I was little kid I remember watching a David Lynch interview and everyone saying that they felt like a collaborator, like part of the process, and I remember thinking that seemed like a really nice way to work. You get more interesting results. For this I wrote, please send me an “ambient” track, in quotes, so it could be interpreted. I had the responsibility to make it something that I wanted to hear. Like with Wendy Eisenberg, I even called her to talk about it, which was amazing to hear her talk about the choices she made. 

AD: Night Winds, that one is like dusk in the expanses of a sci-fi desert. Looking at the tracklist, I don’t know that I had an expectation of what each track would sound like, but each track was still somehow unexpected. Everyone manages to tap into something that maybe they wouldn’t normally do. 

Anthony Pirog: That’s exactly what I wanted. Everyone is a very creative person. The Nels track, Nels is my hero. He sent me this thing, and it was perfect the way it was. I was like, “I’m going to ruin this!” His playing has been so important to me. He sent me a photo of himself recording, and I was like I have all those pedals. What if I view our track as two hands on the same instrument? I set up a similar [pedal] chain to his, learned what the harmonic movements were, and then just played. I was sitting here for two months listening to it, going this is too much. But this is my idea so I have to work something out. It happened like that for almost every track, but Nels was definitely a hard one. 

AD: That’s the beauty of collaboration, you ask then you have to follow through. That’s what I loved about my own collaborative recordings, and what I like about doing these interviews, I don’t know what the conversation will be, but there is no getting out of it once asked, so there will be a conversation. 

Anthony Pirog: Exactly. That’s how I felt about this. It’s the same way I felt about asking Joe and Brendan to play. I’m scared to ask people, but I just do it. It’s cool, they can say no, people have said no to things. For this, Andy was the first person I asked, and I thought, if Andy says yes I’ll do this. He wrote back “Sounds great” and I was like, “Ok I guess we’re doing this.”  

AD: Andy is the name that jumps off the track list most to me as “Wow, really”? Just because of what I most associate him with. Nels has more of a reputation for showing up in seemingly random places. 

Anthony Pirog: I met Nels in a guitar store in Virginia. I opened the door and he was sitting there playing a fuzz pedal and I was like, “Oh my god!” 

AD: And a single beam of light shone down on him from heaven. 

Anthony Pirog: Exactly. I don’t talk very much normally so the people at the guitar store knew me, and I got the introduction. That was crazy. We just stayed in touch. 

AD: DId the Nepenthe Series Vol. 1 prove a relinquishing of sorrow, did it serve as a means to work through things for you?

Anthony Pirog: Well, for me, TM was honestly about that. At the time I made it, I kind of needed to do that. I thought that this would be a really nice recording for people to hear around this time. I never really think about the audience, I’m just working on what’s interesting to me, but that was on my mind for The Nepenthe Series. Obviously with the group of players that agreed to do it, I thought it would be interesting to hear. I liked the idea of putting together a list of people like this, and you expect a totally burning guitar record, but it is the exact opposite of what your expectation could be. It was fun for me because it is feeling based as opposed to technical music. 

AD: With the collaborative nature of the tracks, was it difficult to reign in the compositions, or make decisions of where to take certain things? 

Anthony Pirog: Giving everyone the freedom to interpret it gave me the opportunity to create this narrative and arch. Janel, my wife produced it, which was an incredible help because I have an amazing musician in the house. Sometimes she would say, “It’s great” and sometimes she’d say “You know what” this was the case on the track she gave me, “You just need to play the guitar.” Cause I did a synth thing over it. So I did a track and showed her again, and she was like “That is pretty good but you should make it speak.” Janel is constantly giving me feedback on every project and ideas for all the bands I’m in. That is something I need to acknowledge. 

AD: Was the approach to Glowing Gestures similar to how you and Janel always collaborate, or were you trying to intentionally take a different approach?

Anthony Pirog: For The Nepenthe Series, I specifically asked her to produce. So it wasn’t a collaborative relationship, it was like, “I need some honest feedback and guidance.” So that is the defining characteristic of this project. If I play her something from another project I can tell by the level of enthusiasm whether it’s something worth pursuing. We both have ears of steel. I really trust her. But with Janel and Anthony, that’s totally different. It’s very intuitive. There is this album called The Box Tree, I bought it from Skúli Sverrisson when we were in Iceland. I asked him how he composed it and he was just like “I composed it very intuitively.” That’s what we do. From the beginning we’ve had this very telepathic thing, arranging very quickly. Things just fall into place without very much work. It’s almost immediate. In the studio it gets a little bit more intense. But it’s usually pretty easy. 

AD: My experience of The Nepenthe Series, which perhaps says more about me than the record, is that each track felt like a mini film score. For example, Dense Blazing Star put me in a pseudo 1970’s Sonny Chibba-esque narrative. While listening, I wanted to start writing stories, it’s very similar to how I feel looking at certain paintings. 

Anthony Pirog: I love that. This is why I love instrumental music, because it is expressive of an emotion and you can bring it into such a personal interpretation. You can create your own story. That is very important to me. 

AD: How did the collaboration with James Brandon Lewis come together?

Anthony Pirog: This all happened because I met WIlliam Hooker. He would improvise with people like Lee Ranaldo and Thurston Moore, there is a great video of Thurston doing a Sonny Sharrock tribute with WIlliam Hooker that I really liked. Anyways I played with him down here as a duo, then we did a trio with Luke Stewart. He asked me up to New York to do a recording session. I knew Luke was going to be there playing bass, Jon Irabagon was playing sax, and James was there. I never do this, but James and I went out and ate after the session and I just had the best time talking to him. We just stayed in touch and became friends. He asked me to record with his trio, and I was like the special guest on two tracks for his record No Filter. I did some European touring with him and some US dates. Then for Winter Jazz Fest 2018 he asked me, Jaimie Branch, Luke Stewart and Warren Trae Crudup III to play. We had one rehearsal and it was amazing. Janel was like you guys need to record this as quickly as possible before anyone learns the music too well. So they came down and we recorded it. James was very generous with playing opportunities and I just liked talking to him. 

WIth the Messthetics, I thought James would be a great addition. Every time we were in New York I asked him to come sit in. Eventually we were playing Union Pool and he was like “Are you going to be in New York anytime soon?” I was like “Yeah, next weekend.” He was like, “I want to record some stuff, would Messthetics be into it?” Brendan and Joe were like “This is great, we’ll come up a day early if you can get a studio.” We recorded, kept the 2nd or 3rd take, just learned the song real fast, and everyone was really happy. 

The Messthetics didn’t play for maybe two years. We were starting to write new material. Three Sisters was one of the first songs we wrote after lockdown. When I wrote that melody I was thinking about James, I could see him playing this. It occurred to me, maybe we should ask him to collaborate. Brendan and Joe were really into it, and James was like no problem. He is just the perfect guy for this group. It’s just really really fun. 

AD: The record feels like a progression from the first two, specifically certain grooves almost felt organ trio-ish, like Big John Patton or something. Did adding a member create freedom to explore differently? 

Anthony Pirog: You are absolutely right. Just incorporating another person changes the possibilities, but especially a sax. I try not to actively think about it, but the references are always there, Sonny Rollins and Jim Hall, Sonny Sharrock and Peter Brötzmann, there are a wealth of approaches to think about. The Time is the Place Brenden wrote. What is interesting to me, because what you are saying is true but I don’t know what Brendan is thinking. It’s hard to discuss because we’ve never discussed it ourselves. I see what you’re saying, I get the feeling you are talking about, but it isn’t something we discussed, it just happened. 

For that song, they were soundchecking and James was putting together the melody for it at the recording studio, and the three of them were playing and I was like “Holy shit, this sounds good. I’d better get in there and play something before this becomes a trio.” But I don’t really know how to explain how the music is different. I know that it is. It’s weird because no matter what you do on the guitar it is seen as guitar music. I can play you tapes of these songs without the sax and they would be perceived completely differently. 

AD: Like we were talking about, Brendan and Joe have always had a funkiness to their pocket, even in Fugazi, which perhaps went unnoticed at times due to the aesthetic of it all. I’d never paint this record as being more rhythm & blues, but there is a new element to the compositions that remind me of that at times. 

Anthony Pirog: The writing is looser. As a trio we wrote, Brendan has put it as “over wrote” which is just a way to say the improv is limited to certain sections to maintain the intent and not get too jammy. But with a quartet I can be comping chords and it takes the pressure off of me. What you are saying is accurate. For the rhythm on something like “That Thang,” James sent us the melody as a voice memo on the phone. Joe came up with that bass line, that reminds me of like Afro Blue or something, it’s just intuitive for them. 

AD: Like they say, jazz is a conversation, and this record really captures the living feeling of a moving dialogue. 

Anthony Pirog: None of these songs would be what they are without everyone’s input. It happened so fast. There isn’t even a chance to talk about what it should be like.

AD: Any other releases we should be on the lookout for?

Anthony Pirog: Yes, The Hunger Artist was released in April by Otherly Love Records, the same label as the Nepenthe Series, which is a 30-piece ensemble composition featuring Rob Mazurek. Side B is an improvisational track featuring Janel, Jarrett Gilgore on sax, Luke Stewart on bass and Jason Nazary on drums. In June our third Janel and Anthony album is coming out on Cuneiform Records, a double LP called New Moon and the Evil Age, the first record is all instrumental and the second record has Janel singing on everything. Then in October I have a duo with saxophonist Jerry Gilgore coming out. It’s exciting because there is a lot coming out.
