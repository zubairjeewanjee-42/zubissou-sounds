## Body

Cate Le Bon is an elusive talent, adept at evoking emotional states without fully explicating them, suggesting resonances with other times and places without ever unpacking them. Her latest album, Michelangelo Dying, for instance, has very little to do with the renaissance artist, mentioned in a fragment of “Love Unrehearsed,” and whatever that connection is, Le Bon is uninterested in revealing it. But even so, the piece conveys a fluid, complicated relationship with love and art and longing, one revealed in bits of startling sonic clarity, but also hidden in suggestion, implication and mood. 

Le Bon was more forthcoming about her artistic collaborators, the producer Samur Khouja, saxophonist Euan Hinshelwood, percussionists Dylan Hadley and Valentina Magaletti, keyboardist Paul Jones and a fellow Welshman you might have heard of, John Cale. But ask her about the nature of the heartache documented in Michelangelo, or the meaning its luminous, shards of imagery, and she’ll defer to listeners. “You have to acknowledge that when you make things and you let go of them, then they’re no longer yours and people will form all kinds of ideas. You kind of want to rail against that sometimes when people get the wrong end of the stick. But you also have to accept that that’s how it is. You make things, and you let them go,” she says. | j kelly

Aquarium Drunkard: I wanted to ask you about the image of Michelangelo dying, which comes up in “Love Unrehearsed.” I was looking at his bio and was somewhat surprised to find that he lived into his late 80s and worked the whole time. In fact, he was working on a pretty major project when he died. I was wondering if there was something about him and his genius across multiple art forms, or his creative longevity that appealed to you?

Cate Le Bon: It’s a complete metaphor. It’s absolutely not directly about Michelangelo himself.

AD: What is it a metaphor for? 

Cate Le Bon: It’s layered, I suppose, and I don’t necessarily feel the need to kind of decode the meaning behind the title. It will maybe mean different things to different people the more they listen to the record. I think the ambiguity kind of allows it to be more porous than giving it any kind of definitive meaning for anyone.

AD: You’ve said that this album sort of thrust itself forward when you were planning something different. I was wondering if you could talk a little bit about that. 

Cate Le Bon: I was kind of feeling the need to throw myself into making my own record. I’d worked on a few records for different people and was kind of excited to get sucked into something of my own. I had this outline feeling of what I wanted it to be. I wanted it to be something hard and angular and, you know, industrial maybe, but it just was not coming out that way. 

I realized that the record that is Michelangelo Dying was just… I couldn’t sidestep it anymore. I had to roll up my sleeves and write and experience the heartache I was going through. Through the medium of music and lyricism. It’s a record, I think, that’s really kind of happening in real time. You know, I was writing about it and about the pain of heartache in real time. 

AD: Did you plan to use different instruments or different sounds and had to switch around what you were doing? 

Cate Le Bon: A little bit. I never really give myself too much of a map when I’m making something. There’s no repeatable process as such. It’s more a starting point and a feeling. I imagine this feeling of wanting to make something angular and hard was just complete denial or rejection of wanting to look something in the eye, you know. 

AD: It’s all about love, but it seems not an especially happy kind of love. There’s a lot of falling apart and alienation and missing people in the lyrics. Was that especially relevant during the period you were working on it?

Cate Le Bon:  Yeah, I spent a long time trying to outrun heartache, and it wasn’t really doing me any good. It was just disrupting me and meant that I was constantly on the go. And so I think, you know, this kind is a very necessary kind of, I suppose, looking it in the eye, but with the familiarity of music as a medium to do it. You know, it was kind of just, I think once I kind of allowed myself to make this record. It all just was quite a fluid kind of expression.

AD: It seems like the concept of mothers comes up a couple of times. Was that something you were thinking about? 

Cate Le Bon: I mean, yeah. It was almost like free writing in a sense, the kind of cathartic, fractured nature of your thoughts when you’re going through something that is painful. I was pulling all kinds of threads, and I allowed myself to do that. So, yeah, mothers, accidentally comes up. That’s an image that, comes up actually on a lot of records that I’ve made.

AD: You include things that are happening to you personally, but you’ve got this sort of historical resonance as well. You’re always using images that seem not of the current moment, you know? Is that something you think about? 

Cate Le Bon: I think that love, when it’s big, feels kind of ancient and monolithic. Historical imagery feels right to me. I’m probably gravitating towards those things because of the timelessness of love. You feel all the ages and no age at all when you’re in the midst of it you know

AD: I really like the synthesizer sounds on this album. They’ve got almost like a slippery, uncanny valley feel to them. Like they don’t feel quite real, but yet they’re really beautiful and massive and layered. I was wondering, were they shaped by the kind of equipment we’re using or influences you were thinking about it? And can you talk about how you came up with this or the sound of this album? 

Cate Le Bon: It’s a combination. I use the OB-6 and the H3000 a lot.  It’s blending those things and layering those things with saxophones or sometimes guitars that are heavily effected. It all kind of lent itself to using the record as a way to kind of experience and heal. I became quite lost in this kind of world of making sounds. It felt like healing or like they were expressing an emotion that I didn’t have words for.

AD: You’re working with a lot of the same people on this album as you have before, including the producer Samur Khouja. Can you tell me a little bit about your relationship with him and how long you’ve known him and what you admire about his work and what he added to the project?

Cate Le Bon: We’ve worked together for a long time. It was on Reward where he started to co-produce and Pompeii was the first record that we entirely co-produced together. He is exceptional at listening and he’s exceptional at letting there be space for you, the artist as well as, you know, the co-producer to to explore without… somebody telling you where to look. He’s very good at encouraging you to push yourself, but he’s also very good at reining you back in at the moments where you need to be reined back. It’s never prescriptive advice. I think we just have this lovely understanding of what we both need from each other when we’re working on things and he is very brilliant. And I think this is quite rare, but he’s very good at inspiring you, not by kind of a show of how talented he is, but he’s very good at inspiring you by showing you how talented he thinks you are. And it’s a really beautiful kind of shift, extracting something from somebody rather than putting something on someone.

AD: Do you think the fact that you are a pretty well-established, talented producer with, I’d assume, strong ideas about everything, do you think that makes you easier to work with or harder? 

Cate Le Bon: I think I could only… I mean, Samar and I have this very beautiful understanding… and it works, you know, I wonder if I tried to work with someone else if it would, what would happen?

AD: Do you want to talk about some of the other people you worked with on this album? I know you had the same saxophone player that you used on Pompeii, Euan Hinshelwood. 

Cate Le Bon: Yes, these are people who I’ve worked with for a long time and, also people who push themselves to evolve and master their instruments in different ways. Euan is constantly trying different ways and forms of expression with the saxophone and trying to understand emotionally what it is I want from the instrument. To play competently is one thing but it’s another thing entirely to really understand what someone is looking for emotionally from the instrument. It takes a different kind of skill set. These are all extremely generous musicians who are willing to evolve as I’ve needed different things from them. 

AD: You don’t feel compelled to try new people to get new sounds?

Cate Le Bon: I don’t think I need to, because they’re all so adaptive and so curious and it’s a beautiful evolution, I think, as a unit.

And yeah, and then Dylan [Hadley] she’s exceptional. She’s such a curious, generous musician. Valentina Magaletti, too. I’ve wanted to work with her for a long time and she’s, she’s unreal, such a talent, so it’s really inspiring to be around her. And then Paul, you know, Pau Jones, I’ve known him for years, but this is the first time I had him play on a record. I suppose this is where you find out about my limitations as a piano player. When someone like Paul comes and plays on a record, it really does just kind of open things up in a really exceptional way.

AD: How did you end up singing with John Cale on “Ride.”

Cate Le Bon: I really wanted him to be on the record. There’s a soothing kind of familiarity to him. I mean, his voice just feels like it’s been around since the beginning of time. I wanted to ask him, but, you know, when you really admire someone and want them to do something, it’s hard. I felt a bit frightened and didn’t ask. Then the record was mixed once, and I wanted to try and mix it again in LA with Samur. I felt like I needed to take it back to him. And that’s when I was like, right, this really is like the last call to get John on the record and, you know, you really have to be a bit brave and ask him. So, I kind of took the opportunity in when things went off-piste in the first round of mixing. They were not really working. Instead of seeing it as a negative, I tried to see it as an opportunity to ask John to be on the record.

AD: So, the song was basically finished and you sent it to him and he did what he did? 

Cate Le Bon: Yeah, and he sent back with just beautiful vocals. In the last chorus, he sings, “It’s my last ride.” And, you know, that wasn’t in the song. I asked him to sing the choruses with me. He changed the last chorus to “It’s my last ride,” and it’s so beautiful that it just took the wind out of me made me cry. 

AD: It’s a nice moment. Had you met him before this?

Cate Le Bon: It was about eight years ago he asked me to do a show with him in London and then, following that, I played three shows in his band in Paris with him. And then I’ve done, you know, some bits with him over the years. 

AD: You’re both Welsh. Do you talk about that when you’re together? 

Cate Le Bon: Yeah, kind of. You know, it’s really nice to hear him speak Welsh. And he did when I was doing those shows. I think it was at the Paris show he asked me to sing “Sunday Morning.” 

AD: Oh, wow. 

Cate Le Bon: Yeah, he came into the kitchen and he was like, our management have told me that we’ve got to do “Sunday Morning,” but I’m not singing who you are. So, I’m going, okay. 

AD: So, you got to be Nico. 

Cate Le Bon: I did, but then I started singing it and he started taking the piss on account of my accent. And I’m like, John, you have a very similar accent. 

AD: At this point you’re almost as well-known as a producer as you are as an artist. Is there anything you’re working on in that vein that you want to talk about? 

Cate Le Bon: I’m just finishing a record with a band called Dry Cleaning. But, you know, I love producing, but I don’t want to be a producer. 

AD: What do you love about it? 

Cate Le Bon: I love the puzzle of making music and trying to… I think I love really listening to people in the studio. That’s what I really try and do is just listen to what someone is telling me rather than get carried away with what I want. And I know I’ve been in situations before where I’ve not been listened to. So I think it’s the, you know, the most important part of it. 

But it’s a really lovely, rewarding job, really hard at times. But I don’t want to do too much of it. I just say yes to the things that I think really resonate with me. 

AD: You’re doing a pretty large tour in Europe and the US next. I was wondering how you feel about traveling now with the way things are. 

Cate Le Bon: Do you mean traveling to America or something? I feel okay. You know, I’ve got a lot of love for America. It’s, you know, I’ve lived there and I have a lot of people I love who live there. I guess I haven’t really thought about it too much.

AD: What are you doing to the music to make it translate for the live experience. Does it take a lot of adjustment? 

Cate Le Bon: No I mean I’m just bringing the best band that I can put together. It’s an exceptional band and we are, yeah, we’re going to, you know, play the record as it is. And yeah, I mean, I figured touring is so financially kind of futile anyway. I might as well just… go for it, you know.

AD: Does the new material mix pretty with the stuff that you’ve done before? It seems like it probably would. 

Cate Le Bon: Yeah, I think so. I think so. At the moment, we’re starting to kind of piece what the set would be together. A lot of thought goes into it obviously. We’re starting kind of to circle around it all at the moment and figure out how best to execute things. Euan, who plays saxophone on the record and is a dear friend, he’s [the musical director for] the whole thing. It’s close friends who are brilliant musicians so everyone’s you know, been really, again, really generous with their time and thoughtfulness in starting to go into the touring cycle. 

AD: Are you planning any covers? 

Cate Le Bon: I possibly am. Yeah, there’s two I’ve got in my, yeah, in my back pocket. I think I might try out, but it’s too soon to say. 

AD: You mentioned the moment where John Cale comes in and changes the chorus to “Ride” as one of the things that makes the bottom drop out for you. Are there any other moments on this album that you are particularly struck by? 

Cate Le Bon: Yeah, there’s some. I made the record and then I sat on it for a year because I was too exhausted to tour. And so I decided to just put it off and tour it when I had more in the tank. And, and so it’s kind of a really lovely revisiting it with that space in between and I guess understanding it in a different way because, you know, I was writing in real time. Looking back, I understand things differently, which is nice. I connect with it differently. That’s really nice. But yeah, there are moments that I’ve been listening to recently as we’re trying to get our heads around how to do the record live. There are things that I’d forgotten about maybe. When some of the saxophone solos come in or, you know, there’s a lot of really beautiful, percussive moments that Valentina’s done and they’re really lovely. There’s a lot of layers in each song. So I’ve been enjoying, you know, remembering all the different parts that make up the whole.

AD: Have you heard anything good lately from other artists? 

Cate Le Bon: Yeah, I’m just trying to think now. I’m really enjoying the Cameron Winter record. I think that’s really lovely. Feels real, you know, feels really just like a real expression of something. And Dylan, who plays drums for me. Her band’s record, Sharpie Smile is great. An artist called Linnéa Talp, made a really beautiful record. It’s drone music, I would say, and it’s just got that porous quality where it feels very unique and emotional and it kind of attaches itself to you know your inner or your outer environment depending on what you need from it.

AD: What about books or movies? Anything that’s caught your attention?

Cate Le Bon: I’ve been reading a lot of Rachel Kusk. And Samantha Harvey, her book Orbital was really beautiful. And On the Calculation of Volume [by Solvej Balle] is really great too. And then movies, I don’t know, I’ve just come back from holidays. I haven’t seen anything lately. But okay, . I did go and see the new Jurassic Park yesterday. 

AD: Did you like it? 

Cate Le Bon: Yeah. I don’t know. I’ve got no idea. I came out and I was like, I have no idea how I felt about that. 

AD: I remember the first of those movies, just being floored by like seeing dinosaurs on the screen, but that’s sort of old hat now.

Cate Le Bon: The first one’s one of my favorite movies. 

AD: So, if you could get people to understand one thing about you and your music and this record, is there anything you’d like to get across?

Cate Le Bon: No, I mean, I think you have to acknowledge that when you make things and you let go of them, then they’re no longer yours and people will form all kinds of ideas. You kind of want to rail against that sometimes when people get the wrong end of the stick. But you also have to accept that that’s how it is. You make things, and you let them go. I’m fully, fully accepting of that. You know, it’s no longer mine. I guess I can reclaim it a bit when we play it live. But so, yeah, I just think more of an acceptance that people will think what they want, you know.
