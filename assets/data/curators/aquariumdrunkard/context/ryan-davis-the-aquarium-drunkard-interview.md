## Body

The first time listening to New Threats from the Soul, the new album from Ryan Davis & The Roadhouse Band, is an experience in euphoria as well as joyful frustration because you want to stop immediately and text a certain line to a specific friend, but then three more lines fly by that immediately remind you of three different friends. Davis’s lyrics can cause a belly laugh, choked-down tears, or serve as a rallying cry.

I learned that time was not my friend nor my foe more like one of the guys from work.
I’m pushing this lawnmower down Broadway in a windstorm twirlin’ like a sex tape in a microwave.
My heart sank like an informant in a lake. 
Now it’s a pissing competition between the man I am and the guy I was.
These selections are only a few out of many different little perfect jewels from New Threats from the Soul.

However, it’s not only about the lyrics with Davis; it’s also his delivery of a line, his feeling for the impeccable melodic companion, and his instincts as a bandleader. There are more musical and lyrical ideas in one of Davis’s songs than many artists have on an entire album. While New Threats from the Soul is a beautiful continuation of what Davis and The Roadhouse Band started with their first album, Dancing on the Edge, the new work sparks with artistry, intention, and confidence. 

Aquarium Drunkard interviewed Davis in the run-up to the release of New Threats from the Soul. There’s a version of this interview that could have resembled the old SNL sketch “The Chris Farley Show”: “Remember when you compared yourself to Babe Ruth belting out Danny Boy in a men’s room stall…that was awesome.”

Instead, topics included the beginning of Davis’s songwriting with his first band State Champion, the different struggles he faced in making the two albums, the comedy in his songwriting, and more. This conversation has been edited and condensed. | s bunn

Aquarium Drunkard: What was the first song you ever wrote?

Ryan Davis: When I first started writing lyrics, I was the singer of punk and pop-punk bands back in early high school, probably ninth grade, and that was when I first gave it a go at trying to write songs. But I never actually sat down with a guitar until I was probably like 20, and at the time, it didn’t really have a name. I just taught myself some chords and tried to see if I could figure out how to do it. That became the very first State Champion demo. That would have been, probably to the month, about 20 years ago. In 2005, I had moved to Glasgow, Scotland to study abroad for a semester, and I decided that I was going to get away from home and bring a guitar and figure it out. 

AD: With State Champion and now with your two solo albums, you mostly work on a big canvas, long songs. Has that always been the case from the beginning?

Davis: I think they just keep getting longer, unfortunately. I need to do something to tuck them in a little bit. But yeah, I find it harder and harder to get into the zone with a song where it’s something like a place I want to live. It takes me a long time to write songs. I don’t know if it’s an intentional decision, but I feel like if I’m going to be inside of a song, I want to stay there for a bit and work through all the ideas that could potentially help the song be its best version of itself. So, they do end up getting a verse or two longer than most people would choose, but I personally don’t feel like it’s ever excessive. I’m never trying to write long songs for the sake of writing long songs. I always feel like I’ve done the best I can to tell the story I’m telling, and then I get out and go on to the next one.

AD: The melodies for your songs are universal in that they often seem familiar, but I don’t think you’re cribbing anything from another song. Are your melodies always created in service of the lyrics or do you also write melodies first and compose lyrics to fit the music? 

Davis: Yeah, it’s a good question. Most things with my songs start with the lyrics. But that’s not necessarily starting with the song. I’ve got these binders full of phrases and images and rhymes and things that are not songs, but they’re just things that I like thinking about. And I go through and say, “Oh, this is a good one to start with.” And then I flip through and say, “Oh, this feels like the world of the song.” I think that’s when the melody stuff comes in. As I start, I get three chords I like, and I say, “Well, how would I sing this?” 

I think with the Roadhouse Band, I just have so much fun singing. With State Champion, there’s a lot of passion and energy and howling and late nights in a basement or a bar and screaming to be heard. But I feel like now I’m really enjoying having the band be quiet at times, and I can sing in a way that I’ve never sung. Or sometimes I surprise myself with a melody where I’m like, “I don’t even know where that came from.” Or sometimes I will go back and say, “Oh, that actually sounds like another song.” It is something I’ve thought about more with these two records than I ever have leading up to this point in my life, the melody and the singing thing. It’s not like it’s new to me, but I’m using it in a new way.

AD: You’re also a visual artist and you showed me one of your notebooks. It’s interesting to see that there’s drawings next to the writing that you’re doing. How much do they connect to one another? 

Davis: I was thinking about this recently, how I feel like when I’m drawing something for album art, it’s more in the design realm. I’ve made drawings since I was a kid, so, when I started playing shows and being in punk bands, I would make flyers and stuff. But when I’m drawing just for the sake of drawing, I think of those as poems in this weird way. The way I draw, it feels like it’s pulled from the same sort of cosmos as a poem on the page would be. Whereas I don’t think of my songs as poems at all. I think of them as vignettes or stories, or whatever you want to call it. Even if it’s just purely image or if I incorporate text into my drawings, that feels truly beholden to nothing but whatever the spirit of that thing is. That’s my version of poems. The songs are kind of poetic in their own way. I feel like they all sort of live in the same universe, but they also are independent of one another. 

I think when I first decided to start calling this my name, I wanted to throw it all in there. Because with State Champion, we used to always have photos on the covers, and I made a conscious decision to say, “I’m going to make it my artwork. I’m going to make it my words. I’m going to make this me.” 

AD: You said in an interview that in the time before Dancing on the Edge that you had a period of “struggle and self-doubt” as songwriting was a “battle” and the process of writing the song “Bluebirds in Flight” helped you understand how to write songs again. What was it about that song that helped you unlock something?

Davis: I had a lot of what I guess you would say personal baggage with writing songs for so long and being so tangled up in my identity and who I was and what people knew me as I was. State Champion was very much what people thought of who knew me when they thought of me and how I knew a lot of people I’d become friends with on the road. Then starting to do the Tropical Trash thing; I was becoming more and more of a songwriter in that band. And I think I just really burned myself out. We were going so hard, and we weren’t ever making any money. There were a lot of disagreements on what should happen with the music. Then it seemed like there was a course of events where things were just not fun anymore and kind of sad. And I was feeling bummed out, and I wanted to take some time off from it. And then COVID happened, so that forced me out of that world. Instead of saying, “I’m going to bunker down and just get better at this.” I really took some time away, and that’s when I started drawing a lot again. I started doing everything but writing songs. I bought a coronet. I was like, “Let’s see if I can figure out how to play this.” I bought weird MIDI equipment to see if I chain this drum machine up to this synthesizer—can I get it to work on a clock? And I would spend all day reading instruction manuals figuring out how to do that. There were these little sparks of creative things that were rewarding, but it wasn’t songwriting. 

Then, at a certain point, I felt the desire to get back into that mode again. “Bluebirds in a Fight” — if I had the lyrics in front of me, I could probably even tell you what the first words of it were that came together. And I was like, “I think there’s a song there” for the first time in three or four years. Once I got through that, I was like, “I think this is still good, and I can do this again.” So “Bluebirds in a Fight” is a very important song and what this whole thing has become over the last couple years. It hasn’t gotten easier in a lot of ways. It’s still really hard for me to do it. The obstacle was still worth it. Getting through that first song, it made a path for the rest of it.

AD: Did you have a similar time of struggle before the new album?

Davis: Yeah [laughs].

AD: Was there something like “Bluebirds in a Fight” that helped push you forward?

Davis: The struggle leading up to Dancing on the Edge was more of a personal one and sort of an existential one. I was really not in a place I wanted to be in life, and I was questioning a lot of things and trying to sort of figure out my purpose as a person. And then the actual writing of Dancing on the Edge, while not easy it, was really liberating and rewarding, especially getting in the studio and making it a thing was really fun. 

But I think making New Threats from the Soul was kind of the opposite. I was in a better place in life, and I was enjoying making music again. I had some stuff figured out, but the actual writing of the songs felt virtually impossible throughout a lot of the time that I was working on it, “Mutilation Springs” and “Mutilation Falls,” especially. It was like The Da Vinci Code or something. I would stare at it every day and try to figure out how to make it all work. I would take verses away, and then it would kind of fall apart. Or, I would add things, and it would be too much. I finally got it where it needed to be, but it was the hardest record I’ve ever written. I would think about it 24 hours a day. It started to become sort of unhealthy, honestly. I look forward to it being a positive thing where we learn it as a band, and we tour and stuff. We’re going to pass the struggle of it, but maybe I should have been easier on myself. It wasn’t a ton of fun to write this record.

AD: It’s a blast to listen to.

Davis. I don’t want to make music that’s a struggle to listen to as much as it is to make. I think “Monte Carlo” was the first one I wrote. “New Threats from the Soul” was the next one. I think I had the melody and some of the words for “Simple Joy” floating for the longest. But it was actually the last, I think because I knew it was going to be a fun one to write and an easier one for me to write because there’s not a lot of changes. It’s a nice playing field for lyrics, and you can plug stuff in. I saved that one for last, actually. The words for that one have been circulating since Dancing on the Edge. That’s the one we’ve started playing as a band, because it was the easiest to learn. We said on tour, “Hey, let’s sound check with that” and it was immediately fun to play. 

AD: With Dancing on the Edge, you had two separate songs — “Bluebirds in a Fight” and “Bluebirds Revisited” — with different melodies but some of the same phrases and themes. With the new album, you’ve done the same thing again with “Mutilation Springs” and “Mutilation Falls.” Are these two distinct songs, or a coda, a sequel, or one long piece of songwriting that’s too long to just be one song?  

Davis: As unsexy of an answer as it is, I think it’s the latter. It’s too much for one song, but I don’t think I’m done with the ideas yet, so, how do we do it? Where it kind of splits the embryo and have maybe the more emotionally affective one, and then the one that’s something else. The vocal delivery of “Bluebirds Revisited” is a different tone than the one for “Bluebirds in a Fight.” I love all four of those songs, and I like what we did with them, but I hate that this is maybe perceived as being a move that I make where I do these self-referential songs. It ended up being something that made sense to do. I didn’t intend to do it again on the next record, but it just worked out that way, and I’m glad it did because I think they both have their own thing that they do and they kind of wink at each other.

AD: I hate to point this out, but you’ve also got “Sunbathing I” and “Sunbathing II” on State Champion’s Fantasy Error.

Davis: [laughs] You’re the second person that has mentioned that because I said, “I just started doing this thing where I write two songs” and someone’s like, “Technically, you did it on Fantasy Error too.”Touché.

AD: I mean, if it is a move, maybe embrace it.

Davis: That’s where I’m at now. Maybe this is a thing that I do, and I don’t have to do it every time, but when I do, there’s a reason for it, and I think it provides something to the album.

AD: I want to ask about repetition in your songwriting, but even more about returning to a detail and adding a different little twist to the lyric each time: the doorbell that doesn’t work on “Monte Carlo/ No Limits”; the white flag on “Better If You Make Me” the ATM on “Walden Pawn.” Is it like a comedian doing a callback to an earlier joke or are you riffing on a theme?

Davis: That’s a good way of putting it. I’ve never thought about it like that, but I think the songs are funny. I’m not like Ween or Flight of the Conchords or Tenacious, D. I’m laughing while making them, even when there’s a minor chord. Thinking of it like a comedy act, especially with how long the songs are, is interesting. I never wanted to get into this place of [when the songwriting] feels random, where I’m just spraying poetry at the wall. I like the challenge of sculpting it into something with some sort of perceived meaning, even if I don’t know what that meaning is. Or, maybe it’s still ambiguous or mysterious, but there’s enough detail there to form your own image. So, I think coming back to things that I liked singing about the first time and providing another angle of light on them is a way to make a song feel like you’re still in it, seven minutes in.

AD: There are so many fascinating and beautiful musical flourishes on the new album, like the distortion/feedback flares in “Monte Carlo / No Limits” or the Atari game music intros and changing soundscapes on “Mutilation Springs” and “Mutilation Falls.” How do the words and melodies influence the sonics of your recordings and vice versa? 

Davis: Well, there’s a lot to say. You called it video game music. That was a melody that I really liked playing on piano for years. I would just play it in my studio, and I thought that it was going to be a song, and I could never quite figure out how to write to it. But then with “Mutilation Springs” and “Falls,” I was playing it a lot in the studio, and I was thinking of them in this weird way as TV episodes and that those little piano riffs were almost theme songs to a sitcom. We didn’t have that idea until we were sequencing, and I recorded them on a four track. The preface is like the next episode of “Mutilation Falls,” like you’re introducing the cast of characters again and catching up. 

[The band and I] listen to so many different kinds of music, and, admittedly, we’re not super well-versed in alternative country or new folk music. People play me that kind of stuff, and, oftentimes, I like it, but it’s not something that I’m super-educated on. We listen to a lot of experimental music, electronic music, and avant garde and improvisational music. I think it’s in our nature to apply that to the formal four walls of a country rock song. You get us all in a room and we have some beers and it’s hard to stop us from throwing as many different sounds at the walls as we can. Oftentimes, it doesn’t work, and it’s awkward or it feels like it’s just being weird for the sake of being weird and we’ll cut that stuff out. A lot of the beauty of these records is taking things away. They do seem maximal, in a way. It’s not random. I want to hear a different kind of sound throughout a song about a breakup or a song about someone having hard times.

AD: Does your involvement in the instrumental band Equipment Pointed Ankh play into this?

Davis: It’s totally central to it. I feel an endless well of gratitude for that band because I was feeling pretty stuck during those years after State Champion. So, it was peak COVID, and me and Jim [Marlowe] and basically the Ankh guys went up to Rhode Island with no real plan, and some of the people had to leave during the middle of it were like, “What are we going to do?” Then we just started making some sounds and that turned into the Equipment Pointed Ankh record. That basically established this friendship between these people who said, “Well, we can kind of just do anything, and it doesn’t have to be any one thing.” 

So when I started writing songs again, I was constantly thinking about that Ankh session and saying, “What if there’s no guitars at all? What if all the drums are played with a keyboard?” Equipment Pointed Ankh wears a lot of hats, but, at the end of the day, it’s the same five of us who are on all these records making country music or whatever. I think of it like Wu Tang Clan or something. Ankh is like the Clan, and then you’ve got Flanger Magazine records or Roadhouse Band or Shutaro Noguchi and everybody branches off and does other stuff, but that’s the hub creatively. 

AD: It’s evident by everything you do that you’re a person who has a joy of making things, including songwriting, the bands you’re associated with, running a record label, your visual artwork, the Cropped Out Festival that you did in Louisville for so many years. Where did that commitment to making things come from? 

Davis: The joy of making things has always been there, but I feel like it’s gotten stronger and stronger the older I’ve gotten. Like I said, I keep going back to this time where I wasn’t really touring, and I wasn’t doing the bands I used to do anymore, and I had this realization that doing State Champion—while I liked writing songs and I liked making records—I was really doing it to continue to be able to have something to tour because I really wanted to see the world. I wanted to meet people. I loved getting out there and having weird experiences with my friends. It’s kind of done a full 180 in the last five years, where I really like touring, and it’s still fun for me, but if I never toured again, I would still get a thrill out of getting together in a studio with my friends and solving problems and making things out of nothing. I guess that’s a very simple human desire I’ve always had. How do you make something out of nothing? Doing the label, it’s like, “Well, if no one else wants to be involved, I’ll put out my own records.” With the bands, these are songs that I feel like only I can write. Not because they’re good or bad or anything, I think that they’re very much me. And if I’m going to be a person who’s functioning in this world, I should do the thing I know how to do and that’s making these weird songs or making these drawings. It’s just what keeps me going. To be honest, I get really sad if I don’t have something going on. I have times where I’m trying to schedule just to play Tony Hawk on a PlayStation or something and relax and I can’t do it for more than a couple days before I start losing my mind. I have to be busy. It’s probably not a good thing, but it leads to productive years, for sure.
