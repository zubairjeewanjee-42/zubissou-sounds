# Office Culture :: The Aquarium Drunkard Interview

**Tags:** Office Culture, Mixtapes

## Body

What is enough? And how can you recognize enough when you see it? Pondering notions of contentment, connection, and personal identity, Enough is the latest album from Winston Cook-Wilson’s Sophisti-pop collective Office Culture. Inspired by a dream in which he produced a CD called Enough, the album represents a shift in Cook-Wilson’s whip smart songcraft: employing a wide cast of collaborators, including guest appearances by Alena Spanger, Sam Sodomsky’s The Bird Calls, and Jackie West, he turns his attention to rhythms, textures, and mood, creating mini-movies with each of the album’s 16 songs. From clanking, layered polyrhythms, to melodious fretless bass, to pensive piano ballads, it’s a dynamic listen that feels as personal as it does ambitious. Cook-Wilson joins us to explore the expanses of Enough and share its dreamspace origins and inspirations. | j woodbury

Aquarium Drunkard: When you first sent me this record, you remarked on its sprawl. And it is a big project but I think it’s quite cohesive. You’ve got like 25 people playing on this one right? 

Winston Cook-Wilson: 23, maybe, something like that. 

AD: Did you kind of have it in mind that you wanted to make something big like this, or did it reveal itself as you worked?

Winston Cook-Wilson: Well, I leaned into it at a certain point. I was interested in ‘90s sounds, that turn of the century moment. I love long albums. They had features on them, and worked in more of a four-act structure than side A, side B. You’d have the stylistic diversion. I grew up with those albums and I think in some sense when I got the kind of classic rock education and stuff, I moved away from loving that and thinking more about the side A/B format. 

But I had a dream that I had made a CD. It was specifically a compact disc called Enough and it had a pelican on the cover. This weird dream locked it in. I think I was subconsciously inspired by The Prodigy’s The Fat of the Land CD. I remember seeing that in stores when I was growing up and thinking it was so cool. 

AD: How often do you have dreams related to your musical practice?

Winston Cook-Wilson: Never. Very rarely. I don’t really remember my dreams. I’m not a big “dreams”  person. I get bored when people describe their dreams, to be honest. 

AD: Do pelicans have any special meaning to you? 

Winston Cook-Wilson: You know, I was never really an Instagram animal person until recently, until the period I made this record. I embraced that whole thing as a form of lightness and wonder, with almost a feeling of jealousy at animals’ lives being so simple. I was fascinated with the look of pelicans and certain birds. So I think that is where it came from—but I also have childhood memories of Roald Dahl’s The Giraffe and the Pelly and Me; I was always obsessed with how they could blow their beaks up.

The dream was a Lyncian moment. He talks about having the idea of an ear in grass, which went into Blue Velvet. I always want to have fun and incorporate some aspect of humor into the records, and that was part of it. I feel like a lot of people have talked about my music and called it ironic or jokey or something, when I’m making extremely sincere music. With this I wanted to be like, “Even though this is very heavy and thematically, I just wanted to have some jokes in it. This is what it sounds like when I’m making a joke.” 

AD: I’m glad you brought up humor. I thought “Hat Guy” was a very humorous song. When I Google image search you, I only see you wearing one hat, in your Vice profile pic. 

Winston Cook-Wilson: That’s interesting.

AD: So you don’t strike me as a hat guy? 

Winston Cook-Wilson: I am now. I wear a hat all the time, I’m balding. That was another thing related to this “dream practice.” I would wake up in the morning often and improvise for 30 minutes and record. A lot of ideas came out of that. I wasn’t exactly dreaming but I was trying to access this half-asleep state. I just woke up with that end lyric about being a hat guy in my head. I’ve been making jokes about with my friends who are balding but it took on this different valence because the earlier part of the song is all about wondering how you’re being perceived by another person, throwing your own sense of self into ambiguity. The first part of the song is very serious. It’s based on a real experience trying to figure out what somebody is about and how that feels, like looking in a warped mirror. And so the end of it totally relates to it, but it obviously feels incongruous in the way that it’s said, but thematically it’s in line.

AD: I love the way this record sounds, but I wonder: Are there any sounds on this record where maybe you were attracted to an idea or sonic element, but there was a fear that it was going to be perceived as, like you said, ironic or kitschy or anything like that? 

Winston Cook-Wilson: There are moments, but there always are in some sense. I wanted to create moods with every song. I feel like with my previous music, I employed these kinds of sleeker aesthetics and people would write off the aesthetic and therefore miss the songs. But for me, it was a very earnest musical language through which I could express those feelings. 

With this record, I was kind of trying a lot of dirtier sounds. I wanted to think about every song in terms of mood and texture. A lot of it came from building beats, sampling records. I’ve always been interested in that. But yeah, I just kind of dug into that. I think I was doing production work with other people. I just got more interested in production and interested in challenging myself. I was also getting into painting, and when you’re painting, it sounds corny, but when you’re painting, it’s like you just have this canvas you can fill with anything. That’s how I wanted every song to feel.

AD: The bio even cites the Wu-Tang Clan. In your morning practice, would that sometimes include early morning beat-making too? 

Winston Cook-Wilson: Sometimes. Or I would have a beat that I would then sing over it in the morning. There’s a story about J. Dilla in Dan Charnas’ book Dilla Time, and there’s this whole thing about his regime of listening to records in the morning and then making beats in the early afternoon and then signing off. There was a lot going on that inspired me to have that kind of ethic and believe that that could yield something worthwhile. Then you do that, but then you have to comb back through it and isolate, and that’s the Mark Hollis Talk Talk thing comes in, that approach of “Let’s isolate the phrase that’s totally spontaneous and weird that you would never land on if you were sitting on a piano and just honing it that way you could only get at the moment of inspiration.”

AD: In “The Counting Game” you talk about the “alchemy of the soul,” and you’ve got a good thief in the night reference, but you sing, “Am I doomed to see no flash/feel no thunder.” Is that how it feels for you? Do you often feel like you’re lacking a sign from the universe? 

Winston Cook-Wilson: That’s a very personal line I’m happy to talk about. And there’s also the line that relates directly to the song “Was I Cruel” in a way, which is “it’s so easy to follow your heart. What’s hard is not doing it and doing it well.” I mean, there’s a lot of cryptic stuff where I’m not totally sure where it’s coming from—but it is about wading into the water of trying to trying learn about somebody specifically be in a relationship or grow romantically close to somebody when you have a lot of triggers or concerns  and you went through a lot in past relationships. That line is about trying to shed a certain amount of guardedness. How can I be true to myself while giving space to another person? But there’s a nightmare quality to that song, too. To me, what’s eerie is like, “Am I going to get it wrong? Am I going to get lost in this dark forest of doubt?” 

AD: To go back to the beat-driven thing, I have noticed a marked increase in my own attraction to music of that nature from the ‘80s and ‘90s, The KLF, Chemical Brothers, things like that. 

Winston Cook-Wilson: Yeah, I mean, I’m totally with you. I feel like there’s been a renewed interest in it in recent years. I’ve always been interested in just good beats and different kinds of beats and trying to make different kinds of beats. So I think I was gravitating most consciously towards things that might be termed like trip-hop or something, dustier hip hop beats. But then I kind of started to move towards wanting to do things at higher BPMs that have a cluby quality, which is kind of where my brain is at now, digging into that even more. 

AD: I look back on liking that music when I first heard it as a young person, but not necessarily having the context for it. Moby was a big one for me too, Play helped me start to figure it out. 

Winston Cook-Wilson: Moby’s Play was huge for me growing up. With the song “We Used to Build Things,” I would joke that it’s my version of The Sopranos theme song, Alabama 3 doing their pop version of those club styles. I think that aspect seeped in. I love Portishead, Tricky, and Janet Jackson—she’s huge for me, one of my favorites ever. The stuff she did in the ‘90s, experimenting with practically every type of beat. Plus Nine Inch Nails, people who were serious about production but crafting pop too. 

AD: Nostalgia, pop, cultural interests, all of that stuff is cyclical; I know you’re a big Springsteen guy too. As a huge Springsteen guy, I am bummed out that there’s not a whole record that sounds like “Streets of Philadelphia” and “Secret Garden.” I love Bruce over a boom-bap beat. There’s an alternate world where there’s a sort of synth-pop Bruce record, a synth Nebraska. I’m not holding out hope for it now that Tom Morello shows up on the records sometimes. 

Winston Cook-Wilson: Totally. I think there’s a “Late Era” aspect to this. [Editor’s note: Late Era is the name of a great podcast Cook-Wilson hosted with Andy Cush and Sam Sodomsky, examining the later work of artists like Kate Bush, Paul Simon, Neil Young, and others.] I was also super into what more arty artists were doing at that time. A huge influence was Nine Horses, a project from David Sylvian; Bowie’s Outside, his take on industrial music. Scott Walker. Those are people that are, in terms of their ethos, always in the background for me. They were challenging their own voice to shine through in very different contexts and always trying to make things that didn’t sound like anything else. When you talk about a Scott Walker record, you can only describe it as sounding like Scott Walker. No other way to describe it. That’s what I’m always chasing, but they would always be listening to what was going on and thinking about juxtaposition and stuff.

AD: The late eras of Scott Walker, Lou Reed and David Bowie—all three of their final records to me indicate a triple North Star situation where you’re like, “These don’t sound like anything else in their catalog.” And they are all collabs. You’ve got Lulu with Metallica, Soused with Sunn O))), and Blackstar with Donny McCaslin’s band. But I’m glad you brought David Sylvian. I definitely hear a Dead Bees on Cake-vibe on this record. 

Winston Cook-Wilson: I’m so happy that you hear that and are into that.
