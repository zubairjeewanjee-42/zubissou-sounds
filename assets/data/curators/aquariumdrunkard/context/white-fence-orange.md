## Body

The pre-pandemic timing of the last White Fence record makes it feel even longer than seven years ago. Spurring that sentiment is, of course, also the prolific run of output that preceded it, including the collaborative record with Ty Segall and duo project Drinks with Cate Le Bon. In the span in between, Tim Presley detoured into the visual side of his artistry; his inimitable paintings exhibited coast to coast and collected in the Anthology artbook Under The Banner of Concern. Over the years, the occasional Woodsist Festival set or compilation appearance has steadily kept the White Fence candle flickering. Now we land on Orange, a full-fledged effort equipped with Segall back behind both the console and the drum kit. And not a second of these eleven tracks is wasted. 

For a record rooted in personal themes of love loss and rehabilitation — an unstill life as delineated by Drag City — Orange uncovers an unwavering sonic reality of perceptive optimism. The production is crisper, the vocals unclouded, drums and serrated guitar lines thrilling. If last year’s understated stunner by Cass McCombs felt like a resurgent moment from a vibrant Bay Area scene all of a sudden too far removed (notice the Polk Street thoroughfare reference), Orange certainly follows that sentiment. After the unhurried, lucid and downright foggy atmosphere of I Have To Feed Larry’s Hawk, it’s evident from the jump that the oscillating, jagged rock here harkens back to the sensibilities of earlier gems like For The Recently FoundInnocent. And for those who go way back, perhaps even a touch of the mellow psych guise of Presley’s mid-aughts outfit Darker My Love.

The chiming guitars of “Given Up My Heart” and “Your Eyes” make for pure pop delicacies, and “Evaporating Love” isn’t far behind. It’s quintessential White Fence and a reflective arbiter of San Francisco’s burgeoning jangle pop underground, past and present. “The red herring was the apple in the tree/It was under the nose of you and me”, Presley sings on “That’s Where The Money Goes (Seen From The Celestial Realm)”, a track with Segall’s steadfast drums and a confidently gleaming vocal excursion. Like a warbly and mysterious guiding light, the gently rolling “Unread Books” diverges with subtly spry synths and leaves you pondering the exact questions that the lyrics ponder and convey. In essence, a patented energy that’s as cohesive as it is just a bit in danger of falling apart at the seams. 

Encasing every strung-out stylistic pivot into one, “Blind Your Sun” hits like a thud, all the way through the delivery of Presley’s “I won’t break your heart” line that affectionately culminates the record. Whether or not the record’s namesake purposely represents a bullish vibrancy and warm color palette in contrast to the musical greys and blues of yesteryear, the ripples of such a philosophical shift are felt. Like an unwavering horizon, Orange delivers a collection of songs that weren’t just merely worth the wait, but feels like just the right amalgamation of the musician’s career to date. That’s no “back to the roots” cliché, but rather a steamrolling path forward. | m neeley
