# Catching Up With Phosphorescent

## Body

It’s been five years since the last Phosphorescent album, a dark half decade for most people and especially for those in the performing and creative arts. So it’s not surprising that when Matthew Houck first glimpsed the contours of what would become Revelator, the album seemed like it would be quiet, insular, inward-looking and maybe a bit of a downer. 

But a funny thing happened when Houck began working and reworking this material. It caught an updraft of hope, of expansiveness, or cosmic revelation. First and second takes with a core band yielded a free, unfettered sound, not perfect or finished, but a foundation for further refinement. A marathon day with Jim White of the Dirty Three and Xylouris White added volcanic percussion that Houck says still gives him goosebumps whenever he hears it. And mixing sessions with Dave Cerminara scrubbed the murk from Houck’s initial vision for Revelator, adding depth and uplift and body-shocking drum sounds. “That was part of the process where it really went the smaller record I was envisioning to something else,” says Houck. “It was like, oh no, this can be opened up and it’s better for it.It became a more physical record.”

We talked about the evolution of this latest, stunning Phosphorescent album, as well as Houck’s process, wide-ranging sources of inspiration (including a urinal wall at a club in Atlanta) and his work on the upcoming Paul Schrader film Oh Canada! 

And we talked about an album that transmutes the doubts and frustrations of the last few years into sounds that soar. “I hope Revelator is powerful and beautiful and uplifting. That’s what these songs do for me, but sometimes when I worry about how other people will receive them, I worry that it’s too heavy, too sad,” he says. “I hope the music works the same way for you as it does for me.” | j kelly

Aquarium Drunkard: I wanted to ask you about the name, Revelator, which has all these connotations, a Blind Willie Johnson song, “John the Revelator” and a Gillian Welch album called Time the Revelator and even the book of Revelations. What does Revelator mean to you in the context of this album?

Matthew Houck: Yeah. I actually worried about those connotations a little bit. Words are weird, aren’t they? I worried about it having those connotations because I don’t think it should have those connotations. But to me it was more about the revelatory-ness of what this album is. For me, it was a revelatory album.

AD: I feel like you’re always kind of digging below the surface of the world as we experience it to find something spiritual underneath?

Matthew Houck: I hope so. I like that, the idea of the underneath. I feel like it’s a front brain/back brain kind of thing. You have your front brain. You’re navigating this world. You’re doing things and approaching things with a decisiveness. But there’s something going on in the background at all times which I don’t think levels up or comes to the surface specifically until you’re making this kind of art. 

So for me it becomes revelatory in the sense that I have front brain notion of things, but in fact something else is happening. I’m grappling with those ideas—or just poking at them, grappling makes it sound more strenuous than it should be. It’s more like meeting them, meeting those things and seeing what they’re like.

AD: That song, “Revelator” was your way into the album. Can you talk about that? It’s been a while since you made an album. How did you start working on that song and how did it become the seed?

Matthew Houck: Yeah. I think of albums as albums still. Not to discount a single song but to me a single song isn’t much most of the time. I don’t think of them as a work until there’s a body of work around it, even if it’s a small body of work. To me, there’s a theme and a sound and a feel and a universe around each record. 

I’d like to get away from that, to be honest. I’d like to be able to make tunes and have them be untethered. But I tend to not really even know what a song is until it’s part of an album. A song can go so many different directions and without a cohesive framework, there’s not always a clear direction within the song itself as to what the larger direction is. 

AD: Were you blocked? Because you’ve got this verse that says, “Now how can I get it right/I don’t like what I write/I don’t even like what I like anymore.” It sounds like you were struggling?

Matthew Houck: I don’t know if blocked is the word, but something was bothering me. I think if pressed, you could draw a through line through all Phosphorescent work, with having something, grappling with something that I don’t know where to go with it. It’s easy for me to get into a very jaded or unaffected space. Yeah, it gets to you, you know?

AD: But the songs have a fair amount of wonder and magic in them. Especially “Impossible House,” which has a lot of really evocative imagery about signs and inexplicable feelings and reading the cards and, in one verse, the movie Home Alone. What do you remember about writing that song?

Matthew Houck: I remember that it tied into the record. Once “Revelator” was the theme, once I understood what the record was, all these songs fit into that. “Impossible House” had maybe been around longer than all the others in a fractured form, just bits and pieces. I feel like I knew what the song was going to sound like, but I don’t think I knew what it was on about. That one…

AD: Are they always about something?

Matthew Houck: That’s the other thing. No. Often they’re more about a feeling, and then you try to put words to it. Sometimes the words are there. But I never sit down with a subject. I never sit down and say, “I’m going to write about X.” I don’t think I’ve ever done that. There’s just some sort of feeling that seems like it needs to be expressed.

But “Impossible House” was a frustrated song. Along with a lot of these tunes, I feel like it’s a downer. It’s a bummer of a record. There’s no two ways about that really. But I hope what you say is true, that they still are hopeful and magical and powerful things. 

AD: I found them to be that way, especially musically. 

Matthew Houck: Good. Because that’s what they’re supposed to do. To keep going back to “Impossible House,” I didn’t want the words to be specific in that song. It’s a bit of a heavy song, but it’s also a tough one. It says we can handle this. It’s about facing up to trickier stuff like relationships going bad. Which is what I think it’s about.

AD: I’m really liking the way “Wide Heaven” sounds. It’s just so cosmic and expansive. Kind of a simple melody but painted on a large canvas. Were you thinking about any particular artists or albums or sounds as you arranged the songs on this album?

Matthew Houck: No. Weirdly no. This record took a weird path. It had a weird birth and a weird adolescence. As it started off, I thought that all these songs were very insular. I thought it was a small record.

AD: By insular do you mean private? Things that would mean more to you than to strangers?

Matthew Houck: Yes, but also inward looking. I think of it in terms of small and large, not in terms of its reach or whatever, although there may be some of that. Artists that I like, they have bodies of work. Some of their records, you might think, oh, this is a weird, small thing he did over here. I thought this was going to be a record like that.I mixed it with some murkier vocal stuff, kind of obscuring the lyrics. At that time, I really wasn’t thinking about it as the record that it became. 

It’s on a new label. It’s on Verve. 

AD: A bigger label.

Matthew Houck: Yeah, and I’d never had input from the label before. I would just make the record and give it to the label. But this one, we were starting off on a new footing, and I was beginning to see the value of that. I was like let’s see what you’re thinking. And I was really thankful for that. I think I would have stayed more true to this first thing that I was thinking and they had an interesting way of kind of being like, I think you’re making it too difficult for the listener, too obscure. I think they were right. 

AD: It seems like a dangerous path, though.

Matthew Houck: Oh, I know.

AD: Because you want it to be difficult, right?

Matthew Houck: It wasn’t enjoyable so much, but it’s not like it was painful to hear this stuff. When I’m working on an album, I have the ability to remove myself from it. I don’t get my feelings hurt once there’s a thing in place.I get my feelings hurt while I’m writing the songs, but once there’s that thing, I feel like it’s somebody else doing it. So it was interesting to have another perspective on it. And again, I think they were right. The vocals needed to be not buried. I was making a record that would be much tougher to listen to. I still think it’s kind of tough to listen to, because of the subject matter, but I was sonically doing that as well. 

AD: That’s interesting, because it does have two spirits to it, the lyrics and the arrangements, which are quite uplifting. Whose idea was it to use strings?

Matthew Houck: The arrangements were always me. This was more like a mixing conversation. I was really kind of tucking it down. I really like small, quiet records. So all the stuff was in there, but I was kind of making it a murky soup. 

AD: Have you worked with strings before? I know the guitar is a stringed instrument, but violins and so forth.

Matthew Houck: No I got that. I did this the same way as the last record. I didn’t have strings on the last album, but the record before Muchacho had strings on it. Bobby Hawk did them. At that time, I had him over to the studio for one afternoon. He did some really great work. So I called him again. I hadn’t talked to him in ten years.When I was making this record. I knew I wanted to have some strings. We did a lot of layering so that it ended up sounding like a string section rather than individual strings. I didn’t do that much layering in the past, so it was nice to try that.

AD: It’s almost like a movie orchestra kind of sound. 

Matthew Houck: Yeah. 

AD: Maybe this would be a good time to talk about the people that you worked with on this album?

Matthew Houck: I’d love to. There was a core of four people when we did the initial tracking. Two of them I had never met before—a new drummer I’d never played with [Noah Denney] and a new bass player [Jack Lawrence]— and two of them included my partner Jo Schornikow and my long-time band member Scott Stapleton. I booked these sessions, just four days with an engineer helping me in the studio, and I’d never worked with the engineer either. So it was kind of a new feeling. 

No one knew the songs or anything. This is how I like to work. Because I feel like something really special can happen.It doesn’t always. But rolling tape on the very first time that anyone hears the song, I think is a really great thing to do. So we did that.We rolled tape and just captured everyone’s first instincts.I think, by and large, the first or second and maybe third take are the best framework to then build on. For me, anyway, I don’t like going over and over a song. Something gets lost. 

AD: There are all these great albums that get made in 24 hours, you know?

Matthew Houck: Exactly. I wish I could do it like that. But the core part is really quick. And then it’s six months of layering, adding to it, pulling from it. I don’t think it’s the most efficient way to work, but I feel like the records are better and more unique when you do it that way.

AD: You don’t even send demos to the people you’ll be playing with?

Matthew Houck: No. At that point, I might not have finished a verse or something at that stage, and I just never feel comfortable…I’m too self-conscious about unfinished lyrics and works in progress to really put those things out there. So yeah, we get everybody together in one room and try to run it and see. To that point, it was one of the scenarios where I was not sure what was happening. 

It’s probably my favorite part of the process, by a long shot actually, where you’re making something. Everybody’s playing freely. You’re playing freely. It’s music at its purest.You could just release that and you could move on—to your point about great records made quickly. There’s a threat involved in that, though, to the point where I’m hesitant to do it. So there’s a real purity in those early sessions that I always love, but inevitably, there’s always something—the tempo drifted too much or we missed this chord change or whatever. So trying then to keep as much as you can from that initial frame and build from that. 

AD: You mentioned your partner, Jo, and she has written a song for this album, which I understand is the first time you’ve ever had someone else contribute a song. Does that mean no covers?

Matthew Houck: No, no. I’ve done covers. But as far as Phosphorescent stuff. 

She had written the song “World Is Ending” for herself. It was going to be on an album called Altar. I assumed she was going to put that song on it, but then when she showed me the final track listing for her record, she left it off. I was gobsmacked.I was like, what did you do that for? And she said, I don’t know. And at some point later, I realized what this album Revelator was, and I said, hey, can I record that song? I feel like it’s perfect for what this album is turning out to be.

AD: It seems to fit right in, which makes me wonder, do you have a lot in common in terms of songwriting?

Matthew Houck: Yeah, I think we do. I know we have in common the hesitancy to show them until we’re done. We don’t share much in the early stages. This is probably my fault. I’m probably the one that’s more hesitant. I think she’d be more the one to be working on stuff together. But I don’t know, for whatever reason, I’ve always been very insular in how I work. But yeah, she’s an amazing songwriter.

AD: What’s her background?

Matthew Houck: She grew up playing piano and organ. She studied music in school and came up in the jazz world and got done with that and got into songwriting after that. I couldn’t speak more highly about her. 

AD: The song that cracks me up is “Poem on the Men’s Room Wall.” Did you really find the words in a stall?

Matthew Houck: Yeah. It’s been on my mind for a long time now. A long time ago, there was this club in Atlanta, Georgia where Phosphorescent used to play. I played there a lot, as much as they would have me. And by the urinal in the men’s room, someone had written a line, and then I’d go back a couple of weeks later and someone else had written a line. So these four or five geniuses had written this thing that I thought was great. It stuck with me for so long. 

When I started writing this song, I said, I think this is a song. I still think it’s a song. I grappled with that one a little, too, for obvious reasons. It’s a weird way to start and end a song. Like Jo, for example, doesn’t like it. Her point is, why would you tank an otherwise beautiful song. We talked about it. It was up for discussion. And I was like, yeah, I couldn’t find anything to replace those lyrics at the beginning that did it better, did that feeling better of crude and hilarious but also profound. I thought it was beautiful.It’s a beautiful bookend. It’s perfect.I hope people can roll with it.

AD: But you’re not going to make a habit of it, setting bathroom graffiti to music?

Matthew Houck: I’ll take inspiration wherever it can come. 

AD: What is your process? Do you carry a notebook around?

Matthew Houck: I used to a lot more. I used to put a real premium on capturing ideas. But these days, I trust that the good ones will come and they’ll stick. 

I generally need to take a block of time and go to a different environment with a pen and sit down and write. With the goal of: it’s songwriting time. But what I find is, almost never does anything happen at that point. Or if it does happen, it’s bad and it’s kind of frustrating and you’re like, what is this? But then a day later, all of the sudden things will start happening. It never happens when I’m sitting there doing that, but the process of doing that somehow is a catalyst. I guess what I’m saying is I should do that more, but I don’t feel good doing it. It’s like cooking with grease. It’s like sticking around in the dark. But it opens up things. What I’ve decided to think of it as, and what I call it is pushing out antennas. Just the act of pushing them out, they do start picking stuff up. 

AD: Do you have any favorite sounds or moments on this record? I’m not asking what’s your favorite song but are there any little moments that really stand out to you.

Matthew Houck: There’s a bunch. I’m pretty proud of this record. But you know what, we got off track. I ended up only talking about the first four people that I worked with. This guy Bobby [Hawk] came down from New York. 

AD: The string guy.

Matthew Houck: Yes. And my long-time pedal steel player Ricky Ray Jackson came up from Austin. But one of the people I was able to get in touch with was Jim White.

AD: Oh, yeah, I love him. He’s playing with all kinds of people now. 

Matthew Houck: He’s a tremendous drummer. I knew him from my New York days. But I hadn’t seen or talked to him in forever. But I had this specific thing as these songs developed that I thought, man, I need to holler at him and see if he’s even remotely around. And he was down to do it. He was on tour with Xylouris White. 

AD: We saw that show.

Matthew Houck: Tremendous, right?

AD: Yeah, I love the way he drums. It’s very tuneful. 

Matthew Houck: It’s like no one else. He’s a real rare player. So I reached out, and he was coming through town. They were playing Big Ears Festival in Knoxville.

AD: That’s a great thing.

Matthew Houck: He had one day off before he needed to be in Philly. So I drove to Knoxville and saw his show and drove him back to Nashville and we had truly 20 hours all-in, including some sleep. I had him in the studio for probably 12 hours. I just had him play over everything. Then I went into those sessions and just started pulling out where he was. So that’s a long way around of getting to say that in “Wide as Heaven,” I think the outro of that is just…it gives me goosebumps every time I hear it. Jim played so perfectly during that. 

I think “Revelator” the song is sonically and…at one point, when I was writing little bits about the record, I said that was the best song I’d ever written. People seem to have been jumping on that in a way that I don’t know if I like. 

AD: Were they saying it’s not? Were they disagreeing with you?

Matthew Houck: No, no. I guess people get taken aback if you say something like that because it sounds like you’re being self-aggrandizing. But I guess what I meant is truly…I can pretty quickly end up looking at these things as if it’s not me. And I really think that song is pretty good and pretty special and also I just think it sounds tremendously great. The final bit of that was working with Dave Cerminara in Los Angeles. The record was about 90% done, I’d say. And that last 10% is like, 80% if that makes sense. 

AD: What did he do for you?

Matthew Houck: We took it out and we ran it through the old console at Sunset Sounds. We had four days. We bounced it up to tape, and we massaged so that it started becoming more three-D and expansive. There’s a specific kind of level he was able to tease out of snare and drum sounds that you could feel them in your body, not just your ear. That was part of the process where it really went the smaller record I was envisioning to something else. It was like, oh no, this can be opened up and it’s better for it. It became a more physical record. 

AD: I imagine you’re getting ready to take this stuff out on tour fairly soon?

Matthew Houck: Yeah. Exactly.

AD: Is it going to have to change quite a bit to do it live?

Matthew Houck: I don’t know. I’m really struggling with it. Struggling is the wrong word. I’m in the middle of figuring that out. There’s a case to be made for both. I’ve done tours where you can let a song and a group of people who are playing the song determine what the song is now. If it’s not what’s on the record, fine. If I’ve done my job right as a songwriter, the song should be able to handle any of that. But there’s also something really to be said about taking the sounds of a record that you labored on and made such a nice sounding thing, to recreate and try to bring those sounds live. There’s pros and cons to both, I think, and I don’t know which version is going to go out. 

AD: Are you working on anything else now? Any side projects you want to talk about?

Matthew Houck: Yeah, I just finished a movie score. It’s Paul Schrader, do you know him?

AD: Yes! Wow.

Matthew Houck: A very well-known filmmaker. He had been in touch a couple of years ago, as a fan. He’s really said some nice things.I met him, and he said, maybe we could do something together one day. And I said, totally.Any time. 

So he called me. The timing was actually really bad because I was just finishing this record and had to make music videos and do art work and all these early shows. A lot was going on. But I wasn’t going to pass up this opportunity. 

So I did it. The film is called Oh Canada! It’s based on a book a friend of his wrote… Russell Banks. 

The book is called Forgone.There was a story about why they couldn’t call the book Oh Canada! Banks was always miffed that he couldn’t call it Oh Canada! He was like, well, if I get to make it into a movie, I’ll call it by its real title. 

I got an advance of it and made a bunch of sounds for it and some songs. The film is about a guy at the end of his life looking back at his own life and trying to rewrite his own history or maybe looking back at the ways that he had rewritten his own history. 

I ended up revisiting songs off my first and second albums. Nobody knows those albums, but it was freakish how well those lyrics fit. I couldn’t have written more perfect lyrics. So I said, Paul, I think I’m going to rerecord these early songs and just see what you think. And he loved it. 

AD: Have you seen it with the songs yet?

Matthew Houck: I just did. There’s the songs and also the scoring. We actually did live scoring while it was playing on the wall over here.I got the – I don’t think it’s the final final, but I did have to go through and approve some edits two days ago and saw the whole thing and it’s amazing. 

AD: How cool! I can’t wait to see it. I love film, too. Maybe not quite as much as music but it’s up there. 

Matthew Houck: It’s weird. Same.I was a real film guy for a period. But I don’t feel like I can sit down and watch a two-hour film very often these days. Do you still?

AD: Well, yeah, a lot of times, I prefer a shorter movie. But I saw Oppenheimer and Killers of the Flower Moon and they’re both long movies and I thought they worked perfectly well. I wasn’t bored. But sometimes I am. 

Matthew Houck: Sometimes I think it’s like, if you get burned a couple of times, you’re like well that’s two hours. I’m never going to watch another of those. But I had a period where I was a true film fan. This film may have reawakened my interest. 

AD: We’re kind of running out of time, but if there was one thing you could get across to people about this album, what would it be?

Matthew Houck: I hope it does what I hope it does, which is…I hope it’s powerful and beautiful and uplifting. That’s what these songs do for me, but sometimes when I worry about how other people will receive them, I worry that it’s too heavy, too sad. 

AD: I don’t think it is. 

Matthew Houck: I hope it works the same way for you as it does for me.
