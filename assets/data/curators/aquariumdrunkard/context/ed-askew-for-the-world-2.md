## Body

Jerry David DeCicca looks back on the making of Ed Askew’s 2013 late-period masterpiece For the World — a record assembled through borrowed rooms, broke musicians, chance encounters, and the quiet force of Askew’s songs themselves. What began in a sweltering West Harlem warehouse with Jay Pluck, Tyler Evans, and engineer Keith Hanlon gradually expanded through contributions from Mary Lattimore, Sharon Van Etten, and Marc Ribot, all orbiting Askew’s singular presence. DeCicca’s recollection captures an artist who had moved far beyond the “psych-folk” tag attached to his ESP-Disk years.

I guess it was around 2010 that my old band, The Black Swans, played gigs in the Bay Area and I visited my friend, Nicole, who lived out there and worked for Drag City. She was busy with an Ed Askew archival release called Imperfiction. I gushed my admiration for Askew and my compliment led to one of the most rewarding musical and personal relationships of my life. 

Less than a year later, I found myself touring with Ed in a rented black Grand Marquis. He was performing with his accompanist, Jay Pluck, on piano since Ed’s hands no longer allowed him to play his famous tiple. The night I dropped off Ed, Jay, and my bandmate, Tyler Evans (to live in NYC and join Ed’s ensemble), two incredible things happened: we agreed that I would return to produce an Ed Askew album and, then, as I was alone at a stop sign, a very drunk girl got into the back of the sedan. She thought I was her car service. I drove her to her destination and never saw her again. I did see Ed, Jay, and Tyler a few months later with another Black Swans bandmate, Keith Hanlon (our drummer, who is also an exceptional engineer), to make a record in a very hot and un-airconditioned West Harlem warehouse a couple blocks from the Cotton Club.

Ed sang to Jay’s piano, and Tyler added electric guitar, banjo, and Ed’s famous tiple. It was all very casual and dialed in despite the limitations of the tiny concrete room and 100 degree heat index. The tour allowed me to cherry-pick my favorite songs and I had memorized my favorite Ed phrasings, so the takes we kept were generally the ones that landed on those. At some point, I asked Mary Lattimore, who I’d only met once before, if she’d be up for driving in from Philadelphia to play harp on a few songs. She was a sport and did it for only gas and toll money. 

We were all broke or close to it. So everything about the making of the album became about favors from strangers and leaning on friends that matched the aesthetic, spirit, and beauty of Ed’s music. Though Ed was, and is, primarily known as a “psych-folk” musician from his early ESP-Disk days, I found his current music more compelling. It was observational and without judgment. He was a participant in his songs though rarely the main character. His songs lack the usual singer-songwriter ego and there’s an unrehearsed humility  that feels like it is from another time and place. He has more in common with Bertolt Brecht or Cole Porter than Skip Spence and David Bixby. Back then, it seemed the further Ed stepped away from anything psych-related, the less interest there was in his new music. But he was so much more than a museum piece or outsider relic. 

After 4 days in the warehouse, Keith and I drove back to Ohio where I plotted overdubs – another bandmate, Canaan Faulkner, added upright bass and my now-wife, Eve Searls, sang on a few songs, including what would be the “hit,” “Blue Eyed Baby”. One night when I was scrolling on Facebook, I saw Sharon Van Etten posted about a very moving experience seeing Ed in Brooklyn, so I asked her to sing on the record (thanks, Zuck!). Then, I got a hold of Marc Ribot to play guitar on a few songs. Sharon and Ribot were both enthusiastic and neither charged a fee. Music and money are tricky, competing companions, but it is easy to be so bold in asking for favors when you’re creating and advocating for someone’s art who was marginalized and deserves a spotlight with a new lightbulb. Ed had lived in NYC since the mid-80’s and the fact that it took some weird guys from Ohio to make the Ed Askew Comeback Album was the chip I kept on my shoulder. Ed had given me permission to do what I wanted to do, and with that came responsibility. As Keith mixed the record (he even built a reverb chamber in his garage specifically for Ed’s vocals), I kept touring, bouncing around the U.S. and parts of Europe. When the record was finally completed, I was living on a friend’s couch in South London where I began to shop it around. 

For the World came out on Tin Angel records in 2013, 2 years after we started making it. It cost about $1000, thanks to everyone’s kindness, and it allowed Ed to tour Europe for the first time, accompanied by Jay and Tyler. The record opened up a new audience for Ed’s music, a compelling new chapter, with more new records that followed. When I toured over there last year, people came up to me every night asking about For the World; their focus was on Ed’s late-period songs far more than his imaginative early solo acoustic recordings. One song from For the World called “Moon in the Mind” was used in an episode of Baby Reindeer, and that, too, widened Ed’s audience, something he was able to enjoy shortly before he passed. 

The best sequel in the world will be released on July 31st: The Final Painting. Maybe I’ll write about that in another 13 years. Love ya, Ed!!
