## Body

Emblazoned with the conquering lion draped over the band’s moniker somewhere between menace and repose, The Light of Saba is a rootsy and righteous blend of reggae, nyabinghi rhythms, and free jazz rolled up and sparked by the flames of Rastafari mysticism. Recorded in 1974, the album is the nexus of the musical and spiritual philosophies of Jamaica’s own heavyweight saxophone colossus, Cedric IM Brooks. Over the course of his career, Brooks divined a sound that combined Jamaican musical traditions and jazz that stood on its own ground amid the full bloom of reggae and dub in the 1970s, while also traveling a parallel route alongside the kindred ensembles of Fela Kuti and Sun Ra.

LIGHT OF SABA…a voice echoes out, proclaiming the group’s arrival as drums roll in like thunder, unfurling the regal fanfare that commences “Theme: Light of Saba/Divine Light.” The brass chorus settles as nyabinghi drums briefly quicken pace before the voice returns, offering blessings to Jah while the band eases into a gentle groove that flows like Jamaican hill country mist. This is the music of a master bandleader and arranger in his prime, finding Brooks armed with a knowledge of history and ready to venture into new realms with his vision for the future.

Brooks began his musical education at the legendary Kingston’s Alpha Boys School before travelling to the states in the late 60s to further his studies in Philadelphia. There he encountered first-hand the music of Sonny Rollins, Leon Thomas, and the Sun Ra Arkestra, whose Afrocentric ideology and approach to large ensemble arrangement would prove most influential. Returning to Jamaica in 1970, Brooks established himself as a first call session player at Studio One before forming The Mystics, with the aim of pursuing a more spiritually oriented music. After a performance accompanying the nyabinghi troupe led by drummer Count Ossie, the groups fused into the Mystic Revelation of Rastafari, whose seminal Grounation and Tales From Mozambique remain epic and unrivaled cornerstones in the development of roots reggae.

In 1973 Brooks formed a new ensemble called The Divine Light, recording From Mento to Reggae to Third World Music, which surveyed Jamaican music’s development and progression, hinting at a space where each of those traditions could cohere into one mighty sound. After a year of rehearsals and concerts in the communal atmosphere at his home, Brooks rechristened the group as The Light of Saba and laid their eponymous masterstroke of a debut laid to tape.

The band digs into the intro “Song for My Father” at full intensity, dispersing as strains of tenor sax climbs like a vine through a hazy, languid interpretation of Horace Silver’s hard bop staple. With their percolating polyrhythms, “Abaku” and “Sabasi” hint at the afrobeat taking shape a continent away, while “Love Breda” lets the horns cook low-and-slow over a steady ostinato. The standout “Words of Wisdom” lays out a rough-and-tough intro that downshifts into a fully reclined one-drop, as soulful vocals fervently chime in to extoll the virtues of living with reverence for the almighty. Dipping into the past, “Peanut Vendor” is a plucky little mento standard harkening back to early musical roots, but the group strips everything down to earth and element for the closing “Chants,” a medley of Rastafari hymns sung over an insistent nyabinghi heartbeat that pulses through the mind long after the needle is lifted.

If you wanted to hear Light of Saba previously, you had to work a little bit, and you still kinda do—it’s still not readily streaming and has only been available in its true form to dedicated diggers and those who could settle for scratchy vinyl-sourced rips. While three of these eight these cuts were featured on an outstanding (and fairly accessible) retrospective comp in the early aughts, this fresh LP reissue is the first time the full impact of Light of Saba has been felt since its original Jamaican release, sending us back to the source to revel in this fierce, crucial, and visionary music. | j annis
