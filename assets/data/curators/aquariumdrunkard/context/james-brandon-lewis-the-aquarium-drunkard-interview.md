## Body

Reviewing saxophonist and bandleader James Brandon Lewis’ Apple Cores here at Aquarium Drunkard, writer Reed Jackson noted that the record “derives its inspiration from early free jazz, but it has more in common with [Ornette] Coleman and [Don] Cherry’s mid-period work, when Coleman was investigating the enigmas of electric funk in his Prime Time band and Cherry dove into other cultures’ ways of music-making, from the rainforest of Brazil to the village percussion orchestras of Africa to the temple chants of India.”

Both Coleman and Cherry were on Lewis’ mind as he joined us for a discussion about Apple Cores, his second album for Anti Records. Following last year’s collaboration with The Messthetics, a jazz rock combo featuring Fugazi’s legendary rhythm section—drummer Brendan Canty and bassist Joe Lally—along with dazzling guitarist Anthony Pirog, this record finds him incorporating post-bop, dub, and hip-hop textures into a stunning show of force. Backed by bassist/guitarist Josh Werner and drummer Chad Taylor, it’s a dazzling excursion for the saxophonist, a demonstration of his ability to bring the sounds of disparate peoples—and his inspirations—into alignment. Below, Lewis unpacks his thoughts about the album, the power of a balanced trio, and tells us why it was so fun to get in front of new crowds on tour with The Messthetics. | j woodbury

Apple Cores by James Brandon Lewis

Aquarium Drunkard: Apple Cores is such a cool listen. I understand it was completely improvised? 

James Brandon Lewis: The root of it, the bass and the drums, yes, completely improvised. There’s some post-production stuff that you hear, but the body of it, the basis of the record, was improvised over two sessions.

AD: You’ve been playing with Chad and Josh for a long time. 

James Brandon Lewis: Yeah, a little while. 

AD: So you have an established connection, but I’m curious: with so much up in the air, how do you prepare for a session like that? What is important to have in place in order for it to work? 

James Brandon Lewis: I think number one, it’s important to realize our backgrounds. We all have extensive time improvising in other settings. Basically, there’s not too much preparation, because we all have trained and worked and been playing music for so long—it ends up being a self-governing thing. You just go from piece to piece, it’s in the moment, but you’re like, “Well, okay, if I just did this riff this time, then the next time I have to know not to do the same riff.” And that requires an extensive amount of improvisational vocabulary, either composed or stuff that’s been sitting in your subconscious from when you were a kid transcribing something off of a record. Just like a lyricist, players are doing it off the top—but you can tell who’s been reading, who’s read a lot.

Think about Black Thought from The Roots freestyling. It’s the same notion, going off at the top of your head; it’s no different instrumentally. It’s the same process. It becomes about: what are you doing in your spare time to build a vocabulary so that when you’re called on, it’s there? It’s also about spending time with people. I’ve played with Chad in various situations. I’ve played with Josh in various situations. You end up building a vocabulary. That’s what it is: building relationships, understanding people’s tendencies musically, just getting a feel. 

AD: Does understanding people’s tendencies also mean being able to sort of goad them, or toss something their way you know is surprising and will inspire something different from them? 

James Brandon Lewis: Of course. I mean, some of the elder musicians that I’ve played with, William Parker, Hamid Drake, Marc Ribot, when you’re playing with those guys, you embody a character. Some people are the antagonist, others the protagonist. That’s the beautiful thing about improvisation. It lives on the edge, constantly teetering ,where just when you think it’s about to do one thing, it shifts. 

AD: For me, that’s what unites jazz, noise, punk, whatever, that intentionality. 

James Brandon Lewis: The spirit of intention. Right, exactly.

AD: I love the trio presentation, especially in jazz. What does a trio allow you to do that maybe a bigger band doesn’t? 

James Brandon Lewis: Over the years, I’ve been able to humbly speak with Sonny Rollins. One time he said to me, “When are you going to come up off some of these coins for that trio project you have?” And that got me thinking about the legendary trio albums he made. And the same thing with Ornette Coleman. I’m kind of dating myself here, but back in 2011 I encountered the great pianist Matthew Shipp. He’s been a friend and he’s been a mentor for years. I met him at a residency in Florida, the Atlantic Center for the Arts. Sam Rivers was still alive. He and Shipp were friends, and I was supposed to meet him too but it never happened. 

Matthew had Sam River’s rhythm section come through, and he asked me, “Have you ever just played with a bass and a drum?” I hadn’t at that point; I had already released my first record in 2010 called Moments, which for the most part was really expansive. So he had River’s rhythm section come in, Michael Welch and Doug Matthews—shout out to them—and it was almost as if I came alive. I think about Jackson Pollock, who was famous for freeing painters from the idea that there needed to be a canvas set up on an easel. I think about Ornette in the same way. If melody is the paint and the chord changes are an easel or a canvas, Ornette freed us from that so that now we can allow the melodic line to keep developing without being under lock and key from the chord changes dictating the movement. 

I was like, “Wow, now these ideas that I have don’t have to [stay] within the rigidity of the chords. I can develop that motif if I want to, it’s all about arrival and departure. If I want to come back to the chords, then great, but I don’t have to. You hear ultimately that what chords and melody and rhythm have in common is movement. You hear movement, it’s implying energy, it’s implying emotion. 

AD: I want to ask you about two big influences on the record, Don Cherry and Amiri Baraka. I understand you first encountered Baraka’s work at Howard University. You’re talking about movement, and he’s definitely got that in his poetry. He’s a musical writer in so many ways. 

James Brandon Lewis: That’s why we all enjoy his work, because he has these musical sensibilities. I met him several times and got to open up for him in 2013. His book, Blues People, was required reading when I was at Howard for my jazz history class. Years later, I got a copy of Black Music, which has excerpts from Apple Cores, the column he wrote for DownBeat. And that was the whole spark. 

I was inspired by the way he talked about Don Cherry in the book. I’ve covered Don Cherry’s work before. I did “Bamako Love” on my Days of FreeMan album years ago, from his album Home Boy (Sister Out). And then I studied with Charlie Haden, who worked with Don Cherry, and I played with Hamid Drake, who’s worked with Don Cherry, and William Parker, who’s worked with Don Cherry. There’s all this overlap between the music and conversations with musicians. Talking with Josh and Chat about our influences, Don came up. His abundant curiosity–that made me want to pay tribute to him with this album. 

AD: He stayed so interested in new music. Miles Davis was the same. You’re a trained musician, but when I hear your work, I hear such abandon—not what you might expect from a musician who came up in the academy. 

James Brandon Lewis: I think what you’re hearing in the music is, even though I went to school, I never adopted the practice of listening to music from a technical standpoint. I do practice very technical stuff, but I’ve never listened to music and been like, “Oh, they’re doing this.” I’ve always listened to music because I enjoy it.. I did go through the phases of transcribing and that kind of thing but what I fear most is being complacent. I don’t like the sense of “Oh, I’ve got to do this to keep up with what these people are doing or what this person is doing.” I don’t like that idea. 

I just operate within my natural being, my natural character of this is how I’m hearing the music. And either, either I’m going to play it this way or I’m not. And I’m not afraid of being disliked. I think a lot of people are governed by their musicality and by being afraid of being disliked. And that’s not where to go, in my opinion. I’m the person who, the things that people have told me were wrong about my playing, I leaned into harder. Making music for people and making music to appease people are two different things.

AD: Last year you made a great record with The Messthetics. What was touring with those guys like? 

James Brandon Lewis: Oh, I love playing with those cats. I love playing with them, man. I’ve been friends with Anthony Pirog for 10 years. 

AD: He’s an incredible guitarist. That cover I heard them do of Sonny Sharrock’s “Once Upon A Time,” so amazing. Sharrock once referred to his guitar as a “really fucked-up horn,” so when I think of you with that group, it’s really cool. I imagine that tour took you to places and venues you wouldn’t normally play? 

James Brandon Lewis: Well, I’m joking, but America doesn’t like me. [Laughs] I was explaining this in a previous interview. I’m well known in Europe, and I’m known within jazz circles, but not mainstream in the US. I haven’t toured extensively with my groups in America. I’ve played a lot of gigs in America, but not as frequently as I have recently with The Messthetics. But yeah, it was a different audience, for sure. It was great. I love meeting Fugazi fans.. They’re loyal. When you can get someone to be loyal to what you’re trying to do musically, I think it’s pretty amazing. They built a huge following and it’s great. I love bringing the energy every night. The music can’t get loud enough. I like that. It gives me an opportunity to really play my horn.
