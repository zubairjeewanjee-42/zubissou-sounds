## Body

It opens with an abduction—and only gets crazier from there. Groupies is the latest series from KCRW’s Lost Notes music podcast. Written and hosted by Dylan Tupper Rupert and producer Jessica Hopper, the show’s eight episodes span the end of the ’60s, the birth of the ’70s Sunset Strip culture, and the dawn of punk rock, illuminating the lives of women often written out of the story or viewed as mere accessories to their rock star companions. 

While plenty has been said about “groupie” culture—often dismissively—never has a more empathetic and direct look at the women who fueled, and sometimes greatly influenced, the world of rock & roll: Dee Dee Keel, who booked the Whiskey A Go Go and The Roxy; the infamous Miss Pamela Des Barres, author of I’m with the Band: Confessions of a Groupie, Lori “Lightning” Mattix, and Morgana Welch, who boasted “Source Family” chic looks and occult interests. 

These women were there for the action—side stage at Led Zeppelin show, partying at Rodney Bingenheimer’s English Disco, bounding on and off tour buses—but their stories have remain obscured or only partially told. Until now. Groupies doesn’t shy away from unsavory details—Hopper jokes that at one point in her research, she wanted to “go back in time and chop off Jimmy Page’s dick, kill-baby-Hitler style”—but the show is uninterested in painting with a broad brush. Of course there’s some editorializing, with Rupert peppering in hilarious asides throughout, but mostly the pair turn the mic over to the people who witnessed and experiences all this lore first hand. 

Aquarium Drunkard Transmissions podcaster Jason P. Woodbury caught up with Rupert and Hopper to discuss—the following interview has been edited for cohesion and clarity. Groupies is available now wherever you get podcasts.

Aquarium Drunkard: I apologize for the recording conditions. My dogs are going to bark and the guys renovating our bathroom will make a lot of noise too. [Loud clanging] Ideal recording conditions. I’m sorry about that. 

Jessica Hopper: Oh my god, ask Dylan about “ideal recording conditions.” She had to track parts of Groupies during a heat wave and turn off all the air conditioning. 

AD: Oh man, they couldn’t just EQ out that hum or whatever? That’s brutal. 

Dylan Tupper Rupert: Thankfully, that was just the rough takes. [Laughs] 

AD: I love this show. It’s fascinating and immersive. How long ago did you have this idea?

Dylan Tupper Rupert: We’ve been saying that we’ve been ideating on this for six years or so. There was a day where we were hanging out years ago in L.A. and I said, “I want to do something about Sable [Starr] and Lori [Maddox] or whatever.” And then Jessica was like, “We gotta do something bigger about groupies.” A documentary? A book? It evolved along the way to being a podcast and I’m really happy that’s the medium that we chose because we get to hear their stories from their own mouths the most directly. 

Jessica Hopper: Dylan is an incredible font of ideas, ideas that feel right on time—or, frankly, ahead of their time in terms of how other people are thinking. I’m always the person who’s like, “All right, but bigger, let’s make it bigger. Let’s dream bigger.” If we had done a coffee table, look, it wouldn’t have been effective. It would’ve been more like a “style” thing. And for us, not to be like, “We are really our target audience,” but we just want to know more about these women. 

I’ve been doing more oral history work in recent years. It’s one thing to read people’s words, but to hear their voices and their own stories? That was really important to us. We’re two people who are always looking for our uncredited foremothers. I didn’t know who [pioneering rock journalist] Ellen Willis was until I was 10 years in the game or whatever—which is kind of bananas. We’re women who are really deeply ensconced in music—longtime readers of music journalism, we’re both journalists ourselves. Once something gets in your craw and you just want to know more. We encountered so much media that flattened [these women’s] stories, or some more recent coverage focused on Me Too, which problematizes their stories without them ever saying, “Oh yes, I was a victim,” or, “I wasn’t a victim” or anything like that. We wanted their stories in their fullness and complexity. 

AD: I feel like right now issues of moral, cultural, and relational complexity, can be hard for people—things feel so binary. The review of the podcast in the Financial Times, and I don’t want to throw shade at the writer, but it seems like they were saying, “Why aren’t they stating how we should feel about these women’s stories?” When you revisited contemporary media from that time about the topic, what struck you about what you read?

Dylan Tupper Rupert: There were a variety of stances on whether it’s cool or not cool to be partying, doing drugs, and sleeping with 14 and 15-year olds in a variety of music media. But what was consistent was the degradation of groupies on the whole. No matter whether or not they thought these girls were powerful players in the scene, or if they thought they were inconsequential, or they thought they mattered a lot to these men that they were idolizing, at the end of the day, they were almost unanimously dismissed as accessories. 

That’s predictable. That’s not a shocker to anybody. But I think as we go on into this season, something that we investigate was: “Okay, so it was a different time. Tell me exactly how different this time was.” And so we get a lot of first person accounts of what it was like to grow up in this rapidly changing sexual environment of social mores. Everything is changing so fast and you’re in Hollywood around all the stars. And so they’re giving us sort of this accurate picture of what it was really like and sort of what was allowed, we could get away with how people felt about it. It allows for a lot more accuracy of understanding.

Jessica Hopper: It’s not just nuance, it’s really the big picture story. And as we’ve learned in recent years, and this certainly was part of our own motivation, is that a lot of these stories have been told without women in them. This was a time where there weren’t a lot of women in positions of power as we traditionally think of them, where they had job titles, or had jobs that weren’t secretaries, where they were making big decisions. But as we find out, Dee Dee [Keel] basically discovers Van Halen. She’s like, “You guys should try playing originals!” And then it’s like, “Oh, there were women there.” What kind of tenacity did they have to have? What was the aperture for them to try to get jobs in music or enact their own dreams when there were not necessarily models? 

Dylan Tupper Rupert: Or even just hang out! 

Jessica Hopper: Betty Davis, for example, was a bit of a groupie. But she was like, “I’m a songwriter. I can get these guys to talk me up, but I’m trying to sell them my songs.” There’s all these kinds of different scales. Any woman wanting to be there was up against it. If they wanted to be there just to hang out and party and have a good time, and figure out who they were through the music and the scene—like any of us—what was their avenue in? We are not interested in shaming whatever there “in” was. I think that sets us apart from basically all other music media that has documented this time. We didn’t bring in a lens of shame. We’re not here to get Led Zeppelin canceled. We’re here because we want to know who the fuck Lori Lightning was! 

Dylan Tupper Rupert: Another quicker fine point on that is that everything in our series is already published and already known. It’s all in every Led Zeppelin book; We’ve got stuff from Please Kill Me [by Gillian McCain and Legs McNeil]. We just strung it in this clear line about what their world was. There’s not any bombshells. “Oh my God, Jimmy Page did this?” It’s lore! Everyone knows it.

AD: There’s a quote by Dee Dee I think of as quietly revelatory: “We became part of the story just by being there.” And it’s such a reminder that the rockstars get centered in the narratives, but there were a lot of other people in those rooms. People were there with jobs that put them in proximity to these stories. You discuss a piece written at the time, “Manners And Morals: The Groupies.” I forget what publication…

Dylan Tupper Rupert: TIME Magazine! 

Jessica Hopper: Known underground fanzine TIME Magazine. [Laughs] 

AD: Their designation was “someone who sleeps with musicians.” So who’s a groupie and who’s not? Can other rock stars be groupies? But it’s interesting that some women essentially say, “I don’t care if you call me a groupie,” but others push back on it. 

Jessica Hopper: The thesis question was “How did these girls get in the picture?” In episode six, there’s a literal picture we thought of as “The Last Supper of Groupie-dom.” We talked to Jaan Uhelski, editor emeritus of CREEM, who was one of the first women to ever report on groupies when she was on tour with Led Zeppelin. We asked her about this photo, and she told us, “That photo changed everything.”

It’s literally the dawn of rock paparazzi, and all of these things change and happen with Led Zeppelin with these women, with rock journalism, with Robert Plant’s marriage, because of this one photo. And we get it from two perspectives, someone who was in the photo and someone who was in the media who saw how this changed everything.  Once we start talking to the punks, and four of the people we talk to— Kid Congo Powers, Alice Bag, Pleasant Gehman, and Theresa Kereakas—are among the well-documented first 50 punks in L.A., they told us groupies were rock stars to them. They were like, “They were our idols. 

Dylan Tupper Rupert: They were the reason they went to Hollywood in the first place. 

Jessica Hopper: They were like “Oh, 15-year-old girls run the Sunset Strip? Let me in there.” 

AD: I like the disclaimer you share at the start of each episode. These people aren’t just sharing stories, they are sharing their own stories and their own remembrances. And while I’m sure you strived for accuracy, these are decades ago tales about partying. 

Dylan Tupper Rupert: We’d run into things where so and so would contradict another person. And we had to ask ourselves: What podcast are we making? We’re making a podcast that maps the female experience of rock mythology. Them being able to own their mythology is the power they’ve wrested back from everyone else telling them what shame they should or should have, how little power they had, how naive or stupid they were. Them being able to match the men’s good time, rollicking “you had to be there, it’ll never be like that again” attitude. Them having their own version of that story is what they want and what they gave to us.
