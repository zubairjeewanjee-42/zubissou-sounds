## Body

Throughout his career, Will Epstein has amassed film scoring credits, including an IDA Documentary Award nomination for his score for Nam June Paik: Moon Is The Oldest TV (2023), by attuning to the micro-moments on screen and crafting the music to match them. So it comes as no surprise that, with the release of his latest lyrics-forward album Yeah, mostly (out on Fat Possum Records), he applies this focused attention as well, sourcing his imagery and subject matter from life’s small moments that may otherwise go unnoticed as a person goes on living. Communication, he says, is his goal in all musical forms, to translate an emotion or the essence of an experience as directly as he can, with or without the involvement of words. This conversation with Will delves into his current music-making techniques and philosophies, and also traces his history as a musician with a somewhat surprising beginning. You remember playing the recorder in elementary school music class? For him, that formative experience is the reason he finds himself writing music to this day. Throughout the call, he comes across as warm, curious, and passionate about his influences as well as his various ongoing sonic experiments.

Will joined us virtually from his home/music studio in the Hudson Valley area of New York. | mh coons

Aquarium Drunkard:  You reference the involvement of community in the making of this record. Is that community located there in Woodstock, New York with you?

Will Epstein:  Some of them are in the Hudson Valley area, where I am. Austin Vaughn, the drummer, lives in Kingston. And Eli Cruz—who helped record the record, and plays on it some—is in Kingston. A couple of the other people, like my friend Michael Coleman who mixed and co-produced the record with me, have spent time living up here and come back and forth from the city fairly often. So it’s all the general, New York City-plus kind of scene. In general, there’s a really great music community and art community around here that I feel connected to, and I have another group called Playtime that’s more improvised music based, and that I play saxophone in, and that’s a very local project. So there’s a good music vibe. Plus, we recorded at my house.

AD: Yeah. When you’re the one with the studio, you draw the community to you.

Will Epstein: Yeah, yeah. I do a lot of work myself here at the house, my own experiments and film scoring work, but this was probably the first real session I had here. I had just got this eight track tape machine, and was sort of inspired by the idea of doing the session here at the house. I hadn’t really recorded too much here at the house with other people, so it was kind of a first.

AD: And you fleshed out this most recent album of songwriting on that eight track?

Will Epstein: Yeah. We recorded the whole record here on that tape machine. We mixed it on the computer, but we recorded it live in the room, without headphones, singing live, which I hadn’t really done with my songs before. It was a new experience—super fun, if not a little exhausting.

AD: That would be exhausting,

Will Epstein: Yeah, just singing a song over and over again, having to give yourself to it each time and think about arrangements, it takes a lot of energy. But it was worth it. It gave it a kind of natural feeling.

AD: It sounds like your composer brain is still on a little bit. Even as you’re performing.

Will Epstein: I’ve always loved crafting arrangements and sounds and stuff, so sometimes I can’t help but think about it. Definitely, doing the recording, I was obviously thinking about it too. But I also leaned a lot on my friend Michael, who was there with me, to keep us on track, because I had my emotional investment in the material that can cloud things sometimes. It’s helpful to have somebody to bounce things off of,

AD: That sounds like a helpful dynamic.

Will Epstein: He’s just an amazing musician, so I really trust him. In the past, with my music, I’ve gone in with the sound of it being more forward in how I was thinking about the music, and this time it was the lyrics. The song on its own was played kind of sparsely, was what was guiding me and that was something I wanted, to more fully explore my voice in that realm. And so I was wanting to not get too bogged down thinking about all the fun ways we could make this sound. Most of these, we came up with the arrangements and then recorded it within a couple hours.

AD: That’s pretty fast. So you compose, you write songs in a lyrical sense. What was your entry point into making music?

Will Epstein: My first instrument was the recorder. [When I was nine,] I was taking recorder lessons in a class, and the guy who was teaching them played the saxophone. (His name is Ilhan Ersahin, and he’s a great saxophone player. He has a club in [New York] City called NuBlu.) I was very intrigued with the saxophone, his instrument, and he let me play. And from then, I started playing. 

My mother is a visual artist, but was encouraging of artistic expression in general, and encouraged me to continue playing. Because I started so early, I was always sort of the best in school, because, most kids, they make you start on clarinet first, and I was already playing the saxophone. That gave me confidence in it from an early age. And then around high school, I started really getting into listening to jazz music—Cannonball Adderley was probably the first saxophone player I got really into, then Coltrane, and it was around the same time that I started playing music with friends more. My friend Nicolás Jaar, who I grew up with, who’s a great electronic musician, we met around the time I started playing saxophone, but started playing music together in high school, just kind of improvising. He would play piano or accordion, and he had just started doing some electronic stuff, and we had a band, but really would sometimes just kind of improvise, him on piano and me on saxophone, and we’d play on the street sometimes, kind of Bohemian style. That’s when I started getting into music. I became very obsessive with it. I only listened to Coltrane and some Ethiopian music for, like, two years. It was kind of an emotionally difficult time in high school, and I think music was really my grounding space. 

Also, my parents listened to a lot of Bob Dylan and Lucinda Williams alongside, like, dub music and Lauryn Hill, so there was a lot of song-based music that I was also always into.

AD: I love how recorder, of all things, was like your gateway drug into music.

Will Epstein: [Laughs] It’s an easy instrument. I actually just came back to the recorder recently. A year or so ago, I got this contrabass recorder, which is like six feet tall. It’s this weird, German, rectangular, plywood recorder from the ‘70s, and it sounds insane.

AD: Have you used it on anything?

Will Epstein: Yeah, on this album, actually. On the last song, “Lauren Bacall,” at the end of the song, there’s a solo that I’m playing that on, and I’m playing it on a couple other forthcoming recordings. Actually, the one time I performed with it was at Carnegie Hall. [Laughs] 

AD: [Laughs] Oh! No big deal. 

WE: I was playing with Meg [Duffy of] Hand Habits. Their music was part of this piano recital, and I played one of their songs on the contrabass. It was an incredible experience playing it in that space, especially during the sound check. I played this one note, and it’s such a breathy instrument that it felt like the breath was filling the whole room. [Sarcastically] I will only perform with it at Carnegie Hall.

AD: [Laughs] I mean, you have to have your standards, for sure. Do you, as a composer, have a gravitate toward different instruments that you want to play around with, like the contrabass recorder?

Will Epstein: Yeah. Wind instruments are a natural draw to me, given the saxophone and all that, but I do think when I’m working on a new score or something, it can help the process by thinking, “What is an instrument I can base this around, or dig into, that maybe I’m not that familiar with, or haven’t played that much?” Just to be able to come at it with some fresh ears or fresh perspective,

AD: What are some of your other favorites?

Will Epstein: Let’s see. For this score I did for this documentary on Nam June Paik [Nam June Paik: Moon is the Oldest TV], I did prepared piano and used a lot of this Buchla synth—both of those kinds of elements. That was really fun, because I had never really messed around with prepared piano, and I learned a lot doing that. And this one I just finished for a film that’s going to be at Sundance in January, I did most of it on this Mellotron instrument alongside this microtonal synth called [Ciat-Lonbarde] Sidrax. So those are some random ones. 

I’ve always been really drawn to the electric piano in general, like a Wurlitzer or Rhodes. I’m very drawn to electro-acoustic sounds, the combinations of two different sound sources that are mixing the electronic with the acoustic, or things that fuse them into one, like a Wurlitzer, where it’s a hammer hitting a reed or a tine, and it’s amplified. That kind of inspired me to start working with transducer speakers, which are like little contact speakers that you can place on any kind of resonant object. I started using them with, like, metal chairs that I had around my house that I use for yoga (laughter), sending sine waves to them and creating sequences that I would send through them. Then I would improvise along with the saxophone—sort of creating an acoustic synthesizer with that. You can also put them on drums. I use one in this band Playtime that I mentioned. It’s one of my instruments. I’m wanting electronic sounds to feel tactile, liking the uncanny and bizarre sounds that they can bring, but grounding them in the acoustic world. That’s something I’m very drawn to.

AD: You have an interesting perspective on sound, as a person whose first deep connection to music that was yours was instrumental music.

Will Epstein: Totally. And I’m grateful for that. It’s nice to not need language all the time. (laughter)

AD: What drew you to make something language-based for this?

Will Epstein: Well, there’s two sides to every coin, and I’m also somebody who loves language, who loves playing with language and communicating with people—communicating with friends and family and loved ones—and articulating feelings. In literature, I’ve always been very drawn to autofiction or things of that nature. For instance, I just started reading Patti Smith’s latest book [Bread for Angels]. I remember reading her book, M Train, around the same time I was reading these Karl Ove Knausgård My Struggle books, a long series of auto fiction novels. They both articulate the daily rhythms of life in a really great way, like in M Train, Patti’s constantly going to that cafe and having toast and coffee, and she’s just describing it over and over in this way. But there’s something so satisfying about bringing attention to these smaller moments.

AD: And returning to a practice.

Will Epstein: Yeah, there’s a ritual to it, shining light on these corners of your life that you can take for granted. I’ve always been drawn to that kind of style. I have a tendency to kind of float into the air a little bit sometimes, and I really benefit from the grounding directness of that style of writing, whether it’s in novels like that or [lyrics by] Lucinda Williams, who can be so direct and can kind of cut right into you. It’s not necessarily like with poetry, although her stuff is very poetic—and Patti can be very poetic—but it’s the prose aspect of poetry that actually speaks most to me, the simple directness. I’ve felt it as like a place I’ve been reaching for, like I want to find my way to this. It wasn’t natural, I didn’t really understand how to do it for myself, and I’ve felt it to be a process of taking away some of the restrictions in myself that have been preventing me from being that direct or that simple. I’ve been striving towards that place of not striving. 

AD: Yeah

Will Epstein: In writing this material, I felt like I was able to get much closer to that place and to remove some of those obstacles I had made for myself. It felt very cathartic, like I was awakening. It’s a process of taking these smaller moments from your life and funneling and transfiguring them into something else, and as a result, it can feel like you’re waking up into your life. Everything starts glowing a little bit. That was the place I was wanting to explore, and it can exist, for a lot of people, in these worlds of music, whether it’s more abstract music or lyric-based music. For me, I need both of them. They both are really important to me, and they do explore slightly different realms of expression. They’re definitely connected. I feel glad to be able to explore both realms.

AD: With the instrumental, you get to be more abstract and maybe esoteric, and then with the lyrical work that you create, you get to be more plainspoken and communicative.

Will Epstein: Honestly, when I’m playing saxophone, I’m thinking about being communicative and plainspoken, too, in a way. It’s always about communication, ultimately. I don’t think that’s really different. I’m always looking for clarity in music. That’s always the thing I’m striving for. I’m not trying to ever be circuitous, unnecessarily. [Laughs] Sometimes, you know, going on a winding road is the most direct way of getting somewhere. I’m always looking for clarity—melodic clarity, emotional clarity. Coltrane taught me that—kind of like Lucinda, honestly—he’s cutting right through. She has some great lyrics about listening to Coltrane too, actually. In “Righteously” she says, “Just play me John Coltrane.”

AD: Wow. Your instrumental and lyrical influences are coming together!

Will Epstein: I think that really is the same thing. I’m not ever trying to be diffuse. I have a real instinctual resistance to that. There’s an urgency to doing these things for me that drives the feeling, and it’s just about being in touch with that thing. And if what you’re putting out is not communicating that thing, then it does not feel good. My desire with the lyrics, on this record, was to feel like I was really communicating myself clearly.

AD: And in paying that attention to those small details, like you mentioned, and then listening back, it’s almost like you get to be Patti Smith in M Train, returning over and over to Cafe Ito for your little ritual.

Will Epstein: Yeah, that’s totally true.

AD: Do you have anything else that you haven’t shared yet that you would like to share?

Will Epstein: Not at this moment. I’m always for concision. I had recorded over five other songs for this record that didn’t make it on. And I had two that I cut at the last moment when listening back to the masters, because it just felt like it was getting diffuse, even though I love those songs, and it was sad not to have them on. Maybe they’ll find a home later. It’s nice to keep things taut.
