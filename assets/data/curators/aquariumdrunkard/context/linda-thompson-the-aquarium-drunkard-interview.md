# Linda Thompson :: The Aquarium Drunkard Interview

**Linda Thompson — The Aquarium Drunkard Interview** · _2024-07-01_ · by Jason Woodbury · `interview`
**Tags:** Linda Thompson, Mixtapes

## Body

Thanks to the masterpieces she made with her ex-husband Richard, Linda Thompson’s legacy as a one of the greatest vocalists of the past several decades is secure. But you won’t hear Linda’s voice on her newest solo LP. Since she and Richard split in the early 1980s, she’s struggled with bouts of spasmodic dysphonia, a rare condition that makes both singing and speaking difficult. 

Nevertheless! Linda’s ever-vibrant personality is on full display on the aptly named Proxy Music, wherein she drafts a murderer’s row of talented artists to bring her latest songs to life—Rufus and Martha Wainwright, John Grant, Eliza Carthy and Dori Freeman among them—not to mention the helping hands of her kids, grandkids and even her ex-husband. It’s a record that captures the beautiful, melancholic spirit of those early efforts with Richard, showing Linda to be a masterful lyricist. Dressed up in a hilarious cover that pays tribute to Roxy Music’s first LP (“Bryan Ferry likes it,” Linda reports. “We were afraid he’d sue!”), Proxy Music is one of 2024’s most delightful musical surprises. On the eve of a handful of shows taking place in London and New York City to commemorate its release, Aquarium Drunkard caught up with Linda via Zoom. | t wilcox

Aquarium Drunkard:  What are the qualities you look for in a great vocalist? 

Linda Thompson: LOUD is what I really like! Good phrasing, of course, too. But basically, I have a sort of repertory company of singers that I like to work with. You know, the McGarrigles, the Wainwrights, the Thompsons, the Carthys, or the Watersons. These great singers with a traditional thing running through them, you know?

AD: All these different legendary families that you mention in the last song on Proxy Music — “Those Damn Roches” — they’ve all intertwined over the years. What ties them together in your mind?  

Linda Thompson: Yeah, it’s an extensive knowledge of music, which they all have. And they all have a certain versatility. And as aforesaid, they all have a grasp of traditional American or British music, too. I like that foundation in a singer. I prefer a great singer to a great voice. They’re two very different things. Some people have great voices—beautiful voices. But they can’t sing! Some people don’t have such great voices, but they really can sing. I prefer the latter, rather than beauty. Though I do like a bit of beauty! There is some beautiful singing on this record. 

AD: Absolutely. When I was listening to your new record and listening to some older Wainwright and Thompson and Roches music, what struck me is that they’re all incredibly honest singers and writers — sometimes painfully so. 

Linda Thompson: Well, you’ve given a better answer than I did! [Laughs] That’s very true. They’re honest and true. Well done, you could have done this interview by yourself. 

AD: [Laughs] I don’t think anyone would want to read that! Speaking of all these musical families — did you come from a musical family yourself? 

Linda Thompson: My grandfather —my mother’s father —was on the stage with Harry Lauder, who was a big music hall star in Scotland, and [became] big in America, too. My parents wanted my brother and I to do something artistic. I don’t know why—fools! [Laughs] In those days, we would sing a lot because there was nothing else to do. People came over and everybody got up and sang a song. My mother couldn’t really sing, but my dad and my uncles and aunts, they could all get up and sing songs. We’d sing a lot of Scottish songs—Robert Burns kind of stuff. Or we’d sing an Everly Brothers song or two. My dad used to sing Hank Williams. He played the accordion, but we’d all just sing a capella. We didn’t have a piano or anything. 

AD: Was there a time when you realized that singing is what you wanted to do with your life? 

Linda Thompson: When I was about 16, I started singing the odd song in folk clubs and I thought, “I like doing this.” But it was folk clubs I liked, not the other stuff. I’m an obscurantist. I love obscure stuff. Right from the beginning, I was singing Billy Pigg and Walter Pardon. Old, very old singers. Then I went to university in London, dropped out quickly and just started singing in folk clubs full time. 

AD: What appealed to you about the older, traditional things you were singing back then? 

Linda Thompson: It’s hard to say. I’m half-Scottish, and in Scotland the tunes are very beautiful. The laments, you don’t get laments like Scottish laments anywhere else. They’re incredible. The Scots are infused with a melancholy. The Irish aren’t—they’re much sunnier people. The Scots are dour. So, I think I just liked that kind of sad, keening music. I think keening is the word, you know? Most people hate [that kind of music]. I mean, most people hate the bagpipes. I love the bagpipes, so there’s obviously something very wrong with me. [Laughs] 

AD: One of your compilations is called Give Me A Sad Song and some of the songs on the new record have a really deep melancholy to them. What is it that you like about sad songs? What do they give us as listeners? 

Linda Thompson: Most of the best songs are in fact sad songs, really. There are exceptions. “Isn’t She Lovely” by Stevie Wonder is the happiest song you’ll ever hear, and it’s phenomenal. Abba songs, they’re quite upbeat, but they’re still often sad, you know? And the blues. When I was young, Clapton, Jeff Beck, all those people…I wasn’t listening to them. I was listening to Howling Wolf. The real thing. I liked the source. 

AD: While you were playing in folk clubs back then, you were coming of age with people like Sandy Denny, Nick Drake, John Martyn and, of course, Richard Thompson. What was it like being around these amazing songwriters?

Linda Thompson: It was horrible! [Laughs] Imagine being surrounded by geniuses. I didn’t know … well, I knew they were fabulous, but I didn’t know how far it would go. It was hard to measure up to them. To have them all there in one place was incredible. But you just have to soldier on, really. 

AD: Can you point to anything that you learned from all of them when it comes to your own songwriting? 

Linda Thompson: Well, first of all, if I hadn’t lost my voice, I never would have written a single song. In fact, Richard was always encouraging. He would say to me, “What is that you’re singing?” And I’d say, “Oh, I don’t know, I’m just making it up, it’s nothing.” Or I’d write words and he’d go, “Give me those words.” But I’d always say, “No, no, no.” I didn’t want to because he was so good at it. I think everybody in Fairport felt the same. I wish I’d learned more from all of them. They all had a God-given gift.

AD: How does it feel to see Nick and Sandy get all of this acclaim and popularity years after they passed away? 

Linda Thompson: Bittersweet, you know? Well, Sandy has not had the resurgence that she should have had, at least in my opinion. Nick has. He was just devastated and sad back then, though — you know, nobody listened to him at all. I was as friendly with him as anybody was — which is not particularly — but I used to say, “These songs are incredible, Nick!” And he’d go, “Oh, rubbish. Nobody buys my records!” It’s a shame. It doesn’t do him any good now, so…[shrugs]. 

AD: Jumping ahead to the tunes on Proxy Music — it’s a very diverse collection, a lot of different styles. Is that something you strive for when you’re making an album? Even in the Richard & Linda Thompson days — those records get slotted into the “folk rock” category, but they cover a lot of ground. 

Linda Thompson: We didn’t talk about it [back then], you know. We just had a slew of songs, and we would try to choose the best ones. I don’t think we cared if they were in the same key or not or the same subject matter. It was just which was the best song. We were young. We didn’t give it much thought. On Proxy Music, it’s a bit of a fault because I work piece meal, to say the least. When I’ve written something, then 18 months later and I’m doing something else, I forget what the other one was like. I’m not a cohesive writer. But I do like a lot of variety, to be honest. 

AD: Tell me a bit about how the new record was made — was it a remote recording situation or could you get into the studio with these vocalists? 

Linda Thompson: Either I was there, or Teddy [Thompson, Richard and Linda’s son] was there. Teddy went to Iceland to record John Grant’s vocal. We did a bit of remote overdubbing, but I prefer to be there in the room — I’m not sure if you can tell the difference when it’s live or remote, but I prefer it because then you can say, “That works” or “That doesn’t work.” 

AD: Since you had written these songs but weren’t going to be singing them, what did you feel like your role was as a producer? 

Linda Thompson: I supplied the drugs [Laughs]! Actually, the drugs in this case were just tea and chocolate cakes. Teddy did a lot of it, production-wise—he’s much more technical than I am. I once said to him, “I can’t hear the drums.” And he said back, “There’s no drums on this track!” “Ohhh, I’ll just go have a cup of tea then [Laughs]!” Occasionally, I’ll come up with something insightful. My hearing is not great either, as well as my speech. So, it’s very funny that I do it at all. 

AD: I think you may have written the perfect song for Rufus Wainwright with “Darling This Will Never Do.” Tell me a bit about that one … 

Linda Thompson: It is perfect for him! It’s about a May/December romance and Teddy gave it a very 1940s feel and that suits Rufus’ voice so well. Teddy did a beautiful demo and I asked him if he wanted to [sing] it, but he said, “No, no, I want Rufus to do it.” And Rufus, as usual, knocked it out of the park. I’ve known him since he was a kid. I think I first saw him onstage when he was about 15 and he was fantastic. And he’s had an incredible career — I’m so happy for him. 

AD: I like the new album because it has this multi-generational thing happening — all of these different age groups, your kids, grandkids, friends, etc. Why is that important to you … or is it that you’ve just got very talented kids and grandkids? 

Linda Thompson: Well, they’re always around, you know [Laughs]? You can just say, “Play something!” And they’ll do it. Zak [Hobbs, Linda’s grandson] is extremely talented, as is Teddy. As is Kami [Thompson, Richard and Linda’s daughter], though she’s not as into it all. But when she does it, she does it great. And then I have Richard, who played on the record, which is wonderful. My son-in-law, who plays with the Pretenders [James Walbourne], he’s wonderful as well. And, well … they don’t charge me anything! [Laughs]
