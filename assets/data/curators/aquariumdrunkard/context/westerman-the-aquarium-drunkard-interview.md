## Body

There is a clear dialectic of tension and release in Westerman’s music. The constraints of the vocal melody and rhythmic textures are constantly circumvented and exploded. The conventions of pop are also tightly assumed only to be disfigured once they take proper shape. He pushes them as far as he can without losing his own character, like he is testing how long he can hold his breath. I would compare it to auto-erotic asphyxiation, if only this wasn’t tone deaf to the very context of the releases: that his debut album came out during a pandemic that effectively asphyxiated millions, and his sophomore record was composed in response to the solitude it brought forth. The trilogy formed by those two records and his latest, A Jackal’s Wedding, is also about tension and release, in this case. Where the first one neurotically arranged harmonies into refined ambiences and lush production aesthetics reminiscent of Peter Gabriel and Mark Hollis, and the second succumbed to unsettlingly unformatted bittersweetness, like Nick Drake making a party record, in his latest Westerman tries to put things into motion once again, if only by breaking them apart. First you clinch it, then you stress it, then it bursts and pours out.

For this interview, I went to Westerman’s concert at the Music Hall of Williamsburg, and then I talked to him through video for an hour or so, discussing his influences and collaborations, his impressions of America in the weirdest of times, the sky in the Greek island of Hydra, and the difficulty of putting musical meaning into words. | r moraes

Aquarium Drunkard: First, I wanted to say your debut really marked my pandemic. I think of the pandemic as really indissociable from it, in a way. It meant a lot to me, and helped me cope. Thank you for that.

Westerman: You’re welcome. I’m glad it helped.

AD: Your breakthrough as an artist was during COVID, then. How did you deal with that? And how did your music change once it was over, do you think? I know the second record was also very much marked by the pandemic, the compositional process, but the first one too, I mean, its repercussions happened only during COVID, I guess.

Westerman: Yeah, well, I made that record quite a long time before COVID, but it got held up due to the music industry. It should have come out about nine months earlier than it did, but it was out of my control.

AD: But the repercussions of it… This was getting a lot of critical praise while you… Were you playing concerts? How did that work?

Westerman: It was kind of strange to connect with the idea that it even happened for me. I mean, it’s so nice to hear that it helped people, and that ultimately is what keeps bringing me back to the table with making music is trying to extend that hand in a way that I felt it extended to me, so that it’s an exchange in that way. But personally, I found it very difficult. I had 70 concerts cancelled in the space of a week. I went from basically… I had 18 months of l knowing what I was going to be doing to having just nothing and not knowing how long that would last for. So I found that very challenging at the beginning.

AD: In New York, at some point in your show, you mentioned the importance of concerts in terms of just people gathering together. Almost like there is an anthropological function to rock performances. In a sense, they are no different than sitting around a fire or breaking bread.

Westerman: I think so, or like going to church or something without the same constriction of the religious element. But I think it’s a spiritual undertaking in a way, and it’s ancient… It’s tapping into something, it’s to gather around, and it sort of doesn’t really matter. The value of that extends beyond the music itself. It’s a social function. I think at the moment it’s very challenging to be on the road. The overhead, it’s got so much more expensive, but I think trying to find ways of continuing to deliver that for people I think is a good use of time and energy if you can, because we’re living in a very strange time where people feel more and more isolated. So it’s some small response to that.

AD: Yeah, I often think about people who are evangelical and they believe they’re feeling God or something like that when they go to mass, and then they will go to some sort of pop music concert and feel exactly the same thing, and they’ll just realize, like, oh, I just wanted to sing with other people, together.

Westerman: Yeah, and just share something.

AD: During the concert you also mentioned something about the difficulties of being on the road. You had visa issues, I’ve been told your band couldn’t be complete in the show. Do you want to talk about that? Was it more improvisational, the concert? How did you adapt to it? Is this going to last the whole tour?

Westerman: Yeah, sure. Yeah, I think it is going to last the whole tour now. We were hoping for quite a long time that Dan would be able to join us. But we’re now entering the last week. And even if he gets the approval, I think it would be more upsetting than adding at this point, because we’ve been playing for three weeks in this way. So yeah, it’s going to be just us two. We had 48 hours, I had 48 hours to take apart the whole set and put it back together before starting.

AD: That’s crazy.

Westerman: Which was, yeah, it was crazy. The rehearsal process was therefore very intense and stressful. But you know, life has a way of just You don’t really pick what’s going to happen a lot of the time. And I think there have definitely been a lot of things that I’ve learned out of the process of taking these songs apart and putting them back together. And in a way, it’s more reminiscent of when I first started playing music and I just played on my own and I didn’t have a band. And Jacob is a brilliant percussion player, but it really forces you to look at the songs in their purest form, all of them, you know, because you just don’t have any adornment. And so that’s always a good thing to keep an eye on, I think, as a songwriter, is to be forced to go through everything in such raw terms and analyze them on their merit. So I hope you enjoyed this show. We’ve been really enjoying it. Jacob’s been brilliant.

AD: Those constraints can be creatively productive as well. I’m thinking of Eno’s “oblique strategies” or, like, Oulipo, Georges Perec, those novelists who would go, like, I will write a novel without the letter E. Constraints like those can be materially rewarding. And I’m waiting to see how this tour might affect your next record, it might be interesting too.

Westerman: Yeah, yeah, I don’t… I mean, it’s all part of an ongoing conversation. I think it’s definitely given me things to think about.

AD: You’re not a very political person, it doesn’t seem to me, but you’ve been touring the US in a weird time. What did you learn about America in the process?

Westerman: I don’t think I learned anything about America, because I’ve been coming here quite often for the last few years. And I don’t think that America actually is… America isn’t any different to anywhere else in the Western world in some ways. It’s just magnified, you know, everything is just more extreme. So the scale of everything is larger here. I view it as more of the apex manifestation of a systemic problem that we’re all facing in Western democratic countries. I don’t view it as unique in that regard. I do think that what’s happening here is historic. You know, this is not… This is a new level of corruption in a democratic system. I don’t think we’ve seen… Well, certainly not in my lifetime, in living memory, we haven’t seen something that approaches what’s happening here right now. But the same thing may quite easily happen in the UK. If Nigel Farage wins the next election, then it won’t be an identical thing, but it will be the same kind of shit, you know. And I wouldn’t say I’m apolitical. I just… I think there are ways of talking about things. I try and find the links between things and try to dig underneath the manifestation, try and talk about what the thing is underneath it.

AD: And that’s a very, I think, intelligent way of approaching themes or motives. I think your records in general, since the first one, are already kind of mature like that. The music is very adult-oriented in a strange way. Its sound has been compared to Talk Talk and British sophistipop. In the concert, your visual aesthetic seems to be evocative of that kind of sleek, refined look of bands like the Blue Nile or Scritti Politti. Is that really your lineage in terms of influence? Or if not, what is?

Westerman: I don’t know. I think it’s a funny thing, as artists, talking or thinking in those terms. I think if you asked those people, they would probably not necessarily think that they were aligned with each other either. I think there are underlying principles which cross different genres and different aesthetics, which people are drawn towards. I think it’s alike, though the music that I make isn’t really alike. I was asked about this before. I don’t really take things from Mark Hollis in a kind of cut and paste way, but I think there’s a spirituality to that music, you know. And I think I’m drawn to music which seems to have a kind of a bedding of spirituality involved in it. I’m fairly disinterested in music where I think that’s completely lacking. But that’s, you know, not really an aesthetic or genre-binding thing. There are instances of that across every genre, and the opposite is true as well. So I think… t’s the idea of reaching into the unknown, and there’s a degree of compassion to that music, that I try, even when I’m upset, to find a way into.

AD: You mean, like, a sentimentality?

Westerman: A kind of a feeling of giving something as opposed to taking something, you know? A lot of rock music and a lot of popular music is more concerned with taking. But I don’t… The older I get, the less interesting this is. Maybe it’s because I’m older as well, but I find that less and less interesting. That’s kind of a wishy-washy answer, I know.

AD: No, it’s a good answer.

Westerman: I listen to a lot of jazz, you know? A lot. But specific kinds of jazz. Spiritual jazz music, spiritual instrumental music is something that I listen to more than any of those kinds of bands now.

AD: That’s interesting. What calls my attention more in your music, and especially in the concert, is this streaming kind of… I don’t know if it’s “vocal range,” the term, or if it’s really more of the vocal personae that you can inhabit and change between very quickly. You have this ability to shift between characters through intonation, because you have an elastic voice, a Bowie-like quality to me. It’s almost theatrical. How did you figure out this vocal power? Was that part of your training? Were you a theater kid? 

Westerman: I wasn’t a theater kid, I’ve just been playing around with that more over the last five years. When I started releasing music, I was quite bound by generally singing higher up the register, because I sang a lot in choirs. But I think for the last few years, and particularly when there are so few elements like the way we’ve been playing, I’ve thought more and more about how I can use my voice as another instrument, above and beyond just delivering the melody. If you have the ability to change the texture of your voice, it changes the feeling, you know, and you can do more with less. It’s experimenting with that, really. And it’s different, you know? It’s not the same every night. I try and keep it less prescriptive in that way, just to try and inhabit things as they kind of come up. And sometimes that goes better than others.

AD: You recorded the last album in this Greek island of Hydra, while you lived in Athens, right?

Westerman: That’s correct.

AD: I read that you were really inspired by the Greek skies. Could you expand on that? Because I’ve also read that the record was recorded exclusively at night. What is the explanation for that?

Westerman: When the lights are dying in Greece, everything just gets this crazy, over-saturated quality to it, where it almost seems unreal. And I wanted to try and capture this, I didn’t want to make something that was hyper-real because that’s a very well established thing. But what I wanted to try and capture was the feeling I got from how this light felt in terms of the contrast between the shades. And so I wanted to push the ideas of light and dark, both lyrically and aesthetically, in terms of the textures of the record, more than I had done. And just try and engender the feeling that I got from this visual experience and put it, somehow, put it into music. The songs are not written for that effect, they are stories. But in terms of a setting for it, I wanted to try and translate that, somehow.

AD: Do you think that’s because it is an insular area? Or is it the lack of city lights? A friend of mine once said that places where you have a lot of sea, like in those little islands of Greece, the sky is really different because it seems to mimic or mirror the water instead of it being the other way around. And I thought it was kind of stupid at first, but then I thought it was kind of beautiful. How do you explain that light? And do you also relate to the country’s history, philosophy, and so on? Is that relevant to you at all?

Westerman: Well, it’s relevant to me as much as I studied philosophy and I spent three years reading Greek philosophy. So I had a very… In my head, a very romanticized view… There’s a special place for all of that. And it was part of the reason why I chose to be in Athens. It wasn’t the sole reason I stayed there, but it was part of the reason. And yeah, in terms of place, I think Greece more as a symbol of something else (as opposed to the specificities of the kind of environment itself) is very relevant to the record. I think there’s an idea of motion, and compared with the second record, which was very much a record about feeling stuck and constricted, this record is an embrace of movement. I think there’s more light in the record. There’s a feeling of having moved away from something and, you know, getting over something to an extent. But I think Greece is definitely… Living in Athens was definitely, for me… That was where it happened. But I think it could just as easily have been somewhere else. Apart from the fact that what drew me to make this record sound the way it does was the specifics of the light where I was there. It’s the light when the light’s changing, it has nothing to do with no light in the city. I’m talking about the visual, if you go up on a hill and the light, in the twilight or dusk, you know, in the golden hour. The golden hour there is crazy. Everything seems unreal. The saturation is just… I don’t know. And you can’t capture it with… Maybe you could capture it with a better camera than I have, but I’ve never been able to capture it.

AD: Or with a record.

Westerman: Well, yeah, I mean, that’s what I wanted to try. I wanted to try and bottle that feeling and try and make music feel that way. It’s really intense? It’s an intense feeling, and it’s beautiful.

AD: To that point about the movement, the motion. I think the latest record adds a lot of rhythmic variation and broken drives that the first ones didn’t have. It’s way more unexpected and playful with form. I would say that the pop ambiances of the first record are percussified. That gives it a sense of motion. Do you think that’s a fair assessment?

Westerman: I think so. I mean, the structures are sort of loose. Also, I was recording with Stella Mozgawa, the drummer, from quite early on on these recordings. So the basis of about half of the songs is the interplay between live percussion and drums. And that was not the case on the previous two records. There were no live drums at all on the first record, and on the second one there were but I’d already mapped everything out digitally. and then it was embellishing as opposed to coming at it as a blank slate and forming something together. I’m glad that comes across.

AD: There’s a kind of a looser feel to it, then. Are you also looser in terms of your criteria for putting stuff out too? I’ve heard that this record was a faster process, and that maybe you’re becoming less neurotic about what you’re releasing?

Westerman: I mean, I’m still fairly neurotic about when I decide that something’s finished. We worked pretty hard, you know. The recording is quick and then I spent about a year getting it finished. But I think the more you’ve done this thing… I think, initially, the stakes are very high because of the novelty of it, and the feeling of… It’s like birthdays, you know? When you’re a kid, birthdays have this incredible meaning to you because you haven’t had very many. But as you get older, you just see they’re sort of small markers in a lineage of your life. It doesn’t have the same excitement when you’re twenty-eight as it does when your six. It’s not that I’m not excited about the records either. I think it’s just feeling more relaxed about the idea that it’s an ongoing conversation and no one record needs to do anything other than what it’s going to do on its own, hopefully, with the help of other people, championing it. It’s more just understanding that the work that you do is the work, and then after the fact, you know, it’s just gone. And then it just becomes part of an ongoing conversation.

AD: You played a new song or two in the concert. I often think of Philip Roth, the American writer, who used to say that the worst periods in his life were the ones when he was trying to figure out what the next book was going to be about, because he had just finished something and now his life was meaningless and empty for a while, until he could figure out the next thing, when he would start writing and it would be fine again. But those moments of waiting are like hell, trying to figure out the following work. Are you already preparing a new record? You’re talking about this ongoing conversation. What is the next step in this process of continuous artistic birth? Is it all lighter-born now because of this continuation, repetition, because you’re having more birthdays?

Westerman: Yeah, I don’t know. I need to say I’ve been on tour pretty much since last August in some capacity, so I haven’t… I find it quite difficult to work well, you just get really tired after a while. But I’m looking forward to sitting down after these shows. I have an unwieldy amount of half-finished stuff, and over the next few months I’m just going to sort through that and try and find shape. You can have overriding, abstract, grandiose ideas about stuff. But I’ve always found that, with music, if you start trying to shoehorn things, then it tends to die. So I think I need to go and listen to what I’ve got. Try to find through lines and then think about where all this is coming.

AD: The idea for the album is emergent.

Westerman: Yeah, yeah. When I’m writing music, I’m not really thinking about anything. It’s just going through a lot of that stuff and seeing what the shapes that have emerged from this unthinking process are. And then coming up with some sort of structure of how to present these and sort of come up… and try to find what the narrative arc is between them. I tend to do it that way around. I’ve never had much success completely pre-planning a record and then writing to that brief. I need to sit down and see what it is that I’ve got.

AD: I guess you think with your hands or your body, when you’re composing, as a musician. You don’t need to think explicitly, I mean, it’s a nonverbal thing. I like this book by Henri Focillon called The Life of Forms in Art, and there’s a chapter in there called “In Praise of Hands,” about how hands think through themselves when you’re painting, and you don’t need to think about them much. Like they have a life of their own. Say, Da Vinci didn’t draw because he was a genius, he was a genius because he drew. The drawing is the form of thinking that he directed to the world. People tend to overvalue the intellectuality of music, don’t they? Giving meaning, express meaning, to it. What do you think?

Westerman: Yeah, I think so. I mean, I think it’s a complex one, but with music, even if there are verbal elements to it, you’re trying to combine a few different things to express something which is understandable in a not-entirely verbal way. I’m trying to trick my mind into not knowing what it’s doing and basically trying to get things out before my mind has the capacity to try and order it or shoehorn it into some pre-existing model that it has of what can be understood. I just think that that’s… I think the head just gets in the way so much in life in general. I mean, it’s an important thing to be able to analyze things and think about stuff, probably. But that isn’t really where the music is. The music’s a different thing.

AD: In the second record, you had some… you collaborated with James Krivchenia, from Big Thief, and other people like Luke Temple, who is one of the greatest living singer-songwriters in my opinion. For this one, you had Marta Salogni, who I’d say is maybe one of the most defining producers of the last ten years of experimental pop—she worked with Mica Levi, Lucrecia Dalt, Bar Italia, all those people. How do you see the effect of those collaborations in your work? And what sort of new encounters are you thinking of for those new patterns that you’re seeing emerge?

Westerman: I don’t know. I mean, I’ve been doing some work with Marta. So there’ll be some songs finished from that in not too long. I think we’ll probably finish them in a month or so. But after that, I don’t really know. I don’t know what I’m going to do.

AD: The reason I ask is because I think a central ability of musicians is also to… is relational. Being able to get into this position where you have talented people around you. Organizing them, their affects, and putting them to work together in this beautiful way.

Westerman: I would like to try and find a way of bringing together a lot of these great people that I’ve worked with independently. But it’s difficult when they’re highly talented, motivated and busy people. So I don’t… Yeah, I don’t know what I’ll do next. But I think I really enjoy all of those people, Bullion, James, and Marta, all of these people work in radically different ways. Given I work in quite a solitary way, really, I don’t write with other people, it’s very interesting for me to see how other people’s creative processes work. And I definitely learned from all of those experiences many, many things. Hopefully they learned one or two things too. I don’t know. I’ve never asked. But I certainly learned a lot.

AD: Correct me if I’m wrong, but you moved to Italy and you got married since the last record?

Westerman: Yes, right, yeah.

AD: How have you been adapting to this new life? Emotionally, geographically… Does the lack of solitude impact your work, which is so much marked by it? Or has it been even more lonely now, in a sense, somehow?

Westerman: Well, I haven’t been… I think what’s impacted the work more is just being away. I’ve been away so much. We got married in July and then from the beginning of September until now I’ve only been at home for, like, three weeks. That makes assimilating quite challenging. That’s another thing I’m looking forward to do after these shows, is just being… I need to learn the language. I speak just a bit of Italian. I need to learn the language. I would say I found Milan a more difficult city to crack in some ways. But that’s also because I’ve just been leaving so much to do things like these. It’s hard, I think, to move to a new place if you don’t get chunks of time to actually be present there. Because building relationships and understanding places and learning customs, all of that stuff, it’s very hard to do when you’re there for a week and then you leave and then you’re back for a few days and then you leave. So I don’t have as good a… I definitely never had a native grasp of what it is to be an Athenian, for sure. It’s probably somewhere slightly below the surface of a tourist level, but nowhere near native. But I feel, in Milan, like I still don’t really understand it. Which is okay. I mean, it’s exciting. There’s all that to look forward to. But I don’t know, basically, at the moment.

AD: Is there anything else you would like to say or ask of me? Shout outs or comments that you’ve been meaning to add to this interview but I didn’t offer you space to because I’m dumb?

Westerman: No, I just wanted to plug Otto Benson, who’s on tour with us.

AD: He’s a great musician. I really loved his show as well.

Westerman: Yeah, I just wanted to, like… If this is coming out before any of the other shows, to remind people that Otto’s on tour with us and that they should be there early.

AD: I think he is also opening for Bill Callahan, right? It’s an interesting little combination, being with you and with Bill at the same time.

Westerman: What a life.
