## Body

Lagniappe (la ·gniappe) noun ‘lan-ˌyap,’ — 1. An extra or unexpected gift or benefit. 2. Something given or obtained as a gratuity or bonus.

Since Ezra Feinberg’s return to making and releasing music at the close of the last decade, he’s been on an unbelievable run. After a short early career stint in Piano Magic and three albums fronting the outstanding post-prog group Citay, Feinberg took a step back from music and focused on his professional career as a psychoanalyst. His solo career did not formally begin in earnest until the release of 2018’s Pentimento and others, followed two years later by the wonderful Recumbent Speech, whose gentle kosmische drift provided a balm for frayed nerves in the lockdown summer. Last year’s excellent Soft Power confirmed a renaissance in progress, and cemented Feinberg’s reputation as the foremost practitioner of an especially gorgeous and thoughtful crossbreed of minimalism, ambient and post-rock. Feinberg’s contributions to our ongoing series of Lagniappe Sessions square the circle of his sound, offering up covers of the shimmering folk-pop vocal group The Roches, on the one hand, and minimalist composer and Philip Glass Ensemble stalwart Jon Gibson, on the other. Feinberg’s gift has always been to endow minimalist process and ambient expansion with a real emotional weight, so the balance here between lovelorn romanticism and new music abstraction seems particularly on point. Feinberg’s covers are alternately heartbreaking and harrowing.

Ezra Feinberg :: Losing True (The Roches)

“Losing True” by the Roches is an all-timer for me. I’ve been deeply in love with this song for a very long time. The song is pretty simple, just the soaring, intense vocal harmonies of the Roches sisters over an acoustic strumming pattern, then Robert Fripp’s guitar comes in midway through and lifts it up even further. I aspire to the celestial heights of this song in my own music, and I’ve always wondered what it would be like to arrange the tune for wind instruments. I had a feeling my friend and longtime collaborator, the multi-instrumentalist Robbie Lee, would be up for giving it a try. We started by tracking the chords on hammer dulcimer. Robbie collects flutes and recorders and other woodwinds, and we used different instruments from his collection. I added a bit of tweaked electric guitar at the end, and we both played some acoustic. We did the whole thing in two days, picking apart the harmonies and constructing the track piece by piece. By layering the woodwinds Robbie brought out the yearning and melancholy of the original, and, perhaps, a bit of rainy day romanticism as well. 

Ezra Feinberg :: Visitations 1 (Jon Gibson)

“Visitations 1” by Jon Gibson felt like a good foil to the Roches tune. It’s a sprawling, unsettling, range-y piece, and even the idea of “covering” it seems weird just because it’s so abstract. Gibson was the horn and woodwind player for the Philip Glass ensemble for over 50 years, and played with all the big minimalists. Although a recent and beautiful collection by Superior Viaduct has reintroduced him, his solo work is not widely known. “Visitations 1,” from 1974, is more or less a tape collage using field recordings, lots of percussion, and Gibson’s flute improvisations. I wanted to take his original to a larger ensemble of musicians all in the same room. So much, maybe too much of my recording and music making since the pandemic has been remote, so I wanted try playing this piece live in the studio, all-in-one-take-style. My friend Joey Weiss has this beautiful barn in upstate NY that he converted to a studio, so he engineered the session with help from John Thayer (who produced my record Soft Power). John played percussion too, with Otto Hauser on drums, me on guitar, Will Epstein on soprano sax, Matt Bauder on flute, and Omer Shemesh on piano. We just went for it one afternoon last September. The only directive was an open improvisation beginning with shimmering cymbals and percussion. Later John and I made a bunch of edits and cuts, I overdubbed bits or organ and guitar, and John added percussion and modular synthesizer. He ran everything through a tape machine, slowed it way down, brought that back to the original live take, and, at the very end, included a field recording of rain and the calling of a mysterious bird. If anyone can identify that bird call we’d love to know! 

Session notes: “Losing True” – Ezra on guitar with Robbie Lee on woodwinds, engineered at Lee’s studio in Brooklyn. “Visitations 1” – recorded at Joey Weiss’s studio in Rosendale, NY (upstate) with Ezra on guitar, Will Epstein on soprano sax, att Bauder on flute, Otto Hauser on drums, John Thayer on percussion, and Omer Shemesh on piano. Both tracks mixed and mastered by John Thayer at Friendly Gain studios in Catskill, NY.
