## Body

I’m People represents the beginning of a new era for Hiss Golden Messenger. Presented by the legendary label Chrysalis Records, the new project finds songwriter MC Taylor presenting a set of songs dedicated to, as he puts it in the album’s biography, “Truth, lies, magic, faith.” It’s a potent combo, and I’m People presents Taylor and his collaborators at his most refined, taking all the things that have made Hiss Golden Messenger recordings work so well in the past—country rock shuffles, indomitable boogies, soul and R&B flourishes, and heartland pop—and melting it all down to its core.

Cut in upstate New York, at a studio called Dreamland outside of Woodstock, Taylor produced the album with journeyman Josh Kaufman (The National, Craig Finn, Bob Weir), who also plays guitar, and a cast of collaborators featuring Sam Beam of Iron and Wine, Eric D. Johnson of Fruit Bats, and iconic piano man Bruce Hornsby, among others. Though straight ahead and direct, it’s a loose and freewheelin’ album, one that ambles with ease and reaches peak rave-up in its most spirited moments.

Taylor has always written about the vagaries of the human condition, about faith and doubt, but I’m People presents his most considered songs yet. They are songs that brim with hope, but not Hallmark card grade hope—instead, Taylor focuses on a tougher, more resilient source of inspiration, the power of human connection and shared space, searching for real human contact in an age of disconnection. As he sings on the title track’s ebullient chorus: 

“Well, I can tell by your lines you’re in solitary flight
You may be the first true unknown
Oh, but baby, pretty soon you’re gonna need somebody
Nobody makes it alone”

We caught up with Taylor to discuss the recording, the influence of artists like Curtis Mayfield and Sinéad O’Connor, and the golden age of paranormal AM radio. Filing our report from West of the Rockies, this conversation has been edited for cohesion and clarity. | j woodbury 

Aquarium Drunkard: Congrats on I’m People. Can you tell me about how that title made sense for this collection of songs? 

MC Taylor: I think there was a lot of subconscious work happening, not on the surface, in terms of naming it. I was trying to figure out a title that would work thematically. There [were other] potential titles for the record, but they almost all felt like “AI Hiss Golden Messenger” album titles.  

AD: Like Claude or ChatGPT-generated?

MC Taylor: Right. Like, “Depends on the River.” That’s something that AI would come up with for Hiss Golden Messenger. [Laughs] But thematically, I’m People spoke to a thread that was woven through all of the songs in one way or another. It felt like the title of an imaginary Curtis Mayfield record to me. My music functions differently from the way Curtis’s does, but I’ve always taken a lot of inspiration from his music. He seems like a model. There are lots of layers to what he’s doing. They go from the intensely personal and small to very broad. He’s doing different kinds of work at the same time. 

That’s something I’m attracted to as a songwriter. I’ve never had any luck writing broadly. I feel like I’ve learned over and over again that it’s the most vulnerable, internal songs that end up conversely having the biggest potential to connect with lots of people. Anytime I’ve tried to write broadly, it’s never worked. It gets too washed out. Some people can do it. I can’t do it. But Curtis, in terms of the way that his music works spiritually, was making gospel music. If you’re wired that way, if you’re spirit curious, he’s there for you. If you have no interest in that side of things, you can listen to his music in that way as well. His catalog is a constellation. You see what you want to see in it, which is how my favorite music works.

AD: In the song, “I’m People” you speak of a fortune teller. Did you really have an encounter with a spiritual guide of some sort?

MC Taylor: Was there actually a fortune teller? Yes.

AD: We don’t have to spoil the mystery if it’s important for you.

Taylor: No, that’s fine. But that’s maybe all I need to say about it: yes… 

AD: With that song, it feels like you’re offering an acknowledgement of the “altogetherness” of our collective situation. And that translates, to me, as optimistic. There’s hope on the record. 

MC Taylor: As I’ve been thinking about the emotional tenor of this record, [I’ve come to think] of it as realistic hope. I mean, what choice do we have? I don’t know that I can wake up every morning feeling like I’m living under a dark cloud, because there are a lot of beautiful, joyful and animating things in my life. So much of my life has been toggling between “I’m feeling good” or “I’m feeling bad.” I can’t live in the in-between, because that sense of not knowing, not even knowing my own mind…it makes me very anxious.

But I feel like I’ve come to this place in the last couple years where I’m trying to look around and allow myself to feel joyful, happy, hopeful, silly, inspired in the face of the darkness that is so clearly swirling around. I’m trying to do both. I’m trying to say, like, “Yes, I see how much suffering we’re all involved in,” and at the same time, “I feel good today. I love my kids. I love my wife. I’m so fortunate to have this life that allows me to be creative.” Not everybody gets to feel the things that I do, and I’m going to hold both of these things right now, and that’s the only realistic way. 

I’m going to work towards making the world feel better in whatever way that I can. I might not accomplish the things that I’m hoping for and dreaming of, but I am trying to move the needle a little bit. There’s a song on the record called “Heavy World” and the chorus of it [holds] some tension, because it says, “Heavy world, keep on suffering, we’re all suffering now, it ain’t over”. And there’s an ambiguity in that that even I have been trying to pick apart. I think that what is being said there is, if things are moving forward in the direction that we’re hoping for, we might not get to see the end result in our lifetime. Our kids might not either. So the world isn’t over. We might be over at a certain point, but the world isn’t over.

I'm People by Hiss Golden Messenger

Again, I’m not, I don’t think of myself as a prescriptive songwriter [but I’m] trying to develop the ability to live in the in-between states. To recognize the good and the bad at the same time, and in fact, like the bad, the quote, unquote bad, whatever that might be needing to exist in order to define what good might be in your life. That’s been a hard process for me. Maybe some are easygoing with it, but it’s been tricky for me. Some of the record is  about me on that path, [trying to recognize that] not every little piece of bad news is a catastrophe. It’s just a problem to be solved. That’s what life is, is just a never ending series of problems to be solved. That’s what making a record is. I’m always just saying, “We’re just solving all the problems here that we’re presenting ourselves with, joyfully.” 

AD: The joyful part is the trick. That’s what music offers so many of us. Like, what’s swirling around in my mind is the guitar solo in the song “Spirit Cat”. Who is that playing guitar?

MC Taylor: Josh Kaufman.

AD: He sounds like he’s flying and you, as a listener, get to go along for the ride. Music can inspire a primordial feeling like that. 

MC Taylor: That sense of wonder and impulse—feeling a newness to things that we do regularly—I feel like that’s what I’m chasing right now, and certainly I was chasing that with the making of this record. Sometimes around touring musicians, and that’s who I’m around the most, there’s this attitude of, like, “How’s it going?” And the response is, “I’m fucking beat, dude. We were in blah blah blah last night and I’m just so wrecked.” I have been that way too. There’s a tremendous amount of fatigue. But there’s also that salty, grizzled road dog costume [we sometimes put on]. At this point in my life that doesn’t feel cool to me anymore. More and more, I want to really try and be like, “How am I doing? I’ve been on the road for three weeks—I’m really tired—but this is so beautiful. What a life.” 

I'm People by Hiss Golden Messenger

AD: There was another lyric that I want to ask you about, from “Who You  Gonna Run To.” You sing, “Coast to Coast through the static/As the midnight poet philosophizes all night long.” I found myself thinking of that experience; anyone who ever had a late night drive in the ‘90s, [listening to the] radio as some batshit guy talking about crystal skulls or whatever…I just wondered if that line was inspired by a Court and Spark tour back in the day, or something along those lines? 

MC Taylor: Totally. Even early Hiss tours, or trips I was on with my wife, pre-kids. For anyone who doesn’t know, Coast to Coast was a radio show. 

AD: Is a radio show.

Taylor: Is. [Laughs] I’ve just heard some rumors that it’s veered in a direction that wouldn’t be quite so fun.

AD: All conspiracy culture has unfortunately veered in a less fun direction. 

MC Taylor: But what you and I are talking about is Art Bell. Art Bell was the main man. You’d just hear that deep, skeptical smoker’s voice, transmitting from some bunker in Nevada or wherever he was, giving people the space to talk about alien abductions and ghost visitations. I’ve heard some stuff on there that’s raised the hair on my arms. Driving through the middle of the desert, in middle of the night trying to get from one far flung place to another, with everybody asleep in the van, and you’re white knuckling it…you’ve got a full moon over the mountains, and Art Bell and his cadre of freaks are talking…that is a special place to be. It makes the world seem so wide, you know?

I just love mystery. I feel like I miss it. We live in a time where everything is available at all moments, there’s a real drive towards that. When I was young, you didn’t have all the answers. Like when I was a kid encountering It Takes a Nation of Millions to Hold Us Back as a 13-year-old, I was flabbergasted. I had so many questions. How did they do this? How did they actually make this? How does Chuck D’s voice sound like that? 

AD: Before we wrap things up, there are certain records that I have a fondness for, but I only share that fondness with a few other people. And one of those records is one we’ve talked about, Sinéad O’Connor’s out-of-print reggae album, Throw Down Your Arms. 

MC Taylor: Oh, yeah. Yeah.

AD: All apologies to Sublime or whatever, but it’s the best “white” reggae album ever. And it’s not even a white reggae thing—because it’s Sly and Robbie playing and producing—but what is it about that album that speaks to you?

MC Taylor: The niche for getting into that record is small. You have to take a very specific pathway. I love reggae music, and in particular, I love roots-era reggae music. It’s devotional music, gospel music, with the rhythm arranged in a Jamaican way. It feels to me like Sinéad got inside the devotional aspect of that music. I can’t really think of another white reggae artist that can convincingly sing these devotional songs. Even as I think about it now, it’s even a little hard to know exactly what position to take on it, other than to feel like Sinéad was really feeling the devotion of the music. I feel like a lot of people come to reggae as party music, it’s music that you put on when you’re feeling when things are feeling good and it’s sunny and it’s time to be light.

AD: Right.

MC Taylor: But that doesn’t seem like the relationship that she has to Jamaican music…talk about being able to feel joy and the dread at the same time. I can’t think of a better object lesson, except for maybe jazz music, than reggae music, which is about how hard the world is, but you’re feeling lifted despite it. That’s a long answer to get back to the Sinéad thing. I just feel like she was in it. She was never grubbing for money. I’ve always given her the benefit of the doubt artistically with everything she ever did. She never has disappointed.

AD: You want to reckon with what she makes, certainly. 

MC Taylor: Totally. I feel like there are other people that are considered celebrity musicians that I wouldn’t feel the same way about, but she was a real one. I would be hard pressed to think of another public person as real as Sinéad O’Connor.
