## Body

It’s no accident that Arab Strap’s eighth full album has a couple of emojis in the title. A disgruntled, disgusted masterwork, I’m totally fine with it lives in the belly of the digital beast, spewing blasted poetry at the dits and dots that move through its digestive tract. The disc even opens with the now old-fashioned sound of a modem connection, as a drum kit goes off banging, and a bass carves up an evil racket. Soon “Allatonceness” is underway with Aidan Moffat expressing artful, poetic disdain for online discourse. Seriously, who else writes like this?

“They’ve got your attention, deluders and doxers/self-righteous, self-styled renegades/They’ve got your attention, the groomers and grifters/and they’ve all done their own research/They’ve got your attention, antagonised fanboys/while Nazis and rapists sell merch.”

And that’s just the opener. Never mind the two thumbs up in the title, Moffat and his characters are sinking deep into web 2.0 dependency, kicking all the way down. “Sociometer” sounds like a serial-killer-level obsessive love letter, but it’s addressed to, who else, the internet. “I come to you for confirmation, testimony, adulation; education, excitation, palpitation and flirtation. But you give me aggravation, insult and disinformation; hatred, bias and predation, suicidal ideation,” he murmurs, close to the mic and threatening, letting the rhythm of the words carry him along like a rapper. The backdrop is bright and dance-y, a clangor of piano and shuffling syncopated beat, the saw-toothed blurt of bass coming up from the bottom.

Shiny surfaces gleam atop murderous undercurrents, the sleek rush of dance floor hedonism corroding as it pulses. The music is enticing and toxic at the same time, like the mole in “Molehill” with his kind eyes and tiny hands and a venom that paralyzes its prey so they can be devoured, over time, at the mole’s leisure.

And yet, there’s a deeply human, vulnerable sensibility tucked into these shiny, dystopic songs, in the truth and longing of “You’re Not There,” a song about a man texting his deceased wife, or in the sympathy behind the thump of “Bliss” about a girl harassed on social media. “Safe & Well,” whose protagonist has died unnoticed during the COVID lockdown, prods at the hurt of alienation ever so gently, more an elegy than a social commentary.  Arab Strap tells us they’re fine and they don’t give a fuck anymore, but they do. The caring is in there, along with the satire. | j kelly
