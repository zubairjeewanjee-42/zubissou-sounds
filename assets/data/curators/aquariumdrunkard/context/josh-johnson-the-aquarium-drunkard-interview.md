## Body

As a young listener, saxophonist Josh Johnson would scour album credits, trying to map out the disparate connections. Someone doing the same thing in 2024 would have plenty of chances to spot Johnson’s own name. In addition to his work with Meshell Ndegeocello, whose 2023 The Omnichord Real Book he produced, and artists like Makaya McCraven, Harry Styles, Leon Bridges, and Broken Bells, Johnson is a pivotal member of the Los Angeles avant-jazz scene, where he’s frequently seen collaborating with his mentor, Jeff Parker. 

On his solo album, Unusual Object, Johnson takes the lessons and tricks learned from those disparate collaborations and applies them to his own music. Operating on his own but utilizing sound expanding technology to layer, riff, and essentially collaborate with himself, he’s pulled together a dizzying and engaging mix of jazz and electronic soundscapes, built around his own compositions as well as a cover of Mal Waldron’s “All Alone.” He joined AD way back in May to discuss the record and his unlikely collaboration with the Red Hot Chili Peppers. | j woodbury 

Aquarium Drunkard: Hey Josh, how are you? In addition to your own albums, you’re an in-demand session player and collaborator. What does a typical day look like for you? 

Josh Johnson: It’s kind of all over the place. Sometimes it’s session stuff, sometimes it’s just working on my own music. Some days I’m learning other people’s music. Every day is kind of different. Some days are just for practicing. It’s a little more elusive to me at this point, but there are days where I don’t have much to do and I can kind of just sit at an instrument and explore the things I’ve been thinking about, I’ve been meaning to work on. I love whenever I get the time and space to be able to do that.

AD: The sax is very clearly at the heart of Unusual Object, but sound is treated so beautifully—was this approach inspired by live experiments with looping and sampling, or vice versa where the studio stuff leads to your live presentation? 

Josh Johnson: Yes, all of it. When I’m performing live, it’s just me. There’s a few signals. There’s the saxophone through a microphone, but then a pickup in the saxophone that’s getting processed. And I’m kind of doing stuff in real time as I’m playing. And the saxophone is also controlling a hardware sampler. So there are times when a sampler is functioning like a drum machine. It’s not about the gear, but the sampler allows me to kind of create some spontaneity or explore some different things by myself. But there are some ways that I use a sampler live where certain notes on the saxophone react or trigger certain things on a sampler, so that I can accompany myself or something like that. It’s also kind of exploring the processes in both spaces and they kind of inform each other, I guess.

AD: You produced Meshell Ndegeocello’s The Omnichord Real Book, which won a Grammy in a new category, “Best Alternative Jazz Album.” Do you think there’s some value in acknowledging the way jazz in its modern form pulls from all sorts of sounds and styles now? Is there some necessity in differentiating between those things? 

Josh Johnson: I’ll expand on this, but “I don’t worry about it” is the short answer. The question I’m always asking myself is: Who benefits from genre? And I don’t think musicians are generally thinking that way that often. And I mean, I understand the complicated relationship with institutions such as the Grammys, but I understand the acknowledgement of, “Okay, within this system that represents a very small amount of music made in the world, some sort of acknowledgement is needed that, okay, we can’t try to fit so much music into this one genre.” 

So I guess I appreciate some acknowledgement that there are other things happening that are worthy of attention. But the short answer is, I don’t really think about it and I definitely don’t think about it while making music. I won’t speak for Meshell, but I know that as we were working on that no one was thinking about genre or Grammys or anything like that. 

AD: Now look, I don’t know if you actually coined the term “cyberpunk ambient jazz” which was used to describe Unusual Object, but I wonder how you felt when you heard your record described that way? 

Josh Johnson: [Laughs] Yeah, I did not coin that term. I think that was probably a collaboration with Mark Trecka who wrote about the album after him and I had a long conversation about trying to contextualize it. I just talked with him about my experience with music. 

AD: That’s a surefire way to get someone like me to listen to a record. 

Josh Johnson: It’s hard for me to name something that I am that close to. It’s nice to have something descriptive that can serve as an entry point for people. 

AD: Your production is just as pivotal to your sound as your playing. How did you get into recording? 

Josh Johnson: There were a lot of different entry points for me. I was playing saxophone, but I was also playing keyboard in bands. And there was a band that I was in when I was in high school, and I remember we all put money together and shared a copy of Reason and split the license.

AD: I feel like a few years ago, it was speaking with your frequent collaborator Jeff Parker that really got me thinking about how sampling and jazz can be connected and expanded in the modern era. 

Josh Johnson: Sampling is a pretty broad thing, but on this record, many of the songs are live performances of me navigating all this stuff in real time. I think Jeff has been a mentor to me, but I think we are kindred in terms of curiosity about just kind of dissolving the lines between it all. And maybe it’s connected to what we’re talking about with genre too. Part of the space I’m coming from in working on this music is it’s all fair game. These are the tools to get to a statement or get to a feeling or the creation of a space—there’s no reason that we shouldn’t employ any of the things that we have to get to wherever we’re trying to be.

AD: One of your more intriguing credits is that you play on the recent Red Hot Chili Peppers record. What was it like being in the studio with RHCP? 

Josh Johnson: Pretty bizarre if I’m a hundred percent honest. [Laughs] It’s a thing I never imagined. The connection for me is through my friend Nate Walcott, a great multi-instrumentalist, trumpet player, composer, arranger, and pianist, who for a short time was kind of touring with the Chili Peppers. I don’t know if we met through Jeff, but he’s on one of the New Breed albums and he’s in Bright Eyes. We’ve done a few things together. But anyway, it was a random call of like, “Hey, you want to come do this session?” And it was kind of during the pandemic. 

So for whatever reason, there’s a few different records that feature a horn section of ours, which was me, Nate Walcott, and Vikram Devasthali on trombone. We played on a few different people’s records and the Chili Peppers one came up and basically we did it as the three of us. Flee was also playing trumpet. And he’s a big jazz fan. And I think there was one piece where they asked us all to improvise, some kind of round robin thing. And after we did that later they were like, “Hey, would you come back and play on some more stuff?” And I was like, “Sure.” 

AD: Were they a part of your listening life before? 

Josh Johnson: Their music was not really a part of my life really. And as strange as it, bizarre as it was to me to think about it, I was also like, “If there’s going to be saxophone on a Red Hot Chili Peppers record, why not?” 

I feel lucky. I feel like music is a community-based activity. At the end of the day, I like making music with my friends and I feel like that branches out and ends up looking like different things. When I got into music, I’m was looking at the credits and trying to map it and be like, “Oh, I saw this person’s name over here and they did this thing.” I love being able to see the overlap between different parts of music communities. I feel lucky to get to see that playing out in the different communities that I’m a part of.
