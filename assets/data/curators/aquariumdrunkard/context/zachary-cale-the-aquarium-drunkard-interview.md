## Body

Like most of us during the dog days of the pandemic, Brooklyn singer-songwriter Zachary Cale found himself adrift, searching for inspiration in thoroughly weird times. He found it in a Red Hook art studio, where a piano sat, mostly unused. Cale’s primary instrument is the guitar — you can hear his expert playing all over his previous records. Composing on piano wasn’t his usual mode. But during those long nights in Red Hook, songs started to come. 

We can now all enjoy the magnificent results on Next Year’s Ghost, which Cale recorded with a band that includes the masterful Shahzad Ismaily, along with drummer Jeremy Gustin and guitarist Uriah Theriault. Out now on Org Music, it’s a spectral and lovely collection, one that speaks to the chaos and confusion of our times, but also is shot through with welcome glimmers of hope. With a record release show looming (an excellent co-bill with Ezra Feinberg), Aquarium Drunkard caught up with Zach to get the lowdown on Next Year’s Ghost and to chat about various other related topics. | t wilcox

Aquarium Drunkard: It’s almost 20 years since your first record came out, right?

Zachary Cale: Yeah, 2005 was when the first one came out. I moved to New York in 2002 and that record came out soon afterwards. It felt like a right place, right time scenario. I met John Allen, who has the WFMU show JA In The AM — he’s still up there. I had made this four-track cassette recording, and John heard it and said, “Hey I want to put this out.” He introduced me to so many people, like all the No-Neck Blues Band guys. I actually ended up living in their house for a bit even. It was called the Hint House, situated right next to Columbia University.

New York definitely kicked my ass at the beginning. I debated leaving all the time, the first six years especially. Then I think you just get over the hump and then it’s like, well, I’m here. I found my people… and work! I feel thankful that I have my job because people that make their living from odd jobs and live gigs — just the hustle of that … It sucks. No doubt about it. But when I met the No-Neck guys, it was like — these guys are all older than me, and they just seem like they’ve been here forever. They have crazy record collections and just so much junk and stuff [laughs]. And they’re making it work. They were kind of mentors to me. I still know so many of them — Dave Shuford (Rhyton), Keith Connolly (Gray/Smith), and Jason Meagher from Black Dirt Studio. They are still out there doing great stuff. 

AD: Back then, you had the “New York Rock Revival” stuff and electro-clash, but also what was eventually called freak folk, for better or worse. 

Zachary Cale: Yeah, the NY rock revival was winding down, it had already moved on to electro-clash and new weird America. Not sure how those two things co-existed. When I was getting my first gigs, they always wanted to peg me as freak folk, which I never could identify with. I don’t know. At the time I liked Joanna Newsom, Animal Collective. Ben Chasny, I love Ben especially. He probably never wants to hear the words “freak folk” ever again [laughs]. There was a lot of great music then and a lot of fluff, but the good stuff transcended the fad. But that’s the funny thing about New York, too. I feel like I’ve seen so many things come and go. I’m old! It’s like, oh yeah, this is coming back again! 

AD: We’ve gone through so many different cycles. But back to that first record — can you still connect with the musician or person you were then? 

Zachary Cale: I’m one of those types who doesn’t listen to albums once they’re done. Maybe I’ll hear something from an old record randomly. It’s odd, because I recognize it, but I don’t recognize it. Even if it’s a song I’ve played a lot live since, the arrangement has changed, or my voice has changed, or I don’t play it in that key anymore. I’m always like, wow, that’s what it sounded like originally? You spend so much time mixing and agonizing over it, that by the time you let it out in the world, you’re glad you never have to hear it again. I’ll play the songs live, but it’s not gonna sound the same. It’s going to keep morphing. I will say that when I randomly come across the old stuff, I’m always kind of amazed by the effort and ambition of it all. I was really trying for something at least. It’s not just a puff of smoke. I kind of marvel at how much guts I had when I was young. 

AD: I was listening to your new record and the new Six Organs record and some other things from songwriters of a similar age and thinking that you guys are just getting better. How are you feeling about making music into middle age? 

Zachary Cale: If you think of Dylan — I mean, on his first record he’s 20 and now he’s 83. It’s crazy to think his whole life has been recorded. Even though that kind of career arc is not really possible now, the idea of archiving your life through your records and other collaborations is a beautiful thing. That’s part of why we keep doing it. I got to hang out with Michael Chapman a few times and I asked him about that point blank. “Is that one of the reasons why you do it – to mark the span of time?” He said absolutely. I mean, it’s the art of it, too, but these records are how I mark my time. I’ve always been old fashioned that way. I think most musicians who are in it for the long haul think of it that way, too. 

AD: It’s your life’s work.

Zachary Cale: At the same time, I’m not constantly thinking of it that way, you know? Making music is just how I function. It’s a mechanism to make me feel OK. It’s therapy, medication. I probably ask myself on a daily basis, “Why am I doing this?” Ultimately, I do it because it keeps me together. 

Next Year's Ghost by Zachary Cale

AD: You’ve said that Next Year’s Ghost came out of the pandemic. Obviously, it was a weird time everywhere, but I imagine New York City was especially weird. 

Zachary Cale: Yeah, the songs on this record are shaped by that experience, definitely. I didn’t set out to make a “pandemic record.” I hope it’s more universal in the end. But yeah, I don’t want to say New York was more dismal than any other place. It was hard for everyone, of course. But New York felt like Ground Zero at the beginning. It was just frightening. You didn’t know how it was going to go. How long is this gonna last? In New York, it’s insanely busy, 24/7. And when the pandemic hit, the city just stopped. It was the eeriest thing. This dream-like quietude, a Last Man on Earth feeling. Just the fact that you could take a walk and have the city to yourself. That’s really where all those songs came from. I had the space and time to be patient and slow way down. 

AD: Tell me about how the piano came to be one of the central sounds on the new record. 

Zachary Cale: At the time my friend Ryan Johnson had an art studio in Red Hook. He’d be there nine to five, working on his sculptures and then when he would leave, I’d walk over. I wasn’t going there to write an album or anything. I was just going there to like, get the fuck out of my apartment. But yeah, there was a piano there, and I didn’t even think about what I was doing. I just went and tinkered around as a routine for a long time. Just to zone out and meditate. I was listening to Emahoy Tsege Mariam Gebru a lot. Her dreamy fluid style was a guiding light, mentally and musically. At first, I thought about making some kind of ambient piano recording, like Harold Budd style, but then songs started happening and by the end of 2020 I had a bunch of material. I recorded some of them on a Zoom recorder and thought about just throwing them up on Bandcamp. But I played them for some friends, and they thought I should do something a little more with them. 

I had been thinking about working with Shahzad Ismaily for a little while, so I sent him the demos and he was really into it. I’d seen him play with countless people, but I’d never actually played with him myself. But I had a feeling right off the bat that he’d be the right collaborator. He put the group together, found a drummer, Jeremy Gustin, who I’d never met before — we’re good pals now. Incredible drummer. I needed a rhythm section to keep my playing together, since I’m such a novice [at piano]. Those two are a dream to play with. They’re both master improvisers, I can throw anything at them on the fly and they’re right there.

I was inspired by albums like Bill Fay’s Time of the Last Persecution or John Cale’s Fear. Those albums are stately and beautiful but also kind of tweaked, right? They have this sideways melodicism. Plastic Ono Band, was a big one too — in interviews John Lennon had stated that he wasn’t much of a piano player, he just chose to play piano on that record because the songs needed to be super direct. That’s how I felt. I’m not a hot shot on piano, and so my limited ability forced a more simplistic approach which also made the lyrical content more spare and elemental too. 

AD: What were the sessions like? 

Zachary Cale: We all played live in the room. It’s how I always do it. If I have a band, I’m very much into the idea of recording while we’re learning the songs. It keeps it fresh, lots of happy accidents, etc. I’m not one of those songwriters who demos songs out and knows everything that the song is going to be. Lyrically, melodically, I know what it’s going to be, but that’s it. I’m not gonna write the bass line or tell someone what to play on drums, though, right? I’m going to give it to someone else to put their signature on it. And when you do that, a song can morph pretty quickly. You’re like, holy shit, this song does not sound like what I thought it was going to be. On this record, it’s not rock ‘n’ roll really, but it still moves and there’s a propulsion to it. When it was just me at the piano, it had more of a haunted solo vibe. With the group dynamics it started to swing. It was still elegiac and church-like but maybe more in a gospel way. 

AD: Did the musicians know the songs at all beforehand? 

Zachary Cale: Not really. I’m close with Uriah Theriault, the guitarist, who plays with me in this new group Vague Plot, and we worked out some things. But other than that, it was just the four of us in a room, playing music together for the first time. It was 2021 when the sessions started. We were masked up, it was pre-vaccination and so that separation from each other was strange, and pretty intense. We knew we had to bring it, and all the unrest and uncertainty of the year prior seemed to be moving through the music.

Next Year's Ghost by Zachary Cale

AD: One thing I’ve always appreciated is that your albums never feel entirely depressing. Next Year’s Ghost has a level of melancholy, but it never sinks total despair. Do you see that in your music at all?  

Zachary Cale: Yeah, totally. I’m pretty aware of it. I mean, there’s a lot of sad music that I love that doesn’t really make me sad. Townes Van Zandt is probably the perfect example. It’s pretty devastating, a lot of it — most of it. But it’s still funny and hopeful and positive. There’s a lot of charm to it and kind of a wink. Say your real truth, but at the same time, don’t wallow in it to the point where people are going to think there’s no light at all, like there’s nothing to grab onto at the end, It’s definitely a hard line to walk. It’s funny because I’m putting this subdued record out right now and it’s almost summer [laughs]. I was really trying to get this to come out in the winter, you know? But it’s already been kind of waylaid for a while. And I was just like, ah well.

AD: The closer, “Rough Devotion,” is pretty much a love song, right? 

Zachary Cale: It could be seen as one. It’s more just a song to a friend. It came out of being at someone’s birthday gathering in a park. It was just that we were all together, hanging out, having drinks, but it was still this weird time where even seeing people felt like a privilege. It was this small thing, but it felt special. That basic human connection or lack of during that time. That’s what a lot of the record is about. Holding onto communities and friendships and not taking them for granted. 

AD: Speaking of which, you also started a great new band out of the pandemic — Vague Plot! 

Zachary Cale: Yeah, that started happening when people were getting vaccinated. At first, it was to burn off energy from being in the depressive locked down state for two years. Vague Plot includes five people from different bands that share the same practice space — it started out literally as “jam night”. We’d get together every week for a year. We didn’t write anything, we’d just drink some beers, turn up the amps and play. People weren’t coming in with ideas. But we’d record everything on our phones and eventually certain riffs and structures would materialize. The album we made, Crying in 9 is all live, lots of bleed, half improv, half structured. We played our first show about a year ago now and we just got back from a short tour and Milwaukee Psych Fest. It’s nice to not be the front man. Vague Plot is a democratic thing, more open and loose. It’s been fun.
