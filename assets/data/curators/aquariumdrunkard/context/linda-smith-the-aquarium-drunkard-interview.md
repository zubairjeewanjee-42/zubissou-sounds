## Body

Linda Smith started recording cassettes at home in the late 1980s, painstakingly writing out simple parts for voice, guitar, bass and percussion, laying them down on a four-track, dubbing them onto cassettes and selling them by mail order to a handful of admirers—many of them also DIY musicians. Although she’d been in more conventional bands early on, these tapes became her main form of artistic expression, allowing her to create on her own terms and connect with a few like-minded fans.  

Almost no one heard Smith’s music while she was making it. None of her cassettes sold more than 100 copies. Yet though she made these songs in the pre-internet era, it was online that the recordings took on a second life. Word of her songs began to filter through an underground network of bloggers, radio hosts and deep music heads. In 2016, her song “I So Like Spring” opened the Sky Girl compilation of small press, home-made indie pop and then in 2018 on the soundtrack for the Amazon Prime series Forever.  

Now, following its 2021 compilation Till Another Time: 1988-1996, Captured Tracks has reissued Smith’s two exquisite mid-1990s cassette recordings, Nothing Else Matters and I So Liked Spring.  To mark the occasion, we talked about Smith’s life in DIY, the continuing evolution of self-recording technology that she’s seen, and how the search for distinct and different music has changed—and not changed—since the beginning of the digital age.  | j kelly 

Aquarium Drunkard: You started making and releasing your own records on cassette in the late 1980s, when the record industry was still very much in charge of what got made and released and heard on the radio. Tell me about how you got started and why you did it and what kind of reception you got.

Linda Smith: Well it was a lot different back then. There was no internet. I had been in some bands through the early 1980s around Baltimore and in New York. By about 1986, we were still playing. We were rehearsing and we played out and we did a little recording and released a single, but we didn’t play out enough. At that time, playing out—it’s still a big part of what musicians do—but back then, if you didn’t do a lot of shows, especially in New York, people weren’t going to know what was going on. You couldn’t put your music up on the internet, and you couldn’t use Bandcamp.

So what happened was, while I was in a band, I became aware that four-track cassette records were available to the general public. They weren’t just something that very few people could afford or buy. I found one in an audio video store in lower Manhattan decided to get it so that I could experiment with writing songs. Because I was writing songs for the band, and I didn’t have a way to record them or make demos. I thought this would be a good thing. 

I started playing around with the recorder. As I was working on it, I realized that it was a really interesting way of writing songs.  You could write a song without other people to make the different tracks and different instruments, as you would do in a band. So it allowed me to explore what I’d like to hear. I’d always had other people to create, say, a bass line or a drum pattern, and those were all great. But this was like, I could narrow things down a lot more this way. 

Eventually, later in 1986, the band decided to break up, and I continued recording my songs. I was also aware that, at that time, there were alternative magazines that reviewed cassette releases, such as Option and Soundchoice. There were others, but those are just two that I can name. And I thought, this is really great. I can put out a cassette, very limited quantity. I can run it off myself and make my own covers and send it to a magazine and they’ll review it. And they did and that kind of started the whole thing for me. I got more response that way than by being in a band. 

AD: That pretty much became your artistic output. You stopped playing live shows at some point, didn’t you?

Linda Smith: I certainly became a lot less interested in it. I moved back to Baltimore shortly after I started recording cassettes. The first cassette was recorded in New York, and then when I got back to Baltimore, I did another cassette. I went through the same process of mailing it out to magazines. But I had no real desire to form a band again at that time to play out, and through the 1990s, I played off and on in different configurations, but I never really got into the live thing as an activity.

AD: It sounds very labor intensive, doing all the parts yourself and dubbing the tapes yourself and making the art and the packaging and mailing things out. Did you find it a burden at all?

Linda Smith: Because I wasn’t doing hundreds of copies…

AD: How many copies would you be doing?

Linda Smith: Well, I would do them one at a time as people requested them. In the magazine, they would list your address and people would write you a letter, saying I’d like to buy this cassette for $3 or whatever it was. And they’d send me money. Sometimes they would put cash in the envelope or a check. And I would sit down and run off a cover, xerox, they were all xeroxed.

AD: I was wondering if you did the art separately for each cassette.

Linda Smith: Usually I would make a paste-up board and run them off that. The art and some other information. There’s only so much you can put on a cassette cover. So some of that would be handwritten. Sometimes there would be a typewriter. And it would be pasted on a paste-up board. That was how people did things then. Certainly we had no computer programs to do that. Then I’d take it to the copy shop and run off copies as I needed them. Each cassette maybe sold…well under a hundred copies. 

AD: And they were never in record stores?

Linda Smith: I did have one distributor at the time. It was K Records. Calvin Johnson sold some through his mail order distribution. But everything else was just me answering letters and packaging them up and mailing them out. 

AD: How did the fact that you were doing everything yourself affect the way you wrote songs or the way they sounded? 

Linda Smith: It was definitely very different for me to play a bass part rather than to have whoever I was in a band with play the bass part. I was never a great instrumentalist on any of these things, but for me the thing about recording was layering parts. Each part that I would come up with was very simple and having only four tracks and maybe eight if you balanced things down…it can get a little mushy. I would just try to keep it as simple as I could to get the song across. Whereas in a band, you have more options, and if you have a real drummer that’s a whole different thing than a drum machine or me playing a tambourine. 

AD: I was thinking that they were very spare, personal sounding songs with minimal arrangements. Is that the way you wanted it or was it just the result of the equipment that you had?

Linda Smith: Well, the limitations certainly did make it sound a certain way, but I enjoyed that. I liked that it was under my control. Even for one person to go into a recording studio and try to record all their own parts, it’s still not under your control. You have other people there. I think recording at home is much more like painting a picture. No one else was involved in it, so I could do what I wanted to do without feeling, oh, time’s passing and we’re spending money. I could do this whenever I wanted. 

AD: Did doing it all yourself allow you to sidestep the misogyny and male-centric-ness of the music business? 

Linda Smith: Well that, too. Before that, I always tried to play in bands with other women because it’s a very, very different dynamic. Whenever I talked to people who know how to play, you don’t have that same freedom to experiment. The people I worked with were friends, people I knew in my social world at the time. They played instruments, but they were not trying to be whatever the high end of music was at the time. We were very much of the do it yourself, post-punk/punk aesthetic. We all liked that music. So we had a common interest. 

AD: There were a lot of people home taping at that time. You mentioned Calvin Johnson and that K scene out in Washington, but there were others–Guided by Voices and Sentridoh come to mind. Were you aware that that was all happening? Did you feel like you were part of a movement in any way?

Linda Smith: Well, I was aware that others were doing that because if I looked at the alternative zines I could see all these people making cassettes. I still have some of those issues. I look at them and think, gee, there were all these people who later I came to know their music a little better and they were just starting out recording, too. There were a lot of people who would send me their cassettes in exchange for mine. So I became aware of it simply because the magazines talked about it. They wrote about. 

AD: But you weren’t in contact with any of these other bands?

Linda Smith: Not specifically. The people I heard from, they were making their own music, too. Whereas it may not have reached a broader audience down the road. They may have stopped. I don’t know what happened to what they were doing, but I was certainly aware of K and Beat Happening and these people who had the same aesthetic I did. But there were also home tapers who were doing things that were more experimental than what I was doing. There were people who were home taping noise and ambient music. I was aware of that, too. 

AD: These two albums that you’re reissuing now were from 1995 and 1996, which was about a decade after you started self-recording. Where were you in terms of your career and artistic development at this point?

Linda Smith: Well, I’ve always worked a full time job through all of this. I’m retired now. But through all of the 1990s and the 1980s I was working a full-time job of some sort. I never thought of any of these jobs as careers. But music was very much based around when…and people I worked with, too…it was based around when we could get together and do something if I was with a band. But once I started doing more home recording, I could do that at any time that I had free. I wasn’t relying on people to be in a practice space or whatever. So in the mid-1990s, I was still in the mode of: I’m going to do another album and maybe I can find a label to release it. And I did. The one album, Nothing Else Matters, was released on CD by Feel Good All Over Records. And I So Liked Spring was released on cassette by Shrimper Records. They dubbed the cassettes and did the covers and distributed it. Neither of those was ever on vinyl, but I had done three vinyl singles at that time. Singles were a popular way to release music for the small labels, not so much now, but then it was. 

AD: I understand you were literally working for the circus when you made Nothing Else Matters.

Linda Smith: I worked at Ringling Brothers. They were originally based in Washington DC and then they moved to Northern Virginia. And I worked in their advertising department. The circus operation was based in Florida, but the corporate headquarters was in DC. I worked in the advertising department doing stat camera work. Which was to take pictures of the ads after they were pasted up and before they were sent to the newspaper to print. It was a lot of circus ads.

AD: Was it just a job or do you love circuses?

Linda Smith: No, I never liked the circus. For me it was a job. Now, I think, why did I take a job that was so far away from Baltimore. It was a big commute every day and finally that’s why I left because I couldn’t do the commute anymore. But there were a lot of nice people who worked there, and it’s interesting to see what happened to the circus following that. They had to shut it down and now they’ve brought it back without the animals. You could see that was the direction it was going to go in. 

AD: I may be reading into this but that album Nothing Else Matters seems to have a playful, whimsical tone to it and almost a calliope-sounding keyboard. I was wondering if it was at all influenced by your contact with the circus, but it sounds like probably not. 

Linda Smith: Well, maybe. At the time I recorded that, I had access to an old Farfisa keyboard which has that real 1960s whimsical sound, very pop, 1960s pop organ sound, and I just thought it was great to have access to that because usually I used a little Casio. It was there and I put it to use, as well as a 12-string guitar I had, to kind of give it that 1960s feel. 

AD: Were you thinking specifically of any mod 1960s influences when you made that record?

Linda Smith: I grew up listening to AM radio in the 1960s. I was eight or nine years old when I first got an AM radio. So that is still, for me, what’s in my mind, as far as references. Later there were other things but that is my original reference point. 

AD: I really like the fuzz and clatter of “Little to be Won.” What can you tell me about that song?

Linda Smith: Well, that song, at least lyrically was very much about commuting back and forth and spending so much time on the road and then at a job and feeling like, “Where is this going? I feel like I need to make a change.” And it was kind of about what you would think to yourself if you had a message for your employer and how you felt about being able to communicate with your employer on any kind of real level, because it’s very difficult sometimes. And you’re just another employee. That’s mainly what the song was about and how so much time was being spent here, and how there wasn’t a lot for me to get out of it really. Except the paycheck. That’s a big part of the whole feel of the album. I’ve got to get out of this some way.

AD: Tell me about the Young Marble Giants cover. How did you come across them? Did people know about them?

Linda Smith: Well, I did. I bought their album when it came out. This was very shortly before I bought a guitar to start writing songs. I was going to record stores every weekend in the late 1970s and early 1980s. I’d buy all these records that were coming out because I’d suddenly become very interested in listening to pop music again. And there it was. All this great stuff from the UK, from New York, from the West Coast. All these bands putting out great records. My favorite year was 1978 because I purchased Patti Smith’s Easter, Bruce Springsteen’s Darkness on the Edge of Town, Devo and the Talking Heads. All of them had albums out that year, and they were all different. It reminded me of how different things were in the 1960s. How everybody had a different sound. I’d go to the record store and say, “this looks interesting.’ Or I’d read about something in a magazine and try to go get a copy. So I went to record store not far from where I lived every other weekend. And they sold a lot of imports. And I purchased the Young Marble Giants album. I think I’d read something about it in one of the English papers. Because I kept up with that stuff, too. Again, no internet, so I read the music zines and whatnot. And when I put that record on, I just couldn’t believe it. Just how different that record was from anything anybody else was doing at that time. That’s one of those records that you think, this is a direction here. This is a different way of approaching pop or rock music. It’s very individual. 

AD: Was there something about the song, “Salad Days” that particularly spoke to you?

Linda Smith: Well, yes. Later on in the 1990s, I think it was 1997 or 1998, there was a small label called Lance B records. I can’t remember the person who ran it, but he was looking around for indie artists to do covers of songs from that album. He contacted me, sent me a letter or something, asking if I was interested in contributing a cover. There were a variety of bands that were supposed to be on this compilation. It never happened. Nobody knows why. The label folded I guess. But there were some big indie names that were going to be on this thing. More well known than me. And I just thought, this is great. I get to pay tribute to this music that was important to me in the late 1970s and early 1980s before I started doing very much, myself. 

I picked “Salad Days.” Nobody else had chosen it. It had four lines.  And it would allow me to kind of create this little cinematic situation. When I do covers, I like to give them a different slant. I don’t like to do an exact copy. So to take parts of it and create something new out of it. Because it allowed for this open-ended thing, not real verses, no real choruses. I could do what I wanted with it real easily. 

AD: Is that the one with the laughing?

Linda Smith: Yes.

AD: What’s going on with that?

Linda Smith: I was kind of alluding to how “Salad Days” is about being a child. Because when you’re a kid you have all this freedom. Because that’s in the song. You didn’t have the responsibilities of adult life, at least that’s how I was interpreting it. So I wanted to give it this, maybe a little literal but a little atmosphere.

AD: A year later, you came out with I So Like Spring. The title song is probably the one that most people first heard from you and most associate with you. Were you surprised that so many people connected with that song?

Linda Smith: I have to say I was. It happened through a process that I wasn’t even aware of. One person found it somewhere and another person picked it up and eventually it ended up on this Sky Girl compilation and then it was chosen for a TV series. All that was kind surprising. But I felt that it must be resonating with people on some level to have it be picked up like that and carried along without me doing anything.

AD: Tell me about the connection with Charlotte Mew, the poet. 

Linda Smith: Well originally I did a version of “I So Liked Spring” on one of my cassette releases in 1992, and it wasn’t until 1996 that I decided I was going to do an album of all Charlotte Mew lyrics. There were two recordings of that song. There’s the tape recording and the one that’s on this vinyl. I thought, these are great poems. I’d like to explore them more. And I wasn’t so much interested in writing lyrics at that time, so I thought, let me see if I can set some of these poems to music. It made me think a little bit differently about songwriting.

AD: What can you tell me about Charlotte Mew?

Linda Smith: Well, she was an early 20th century poet. She didn’t have a huge output, although there is a book you can get of her prose pieces and her poems. I think that’s still in print. There was a recent biography put out of her. She was very much an independent person, some might say a bit quirky, but very much a strong personality. She’s very interesting in that she went in her own direction as far as being a poet. She didn’t really hang out in poetry circles too much. Other poets knew about her. Virginia Woolf knew about her. Thomas Hardy was a friend of hers. But she was not somebody who was a celebrity of that time. Like the Bloomsbury Group were celebrities. 

AD: Is the fact that they came from her why a couple of the song titles are in French?

Linda Smith: Yes. She used French, occasionally, in her titles and in some of the poems. I chose poems that were shorter, so that I could develop short songs out of them. Some of the poems are rather long with many stanzas. I didn’t use those at all because that would have been a whole different thing. 

AD: This album seems a little bit more subdued than Nothing Else Matters. Do you think that’s right? Was it intentional?

Linda Smith: Yes. I think it was. It was just me playing. I didn’t have any other friends playing on the record. And I just tried to keep it as simple as possible, not overload it with too many tracks. I had the eight track reel-to-reel at that time. I don’t think I balanced anything down. I was able to keep everything in its own track. Because I like to double vocals a lot.  I wanted to have those separate. I wanted everything to be separate so it was easier to keep it simple. I think the poems really required a more minimalist approach to them musically. 

AD: How do these albums sound to you now? Do you have any favorite bits on them or is there anything about them that bothers you?

Linda Smith: Well, it’s interesting. I hadn’t really listened to any of them from beginning to end for a long time until there was this renewed interest. And Captured Tracks used one or two of the songs on the compilation they put out in 2021. So I was just hearing them kind of like, “oh this song is from that album,” but I wasn’t listening to them as a whole album. 

I have to say that when they sent me the test pressings of these on vinyl, I just thought, oh my goodness. This is like a whole different experience. Hearing them like this. I don’t know whether it’s just my imagination but it gave it some kind of validity that it didn’t have as a download or even a cassette or a CD. I grew up with vinyl. There’s a sense that this is somebody’s record. It’s a serious object. Time was put into it. 

AD: What happened after these two albums? Did you continue to make music in the same way?

Linda Smith: What I did was, a couple of years later I worked with a friend on some music that he wrote. Paul Baroody. We put out a CD on BaDaBing! I recorded some of his songs in my apartment on my machine. I did the singing and played some things. That was under the name Yours Truly and the album was called Domesticated. That was a CD release. In the 1980s, no one was doing 12” vinyl albums. It was all CDs. 

And then I decided, a couple of years after that I decided to record a CD that I put out myself called Something New. And those are all recorded on the eight-track too. Then in 2001, I did the last proper album that I would do for a number of years, called Emily’s House and that was also a CD that I self-released.

I was kind of at the point where I thought, well, I could just do this myself, too. I could do a CD myself and, you know, I will have control over where it goes and who gets it. But it was a very different kind of feel, releasing a CD.

AD: It’s interesting that you’ve been doing this home taping, home releasing through a long time period where there was different technology and different distribution systems. What’s your view of how it is now? Obviously it’s easier technologically but it seems like it’s harder to break through.

Linda Smith: I think probably with the internet—I do spend a fair amount of time seeing what other people post. I look at bandcamp and I have my own bandcamp page. I see that there are so many people who are making great music on bandcamp. Some of them are working with a label. Some of them aren’t. Some of them are just doing digital releases. But in a way, I think it’s …I don’t want to say better, but everybody has more opportunity to be heard. Somebody can say, I released something and it’s on bandcamp and you can buy a tape if I made a tape or an album. But you can hear it. Somebody can just put headphones on and play it on their stereo and if they like it, they can buy it. Whereas, you know, when you went out and bought music before, you’d go to the record store and maybe ask someone to put it on for you so you could hear it a little bit. But now you can hear it. If you’re really a music person, you can explore all this much more easily. 

I do think it may be harder for people to gain some traction as independent artists, but then again, another thing that I notice is that there are a lot more indie labels, too. Who are willing to put people’s music out. I have no problem with it. If I was just starting out myself, I would be very happy to be able to put up a song, and say, “here’s my song” or put up an album. 

AD: Do you think that there’s anything that the young people who are self-recording now could learn from the people who were doing it in the 1980s and the 1990s?

Linda Smith: I think it’s so different. They’re not necessarily going to easily get a review in a magazine with a very small release like that. You could maybe get a blog review. Those are where to find music, too, sometimes. I would just say that they can’t do it the same way. There just weren’t all these things then. But they can post on social media as much as they want. They can put it out there and say, “I made this.” And if they’re in live performance, they can make copies and sell them at shows. Though people always did that. I’m thinking that there are more opportunities now, especially for women, to make music. 

AD: If you could only get across one thing to people about you and your career and the way you’ve done music, what would it be?

Linda Smith: For me it was about making music, writing songs. It wasn’t necessarily about becoming a big-selling recording artist. I don’t know how many people really had that idea anymore, unless you’re one of the ten big selling artists. I think there were more best-selling artists then than there are now.

AD: There was more of a middle class then.

Linda Smith: Yes. And now it’s like the way the economy is. There’s these people way, way up who the moment they put something out, it goes viral on Spotify and sells millions of copies. And you know who those people are. There’s such a big divide. People I know who do music here in Baltimore or elsewhere, they’re doing music because they love it. And of course they want people to hear it. So they’re going to find their way to have people hear it. When I was doing this way back, there were very few people who heard it. So I don’t know what to think about this, that this many years later, all these people know about it. It’s internet related. I can’t even compare it to how many people heard it then. It was very small.

AD: Anything else you want to say?

Linda Smith: Gosh, just that I hope to continue making more music, now that I record digitally, I’m going to be doing some more things. Some of them will be put on bandcamp as digital releases. Some of them may be released by somebody else. I’m taking the same attitude. I have this ability to reach more people with the music than I did back then. But I’m always hearing from somebody who just came across the old stuff, too.
