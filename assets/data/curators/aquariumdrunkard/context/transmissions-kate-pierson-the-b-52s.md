## Body

This week on Transmissions, Kate Pierson, vocalist and keyboardist of The B-52s. Writing about the legendary Athens band, AD founder Justin Gage says, “The B-52’s 1979 debut album ushered in a practically fully formed sound/band. No one else was doing this…whatever ‘this’ was.” Indeed, The B-52s created a one-of-a-kind sound, blending punk, funk, and art-pop, and while they broke into the mainstream with ubiquitous radio hits, they never sacrificed their avant-garde edge. This fall, the band embarked on a co-headlining tour with Devo—we recorded this talk just before they departed on the jaunt—and last week, Kate Pierson released a cover of Patti Smith’s “People Have The Power!” featuring the Uniting Voices Chicago teen choir. Benefiting the choir and the anti-gun violence organization Sandy Hook Promise, the recording reifies Pierson’s radical bonafides. 

Transmissions :: Kate Pierson

Pierson joins us for a loose chat about her life in art, solo projects, and the band’s longtime association with Devo. Along the way, we get into their status as queer icons, history with Yoko Ono, and reflected on the passing of Julee Cruise, the Twin Peaks vocalist who also served as a member of The B-52s. But we love disco, so definitely we love Chic and the songs are incredible and great dance songs. So I don’t really see any dividing line like, “oh, this kind of music has to stop and this other kind of music has to start now.” So we love Nile Rogers. Kate Pierson: Yes, ‘89.

Kate Pierson: I think what did feel rushed was Mesopotamia, because our manager at the time was like, you guys got to get another record out soon. So we hadn’t really finished it, and we were just doing an interview at  Sirius Radio with Andy Cohen, and he was mentioning the song “Cake,” which wasn’t really finished, and that I guess is why it’s so weird and interesting because it really wasn’t fully formed, but it’s very spontaneous and it’s almost like a jam. And there’s a part where Cindy and I just sound, we’re just stoned bakers there. And we’re talking about what kind of cake and has a lot of sexual innuendo. But that one was rushed. And sometimes Keith Strickland and I say, “wow, I wish we could go back and sort of finish,” but what’s the point of that? You can’t go back. So I think Cosmic was very, very fully formed. We had time, it was after Ricky died and we took time off. We didn’t even know if we were going to continue. And then when we did start writing Cosmic, it was as a healing thing and we conjured a lot of Ricky’s presence. And we also said, “Hey, we’re not doing this for any hitsville or anything. Plus the record company did get behind it after sort of Bouncing Off the Satellites sank like a stone.

Kate Pierson: They realized that Bouncing Off the Satellites had some great songs, and “Summer of Love” was running up the dance charts, but they didn’t follow up on it.

Kate Pierson: No, I guess at that time we were so obviously queer, but it was a long time before anyone really said to Keith Strickland or Fred, are you gay? And there were so many David Bowie and The Cure and just so many bands that just dressed, wear more makeup, and blurred the lines for sure. And so nobody was going to really question it, I think. But I think our queer identity was very front and forward too, and just our attitude and aesthetic. And yeah, I hate to use the word camp, but we certainly had a campy element. So that kind of thing, we definitely had an attitude. So that was really maybe underplayed. We didn’t really try to underplay it or overplay it. We just are who we are. And I think that’s one of the magic, our band, we just were who we were.

It was like that just, “oh, she would fit in here.” And I remember the first time she came to a rehearsal or we met her, but actual show, she had these really spacey, outrageous outfits. Even on good stuff when Cindy was gone, making that album, I knew it was kind of like, “oh, everyone’s going to say, where’s Cindy?” Of course.
