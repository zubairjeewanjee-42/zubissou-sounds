## Body

Richard Metzger has been a beacon for high strangeness for decades. First breaking onto the scene with his public access show Infinity Factory, broadcasting on Manhattan public access TV, Metzger then launched the wider-reaching Disinfo media project, a production company that produced the Metzger starring Disinformation/Disinfo Nation, which aired BBC Channel 4 and the Sci-Fi Channel in America. At the turn of the century, Metzger staged DisinfoCon, a presentation of mind-bending talks from the likes of Robert Anton Wilson, Kenneth Anger, Marilyn Manson, Grant Morrison, and more. In 2009, he founded Dangerous Minds, an essential archival blog fueled by his esoteric pop counter-cultural enthusiasms. 

Recently though, Metzger announced a dramatic return to his telecommunication roots with Magick Show, a forthcoming survey of 50 occultists who explain their practices and occult worldviews in their own words. A wide cast comprises the program, including longtime compatriots like Rushkoff and Morrison, along with the last interview with Kenneth Anger, and conversations with musicians like Luke Haines and Randy Orpheus, occult historian and TV host Mitch Horowitz, author and one time Blondie guitarist Gary Lachman, Bri Luna, the Hoodwitch, and many more. Metzger has been making the rounds ahead of the impending completion of the project’s Kickstarter, so we jumped at the chance to ask him a few questions about the state of online media, counterculture, and art, pondering how exactly “magick” and the occult plays into it all. | j woodbury

Aquarium Drunkard: Dangerous Minds was a beloved blog, but you got to the point where, as you put it in a message that preceded the announcement of your new project, “We simply couldn’t take it anymore. The Internet has just gotten too fucking dumb. And it’s hardly a case of the culture being dumbed down. On the contrary, it’s being dumbed UP.” 

This year, Aquarium Drunkard transitioned to a subscriber-only model, our best effort to move forward in this new digital age. Magick Show is your response to the ever increasing strangeness of the internet. How is the project different from most of what we encounter online?

Richard Metzger: The short answer to that is Magick Show isn’t frivolous. It’s got intellectual heft. With rare exceptions—increasingly behind a paywall like you guys or Talking Points Memo, something I don’t think would work with Dangerous Minds—the internet is like strolling through a landfill. Or swimming in an ocean of plastic bottles. Internet culture used to be utterly fascinating, but few people have the energy or inclination to create that sort of idiosyncratic content anymore and so the lowest common denominator stuff is all there is. TikTok is Wall-E’s planet of just…dumb shit. If I ever decide I want to watch videos of someone getting kicked in the balls, how to make cocktails from gasoline or use half and half as sunscreen, I know where to go. Admittedly I am addicted to YouTube, it’s mostly lectures that I tend to listen to, and podcasts about stereo gear. I’m really into that sort of stuff, like speaker reviews. My wife can’t understand the appeal.

AD: What sparked the idea for Magick Show? 

Richard Metzger: I was in LA for a wedding right before the pandemic hit and I had lunch with my old friend Chris Holmes. Chris is one of these people just bursting with energy—his website is called Fascinated By Everything and it’s the perfect name if you know him. He’s pioneering a new immersive art form that involves extra-trippy psychedelic visuals—either in a dome-type situation like The Sphere in Las Vegas or with a headset—with carefully chosen music and the voices of people like Timothy Leary or Alan Watts floating in and out of the mix. He’s collaborating with acts like Radiohead, it’s really amazing stuff. Hypnotic and relaxing.

Anyway, he suggested that we collaborate on a show about the occult. I went away for a few weeks and wrote a really long treatment—it was about 45 pages—and it simply wasn’t what Chris had in mind. He was thinking more about subjects like sacred architecture and the Golden Proportion in nature. Loftier topics. My concept was a bit darker than I think he was comfortable with. But I interviewed Chris, and also his wife Melinda Lee Holm, an author and prominent tarot card reader, for Magick Show. 

AD: The internet has certainly demonstrated substantial drawbacks in terms of our attention spans and our ability to comprehend and synthesize knowledge, but it’s also facilitated an interesting resurgence of mystical thinking and occult practice and theory. What thoughts does a new generation of people embracing Wiccan thinking or other more far out, new age ideas about manifestation and the like through apps like TikTok spark for you? 

Richard Metzger: Let me first say that I’m all for anything that encourages, shall we say, mental mutation. Of course, I’m supportive of people getting interested in magick no matter how they first encounter the subject or what causes them to gravitate towards it, but the 30-second #witchtok videos on how to practice divination are only going to get you so far. If it’s a gateway drug, that’s fine with me, but it’s not for me to partake in.

AD: You’ve been tapped into the occult milieu for most of your career. Your work with Disinfomation and with projects like Infinity Factory, helped to introduce a whole generation to subrosa interests, the kind of topics that would raise eyebrows in mixed company. 

But now, if you say “conspiracy culture” to the average person, they’ll likely think of QAnon. Mention the occult and you might get some responses back about the Illuminati or a dose of revived Satanic Panic rhetoric. What has it been like watching the culture of “high strangeness” intersect and impact the mainstream? 

Richard Metzger: Conspiracy theory culture was basically hijacked by the Russian military’s troll farm-type operations. It went from being a subset of the classic counterculture umbrella that would include under it the Beats, hippies, psychedelics, punk, rave culture, Mondo 2000-defined cyberculture, and so forth and then a Russian amplified reaction arose against that and has become the counter counterculture—the alt-right, the 8Chan, Kek, Pepe stuff. QAnon wasn’t started by the Russians—it was that oddball father and son duo, the Watkins—but they amplified it and this has been proven by looking at how fast the QAnon signal went from a few dozen social media mentions per day to thousands upon thousands of them. The graph doesn’t grow gradually, it goes up in a straight line in one day. There was nothing organic about how QAnon spread, it was boosted by the Russians. 

They’re geniuses. Think about the long game they’re playing. If the Russian military can get people to believe in utterly nonsensical political fairytales, they can probably deceive a sizable portion of the American public on any subject moving forward. It’s a kind of black magic. They’re harming their enemies from a distance, that’s the very definition of black magic. It’s black magic that takes advantage of technology and social media to fuck with people’s heads and turn them against their neighbors.

AD: At the same time, authors like Mitch Horowitz have done a great job showcasing the ways “magick” and other outre spiritual ideas have long impacted modern culture; people like Jeffrey Kripal have written great works that examine how magic is interwoven into our pop culture. Has magic always been mainstream to some degree? 

Richard Metzger: That doesn’t begin to happen until the 20th century. Before that, it was a very underground thing. But by the late ’60s, there’s a lot of interest in the occult. Carlos Castaneda, Morning of the Magicians, Colin Wilson’s books, Rosemary’s Baby, Aleister Crowley’s books were reissued by mass market book publishers. It was the Age of Aquarius, of course. In the 1970s the occult became a mass cultural phenomenon and you can see that even in the women’s magazines of the era. McCall’s, a magazine sold at supermarket checkout counters, did an entire occult-themed issue! 

During the ’80s the satanic panic pushed it back into the closet a bit, but at this point, there are two generations who were raised on Harry Potter. Today magick is absolutely mainstream and I can’t ever see it going back again. The barrier to entry isn’t what it used to be. You don’t need to locate dusty old occult books, just google what you want to know. Now I think it’s here to stay.

AD: How did you go about selecting the folks you interviewed? Some folks, like Douglas Rushkoff, Kenneth Anger, Grant Morrison, et al, have long been compatriots—were there some new faces and voices in the mix for you? 

Richard Metzger: Oh lots of people, yes. Like Mitch Horowitz and I had never met before. I’ve always loved his writing. You’d assume we would’ve crossed paths but no. I have greatly enjoyed getting to know Bri Luna, the Hoodwitch. She’s carved out an interesting life for herself and she carries herself in a sort of naturally regal manner. She’s an impressive person. But there were so many—I interviewed over 50 people—who it was a pleasure to meet and get to pick their considerable brains about magick and witchcraft. The vast majority I was meeting for the first time, at least in the flesh. Luke Haines and I have been email friends for eight years and I met him for the first time when I interviewed him in London.

AD: Sidenote, how often did Anger make you feel nervous to be around? 

Richard Metzger: Well Kenneth was under the impression that I was wealthy, so he was always perfectly charming to me. But Ken was nuts. Homicidally crazy. I know of three, arguably four people he tried to kill. The police were called after one incident that took place in the Gershwin Hotel in Hollywood in the early ’00s. He’s just lucky that he never did actually murder someone. It wasn’t for lack of trying.

AD: I’m a big fan of Rushkoff’s Team Human project and his assertion that somehow, “magick” is a part of what we need to resist the data-fication of our brains, and the behavioral dictates of algorithms social media companies employ to keep us addicted to their products? 

Richard Metzger: Doug’s been having a real change-of-life moment and he’s practicing what he preaches. It’s been interesting to see it happen. He recently told me that he’s simply no longer interested in writing the sorts of books he’s been writing for his entire career. He’s more interested in creating graphic novels and TV and film ideas. Fiction as opposed to non-fiction. He played keyboards in an incarnation of Psychic TV several years ago, and the group was reformed during the Tribeca Film Festival to celebrate the release of the new documentary about Gen. That event proved to be a turning point for him and you can see it in his recent columns, which are all about how practicing magick is a sane reaction to the current state of the world. Doug is actually one of Magick Show‘s executive producers. He’s been behind the project since the start, he’s one of the prime movers.

AD: The Magick Show project recently created a sigil with the intention that “Kamala Wins.” Were you at all nervous about taking a political stance with the project? 

Richard Metzger: Not really, I think that the audience would immediately understand where it’s coming from—anti-fascist, anti-Idiocracy, in a nutshell—but the “Kamala Wins” idea was not mine but that of promotional wizard/PR hype man Jeff “JahFurry” Newelt. At first the idea was that Charles Glaubitz would explain how to make a sigil. It could’ve been anything, the original one I came up with was “Send us all of your money”—and then Jeff came up with the Kamala idea, which was better. We consulted with Michael M. Hughes, who came up with the famous “Trump binding spell” and he thought we should keep it simple with “Kamala Wins.”

AD: You write, “We’re living through a time when the very worst people among us—shameless liars, religious extremists, literal fascists—are amassing power all over the world. These bad actors use elevated tactics to wage a tug-of-war over reality, and no one’s stopping them.” What kind of tactics do you envision as ways to fight back? 

Richard Metzger: Media jamming is what immediately comes to mind. Taking advantage of AI text to video generation for reality hacking is another, but I’ve not thought it out to any great extent. The first step is for people to come together. Tim Leary used to say “Find the others” and I’ve been using that as a rallying cry myself for the past 25 years. This is my contribution to seeing that happen. The group mind needs to form a conventicle first. I’m trying to do my part in starting that conversation. I don’t have delusions of grandeur, I’m just one node in the system. Changing reality is a group sport.

AD I’m curious to learn more about the music in the series, and what kind of “mood” you were interested in cultivating with it?  

Richard Metzger: I’m glad you asked! The music is all from the London-based record label Library of the Occult. It’s run by an incredibly talented young guy named Tom McDowell, who is both a brilliant musician and a good businessman. What the label puts out, in effect, is library music for occult films and TV shows that don’t exist. Tom is allowing us to use music from the entire LOTO catalog and he’s done some custom music for Magick Show as well. It’s all very atmospheric and dramatic. Libary of the Occult recently staged its first live show in New York City and he’s got another event coming up soon in London. Tom and his label would make a great subject for Aquarium Drunkard, actually. He’s really doing something unique.

AD: Many years ago, while doing an episode of Amoeba’s What’s in My Bag, you extolled the virtues of SACD audio. Do you still listen to discs? Picked up anything good lately?

Richard Metzger: I remember that video. I was also taking about the Judy Garland tapes! But you know, I’ve done a real about face where it comes to music formats. For many years—decades—I’ve hunted for the very best digital formats of my favorite albums, so a own a lot of SACDs, which now seems to be a dead format everywhere save for Japan. And I was very enthusiastic about music that had been remixed for 5.1 surround as well, DVD-A discs and Blu-Ray audio discs, but a few years ago my good friend Alex Rosson, who is a world-renowned headphone designer, sent me a gorgeous limited edition turntable that he produced. I had a turntable, but it was in the garage and needed servicing that it was unlikely to get, but when this deck arrived I started buying records again and it began to occur to me that they sounded better, and in some cases—like the David Bowie catalog, which is a particularly depressing example—a lot better than what was on offer in terms of “remastered” digital and new vinyl releases. 

A lot of new vinyl—most of it—is mastered from digital sources. It sounds like shit to me, so this started me down the expensive rabbit hole of buying all of my favorite albums for what was now the fifth or sixth time…I wrote a really long blog post about it on Dangerous Minds titled “Extreme Record Collecting: Confessions of an analog vinyl snob.” I think your readers might get a kick out of that one.
