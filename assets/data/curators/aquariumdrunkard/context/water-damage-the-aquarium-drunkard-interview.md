## Body

Maximal repetition with minimal deviation: This is the guiding principle of Water Damage, the amorphous Austin, Texas-based collective specializing in 20-minute slabs of hulking, relentless drone-rock for people who think two notes is one too many. Formed in 2018, the band’s membership fluctuates depending on who’s available, ranging from a half-dozen principals to nearly 20 folks in the extended family. With two bassists and two drummers on board at all times, Water Damage makes an awe-inspiring racket that sounds like Earth taking a crack at making dance music, or a bulldozer covering Faust.

“I think it started with (bassist Nate Cross) and I talking about starting a band that sounded like Emeralds,” says guitarist and synth player George Dishner. “At first we would try to structure it a little bit; we’d have these 15-minute songs with like three movements. And then we kind of just decided, ‘Well, fuck the movements.’”

Inspired by the heavy drone band France, Water Damage dove headlong into long-form composition, first on its 2022 album Repeater – three tracks stretching beyond 41 minutes – and then on last year’s aptly named 2 Songs EP, which clocked in just over 40 minutes long. The latter expanded the band’s sounds slightly – pulsing synth here, twinkling percussion there – but both followed a pretty basic blueprint: Pick a chord and a rhythm, and play till the tape runs out.

“I think after the first record I was like, ‘What are we going to do? How many of these do we have in us?’” said Travis Austin, who plays bowed guitar. “I’ve been pretty astonished by how many different things we’ve gotten out of just one plan, and it continues to yield new results.”

On April 12, Water Damage released its third album, In E, via Austin’s 12XU Records. All the songs are in the key of E, its longest track is 21:19 and its shortest track is a 19-minute, 45-second cover of “Ladybird” by the noise band Shit and Shine. It sounds sharper and clearer than the band’s first two records, which helps bring Water Damage’s deep, satisfying grooves up and out of the roar.

Recently, we caught up with Dishner, Cross, Austin and drummer Greg Piwonka – whose brother Jeff Piwonka plays bass – to discuss Water Damage’s roots, its music and what it all means. Here is that conversation, edited for space and clarity. | b salmon

Aquarium Drunkard: So how do you go from talking about starting a band that sounds like Emeralds to 20-minute one-note steamroller drones?

Nate Cross: Honestly, I feel like everything changed when we went in to do that first record. It was still kind of amorphous at that point. We just had some ideas, and it was a little bit looser, but it wasn’t like, “We’re going to play one note for this long.” But after we recorded the first side of the first record, that’s when we were like, “Oh yeah, this is what we need to start doing.”

George Dishner: So we got together and we would jam on like a very, very, very, very basic riff, and then at the end of it, we were just like, “Fuck, that was fun.” With this, man, what I love about it is it’s all id. There’s no lyric to think about and you can just kind of get lost in it for a while and then you come out of it. Which I think is why we just thought it was fun, but we didn’t know if anyone would actually be interested in it, y’know?

Nate Cross: I remember when we were first recording and (Austin percussionist Thor Harris) wasn’t really a member of the band yet, but we invited him to the session because (drummer Mike Kanin) couldn’t make it. So we’re in the middle of recording and Thor just walks in, the drums are set up and he just played a fill and started to play with us. And it was really beautiful. That was the thing for me.

AD: How did the band grow from a core group to 15 people or more?

Travis Austin: I think it was just by virtue of needing people to play shows, and we just started absorbing people.

Greg Piwonka: There were six or seven of us but somebody was always not able to make a show, so we were like, “We should just make it so we play with a rotating group of people so we can say yes to pretty much anything.” We just just book shows and not worry about it. We’ll put something together. Also, it’s really easy for anyone to just drop in and even if they’re not familiar at all with Water Damage, it’ll work.

AD: With that said, here’s a question: Some people will listen to what you all do and say, “That’s easy.” Are they right? Or is it harder than it looks?

Nate Cross: I think it’s both.

George Dishner: It’s very easy, but you can also get mixed up with wherever you are in the riff. I know Greg gets, y’know, psyched out by the repetitiveness of it. So it’s easy on the surface. It’s probably super easy for anyone who really knows what they’re doing.

Travis Austin: I mean, I have to remind myself of ergonomics, you know? I have to remember to keep my muscles loose or I’ll catch myself five minutes in going, “Oh shit, I’m going to tire out before we’re done.”

Nate Cross: The thing is, I want it to be both simple and easy but it’s also hard. I think we all kind of want water damage to be really lowbrow, kind of boneheaded one-note riffs, but it’s also this highbrow, conceptual minimalist thing. All of that can live together and be great.

AD: Even jamming on one note and one riff, you can hear little evolutions as you go along. Is that something you all plan and write and insert into the songs or do they just happen?

Travis Austin: It comes out organically. I think it’s just impossible for a person to play the same thing over and over again for that long. It’s always going to change a little bit. But there’s no explicit rule that you have to do this or you can’t do that.

Nate Cross: A couple of times, we’ve been practicing or playing a show and someone will change something and it’ll create this whole new part, and then new songs come out of that change.

AD: It’s like you all are mining – like you’re boring a hole into the earth and every once in a while you bust through into an uncharted cave and find a new path to follow.

Travis Austin: One of the unintentional things that happens with this band – and it’s something I’ve noticed while mixing the records – is a voice that comes out of the combination of tones that everyone is playing. I’ll hear it and isolate the individual tracks and it’s not to be found anywhere in the individual parts, and that’s really cool. It’s like the band kind of turns into an instrument for these ghostly voices.

George Dishner: The whole band is built around microtones as far as the drones. Being just slightly out of tunes. It’s like waveform detritus that comes out that I think is nice.

AD: In E, to me, feels like the percussion is more prominent than the first two records, which brings your groovy tendencies to the surface. Was that intentional?

Travis Austin: I think a large part of that is that we got a better recording where the drums weren’t buried behind a lot of bass bleed. So the kick is a bit more prominent.

Nate Cross: But I think it’s also the first one that has more syncopation between the drummers and stuff like that. I don’t know how consciously forward it was, but I think our thought was to have the four sides of the two LPs each in the same key, but with a different feel.

AD: Speaking of intent, people like to use words such as “transcendental” and “hypnotic” when talking or writing about Water Damage. Is it part of your intent with this music to have those kinds of effects on listeners?

Travis Austin: I wouldn’t use the word intent as much as it is, like, intent receding into the music and just letting go. I mean, maybe that’s splitting hairs a bit.

AD: That’s okay, I’m interested in that split hair. Is it about letting go for you all?

George Dishner: For me it is, yeah. I just like being in a band where I get lost and don’t have to worry about lyrics or anything for 20 minutes or whatever.

Greg Piwonka: When we first started playing, it was just the six of us during the pandemic and we had a little bubble and we’d all meet in our tiny practice space and just go. Nobody knew, like, what was going on in the world. It was insane. And we would just meet up and play these songs and forget about everything. That felt good. And so we just kept doing that and that’s what the shows turned into. It organically happened but then it became intentional, you know? We didn’t even know if we were ever going to play shows again, but we did it because we wanted to and it felt good.

George Dishner: I will say that when we sent it to (12XU’s Gerard Cosloy) and he was like, “Yes, I’ll absolutely put this out” … if he hadn’t said that, I don’t know if we would’ve had the gumption to, like, shop it around and try to make something out of it. We’re all in our forties or older. Everyone’s got wives and kids. None of us are spring chickens here. No one’s trying to make a living by playing in Water Damage.

Nate Cross: But that’s kind of the beauty of it!

George Dishner: It is, yeah. No one really has aspirations for this. It’s all a bonus.
