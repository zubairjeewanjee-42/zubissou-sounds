## Body

With his new EP Roseway, Jeffrey Silverstein continues his journeyman drift, wedding country ballads to funk boogies and laser-guided motorik drive to languid, swamp-ready guitars. With his Telecaster Deluxe strapped on, Silverstein cut the recordings with collaborators Dana Buoy on drums, Connor Gallaher on pedal steel, and Alex Chapman on bass. The music picks up where his last full-length, Western Sky Music, left off, but shares even more similarities with his previous EP, 2021’s Tori Gates. In fact, both albums are packaged together on vinyl, which makes for almost a whole new album. We caught up with Silverstein to discuss how it came together. Our conversation, edited for clarity and cohesion, is presented here. | j woodbury 

Roseway by Jeffrey Silverstein

Aquarium Drunkard: Roseway is its own EP, but for the physical release, it’s packaged with your previous EP, 2021’s Torii Gates. I love the way releasing them together makes it feel—it’s almost like a makeshift LP. 

Jeffrey Silverstein: The idea for that came as we were thinking about releasing this new one. I naturally realized “Hey, both of these are six songs each, both have exactly three songs with lyrics, three instrumentals, even the runtimes are similar.” [My debut] You Become The Mountain was tape-only for a long time, and people would ask when I was going to put it out on vinyl—Torii Gates was similar, people would ask about that one. The more I thought about it, the more I thought they made for a nice pair.

AD: In Josh Terry’s bio, he calls your sound “New Age for the working man.” I really like that evocation, because I see your music functioning for people the way New Age might have for listeners in the past—as a way to help chill, relax, meditate even, if that’s how you want to use it. But the sounds aren’t all placid—you draw from country funk, boogie, you indulge swampy feels, a la JJ Cale. I think it’s interesting that in this moment, people are creating expansive, meditative music, but no one seems all that concerned with strict definitions of these things.

Jeffrey Silverstein: With You Become The Mountain, it was after the fact that I got placed in the ambient/meditation music camp. As per usual, it’s human nature to ascribe a label to things. I think that was an interesting moment. Ambient was kind of having its time in the sun during the deep pandemic. It’s kind of funny—I don’t know if ironic is the right word—that now I’ve kind of gotten put in this “cosmic country” camp, because I kind of just fell into that stuff, too. I just did some more shows with Bobby Lee. His music’s different enough from mine, but the way he plays, melodically he speaks to me. Whatever you want to call it, “far out,” “psychedelic,” “cosmic music”—all of that is made more accessible through melody. 

I think the important distinction for me is I don’t want to ever be seen as doing country cosplay or anything like that. You’re not going to find me doing the full getup with the boots and the hat. That’s not what I come from. [But] it’s music that I love and have grown to understand and appreciate, so calling it “New Age or cosmic country for the everyman” just made sense to me, especially for the fact that music is a second full-time occupation for me in addition to teaching.

AD: How do you balance those callings? A lot of your interviews with The Creative Independent point toward an interest in understanding how people structure their creative lives. As you’ve made more and more music, how has that balance changed? 

Jeffrey Silverstein: Sometimes I try to make sense of it for myself and I can wind up struggling to do so. What I’ve come to accept—realize? acknowledge? I don’t know!—is that the schedule of being an educator puts you in these very natural rhythms. Both in your kind of body clock—things as simple as when you get up—as well as moments in the year where you have more breathing room. I’ve learned to embrace that. I forget who first put it in my mind, but sometimes your creativity or writing can just be listening to music, going for a walk, taking things in. That’s still part of being creative. I’ve allowed myself to have seasons for that, and then I have seasons for what you might more traditionally call “the work.” I feel like I’ve tried to honor that and lean into that. 

AD: Sinking into those rhythms is so helpful, but it can be a challenge too.

Jeffrey Silverstein: We’re kind of talking about rhythm, literally. I’m walking on my school’s track right now. I remember having a very fascinating conversation with Marisa Anderson, who earlier in her life walked across the country numerous times. I kind of asked her if sees a relation between the cadence of her walking, the speed and rhythm, and the way she plays the guitar. And ultimately, the answer was yes. I’m noticing my own cadence and rhythm right now as I’m talking to you. 

AD: Walking is such a good example. Because you instinctively do it at a natural pace. You don’t question it and you don’t police your speed. With creative work, it can be so easy to monitor your output and measure it against other people’s and feel lacking or like you aren’t doing enough. When you’re walking, you’re not trying to walk or think about walking, you’re just walking. 

Jeffrey Silverstein: It’s like we can be so hyper-critical about so many things, and yet when we’re doing something [naturally], especially if you’re just by yourself, you’re just like, “Hey, I’m just cruising.”

AD: Speaking of cruising, “Gassed Up” is about as funky as you’ve ever gotten. That drum stuff at the end, that’s Dana Buoy right? 

Jeffrey Silverstein: Yeah, that’s Dana. I mean, that song took us up a notch. We cranked the funk dial, which felt really, really good. And it’s another great example of a song that almost never happened, because I just had those two chords pedaling back and forth for the verses but I had no idea what else to do. It’s easy to get locked into your “guitar moves,” so I was thinking about finding a different picking pattern. Once I locked in with Dan and Alex [Chapman, bass] on that beat, I was just like, “Oh, here we go off to the races.” 

AD: I get the sense that’s a song that could really only come about after playing as much as you all have, being on the road, getting fluid together. Does it feel that way? 

Jeffrey Silverstein: I couldn’t agree more. That’s not a song that would’ve just “happened” on some of those earlier releases for me. For me, it’s really important to know the people in your band, not just play with them. Have you noticed this? When you’re younger, band practice is like 80% hanging out, 20% working on music. Then you get older and everyone’s got stuff going on and it kind of flips 100%. The new EP is called Roseway. We all live in Roseway, we’re all neighbors, we can get together easily. We see each other in a neighborhood in passing. I think all of that has just made an impact on being a core unit.
