## Body

“Making words up while we make love”. That’s an evocative lyric that arrives within the first minute or so of The Mirror, Buck Meek’s fourth solo LP, activating the essence of this work. In those seven words, we think of how they represent a desire for creation, communication, curiosity, and forging connection, be it with oneself or another person. Those aspects are integral ingredients at The Mirror’s earnest and energetic core. An alive quality to Meek’s musicianship has always anchored both his solo output and contributions within Big Thief; that inherent sensibility, which feels elevated in his constantly growing artistic confidence, is abundant in his latest work. 

Exploring themes of love and reflecting on the ways relationships not only invite us to expose ourselves to others but, crucially, to ourselves has led Meek to pen some of his most unfiltered and fulfilled lyricism as he sings, “Now, I’ll try to write a song that is not for others.” A decade has passed since many of us were introduced to Meek alongside his Big Thief bandmates with their debut LP, Masterpiece. Now feels like the time for him to share the ways that time has changed him. The songs are elevated by earnest emotion and beautiful details that instantly make you feel connected to his world. While The Mirror is released under his own name, Meek opened the door to a terrific cast of collaborators, including Big Thief’s Adrianne Lenker and James Krivchenia, who produced the album, as well as Alex Somers, Mary Lattimore, Jonathan Wilson and many more, adding to the warmth of these uplifting songs. 

We spoke with Buck Meek about recording The Mirror in his LA-based studio Ringo Bingo, his relationship with collaboration and solitude in songwriting, giving himself permission to be uninhibited in frankness, a childhood obsession with Garth Brooks’ Ropin’ The Wind and more. | z hedderman

Buck Meek: I’m really excited to get on stage with the songs. It’s always a little abstract, at least it’s always very abstract for me, releasing an album online. It doesn’t really feel real until I’m on stage with an audience. In saying that, I’m getting excited. I’m starting to rehearse and get prepared for the tour. That’s really exciting. 

AD: Earlier today, I listened back to your great interview with Jason Woodbury on the AD podcast around the release of Haunted Mountain. Something you spoke about was the importance of conversation, and having four-hour-long conversations during recording sessions with Big Thief. Along with that, the importance of taking away ego and engaging in self-criticism. I’m curious to hear how that translates to your solo material, which I know is also an incredibly collaborative process. 

Buck Meek: So much of this album is about communication, in general. The songs themselves are really pulling from relationships in my life, both romantic and with friends and family, loved ones like my grandmother, my grandfather. There are songs about all different forms of communication. To a huge degree, our lives are defined by communication. And also, communication with ourselves, too. Pulling back layers of myself and trying to learn to communicate with my own demons. For instance, in the song “Demon”, teaching it to read and write and bridging all the gaps. 

AD: Listening to your lyrics, love is an anchoring theme across the songs. In “Heart in the Mirror”, you sing, “I’ll try to write a song that’s not for others”. Later in the song, you mention looking in the mirror to see your fears. How did this experience differ from working on previous albums, and how has this album gotten you to a place where you’re thinking about what’s next?

Buck Meek: Well, I think with this album, with every album, I allow myself more and more to say what I want to say and to be really abrupt and honest and say the thing that I’m afraid to say. I was able to do that on this album. Whenever you sit down to write a song, inevitably, so much self-doubt comes up. There are moments of self-consciousness, self-awareness or self-doubt, and there are so many forces at play from within and without, the forces of society and other people’s opinions. Of course, so much of that is projected, but some of it is not. It’s this wild tapestry of projections. Trying to really go into the core of what I want to say is like a game you have to play with yourself in the songwriting process.

At the same time, it’s valuable to embrace all those voices too, because to a huge degree, that’s what we’re made of. Our ideas are so influenced by everything that surrounds us, and we’re an amalgamation of everyone we’ve ever met and every conversation we’ve ever had, every song we’ve ever listened to. To some degree, it’s valuable to embrace that and learn to use that as a source of energy, but at the same time, to not be completely beholden to it and know when to be yourself. To answer your question, I think with this one, I felt like whenever that would come up, I would lean into it. I would say the thing I was afraid to say and say it louder or emphasize it. Even in a meta way, talk about the very fear that I was experiencing in that song, “I just want to write a song that’s for myself and not for others.” I wrote the line about kissing the fruit in the grocery store and kissing the carpet; it felt vulnerable to write that because it was just a funny thought I had of, ‘You couldn’t put that in a song,’ but then I just went ahead and did it and it was really exciting. It felt extremely freeing to break my own rules. 

AD: When we challenge ourselves or embrace fear, it can make us feel more alive. From the get-go on The Mirror with opener, “Gasoline”, there’s an alive quality to your arrangements and musicianship. I’d love to hear about the experience of taking these songs from seed to, I don’t want to say final form because a song continues to grow when it’s brought to live settings, but it’s recorded form, shall we say. Based on the energy coursing through the album, it feels like it was a lot of fun to play. 

Buck Meek: Thank you very much. As far as the songs themselves, I often start from a pretty abstract place. Usually, I’ll start on my guitar and make abstract word sounds, gibberish. From there, I’ll intuitively create shapes and word sounds, and then observe the narrative that wants to come from that. I let the words form into the sounds, form into words, and then once a narrative forms naturally, I’ll take a step back and think about it more logically to unpack that because, usually, I feel like your subconscious wants to say something and if you can just observe that, then at some point you can change your approach to unpacking that more consciously. 

Once the song is formed, building that with my band, I put a lot of intention into choosing players that I really trust and admire. When we actually sit down to record, I don’t give them any direction at all. Other than the song itself, which I really trust because I put so much intention into the song. I see that as a map, essentially. A treasure map or something! There’s so much structure, like architecture in a song. Melodically, rhythmically and lyrically, there’s so much information there. I’ve chosen players I trust to use the song as a guide and add dimension. Also, I’ve chosen players that I feel my songs will add dimension to their playing. In that way, it’s a reciprocal thing. 

When we sat down to record this, I barely gave any direction at all. I just played the songs and let them run free. And so, all their parts were coming naturally. We recorded very quickly, over the course of a week, all living together in a room. We recorded at my little studio called Ringo Bingo here in LA, and I stood on the front porch outside and isolated the vocal mic from the drums, which were inside the living room through the window. We just played all the songs live for a week, basically. It was really great. 

The Mirror by Buck Meek

AD: I saw a picture of you on social media, recording vocals for this album on the porch, and I couldn’t get my head around it because they sound so crystal clear. You’d never think they were done outside, so it was cool to get a peek beyond the curtain!

Buck Meek: We’re up in the mountains, so luckily, there wasn’t much noise pollution. The porch has two walls, so the wind can be a little bit blocked, which was perfect. It was nice for me because being in a vocal booth in a studio all day can be pretty tiresome. It was really nice to be outside in the sunlight and the wind. That’s also where I wrote most of the songs, so it felt really natural to sing the songs where they were written. 

AD: Was that your first time recording vocals outside on the porch? 

Buck Meek: Yeah, pretty much. We’ve made a couple of records at our place with a similar setup. It’s been really useful. 

AD: You were the guinea pig for it! You spoke of writing with intention, but also the importance of following instinct, say with not giving your band specific instructions as well. I read that, for these sessions, you had set up an atmosphere for “simultaneous experiments” and that the “musicians respond to each other, and their instruments triggered modular synthesizers.” Can you tell me about that? 

Buck Meek: Right on! Yeah, that was a big part of this record. Our producer, James Krivchenia, who’s also the drummer in Big Thief, has a really wild side, like a parallel world and life in electronic music. He has a bunch of electronic albums that he’s released, really experimental ambient and field recordings. He produced The Mirror, and he set up this whole parallel world in the control room with modular synthesizers and loads of electronic instruments. Also, our sound engineer, Adrian Olsen, is a really wild modular synthesizer player, also. And so, he had his whole modular synth set up. 

They had two separate channels of modular synths and a bunch of electronic instruments that they were pulling from; they were able to pull any channel from the live band into their rig. They could pull a snare drum as a trigger into their modular synth rig or, the vocal or whatever instrument. For every song, there was a recipe of sources that were triggering their electronic world and creating this parallel signal that was being fed back into the mix. Then, also in mixing, they experimented quite a bit with a similar process; there was a lot of electronic parallel processing happening. 

We also brought in a couple of ambient artists, Alex Somers and Mary Lattimore to contribute ambience. So, there was the rock band, you know, the rhythmic acoustic rock band with amps and guitars, and then simultaneously, this kind of nonlinear, nonrhythmic, ambient landscape happening, and the two worlds were working together. 

AD: Yeah, I was very jazzed to see Mary Lattimore’s name feature on the list of guests on this album. Had you worked with her before? 

Buck Meek: I never worked with her before, but my friend Alex Somers has worked with her a lot, and they came to record on the same day. It was really nice that they were able to collaborate in the studio together. Mary was so incredible. She had her harp in the studio, in this log cabin with this big, tall ceiling, and her harp sounded so amazing. We have this dog, a little husky named Ringo, who was just completely hypnotized by the harp. She would just lie on her side, staring at the harp for the whole day. She couldn’t move; it was like she was paralyzed from it. It was really cute! 

AD: We’ve talked about your different collaborators, going back to your interview with Jason Woodbury that I mentioned earlier, you told him about working with Jolie Holland and how she would send you songwriting prompts. I can’t help but think of how vibrant and varied your career has been. Whether it’s going between Big Thief, your solo work and other amazing projects that you’ve been part of. Collaboration feels incredibly integral to how you make music. What’s your relationship like to the solitude, or the isolated elements, such as writing, required as an artist? Do you enjoy it, or is having someone to bounce off more invigorating?

Buck Meek: I think I really value both. I like oscillating between the two. I definitely really need solitude in the process. I think it’s important to have that time where there’s no accountability whatsoever beyond yourself. Whether it’s writing a song or practising guitar, being able to spend an entire day just working on one new little concept on the guitar or spend an entire day laboring over one line of a song in isolation and probably sounding insane, repeating it over and over again, just talking to yourself without the witness of another person. That is really important. At the same time, though, it’s also important to come out of that and to be in the witness of others and to have a bunch of stimuli to respond to outside of your own mind. Because, at the end of the day, I feel like music is really listening; the moments where I feel like I’m playing music or when I’m actually listening to everything else around me and not focused on myself. Ideally, if everyone’s doing that at the same time, it’s something you arrive at, this transcendent space that, to me, is the essence of music. The more you prepare for that, the better too. So yeah, it’s a balance, I guess. 

AD: Listening to everything around you, obviously, you’re referring to the music you’re making at that moment. However, I was curious to hear about your relationship to listening to other artists or albums that you love while in the headspace of recording. I ask because there were two moments, “Soul Feeling” and “Deja Vu”, which reminded me of Neil Young, and then “God Knows Why” has a little “Positively Fourth Street” saltiness to it. Do you find comfort and inspiration from your favorite artists while recording, or do you have to divorce yourself from other people’s works? 

Buck Meek: That’s a good question. I definitely do listen to music whenever I’m writing. I don’t feel like I need to divorce myself from it necessarily. I think what I’m looking for in listening to music while I’m writing is not necessarily to mimic or to copy whatever they’re doing. It’s more to find permission and empowerment, I think. It’s to find permission to be myself by hearing other artists really be themselves and just be bold in themselves and their own discrepancies, and hearing a songwriter say something that really only they could say and to say it bravely. Even if it’s flawed or crooked, it still feels really empowering and gives me a sense of permission to then go and do my own thing. I do listen to music for that reason. Whether it’s lyrical or instrumental, too. 

AD: Was there anything in particular that you were listening to while making The Mirror?

Buck Meek: Yeah, an album that I was listening to a lot was Life Without Buildings’ Any Other City, which Sue [Tompkins], the vocalist, seemingly was making up words throughout the whole recording. It’s a whole album of her poetry, but it’s half word-sounds and nonsense weaving in and out of narrative, and then these incredible narratives will arise for a second and disappear. That was really empowering to me, again, in the realm of making up words. They’re playing shows again, which is really exciting. 

AD: Yeah, I was just going to ask whether you’ll try catch them live? 

Buck Meek: Definitely, I would love to. Hopefully, we can play a show with them someday because they’re one of my favorites. Actually, another person who is always a touchstone for me whenever I’m writing songs is Michael Hurley. He’s someone I really look up to because he’s incredibly brave with the way he writes. He’s not afraid to say something really specific and obscure and oblique and simple. Then, the next verse will be something completely different and oblique to that first one. There’s a lot of space in his songs that he leaves for the listener to inhabit. And somehow, that ends up feeling like the song is different for every single person who hears it. That’s really encouraging to me. 

AD: In sharing this album with those around you, have you had an experience of people coming to you with different interpretations of songs? 

Buck Meek: That’s a good question. Not so much on this record because not many people have heard it yet. With previous records, I’ve had people tell me some really personal stories. The song “Dream Daughter” on my record Two Saviors has been interpreted in a couple of different ways. I have had some people tell me some really personal stories about their kids regarding that song. I don’t necessarily want to divulge, but they’ve shared really, really beautiful personal stories that I was so surprised by and that they were interpreting in different ways. That was very special.

AD: Within The Mirror, we’ve talked about love as a unifying force, but in your lyrics, fear is just as present. This is your fourth solo album. I wondered how you would describe the ways that these songs encapsulate the period they were created in.

Buck Meek: Yeah, regarding fear, I did talk about it a lot on this album, or unpacked it. The cliché of a relationship being like a mirror, reflecting yourself to you, is obviously a theme. I was thinking about that a lot, about how being in love or any relationship, whether it’s a friendship, with family or a romantic partnership, is this reflection that gives you the opportunity or the accountability to grow as a person. They make you face your own fears, your own darkness, but also your own innocence and the whole spectrum. Love is kind of like this reward system that pulls you into that process. 

Often, what’s beneath the fear is often something very innocent and vulnerable. Nonetheless, it’s something pure that was a little misguided along the way. Admitting the fear and being really honest about it, or being forced to admit it to yourself through the reflection of a relationship, is the first step. That’s something I was thinking about a lot during this record. Hopefully, I’m able to, as I get older, just admit that to myself. As a person, in general, I want to be more honest about my own fears and hang-ups and be willing to face them.

The Mirror by Buck Meek

AD: Totally, and what’s great is that there’s no deadline for when we should reach that state of being. You don’t have to unlock by a certain age. On the song “Ring of Fire”, which is not a Johnny Cash cover on The Mirror, you have a line about rock and roll being in your blood. With that in mind, what was the first album you bought for yourself that unlocked a love of music and set you off on a path of independently discovering your own taste?

Buck Meek: Oh yeah! Probably the first album that I bought, of which I remember buying, or my parents bought for me, that really started a love for music was Garth Brooks’ Ropin’ the Wind. There’s a song called “Rodeo” on there, which was my favorite song as a kid. I think I was seven-years-old, maybe. I was obsessed with Garth Brooks and George Strait, and all those artists. Yeah, Ropin’ the Wind, that was the one. I would just sit and hold the CD and look at Garth Brooks! I was just so obsessed. 

But then, something that really sent me towards, as you so beautifully put it, the path of creating my own tastes independently was a NOFX EP called The Decline. When I was maybe in sixth or seventh grade, I bought The Decline, which is kind of like a pop-punk opera. It’s like this 15 or 20-something-minute-long song that covers so much ground, it’s all just super fast pop punk. That encouraged me to set my own path. I started a little punk band in junior high and started playing really loud guitar and everything. 

AD: What was the punk band called? And what was the first song you learned to play on guitar? 

Buck Meek: Oh, wow. We didn’t even have a name, and we never played a show. That’s how punk we were. So anti-establishment in seventh grade! It was me and my buddies, Colin and Jack. Ok, and then the first song I learned to play on guitar was the Rolling Stones’ “I Can’t Get No (Satisfaction)”. 

AD: Incredible. Finally, the closing track on The Mirror is called “Out of Body”. I said it earlier, but you’ve really had such an amazing career. I didn’t bring up Shadow Kingdom because I imagine the NDA is fairly tight, but my mind would be forever blown if I had been part of something like that with Bob Dylan. I’d love to hear what moment or experience from your musical career–it could be being in the punk band in 7th Grade, collaborating with a particular person or playing in a certain venue–has been the most out-of-body experience for you? Something that in 40 or 50 years, when you’re looking back over your life, you’ll still be like, ‘I can’t believe that happened to me!’.

Buck Meek: Hell yeah! Great question. Oh, there are so many moments. That’s the thing about music, it’s this place you arrive at that feels out of body, and it’s hard to compare to. I’ve been lucky enough to experience that feeling countless times through playing music. That’s been with Bob Dylan, and I felt that again when I was able to write a song with Lucinda Williams last year, which has just been released. That was definitely an out-of-body experience, for sure. I couldn’t believe it!

At the same time, playing with my friends in the garage in seventh grade was out-of-body, too. Growing up playing jazz with Django Porter and playing blues with Brandon Gist in little ice houses around Texas, playing big swing dances in Boston with huge big bands and playing jazz clubs with my brother in New York City, and also rock and roll. When you arrive at that place, where your body disappears, and you’re living in this world of sound, especially with a group of other people, and suddenly the boundaries of your bodies and your minds just merge together. That feeling is hard to compare. It’s a place that feels beyond identity or the individual. Definitely beyond any kind of status or commercialism or anything like that. That’s why I do this, to feel that.
