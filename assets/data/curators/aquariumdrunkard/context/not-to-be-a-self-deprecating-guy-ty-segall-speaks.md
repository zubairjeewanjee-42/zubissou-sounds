## Body

Ty Segall has been making records since 2008, and he’s recorded a lot of them — 16 including his latest Possession, out May 30 on Drag City — but he’d trade those numbers for one perfect one, a classic like the albums he reveres: Love’s Forever Changes, The Kinks’ Arthur, Neil Young’s Harvest to name a few.  

Possession may or may not be that career-defining touchstone. Segall insists that it’s not for him to say. But it is definitely a good one, vividly imagined and written in conjunction with the filmmaker Matt Yoka, exuberantly played in vibrant psychedelic style and lushly arranged for not just the standard palette of rock instruments but strings, horns and a mini symphony. “I usually tend to think less is more,” says Segall. “But this time I, I was like, you know what, more is more. Let’s do more. It just felt good to try to put in as many moves as possible.”

We caught up with Segall recently to talk about his dense but uncrowded new set of songs, his partnership with Yoka, his love of old soul and California and the revelatory string of acoustic shows he recently performed across the U.S. | j kelly

Ty Segall: I’ve known Matt for, wow, almost 20 years now. He’s always come from the storytelling and filmmaking perspective when it comes to writing. We’ve collaborated a lot with making music videos. So, it’s cool to work with a pure visual storyteller. He does language-based storytelling, and I, obviously, what I do is music and language, but the music part of it is almost like the visual aspect of the film. Obviously, the film has music and visual elements, and it’s deeper. There’s more stuff going on. 

My lyrics tend to be more like surreal and definitely more personal. They’re inside my head but abstract. With this record, I wanted to make something less abstract. Well, still keeping my vibe of abstraction, you know, it’s more fun that way. 

Working with Matt, it was cool to say, okay, the goal is to tell stories, how are we going to tell stories? He came from that angle, pure storytelling. And then I came from the angle that, okay, this has to fit into a song. That was a nice way to meet. 

AD: He wrote the words to the title track, I believe, which just has a very different feel from your lyrics. They’re archaic, about something that happened a long time ago. And as you say, very, very visual. I was wondering what you like about the way he wrote that song?

Ty Segall: I don’t know how to describe it from a tactile performance perspective, but we crammed the maximum amount of lyrics into that song. It’s a relatively short song and it’s just nonstop. I don’t usually cram that many lyrics into a song. I thought that was cool. Usually, you’re at other end of that experience where you wish you had another great line to throw in there. In this one, there are so many good lines. It’s just jam-packed. 

“Possession” was special because, out of all the songs, it was the one he came to me with the song complete. Every other song had a genesis or something that we had to shape. Maybe I came up with the thing and then we tweaked it, or he came up with something but this was just done, so it’s pretty cool. He came up with that idea and I love it.

AD: The music for that one is very driving and psychedelic and electric, and it reminds me a little bit of a band called Wolf People. I don’t know if you know them. I was wondering if you were thinking of anything in particular in terms of the album’s sound. Were there any particular albums or bands that were front of mind when you were deciding what it would sound like? 

Ty Segall: I mean, the references that I have are ridiculous and unattainable. If I were to throw one classic album around, Forever Changes. There’s also a little Neil in there, but more the grand Neil, not the Ditch era, maybe more of a Harvest vibe. 

But I think I’m more rooted in West Coast classics like Forever Changes or, you know, maybe Smiley Smile or some kind of grand LA 1970s situation.

AD: So, nothing like British electric folk? 

Ty Segall: Oh, well you know, sure, you could throw in something like SF Sorrow, a Pretty Things vibe. For the strings, I guess you can throw in a little bit of Abbey Road, not the Beatles album but the studio’s lushness or whatever. 

AD: I get the sense that you listen to like a really broad spectrum of music all the time and it’s always interesting to hear what was floating to the top.

Ty Segall: Yeah, maybe a little bit of Bill Fay like that Time of the Last Persecution record with the horns. I like the records that feel really personal. There’s a dual nature of a record like Smile where it sounds like you’re inside of this person’s world, but then these huge moves show up and break it open and make it ginormous, like the strings and things like that. I love those moves in records.

AD: You got like a mini symphony orchestra in some of these tracks, and you’ve done the impossible thing of loading all that stuff onto it without making it seem crowded. Can you talk a bit about why it was important to add that stuff and how you worked with Michael on it and what you think about how it came out?

Ty Segall: Okay, but another good reference is the Kinks. In Arthur, like, those strings on “Victoria,” for instance. That’s a big reference. 

But no, I think that, you know, with the kind of music we are trying to do and the kind of stories we’re trying to do…We’re trying to get into a musical tradition with a larger emotional palette to pull from when we’re making these, these broad strokes, these moves. 

You don’t always need that. I usually tend to think less is more. But this time I, I was like, you know what, more is more. Let’s do more. It just felt good to try to put in as many moves as possible. The lyrics are different, and we’re telling these different kinds of stories, and we’re making a larger album, instead of just these weird vignettes of things in my mind. It’s less of a brain scape than my records usually are.

AD: Are you going to tour it that way or are you going to have to pare it down for the road?

Ty Segall: It’s too expensive. I wish. I gotta have a Tik-Tok hit to bring an orchestra. 

AD: Maybe you could do one or two shows with the whole thing?

Ty Segall: Well, we are playing the Greek. I don’t know if there’s time to do it, but we might play the Greek with at least a couple of people. 

AD: I was thinking about “A Fantastic Tomb,” which I really like a lot. It’s got this wonderful bassline that reminds me of old soul like the O’Jays. I know you’ve covered Hot Chocolate in the past, and I was wondering if that sort of soul, Motown or Philly Soul, is that a part of your toolbox?

Ty Segall: Totally. I love soul funk. It’s a big part of what I do, especially the low end, the rhythms, the grooves. I’m always thinking about that. That’s my favorite shit.

AD: There’s something about the combination of the rough and the smooth.

Ty Segall: That was a life-changing, life-changing musical moment for me, discovering Motown and soul and all that stuff when I was 12. It was the big “aha.” There’s the Beatles, there’s punk, and then there’s like soul. 

AD: So how did that happen? How did you stumble onto it? 

Ty Segall: Well, in Orange County LA, there’s K-Earth 101. It was great when I was a kid. It was the oldies station. And now oldies means Nirvana or something, I guess. 

AD: People keep getting older, you know? 

Ty Segall: But it’s crazy how 40 years can happen in 25 years of oldies or whatever. When I was a kid, you know, it was 1950s and 1960s music. They barely played 1970s music. I mean, barely. By the time I was 18, it was like now disco is oldies. Now 1970s music is oldies. So, yeah, that’s all I listened to from about like 10 to 13.

AD: Were you already playing guitar and singing then? 

Ty Segall: I think by the time I was 13, I was back at playing stuff, but not at first. 

AD: I also really like “California Song,” which you played on your recent acoustic tour. I know that California has always had a big role in your imaginative universe. It’s at the center of a lot of things right now, the migration and the climate change and the fires. I was wondering if the way you think about it has changed at all. 

Ty Segall: I mean, I love California so much. It’s my favorite place. I’m a lifer. I’ve thought about going anywhere else, but I can’t. I just am a Californian. 

AD: What do you like about it? 

Ty Segall: I love everything about it. I mean, there’s a ruggedness to it in a, in a natural sense. It’s a wild place. I’m also just an ocean person, and I think the Pacific Ocean is the best. Sorry to anybody else who ….

AD: Rabid supporters of the Atlantic Ocean. 

Ty Segall: Yeah. I love the Atlantic Ocean. I really do. I really, really do. But there’s something about the briskness and the wildness of the Pacific. And actually, strangely enough, how it’s a little colder. You know, it wakes you up. It perks you up. But, I mean, what can I say? I’m from here. I love it. I love San Francisco. I love L.A. and I love everywhere in between. 

It was interesting on tour. There was… I got a couple of boos when I played that song, which I was very surprised about, but I guess that’s the way the world is now. It genuinely made me sad, but what can you do? 

AD: I do want to ask you about those acoustic shows, because I went and I had my doubts about it. I would probably have preferred to see you with a band, but I got there, and it was just so much fun. 

Ty Segall: Oh, thanks. Wait, which one did you go to again? 

AD: I went to Brattleboro, Vermont. 

Ty Segall: Oh, that was a cool one. I like that place. 

AD: Yeah, it’s a great town. Why did you end up playing there? You’re pretty big for Brattleboro. 

Ty Segall: These shows were smaller. It’s a good vessel to go to places that we can’t otherwise. The band, it’s a lot of people, the expenses are high, and we can’t necessarily go to a smaller town and break even for the night. But if it’s just me, in a minivan, oh yeah, I’ll go to Brattleboro. 

It was great. It was packed. It was awesome, and I love that town.

AD: You know Kyle Thomas right? 

Ty Segall: Kyle’s great. I saw his folks when I was there. They’re amazing. We went to the Co-op. It’s a cool town. 

AD: I felt like that show really revealed what a really good and varied guitar player you are, because it stripped off all the amp buzz and the big loud sounds, and you could really concentrate on what you were playing. And it was really good, and it was really, there were a lot of different styles in play. And I was wondering, how you feel about your guitar playing? Are you still working on stuff? Are you still learning things? 

Ty Segall: Yeah, well, thanks. I don’t know if I’ll ever be able to shed this one, but I genuinely don’t feel like I’m a technical guitar player. I don’t think I’m, you know, proficient in that sense. I just feel like I stumbled upon like an emotional style of playing or just like a wild thing that I do. It’s just the thing I do. I’ve learned a lot from the people I’ve played with, you know, like… Emmett and Charles, they’re insane guitar players, and I learned a lot from them. And, you know, I’m constantly trying to figure different things out. I don’t sit and practice every day because I don’t think it’s possible for me to do certain things. But, yeah, I think I’ve just kind of leaned into my vibe, whatever that is. I don’t want to be a self-deprecating guy, but I don’t think I’ll ever feel like a quote-unquote guitar player. 

AD: I also really liked the way you reinterpreted some of the older songs like “Fanny Dog” and “Girlfriend.” And I think people there, especially because it’s a small town where people don’t see Ty Segall very often, were very excited about those older songs. And yet you sort of transformed them so they weren’t really the old songs anymore. I wonder how you think about that. I mean, do you get bored playing those songs and you have to screw with them a little bit? 

Ty Segall: No. Well, selfishly, I usually feel like the album version is not perfect and should be worked upon, you know? Like, to me, “Girlfriend” is a boring song, you know? It’s just, it’s very whatever. It’s cool, but I have fun trying to figure out how to make it more fun or more interesting. Yeah. Especially on an acoustic guitar, that’s one of the best things about doing that show is you’re like, How could I play “Girlfriend”? That’s a punk song. So you’re like, oh, maybe you’ll try it like this. It’s a good way to keep the song fresh. Keep your mind fresh. It’s almost like giving your dissertation on your musical thesis but really more fun than that. In a weird way, it’s like here’s my life’s work, reimagined. 

AD: Do you have any favorite bits or sounds or lines or anything like that from the album, just like moments of that album that really hit you?

Ty Segall: I think that “California Song,” the way the strings and stuff happen are pretty cool. I do, I like “Shoplifter,” and I love “Fantastic Tomb”. I don’t know about any specific moments, but I think that certain songs ended up in a cool place. In “Hotel,” the one about eating a peach, the strings on that song are cool. Maybe the coolest thing for me is the idea that you have a live song, a guitar song, just a rocker, and you try to replace the guitars with strings. It seems like you could just do it but it’s pretty weird and hard to figure out. It’s still heavy, but there are no electric guitars. 

AD: Are you working on anything else right now? 

Ty Segall: Yeah, I’m trying to write some songs. Yeah. But I’m kind of in the middle of just doing a bunch of other stuff, so it’s kind of hard to… 

AD: There’s a lot of business to do around launching a new album.

Ty Segall: Yeah. So, I’m working on some songs. I got some ideas. I probably got like four or five unfinished things. But, you know, I’m just trying to rock on this next one. 

AD: This is, I guess, album number 16. 

Ty Segall: Who’s keeping count? 

AD: I was wondering, you’ve been doing this for a while now. Have you thought about legacy and how you would prefer to be remembered? 

Ty Segall: Too late for that. Well, no, because I think that I screwed up. I think that everyone just is going to remember me as the guy who put out a million records, and that’s definitely not how I want to be remembered. Hmm. You know, I think I would want to be remembered for a couple of great albums. I don’t think I’ll be remembered that way. But maybe. It’s not too late. 

AD: No, it’s definitely not. 

Ty Segall: I’m like, I’m like, just forget about the first five records I did. Just, just forget about it. 

AD: Really? I love some of those records. 

Ty Segall: I don’t know… I guess the only thing I ever really tried to do is make one, like, classic record. I don’t think I’ve done that. 

AD: Did you come close? 

Ty Segall: It’s not really me to say. But no. 

AD: I thought Freedom Goblin was… just about the best iteration of what you do. And I like the new one a lot, too. 

Ty Segall: That’s cool. Yeah. Or maybe I should get more, you know, no rules again or whatever. 

AD: Yeah, you’ve made a lot of records where there were artistic constraints, like the one you made with no guitars.

Ty Segall: Yeah. I think that’s a cool thing. I think that’s good to do. It definitely was a good thing for my brain. I think one of my main problems is I have a hard time differentiating between this is a good experiment or a good case study and… Maybe I’m not giving myself enough credit.
