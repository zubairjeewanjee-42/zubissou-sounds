# Director Ethan Silverman on AngelHeaded Hipster: The Songs of Marc Bolan & T. Rex

_2025-08-07_ · by Jason Woodbury · `interview`
**Tags:** T. Rex, Mixtapes

[source](https://aquariumdrunkard.com/2025/08/07/director-ethan-silverman-on-angelheaded-hipster-the-songs-of-marc-bolan-t-rex/)

## Body

AngelHeaded Hipster: The Songs of Marc Bolan & T. Rex isn’t a typical music documentary. While it does do what a viewer might expect in terms of using talking heads, archival footage, and critical analysis to tell the story of glam pioneer Marc Bolan and T. Rex, it also presents behind the scenes footage of artists like Nick Cave, Joan Jett, U2, Macy Gray, and many more cutting Bolan’s songs in the studio for the late producer Hal Wilner’s T. Rex tribute album (also called AngelHeaded Hipster). 

The result is a film that places Bolan’s songs—tricksy, playful, and heartfully poignant—at the center of the narrative, providing a look at how those songs still live, long after their creator’s passing. Here, director Ethan Silverman speaks with us about how the project came together—and how it revealed new insights into Bolan’s life, art, and story. | j annis

Aquarium Drunkard: What was it about Marc Bolan’s music that first struck you, and what was the genesis of the whole AngelHeaded Hipster project?

Ethan Silverman: My background is actually in theatre, and I was developing a musical with a friend of mine, and he wanted to use music by a British band from the 70s. We were tossing ideas around and he asked me how well I knew the music of Marc Bolan and T. Rex. I knew “Get It On (Bang A Gong)” of course, and I knew peripherally there was a relationship with glam rock, but not much else. So, in the space of about two weeks, I listened to every single song and album that I could, starting with early Tyrannosaurus Rex all the way through to Dandy in the Underworld. When it eventually became clear the musical wasn’t going to happen, I was still completely obsessed with Marc Bolan and T. Rex, and I had to figure out what to do with all the ideas I was having. I woke up the next morning and said “let’s make a tribute out of it!”

AD: Marc Bolan is often relegated to this place of being glam rock’s progenitor and its perpetual Holy Ghost—that photo of David Bowie, Iggy Pop and Lou Reed arm-in-arm with Iggy wearing a Marc Bolan t shirt. That seems to be Bolan’s eternal like place in all of this. But Bolan really predated and set the template for glam while others wound up in a more prominent place in the public consciousness. Is that something you wanted to address with AngelHeaded Hipster?

Ethan Silverman: Yes, that was it completely. I was blown away because I suddenly heard Marc Bolan in everybody’s music…everything that came before Marc Bolan, and everything that came after—from  early rock ‘n’ roll from the ‘50s, glam, punk, metal, underground folk, just everything. I couldn’t believe it. I became obsessed and just kept going further down the rabbit hole until it overtook me. Then I started asking musicians about Marc, and they’d just tell me flat-out how much they’d cribbed from him over the years.

To be clear though, Marc was very successful in his time. He had 10 Top 10 singles in the UK in the early 70s, when the whole “T-Rextasy” phenomenon was happening. While he didn’t quite make it to the same level as David Bowie, Elton John, or Queen, it wasn’t like he was a completely unrecognized artist. But I do believe that he’s not recognized in the way he should be. In the UK he’s regarded as an icon, but America never knew quite what to do with him.  And he would actually fit side by side with any young artists performing today.

AD: Did the vision for the album or the movie come first?

Ethan Silverman:  It was music first. Honestly, I started the film just to get the money for the record, but once I took the deep dive into the archive, it turned out to be something much bigger than simply filming the making of the album. Marc Bolan was an unbelievably charismatic, beautiful songwriter and performer who just stands head and shoulders above everybody else. I knew that had to be part of the film.

Nick Cave was the first person to record with his version of “Cosmic Dancer,” and things started to fall into place once he got involved. And then some of the performances were so exquisite and the interviews that I started getting were so good that I just kept going. And I knew that the film could be special. I had this vision in my head that I could do both things in one film.

AD: It would’ve been easy to do a ‘greatest hits’ project, but the film and album draw from every phase of Bolan’s songwriting career. There’s stuff from Electric Warrior and The Slider, of course, but there’s also some much deeper cuts like “Scenesof” and even “Hippy Gumbo,” which is pre-Tyrannosaurus Rex. Did that happen naturally?

Ethan Silverman: Many artists came in with a song they wanted to do and some of the songs were suggested to them. It was different. Sometimes it came from Hal [Wilner], sometimes it was somebody else’s idea or the artist’s idea. So we got a broad range of material.

AD: I did want to ask you bout working with Hal Wilner, because this was his final project. He’s a presence all through the film but maintains a low profile. I’m curious about how he guided the music through the variety of interpretations such a gentle hand, so to speak.

Ethan Silverman: I think his approach to record producing was very similar to that of his film idol, who he worked with on many films, which was Robert Altman. Everybody would just hang out and create these things. It was a mix of artistic humans getting together while Hal subtly and benignly manipulated a collaborative environment. Depending on the song, Hal would choose the arranger and, you know, most of the arrangements were done before the artists came into the studio. But there was this overriding belief in the art of old-fashioned music making where everybody’s together in the studio, and you’re creating something special. That approach was fun to film.

AD: Did you know Brother Wayne Kramer [of the MC5] was going to be in the band?

Ethan Silverman: No! One of the songs he played on was “Children of the Revolution,” and I do have an extended interview with Wayne talking about what the word “revolution” meant to him playing at that time, as a young man in the pre-punk era. He really was a revolutionary, musically, and a great guy. So many of these musicians were so fantastic and easy to be with.

AD: Given the presence and involvement of Gloria Jones and Rolan Bolan [Bolan’s widow and son], I’m curious to know how Marc’s personal life shaped your understanding of his music?

Ethan Silverman: Rolan was always hanging out with us in the studio and we became friends. He was only five when Marc died, so most of what he knows about his dad comes from his family—Marc’s brother, and his mother, Gloria. They made an effort to imbue him with who his dad really was, and what kind of person he’d been.

I’m indebted to Gloria, because everything changed when she got involved. The film became a real story. Our interviews were incredibly emotional. There were tears and laughter. I could do a a whole documentary just on Gloria alone, what a hell of a life! She’s an amazing person, and a great spirit with so much to offer. She’s had so many intense relationships with musicians, composer, producers…just incredible!

Gloria was very explicit about Marc— as a man, as an artist, as a songwriter, as a performer, his perspective of the world, about the way he worked with people and how he created. She confirmed some rumors and dispelled others that I checked out with her like, for instance, did Marc play on “Nutbush City Limits”?

AD: Not that far away from a T. Rex song, really. And…?

Ethan SIlverman: I know, and yes, he did! Gloria was singing back-up in the studio with Ike and Tina and she told them Marc was around in LA, so they told her to have him come by and bring his guitar. If you listen to the song, you can hear it. You can just hear his guitar. Gloria doesn’t make up stuff like that. She doesn’t need to.

AD: Whenever we talk about Marc Bolan, I feel like there’s an ever-present specter of David Bowie. You address this in the film—not only their perceived rivalry, but how foundational their friendship was to both of them. Of course we know that Bowie was certainly influenced by Bolan— if “Lady Stardust” isn’t a glowing tribute to somebody, I don’t know what it is—but he seemed loyal and supportive during Marc’s comeback in the late ’70s with his TV show, which was really subversive in its way.

Ethan Silverman: Yes, it was a silly little late afternoon after-school kids show almost, but Marc would showcase a lot of burgeoning punks bands—The Jam, Generation X, The Boomtown Rats. But that’s where David Bowie chose to debut “Heroes,” which is really fascinating and says a lot about their relationship. That was three weeks before Mark died, and when you see the way they’re looking at each other when they’re jamming on the TV show, those are two people who love each other. People forget they really were friends.

AD: AngelHeaded Hipster is a real who’s-who of artists involved—you’ve got everyone from Ringo Starr and Elton John to Nick Cave, Joan Jett, Macy Gray, Devendra Banhart, Father John Misty, the list goes on. That covers a pretty wide swath of music and time, and it’s kind of astounding to see his Marc Bolan’s breadth of influence come through in the film.  

Ethan Silverman: I think that authentic artists come along rarely. Marc Bolan was truly authentic, dynamic, and fun. That’s why musicians of a certain caliber all recognize his gift.
