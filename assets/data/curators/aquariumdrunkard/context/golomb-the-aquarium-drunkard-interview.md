## Body

There are several standout moments on Golomb’s The Beat Goes On, a record whose DNA is shaped by traits synonymous with Yo La Tengo, the Velvet Underground and Silver Jews. Each influence is applied conscientiously in these dynamic arrangements to demonstrate the Columbus, Ohio trio’s appreciation for those artists rather than resting on the merits of sonic achievements. 

“I wanna play music that takes me away,” sings Golomb’s vocalist, guitarist and chief lyricist Mickey Shuman, and with that declaration, as a listener, you immediately sense that this is an album lovingly crafted by three astute music fans. It also marks an impressively early maturation in the band’s recording approach (this time favoring a cleaner and more concise style of songwriting and fidelity, culminating in eleven catchy pop-inflected rock numbers) that effectively announces Golomb as a band underpinned by a constant desire to challenge themselves creatively to ensure that their sound is always evolving. The various threads tying their tastes together may come from the past, but there’s an undeniable push towards an exciting future with their second LP, The Beat Goes On.   

We spoke to Golomb in the lead-up to The Beat Goes On’s release via Zoom, in which we covered a range of topics such as their experiences of recording using analog and digital techniques, being inspired by apathy and collaborating with new and old friends on their latest material. | z hedderman

Aquarium Drunkard: I’ve enjoyed jumping between The Beat Goes On and your self-titled debut LP from 2022. Sonically, there are some differences between the mixes of those records, which make for an interesting contrast. What were some new approaches and techniques you applied to elevate Golomb’s sound?

Xenia Shuman: It was a pretty stark contrast because for the first album, we recorded everything analog. It was a cool experience, but coming out of it, we were all like, ‘Okay, we’re ready to do it digitally and have it be a little bit simpler.’ Going into this one, we recorded everything digitally, and we recorded this one a lot quicker. It took us a long time to do the first one. With this one, we started in October of last year and finished it…

Mickey Shuman: It was maybe four sessions that were all less than six hours. 

Xenia Shuman: Yeah, it was pretty quick. I can’t remember exactly when we finished everything. Was it February? It was some time early this year. It was pretty quick, and it was so nice to do it that way. This time, we’d had more time to marinate on the songs, for the most part. Some of them were still pretty fresh when we went in to record them, but it was nice to do it in a much quicker way. The digital medium lends itself to that way of working compared to analog. 

Mickey Shuman: Believe it or not, technology has advanced, and, in some instances, for the worse, but they invented a lot that certainly helped make a record quicker! 

Hawken Holm: The songs also felt a lot fresher when we recorded them this time. 

Mickey Shuman: Yeah, and I feel like we put, or maybe I did, a lot of pressure on the first one. Going into [The Beat Goes On], we didn’t even have the record finished when we started recording. I think that helped to make the whole experience feel a lot more relaxed. 

AD: Had you come off a period of playing a lot of shows where you got to test out the new songs on stage and use the audience’s reaction as a guide when developing arrangements?

Xenia Shuman: Yeah, there were a couple of songs that we added late into the recording process, but some of them were quite old songs. 

Mickey Shuman: Yeah, I wrote the song “Play Music” maybe six or seven years ago. 

Xenia Shuman: We used to play that song years ago, but then we just stopped. I don’t know if we had even played that with Hawken. 

Mickey Shuman: I don’t think so, not at a show. We’d maybe only played it once before the pandemic. Most of the record we had played, but also some of it developed while we were recording it. “Staring” comes to mind because I feel like how we play it live now is often us trying to accomplish what’s going on in the recording. 

Xenia Shuman: I feel like when we start playing shows again, because we haven’t played a lot of shows since we finished recording the record, the way we do it live will definitely be informed by the way we recorded stuff. Which is interesting because we haven’t done that before. 

AD: That’s interesting to hear that ‘Play Music’ goes back six years. Going through all your different releases, there’s a wonderful spontaneity to the way you play and it feels like you record songs as they come to you in the moment and move on to the next thing. I didn’t expect to hear that a song from that long ago in Golomb’s life would feature on this LP because it just seems like there’s constant movement with the band. Why did you feel that The Beat Goes On was the right LP and time for the song to become part of the band’s output? 

Mickey Shuman: I feel like there’s like such a broad range of stuff we’re touching on across The Beat Goes On. We had a good chunk of the songs finished and knew what we wanted on there. The last additions were “Sweet Release (Ain’t No Devil)”, which is the country one, “Be Here Now” and “Experience Humaneness”, which were all outside of our usual sound. The addition of “Play Music” felt like a good bridge between all of the maybe not straightforward or typical Golomb-sounding songs. Also, we like to play music! I was proud of that song, and I didn’t want it to just be in my voice memos forever. 

Xenia Shuman: It’s also thematically relevant to the record in a way that was surprising. Looking back and knowing it was written so long ago, you know, realizing that the lyrics were very in tandem with everything else, made it feel like a natural addition. 

AD: Even though The Beat Goes On is your second album, these songs provide a really strong introduction to first-time listeners. Your love of music shines across the songs, both lyrically and with the sonic references you employ in the arrangements. I was wondering what your earliest music-related memories are: first CD you bought, first concert? 

Xenia Shuman: I know the first record I bought was the Strokes’ Room on Fire. I was 15 and I was so stoked. My first concert was Kings of Leon in 2007, it was so great. I was eight years old and I never had a better time in my whole life. 

Hawken Holm: I didn’t buy many CDs because we had a lot of them in our house growing up, but the first record I remember buying was Yip / Jump Music by Daniel Johnston. My dad had the whole set of all the tapes that he picked up, maybe within the same year, so I was stoked to find the vinyl. I thought it was rare but then I realized that it wasn’t rare afterwards. My first concert was, I mean, we grew up going to a lot of smaller shows, but the best first concert that I ever went to, though, was the Rolling Stones. 

Xenia Shuman: Wasn’t your first concert the Arctic Monkeys? 

Hawken Holm: Oh, yeah, it was Arctic Monkeys. Those shows were within six months of each other.

Mickey Shuman: I’m trying to think of the first thing I bought. I used to go to the library a lot to get CDs. That was how I first started getting CDs to listen to. The most formative one has to be getting Merriweather Post Pavilion in middle school. I remember being like, ‘Dang, that’s such a cool cover. What’s this all about?’ That had a lasting impression. I was lucky to have an early Emo-Goth phase in elementary school where I definitely Slipknot and Marilyn Manson CDs, but those have not had as much of a hold on my lasting musical experience. Although, I will say, I’ve gone back and listened to some System of a Down albums and they’re still just as fun to listen to. 

Then, my first concert, that I remember, was “Weird Al” Yankovic. I think it was at King’s Island, which is an amusement park. It was insane! He had all these extras on stage and costumes. They did the Star Wars song, and they were all in Stormtrooper outfits. It was a pretty awesome childhood memory. I don’t know if that’s had the most lasting, profound effect on me as far as first concerts, but that’s the one I remember. And, I do feel that maybe, nowadays, humor is not as spotlighted in music as maybe it should be. Maybe that stems from rocking out to Weird Al as a kid. On the song “Other Side of the Earth” that’s on the record, even though that’s, in a way, the most serious song on there, I love that Hawken started cracking up after we finished playing it. You hear that at the end of the song and that was just a genuine reaction to what we had just done, which is why I wanted to keep it in. 

AD: I’m happy you mentioned “Other Side of the World,” it always stood out every time I listened to the album. It feels particularly pertinent at the moment when individualism is taking over and making people neglect fostering a strong community spirit. Can you tell me about writing that song? At what stage of writing the album did it come into existence and did it inform how you approached other songs? 

Mickey Shuman: It’s funny because it has the dumbest origin story. Xenia and I have a little dog, and we went to the dog park one day and it was pretty muddy. While we were there, we saw a guy fall in the mud and all the other people at the dog park just looked at him. No one went up to help him, so I went over to him and asked if he was ok, which he was. I then wrote the first line of that song, “So nobody gonna bat an eye when a stranger get hurt.” We all really like reggae and dub music, but didn’t intend to make something like that. I just made a Garageband backtrack so that I could just fuck around with it at home. Eventually, I was like, ‘Damn, this is kind of cool!’ Then we’d play it at home…

Xenia Shuman: We were sort of noncommittal about it at first. We were like, ‘I don’t know if we can do this!’

Mickey Shuman: Yeah, initially we didn’t know if we wanted to make something like that, but then we would play it at home because it felt really good and fun. The more the album fleshed out, it seemed like it was lyrically the most on the nose thing we’d done and also it was a challenge of asking ourselves how do we take this thing that maybe people wouldn’t be hyped to hear, us playing a reggae song, and make it feel genuine and serious and not do it in a goofy way. Also, people could hear it and be like, ‘Oh, it kind of reminds me of The Clash,’ who I like, but Xenia and Hawken have more experience listening to them than I do. In the end, I came through that experience thinking about how people make music that they like to make, and it’s OK. I don’t give a fuck what people are going to think or not think. Frankly, the three of us have a blast playing that song. But, yeah, it stemmed from this guy falling at the dog park. 

It got me thinking about how, and this is something Xenia and I talk about a lot, of how people act online like they care so much about their community and external communities. On a human-to-human basis are completely the opposite because they don’t express care for a neighbor. It’s like, ‘Well, you’re trying to stand up for the world on the internet, but you can’t say thank you to a person serving you at a restaurant?’ I don’t have any answers, but I know that it’s important to be nice to people. 

AD: Going back to the recording process, your debut has so much energy and fun jams towards the end of the tracklist. With the new record, how much room did you leave for spontaneity and improvising?

Mickey Shuman: I feel like, in a way, it was all pretty structured. “Staring” is a good example because the end of that was very open for whatever happened in the studio. I would say, in a live setting, that sense of openness and spontaneity is pretty present with any song. “Dog” will sometimes have in the Grateful Dead spirit of things, of transitioning into another song, or we’ll do a lot of song transitions in a live setting. I feel like the songs, to me, feel really catchy on this record. In a way, we were trying to stray as far away from that kind of energy song as possible, which we still play at most shows. It’s still a highlight in our live shows, I would say. We were just trying to make pop songs, essentially. 

Xenia Shuman: Yeah, we were trying to tighten things. When we were writing the songs, usually, how it goes is that Mickey will bring something to us and will have quite a bit of it already written. Sometimes, unfortunately for him, the role that I take on is like, ‘You need to take that part out and you need to like restructure this section.’ A big part of this record was about paring stuff down and taking out stuff that felt extra because we wanted to be more intentional about what we were doing. And, I guess, in the name of catchiness and making more pop-adjacent music, we were motivated to strip things down and make it so that it’s a good little package when you finish. 

Mickey Shuman: Yeah, but even “The Beat Goes On”, we’ve played that live, and we’ll jam on the beginning for a couple of minutes before it even starts. It just depends on how we’re feeling when we play a show or what the show is. If there are 10 people at a show, we might do something differently than if there were 100 people.

AD: It’s also a nice way to keep the songs alive and continue to grow.

Xenia Shuman: Oh, yeah. The nice thing about having them be a little bit more concise is that it gives them either the opportunity to stay concise or expand it a lot more than it was recorded. 

AD: Speaking of expanding on the songs, I loved on “Sweet Release” the addition of violin and the saxophone on “Be Here Now”, as well. Throughout, there are nice textures from different guitar tones and synths. Those details do a great job of transforming the songs into nicely lived-in spaces. How was the experience of developing the arrangements by inviting guest collaborators into the studio?

Hawken Holm: Mickey does such a great job of outreach for delegating the work of getting additional tracks of stuff that we’re not necessarily the most equipped to deliver ourselves. 

Mickey Shuman: My secret dream job is as a music producer, for sure. 

Xenia Shuman: That was a really fun day. Mickey was a force to be reckoned with, for sure, by just totally steering the ship. Hawken and I were like, ‘All right, we better go do what we’re supposed to be doing because this guy is really driving us!’ 

Mickey Shuman: Also, “Sweet Release” and “Play Music” were both recorded live with everyone in the room. With all the extras, too. The only things we redid were our vocals. On “Sweet Release,” I redid the guitar solo because the guy who played on it, whose name is Austin, he made it too good and I was like, ‘Well, if we play this live, I won’t be able to do this!’ 

Xenia Shuman: That was a really fun day because it was a combination of people together, our dad was there and he played on the songs. The fiddle player, Abby, was there and we had never met her before that day, but she just came in and she killed it. So it was a nice day of old friends and new people. 

Mickey Shuman: That, for me, is the best part of recording, when you get the basic tracks and then you’re figuring out and exploring what can happen afterwards. Also, to the question earlier about the difference between recording The Beat Goes On and our first record, with the debut, we were so constrained with the eight-track and I felt so ready to just be able to try something else and be like, ‘That didn’t work, but that’s OK.’
