## Body

As a member of The Crusaders and a versatile and in-demand sideman, drummer Nesbert “Stix” Hooper’s life in music is a testament to the power of the groove. Coming up in Texas, he formed his first bands alongside pianist Joe Sample and saxophonist Wilton Felder, and their group would eventually become known as The Jazz Crusaders, before shifting in the ’70s to simply The Crusaders. Hooper’s drums laid down the foundation for the group’s sound, a soulful blend of R&B, jazz, rock, funk, and eventually fusion, and his playing would go onto provide crucial samples for beats utilized by artists like J Dilla, Madlib, Three Six Mafia, and many more, making him, according to his label, “arguably the most sampled drummer of all-time.” 

On May 8th, he released Cookin’ Up The Groove, a 10-track collection that features his final collaboration with Sample, “Stix and Joe’s Gumbo Groove.” On the occasion of the new recording, he joined Aquarium Drunkard to discuss his heady days with The Crusaders, the secret to musical collaboration, and of course, one necessary ingredient that should be in every pot of gumbo. | j woodbury

Aquarium Drunkard: One of the songs featured on this new album, “Stix and Joe’s Gumbo Groove,” is your final collaboration with the late Joe Sample. How did this particular song come together? 

Stix Hooper: Joe sent that track to me just prior to his passing in 2014. He titled it “G Minor Groove.” It was a G minor groove and a vamp, a continuous, repetitious kind of a thing. He hadn’t finished it but said, “Take a look at it and see what we can come up with.” And before he could finish it and establish some more communication with me, he passed on. So I came up with the title and an extended melodic structure.

We both liked gumbo very, very much. With his Creole heritage and the time we spent in New Orleans and Texas, gumbo was a major thing for us, and Joe’s mom was a major maker of a particular gumbo. She made good food. So it just it just melded itself to do that. He never got to know that title, but his widow, Yolanda, is aware of how it came to be called that as we’re in partnership with the song. 

AD: You and Joe made so much music together in The Crusaders and beyond. The song is a really funky and upbeat track, but you mentioned, of course, that he sent it to you just before he passed away. When you listen to it now, what does it make you feel as you reflect on your relationship with Joe?

Stix Hooper: It’s very complex, because I get both smiles and tears, almost at the same time. We shared many moments laughing about things and so many moments where we were almost in tears, often about the extended racism we experienced. We grew up in Texas and we went to high school together. We shared so many moments, and I felt inspired to complete that song. It brings both sadness and happiness and reflections on the things we have shared together. 

AD: You reprise your song “Greasy Spoon” on this record. That’s one you wrote for The Crusaders’ album Southern Comfort in 1974. Were there a lot of good greasy spoons growing up in Texas?

Stix Hooper: Oh yeah. That’s what was used to call particular diners, “greasy spoons.” It was a designation. But there was a lot of grease going on. I mean literally: sophisticated organics went into the fat and lard. 

AD: Listening to the original version, I’m struck by Larry Carlton’s wah-wah guitar. The Crusaders worked with a lot of different musicians, especially in the guitar and bass roles. Was it ever difficult to coordinate the work that The Crusaders did, given that the lineup was somewhat fluid, aside from the base combo of you and Joe? 

Stix Hooper: It was a combination of both. But you bring in other musicians who can express themselves within the framework, and who respect what you do and can advance it. Larry Carlton certainly he had his own style with the wah-wah guitar, and we thought that might be a little different thing to incorporate into the compositions we were writing. I used to say, jokingly, he was “the white boy that could play the groove.” 

AD: It’s true! 

Stix Hooper: We had a lot of fun with Larry. To see him in the middle of the Rumble in the Jungle with Muhammad Ali vs. George Foreman [when The Crusaders played as part of Zaire ’74, a three-day festival hyping the fight]…all of us were sitting there and he’s in the middle of the Congo, standing there with his pale face. [Laughs]  

AD: I love the Jazz Crusaders-era too, but I listen to the ‘70s Crusaders records over and over. For the last couple of years, that’s been a huge part of my listening life, especially a song like “Mosadi (Woman).” I feel like there’s a whole world in that song, and I can get lost in it and exist in that zone. And there are a number of songs like that on those records. You guys were mutating, blending R&B, rock, predating fusion even. Back in the day, did you guys face music industry pressure to stay on the same path stylistically or was the industry more open to what you guys were doing, which was really synthesizing and blending a lot of types of music?

Stix Hooper: I think it’s kind of loaded in the sense that yes, we did have a lot more freedom. Our labels respected us. But the one time where the record company said, “This is what we you want to do,” that became one of the biggest commercial hits of ours, which was called “Street Life.” They wanted that to not have an introduction, they wanted to cut that out and make it shorter. And we said, “No, we want to keep that.” That was a fight. For some reason, they were embarrassed about the legato-type introduction before we got into the groove; but we wanted to show people that music doesn’t always have to start in what we call “the pocket.” We had to convince MCA. We stood our ground and we were very happy with that and the public was happy with that too, because it allowed them to see another side of us. 

AD: You’ve got a truly insane CV. You’ve played with Quincy Jones, Bill Withers, Shuggie Otis, Grant Green, you’re on Lalo Schifrin’s Enter the Dragon soundtrack. We could spend all day listing your credits. What, in your experience, is the secret to being a good collaborator? 

Stix Hooper: Most of the time when I worked outside of The Crusaders, I was called for my musicianship and craftsmanship, whether I was working with a hardcore jazz group, whether it’s a Grant Green, or whether I’m working with Bill Withers, a singer. I was usually called in because I would be able to execute what was needed and enhance what they were doing.

AD: One of my favorite Crusaders songs is “Journey From Within,” from Second Crusade. I mean, it’s just such a powerful song; it’s psychedelic, and I don’t just mean in the sense of the so-called “psychedelic” genre. You made a solo record in 1979 called The World Within. That got me thinking about that notion of an inner world and what it means to journey within. Is there a spiritual component to your music? 

Stix Hooper: There’s a certain spirituality to the music, and the sound of the music definitely. It can be expressed even without lyrics; it’s a language that is spoken through sound. There’s no translation that has to take place except what the ear hears and what the ear accepts. 

AD: I think about, for instance, John Coltrane’s version of “My Favorite Things,” which is from The Sound of Music. He doesn’t need the lyrics to convey the emotion of the song. It comes through.

Stix Hooper: That is true. It’s because musicians are inspired by the quality and the tonality of the music. Music is a complex art. It’s similar to another art—one where there’s spontaneity and creativity—which is cooking. I’ve known some of the greatest chefs in the world and they get in tune to what they’re doing. It’s a combination of experimentation and proof, they are collaborating with ingredients. That’s very inspiring too. So cooking and music, those are two elements of the world that are connected.

AD: We started off by talking about gumbo and we made our way all the way around to discussing food again, so now I’m hungry.

Stix Hooper: I hear you. Just make sure when you make your gumbo that you have some good okra in there. You probably never heard of okra at your age.

AD: Oh no, I know okra. I’m a fan. I’m married to a vegetarian. I know all the vegetables. [Laughs] Stix, thank you so much for taking the time to speak with me about your remarkable career in music and your life. I sure appreciate your time and your insight. So thank you for sharing it with me.

Stix Hooper: [Laughs] Thank you for being so open and for expanding my creativity and approach as I answer your questions.
