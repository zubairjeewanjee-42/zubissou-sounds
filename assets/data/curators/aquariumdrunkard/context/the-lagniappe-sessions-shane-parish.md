## Body

Lagniappe (la ·gniappe) noun ‘lan-ˌyap,’ — 1. An extra or unexpected gift or benefit. 2. Something given or obtained as a gratuity or bonus.

No stranger to transformation, Shane Parish has built a career that moves fluidly between original composition and interpretive work, distilling and reimagining inspired source material—from the intimate lyricism of Chet Baker to the transcendent soundscapes of Alice Coltrane—while continually expanding his own voice, most recently on Autechre Guitar. This installment of the Lagniappe Sessions finds Parish taking on late 90s southern gothic Cat Power, the croon of Lana Del Rey, nascent Bjork, and the nearly lost hushed brilliance of German folk singer Sibylle Baier. Parish in his own words, below

Shane Parish :: Cross Bones Style (Cat Power)

“Cross Bones Style” is the first song I heard by Cat Power, not long after its release, around 1999. I was hypnotized by the droney driving guitar arpeggiation over Jim White’s break beat. And then Chan’s voice enters, singular, tender and full of sadness, floating against the pulsing drone. It would become immediately recognizable, like a Billie Holiday or Karen Dalton. For my arrangement, I tune my low E string down to a B so I can get deep into it. I capture some of the vocal harmonies with double stops on my treble strings. I play the form from the music video, the one with all the dancers. It’s about a minute shorter than the version on the record.

Shane Parish :: Video Games (Lana Del Rey)

Lana Del Rey is the greatest crooner of the 21st century. There’s a video of David Lynch that occasionally pops up on my feed where he says “Today, I was thinking about Lana Del Rey and her song ‘Video Games’ from 2011.” And I know the feeling. Sometimes you just start thinking about that song. I play the whole form straight down and I was excited to capture the backing vocal tag “and now you do” at the end. 

Shane Parish :: Birthday (The Sugarcubes)

This song goes back and forth between C & F. So I tune string 6 to C and string 5 to F, and I try to hold it down. Bjork’s melody here is sweeping grandeur, at times reminding me of an Appalachian folk ballad, even though the Sugarcubes’ recording sounds like it was made on another planet. I love to interpret this one more freely, see where the melody takes me, find secret rooms within its angles and contours, and revel in them.

Shane Parish :: Tonight (Sibylle Baier)

This song is a killer. It makes me cry to hear it. And yet the lyrics are so sweet and loving, something to be happy about. The original recording by Sibylle Baier is intimate, almost private. The form is a bit of a labyrinth, which I render here exactly as it is on the recording, with random measures of 2/4, and little tags that only happen once. It makes me wonder if she played it different every time or if it is a fixed form that she repeated the same every time. I guess it works both ways, each phrase a glowing cell that you can repeat, or not. Masterpiece.
