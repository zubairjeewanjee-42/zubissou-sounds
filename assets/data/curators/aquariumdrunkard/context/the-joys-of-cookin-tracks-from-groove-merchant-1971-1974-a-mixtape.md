## Body

The Groove Merchant label has become such a revered name in cratedigging and acid jazz circles that it is difficult to remember its somewhat hokey pedigree. Label founder Sonny Lester was an old A&R man whose name had primarily been associated with easy listening schlock like Those Fantabulous Strings and novelty burlesque records like Ann Corio’s How to Strip For Your Husband. Lester was the bandleader on a nifty exotica record After Hours Middle East that still gets some love from connoisseurs, but no one would have mistaken him—unlike his friend Bob Thiele—as a man with his finger on the pulse of modern jazz. Lester’s mid-to-late 60s outfit Solid State Records earned some credibility with early releases by Chick Corea, but it mostly stuck to more mass appeal fare like big band records and the popular soul jazz of Hammond B3 preachers like Jimmy McGriff. Solid State’s successor Groove Merchant, founded in 1971, seemed no less interested in chasing hits and pleasing crowds. The grooves got thicker, to be sure, but the listening was still pretty easy.

There was a revolution upending jazz, but nobody bothered to tell Groove Merchant. Nothing on the label really resembles what was happening to jazz in 1971. The year of Live-Evil, Mwandishi, Journey in Satchidananda, Escalator over the Hill, Weather Report and The Inner Mounting Flame might as well have been taking place in another dimension. Only Chick Corea’s pensive Sundance (with Dave Holland, Bennie Maupin, Woody Shaw and Jack DeJohnette) seems like it is breathing the same atmosphere. And pianist Larry Willis’s ferocious Inner Crisis from 1973 points toward the politically conscious deep jazz indie labels of the day like Strata-East and Tribe. Those aside, Lester mostly stuck with bread-and-butter soul jazz: organ combos cranking out pop covers; funky crossover guitarists; wicked flute blowouts; a smattering of jazz standards given the good-time treatment.

This is the irony of the late twentieth century cult of Groove Merchant among cratediggers and sample-hunters: it was already something of a throwback in its own day. They were going retro for a sound that was already retro. Most of its releases (hell, many of its artists) spiritually belonged to the early 1960s—before fusion, before spiritual jazz, before black power, before ECM.

Ultimately, Groove Merchant was a conservative project: it served up sweaty, funky old school party jazz to a world that had largely moved on to more immersive, mind-expanding, consciousness-raising material. Just look at the label’s often outrageously risqué cover art: Groove Merchant fucked. You could go chase revolution and enlightenment elsewhere. It is no coincidence that the label’s sound was rediscovered and championed in the late 1980s acid jazz heyday, when the reputation of 70s psychedelic jazz excess was probably at its nadir.

Fortunately, we don’t have to choose between those moments. Aquarium Drunkard has been a tireless evangelist for those other faces of the 1970s revolution in jazz. Today, we thought you could use a little get-down. | b sirota
