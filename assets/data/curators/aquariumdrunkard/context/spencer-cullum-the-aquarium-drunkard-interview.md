## Body

Having relocated from the United Kingdom to the states, Spencer Cullum began creating albums in his Coin Collection series as a way to gain some semblance of understanding of his own identity. But as the series has evolved, it’s become something much larger, a multi-textural exploration of Cullum’s interiority while also addressing the ever-crumbling global environment.

The first instalment of the Coin Collection trilogy landed in 2021, finding the British-born, Nashville-based songwriter and pedal steel aficionado welcoming a cast of players to contribute to his otherworldly visions, teetering between lush jazz-tinged numbers and unnerving folk-horror melodies. On his latest release, the closing chapter to the series, Spencer’s focus shifted from curiosity towards his own identity to channelling the frustrations incurred from witnessing a rapid decline in humanity. In trying to make sense of what’s unfolding before our eyes today, it helps to go back to the past. That’s exactly what Cullum has done with his most recent work. Explorations of late-stage capitalism, inhumane political acts, and issues arising from the ongoing climate crisis are deftly picked apart under the guise of the enduring folkloric style of his motherland. 

Drawing on a foundation of 1960s and ‘70s musical influences, notably artists associated with the Canterbury scene of that time, such as Robert Wyatt and Kevin Ayers’ Soft Machine, Cullum’s delicately evocative material presents itself as something of an endearing hauntological curio. Writing about the horrors of modern living is not exclusive to this century. The application of an old-timey patina on these charming arrangements, along with his unintrusive vocals and doom-inflected melodies, takes Cullum out of the present moment. The disparity of time works similarly to how Cullum was afforded an unfiltered lens to write about his beloved hometown when positioned thousands of miles away from it. Here, songs such as “Don’t Go Downtown” or “Washed Upon The Shore” are stark reminders of Coin Collection 3’s contemporary creation due to the all too familiar grim descriptions of society and our cities depicted daily in the news. On the latter, he quietly intones, “What’s the point in celebrating when there’s nothing left to say?” 

In that moment, the feeling of escape Cullum and his trusted collaborators create across this masterful LP comes tumbling down as reality rears its ugly head. These days, there’s very little to feel hopeful about. However, listening to Cullum’s Coin Collection trilogy inspires optimism in its way of melding traditional storytelling as a means to cope with horrors that often feel beyond our capacity to fully comprehend. It’s a reminder of the importance of staying in touch with your identity, how it’s shaped by external factors and expressing it not only as an act of exorcism but in the hopes of encouraging others to do the same for themself.

Speaking from his home studio, Spencer Cullum spoke to us about what the Coin Collection trilogy has meant to him and his artistic confidence, casting a folk-horror film based on his music and a lot more. | z hedderman

Aquarium Drunkard: I’ve really enjoyed spending time with your new record and the final instalment in your Coin Collection trilogy. When we’re speaking, it’s a few weeks away from its release date. How are you feeling about it coming out, and have you had a moment to reflect on the last five years of this trilogy, given that it was founded on your desire to rediscover your identity, having moved to Nashville from the UK?

Spencer Cullum: Yeah, it feels quite nice to wrap it up. I like things in threes, as they say. When it first happened, I wrote a lot during the pandemic, and I feel like everyone was in turmoil. The whole world was a bit in turmoil. To find identity felt rewarding and comforting. On the third album, the world is still in turmoil! Even more so. It feels a little bit like therapy, maybe. With this one, it was nice writing songs about peace and being at home. There’s a frustration in America, in England as well, but with what’s happening in America, with the devastation caused by ICE and everything else. It’s hard to be political and to be like, ‘I don’t like this,’ as a privileged white man. There’s a frustration there, though. It was nice to write and use that frustration instead of being angry, by making up folk tales about getting revenge towards it all. That’s given me a bit of comfort. It was sort of like writing my own fucked up A24 film. to help not be as angry, or being the same level, because when you see the videos, your immediate reaction is that you want to hurt them back, or to be like, ‘I really don’t like this. This is unfair.’ But then I feel like if you do that, you’re just as bad as them. Processing all of that through writing has been quite a peaceful thing. To make fucked-up folk stories where nature has its revenge! 

AD: Yeah, absolutely. I’m glad you mentioned how writing this album was like creating an A24 horror film because the LP opens with a song titled “Rowan Tree”, which, of course, made me think of The Wicker Man. If you could transform this album into a film, who would you have as the director and in the cast? 

Spencer Cullum: Wow! Ok, well, you’d want someone you’d really hate as a cast member because you kind of hate Edward Woodward in The Wicker Man. Even though he’s not that bad, by the end of it, it’s quite nice that he gets it! I’m trying to think of an actor that I don’t like. Oh, Chris Pratt. It would be nice for him to get it at the end. Then, again, you need someone cool, so maybe not him. I love Dev Patel. I think he’s fantastic. He’s been in so many great folk films. 

Now, a director. Well, I love Mark Jenkin. He’s fantastic. He made a great film called Enys Man which is just amazing. A lot of the stories I wrote are rooted in the setting where he set that in Cornwall, so it’s got a similar feel to it. I love his film Bait, as well. So, ok, he’ll direct it. I saw Hamnet recently, and Jessie Buckley was incredible. I think she should be one of the leading cast in this film version of Coin Collection 3. 

AD: I’d watch that for sure! You moved to Nashville a long time ago, I spent a large chunk of time there about a decade or more ago, and even back then, I remember hearing people talking about how gentrification was creeping in and changing parts of the city. I read an interview you gave a while ago where you mentioned that a place I used to love going to, Fond Object, had closed down. The Coin Collection trilogy was made over five years, that’s a substantial period of time to observe the ways that, not only the environment around you has changed, but how you’ve grown as a person. How do you feel you’ve come out of this trilogy, and that period of time, different to how you entered it? 

Spencer Cullum: It was really sad with Fond Object closing. That was such an amazing part of the community that nurtured that side of the Riverside locality. I help my wife with her bookshop in East Nashville, and I really enjoy working there. We’ve taken a little bit of Fond Object-community vibe, and that’s made me feel really present and grateful for the Nashville community that I never knew existed, not just in music, because the shop is a photography and art bookshop. It’s been nice to see that and be settled in that. I felt more comfortable within that than being part of just the music community. There is an amazing music community here, but then there is also the eye of Music Row, watching, like the eye of Mordor, that’s looking at us, which is fine! It keeps the wheels running in this town, it keeps money flowing down it. 

The general community of East Nashville, and that is one thing that Nashville has really got going for it, the world may be on fire, and you know, Tennessee State, with some of our representatives, is pretty bad,  but the street where my wife’s shop is and meeting the people there has been fantastic. That was something that inspired me a lot with the record. That’s the only thing we’ve got at the moment. 

AD: You’re from Essex originally. Reflecting on your life there before you left, did the physical distance help you be less restrained in how you wrote about that place and how it shaped you? 

Spencer Cullum: Yeah, definitely. The last record I wrote was my bitterness towards Romford. It felt quite tragic in Romford. Hometowns can be like that for everyone, really. That’s part of growing up. Obviously, I’ll go back to Romford to see my parents. A lot of the last record, number two, there was a lot of reflecting on going back to your hometown and seeing the different personal landmarks there; seeing the lamppost where a friend who died in a motorcycle crash, or a corner where my brother and my mate got the shit kicked out of them. The thing about your hometown, and I’m sure you have this, too, there are moments with friends that you gather together and carry with you. But yeah, that one was from a bitter angle. This album, however, has a joyous angle of community and definitely explores the ways we bond together, which I’ve experienced in Nashville. I feel like the reflection has become a little bit more joyous. Even if the rest of the world is bleak, there are more joyous overtones to it with regard to community. 

AD: The three albums are so incredibly produced and are abundant with personality and colour in their textures in a way where each LP feels quite individual but also really cohesively part of something bigger outside of itself. What were some of the constants in your songwriting or production choices that you kept to provide a solid foundation across the trilogy? With that, what were some of the new things you incorporated to challenge yourself or lend a different quality when approaching a new body of work?

Spencer Cullum: The constant theme was using a similar cast of people. So, Sean Thompson and Erin Rae I’d record it at a place, or at least work with a guy called Jeremy Ferguson, in Battle Tapes. There was a constant flow of people I’d use. That was part of the whole collection; the people. I think the reason I’m wrapping up things with this trilogy is that I felt that I was slightly milking this a bit! 

Changing it slightly each time, I’d never really written before, I’ve been a pedal steel player who plays in the background. I started this whole thing just wanting to play music that I liked when I was 16 or 17; Kevin Ayers and Robert Wyatt, all that sort of music. I’ve never really played the music that I loved when I was 17. It started like that, I was mid-30s, and songwriting was really new to me. With the first record, the whole process of songwriting was completely new, and it was a bit scary, even being a front person. That was daunting. I still don’t really like that. Three records in, when you do something, your confidence grows. Before the first record, I was like, “Oh, I’m being subconscious,” and I listen to it now, and you can hear that. Even when you listen to old 1960s records, and they’re being subconscious, and strange. Definitely, with the first record, I was like, “Oh, if they do it, then I can do it!” 

The other thing was that I met Rich Ruth on the second record, and I got very inspired by the way he works. He listens to a lot of artists, but it doesn’t feel like he said, “I want my song to sound like this song that I’ve listened to.” He tries to get away from it, and I was inspired by that. From that, I think I got better at writing. 

AD: What you’ve just said reminded me of something that I was struck by in one of the new songs, “Jackie Paints”. That song was inspired by your mom and her working in her studio, which is in her garden, I believe. There’s a line where you sing, “She’ll stop and start again, not knowing when to end.” That made me interested in hearing about your own relationship with knowing how to serve a song with just the right amount of texture and colour in the arrangements, because your work is so richly developed. What have been some of the greatest lessons or pieces of advice in this regard to know when a song is complete that you learned during the Coin Collection trilogy? 

Spencer Cullum: Yeah, you know, I think it’s getting older as well, being not as bothered by perfection and being limited. I wrote all of the songs for the record in this shed that I’m chatting to you from. There are cassette machines around here that I used, and also ProTools and reel-to-reels. There’s the idea of being limited and not being able to carry on. I record a lot in big pop studios for various session work, where there are unlimited resources, and sometimes, I think that can restrict, or can drive you insane with thoughts of, “It needs to be better and better and better.” A lot of the recordings I’ve done for this record were made on people’s iPhones on tour, and it’s being really limited and having fewer resources, which stops me a little bit in a very helpful way. That’s a thing that comes along when you get older, you learn to feel OK with a take that feels vulnerable. I think that’s better. 

The idea or worry of looking vulnerable, where you don’t want to make mistakes, whether it’s with lyrics or pitchiness, I think that goes away when you get older. That’s where there’s magic. I’m sure our favorite records will have that vulnerability to them. Even if they’re made in massive studios, they’ll try to capture the vulnerability, but it’s washed away with perfection. That line from the song, my mum has always had a throw-it-against-the-wall-and-see-if-it-sticks approach, as well. I like that with her art a lot. She’s definitely inspired me with that approach. With that line, as well, she’s still in Romford, and she’s always wanted to move to Cornwall. That’s her escape. There’s also something in that song about thinking about parents getting older and dealing with that, which, you know, is hard. 

AD: Do you have specific memories from making the last three albums that you’d like to share, and what has stood out as a crucial moment in bringing confidence and joy from making these songs?

Spencer Cullum: The first memory is from the pandemic, when it felt like you were sneaking out. I went over to Sean Thompson’s house. I owe a lot to him, as he introduced a lot of these Nashville people to who were listening to a lot of the same music that ultimately inspired the record. We were listening to the band Trees, and I remember thinking, “Wow, this guy’s into English folk music, and he’s as Tennessee as it gets!” That was definitely the thing that kick-started it all off. 

Hearing Erin sing and bring another dimension to the arrangements was incredible. Us all being together  in the studio to record, in general, is something I won’t forget. That nervousness and feeling out of my depth a little bit, and singing and being scared about having a group of amazing people around. That was a very special memory. 

Doing a tour for the first time in a shitty Volkswagen, going around England and Europe. There were stressful times when we weren’t allowed to take our van into Europe! Those moments always bring people together, and you remember those moments. But it was nice doing shows and seeing people being into it. I also think just being creative in the writing world and in the production, I’d never really experienced that before. That definitely felt nice. At the end of the day, though, it all boils down to that community. The whole collection is a weird, messed-up thing. I don’t want everyone to see me in the front, so I deflect and want people to pay attention to all of the amazing people who have been part of the collections. 

AD: You brought up session work earlier. I saw that you spent some time in Las Vegas on a residency with Miranda Lambert. It must be wild to have so many different types of experiences across your career, going between your solo work and session jobs. I have to imagine that you’ve learnt so much seeing the different approaches and processes people have in their own music. 

Spencer Cullum: It’s definitely helped a lot with my music. Miranda’s great, I think the world of her.  She’s a fantastic singer and is a great historian of old country music. She can name and sing every Guy Clark song, word-for-word. She’s fantastic. With the session world,  it’s weird. I dip my toe, a little bit. You have to run. [You have to] be so fast [when they are] putting a chart in front of you. That aspect has definitely helped keep my music moving. I recently made a record on the Isle of Wight with Hollow Hand and Erin Rae. It’s a “banquet rock” record, that’s what we’re calling it. It’s very sort of Clannad-based and Steeleye Span influenced, but we made it in four days. I definitely think the quick Nashville aspect of session work has helped me move sessions along as quickly as I do. 

AD: You’ve always been very clear in citing your influences, whether it’s Robert Wyatt or Fairport Convention and the Canterbury scene of the 1960s and ‘70s. Your music is so ingrained with that era, but I’d love to hear what new music you’ve enjoyed in the last while? 

Spencer Cullum: Oh, okay. I love Bitchin’ Bajas, they’re great. Richard Dawson, I love his new record and the song about gardening. He’s been pretty big on my list at the moment. Modern Nature is fantastic, I really enjoy them. My wife does the playlist for the shop, and she had Katy Kirby on there. I’ve been really enjoying her music. There’s a record I heard recently in the shop, and I was like, “Oh, this is amazing,” and it was called “Dancing In The Rain” by Meaningful Stone. It’s great.
