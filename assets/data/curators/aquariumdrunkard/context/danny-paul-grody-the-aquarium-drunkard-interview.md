## Body

Coming relatively hot on the heels of last year’s Arc Of Day, Danny Paul Grody’s latest LP picks up right where he left off. Arc Of Night(credited to the Danny Paul Grody Duo, thanks to drummer Rich Douthit’s invaluable contributions) sees the Bay Area-based guitarist heading into more nocturnal zones over the course of seven transformative instrumentals. These fully electrified pieces, naturally, have darker, more mysterious hues when compared with its sunlit predecessor; together, Grody and Douthit wander moonlit streets and trails, patiently exploring an uncanny landscape of the imagination. Like Arc Of Day, Arc Of Night is a total masterpiece, only bolstered by the guest spots from Chuck Johnson and Trevor Montgomery. Aquarium Drunkard hopped on Zoom with Danny to get shed a little light on this Night.  

Aquarium Drunkard: How did the 12-string guitar become such a big part of your sound? 

Danny Paul Grody: I can’t mention the 12-string without talking about Robbie Basho. Hearing him was where the seed was planted in my mind. But it’s so ubiquitous on so many records that I love; I don’t think I even realized the extent that the 12-string had seeped into my psyche early on. It all started to make sense when I had one in my hands—I suddenly knew where that sound that I loved was coming from, you know? I was getting bored with the six-string back then. I didn’t want to necessarily jump ship entirely, but I wanted to keep expanding my sound. I was already working with open tunings and things like that to keep it interesting. Finally, I picked up this Eagle 12-string that I ended up falling in with and to this day is the same guitar that I play and really enjoy. I lucked out with that one. It’s a very friendly guitar as far as 12-strings go. They can be pretty difficult to fret and obviously tuning is a huge issue. But I got this one from a local shop and I knew right off the bat that this was my path. Between Two Worlds came out in 2013 and that record was largely shaped by my burgeoning love affair with the 12-string. 

AD: Do you feel like the 12-string changed how you write songs?

Danny Paul Grody: I think so. Like a different tuning, it brings you somewhere else. It inspires a different approach, because there’s all of the octaves and harmonies that you can create. I still will write something on a six string and then play it on the 12-string and go back and forth. And it never ceases to amaze me how different it feels. It’s not in terms of bad or good, it’s just that [on the 12-string] the songs take on such a different characteristic. It’s a bigger sound, a much more lush and harmonic sound. The 12-string has become the go-to instrument for when I want things to feel more three-dimensional, almost like you’re inside the body of the instrument. 

AD: Let’s talk about Arc Of Day and Arc Of Night. Did you have these interrelated concepts in mind for the albums right from the start? 

Danny Paul Grody: Very rarely do I go into anything with a grand concept. That can put some pressure on you and there shouldn’t be pressure on the creative process. For these two records, it made sense of itself quite naturally. It also had a lot to do with reconnecting with Rich and that process of circling back together and playing music together after a long hiatus. When we first started playing again, it was just like — let’s get together. It wasn’t about making a record. It was in the thick of the pandemic, we were all going crazy. I was hungry to play music with somebody else, and I had committed to doing another record with Three Lobed. But it was all just a big experiment. I was playing around with a lot of stuff at home and playing with Rich and eventually I asked him if he’d like to like to develop it more and more. And he was very much into the idea. Still, it wasn’t formalized at all. We went into the studio, but it was all pretty new. There was no real plan other than this skeletal work. Let’s flesh it out and let’s invite people that we trust and love to be a part of that and see where we can go. 

By the end of it, we had the record and it felt very light and it elicited for us the feeling of daytime. Thematically, I was interested in that. At the same time, I was working with my friend Ian Albert, who’s a cinema photographer. We would go on these like little hiking outings, and he’d bring his camera. That was also a pandemic situation—just an excuse to get out and have human contact and appreciate this quiet place that was even quieter during that time. It was just like medicine, you know? So, we were doing a lot of like daytime hikes, and it just felt like so much of the material that I was doing with Ian was also feeding into what I was doing with Rich. It all felt very much connected to the daytime hours. That’s where the title Arc Of Day came from. 

But then there was a lot of material left over from those original sessions that we had grown attached to. We weren’t sure if we ready to just end it, so I started talking to Cory [Rayborn of Three Lobed Recordings] about helping us to release it in some form or another. He said, “You never release records close together, maybe we can think about doing a tighter cycle and having it be thematically connected.” That’s where the conversation with Rich emerged. We thought that this other material had a much darker tone; it was all electric. It was kind of a no-brainer to build off of that DNA. We were both interested in time and our relationship to it—especially in the last few years, where it became so warped and strange. So, we focused on the nighttime and whatever that evokes. 

AD: I know that Indian classical music has “day” and “night” modes. Can you put into words the difference you hear between “day” and “night music”? 

Danny Paul Grody: From my early days of record collecting—or even before that—I’ve always had music set aside for evening listening. My interests in music may have changed but my relationship to it has always been associated with those times of day. You can attribute it to Indian classical music, but I don’t think it’s exclusive to that. I think it’s just the nature of being human. We orient ourselves around light and our circadian rhythms attach themselves to all sorts of things. Our energy waxes and wanes throughout the day. I don’t have a big philosophical meaning behind it, other than that it feels very natural to listen to music that has a certain quality that lines up with the periods of the day. I remember way back there was one Labradford record that was always my evening record. “He Loved Him Madly”—that’s never on in the morning. For me, anyway. It’s subjective. There’s no one-size-fits-all thing. 

AD: I love that there’s kind of a melodic motif that wafts through the two records. Is there any significance to it, or was that something you just kept returning to? 

Danny Paul Grody: For Rich and me, it came from a place of curiosity. It’s almost like when I pick up a six string and write a melody, I’m also drawn to seeing what that sounds like on the 12-string, right? So, when we were writing a lot of this material, especially towards the latter stages of being ready to record Arc Of Day, we started play around with amplified guitar and different settings for these pieces. When we go into the studio, Rich and I always try to be intentional about setting aside time to have unplanned recording. Some of it is very planned, so that we’re using our time wisely and being mindful of, you know, the energy of the engineer and all of that. We had very specific songs we wanted to record and then we had “versions” of those songs. So, “Path to Olema” [on Arc Of Day] and “Coyote Valley At Dusk” [on Arc Of Night] are examples of versions that are based on a thematic intro, essentially. Those were fully fleshed out pieces when we went into the studio for Arc Of Day and we knew for sure we wanted to record both of them. And by the end, those two songs became sort of what everything was based around in terms of making sense of how we sequenced Arc Of Day and Arc Of Night. “Coyote Valley” became the centerpiece of this new record. And it’s just funny how that worked out. But we always like play out an idea acoustically and play out an idea with amplified guitar. And they really do take on a different tone, you know? 

AD: I love how there are those melodic echoes happening across both records. 

Danny Paul Grody: It’s cool to know you picked up on that. Some of it is unintentional. Like no matter what I do, some of those echoes are going to come through, these little melodic turns. As I get older, I’m a lot less self-conscious about embracing that as part of the process. In the end, isn’t that what folk music is? It’s all just recycling through different lenses and players and cultures even. 

AD: The new record is credited to the Danny Paul Grody Duo. We’ve mentioned Rich a bit, but can you talk about the decision to give this collaboration a little more of a foundation in that way? 

Danny Paul Grody: It became more and more evident to us that we needed to make a distinction of some kind to really honor the collaboration and. After Arc Of Day, we just started talking more and more about our creative relationship and how to make sense of it. We knew that we were going to be doing this follow-up record and one of the considerations was whether or not we wanted to make it as fully fleshed out in terms of additional players or make it a strict “duo” record, and how necessary it was to be super purist or dogmatic about it all. But it all boiled down to the fact that Arc Of Day led us to this point—like, this is what we’re doing. It contains a lot of the DNA that he and I shared in the past. And as we were talking, I could tell that it was very meaningful for him to be acknowledged in some way. So, we deliberated and finally came to calling it the Danny Paul Grody Duo—after like a billion ideas [laughs], It almost became a little absurd, but in the end that worked for us, and hopefully it’s self-explanatory for everyone. 

AD: You’ve been working with Cory at Three Lobed for a while, right? 

Danny Paul Grody: Yeah. Much to Cory’s chagrin, I have to give him a shoutout. This music doesn’t just happen. It’s because people support me. I have to say that I so appreciate the home that I have at Three Lobed. I don’t take it for granted one bit. 

Arc of Night by Danny Paul Grody Duo

AD: The best in the biz! So, the last two songs on Night take up the entirety of side two. Is it a challenge to get into the right frame of mind to play that kind of long, durational music? 

Danny Paul Grody: It’s not a challenge. It feels like a natural state for me at this point. I’ve been playing longer instrumental music in various settings for a while now and it’s like my home. The opposite is almost true—it can be hard for me to play short things. I challenged myself to do that with the Furniture Music releases. How can I make something short and sweet? The longer form feels easy for me to sink into. I don’t want to say it’s selfish, but I like to live there. It feels good to be in that space for an extended period, to let it sprawl out. And fortunately, Rich is down for the ride. You know, when we’re recording, I try not to drink too much caffeine. I do have to think about the ritual a bit. But it’s like a meditation that I can’t wait to give myself. In the studio, it’s a little more structured, of course. You have to do your best to get rid of all the noise in your head. Jay Pellicci and his brother Ian are so amazing in the way that they offer you a setting that is really unique and special, though. For the most part, it’s my own shit that I have to deal with. 

AD: Judging from your song titles—“Hawk Hill,” “Cloud Forest,” “Coyote Valley”—the natural world seems like it’s a big inspiration. Are those songs meant to evoke those places or is it not all that specific? 

Danny Paul Grody: It’s not that specific, really. But nature is definitely a muse for me. It’s where I go to take care of myself. It nourishes me. We live in a place where we can go to such beautiful places. It’s never lost on me how lucky we are—even being a parent and having access to parks, in addition to this huge city that’s so crazy and layered. Having access to nature has always been essential for my mental health and my creative process. It’s always something I refer back to — not always literally, but more of a feeling, a state of mind. A place will start to map itself onto whatever feeling I might have, and it can just naturally seep into a song. 

I’ve been enjoying going to Hawk Hill for the last couple years. It sits high and has an amazing view of the mouth of the Bay. You can go above the marine layer, so you’re able to catch a sunset on a typical foggy day out here. It’s really raw—like you might get blown over by the wind sometimes. Because I’ve been going there a lot, I have a very personal relationship with it, and as the music started to take form, that seeped in. Rich, similarly—he’s been a surfer his whole life. He’s always out on Ocean Beach, which, as far as conditions go, can be pretty intense. It’s not for the faint of heart. But his devotion to the practice of surfing—without being cliched—is very spiritual, I think. He spends a lot of time in the water. It’s a unique relationship, but there’s a similar thing we have there. When we’re talking titles, it’s always like “Where have you been going?” 

AD: How did you end up in San Francisco — you’ve been out there a long time, right? 

Danny Paul Grody: Yeah, I’ve lived here since 1995. I need to get the hell out of San Diego, which is where I grew up. I need to come out and I did not want to do it in San Diego. I didn’t even know what the hell was in store for me, but I had visited San Francisco a few times. I grew up skateboarding. But San Diego—it’s suburban sprawl and homogeneously white, conservative, socially conservative and skateboarding was kind of an escape. Music, too. I grew up with all the guys who ran Gravity Records, so I had access to a built-in community of weirdos, kids who were trying to push back against the mold. And the more I was exposed to bands like Three Mile Pilot and Pinback, my mind was just blown. I knew I needed to be somewhere bigger. And I knew that skateboarding was associated with San Francisco, like at the Embarcadero, all these different spots here. I was already fascinated by the idea of San Francisco having watched all of these skate videos. So that was weirdly part of it.  

But getting away from San Diego, even if it was not that far, was essential because I needed a safe place to come to terms with my sexuality. I had plans to go to college, but the truth is I wasn’t ready in any way, shape or form. I moved there in 95 and pretty soon I met Jeff Cantu and we started Tarentel. We were really fortunate. We started touring and making records pretty early on. It was an amazing whirlwind of transformation, identity-wise and otherwise. And then, after the glamour and interest in touring all the time faded, I was like, “Ohhhh, what’s next?” And then I went back to school, and I got my undergrad and then I got my masters. It was just an 11-year gap [laughs].  

AD: You’re a parent now. Has that changed your relationship with music? 

Danny Paul Grody: Absolutely. I changed so fundamentally when I became a parent. It impacts everything, every aspect of my life. You know, it was one of the greatest gifts I could ask for, just to take my focus off of myself. I didn’t realize what a gift it would be, but it’s taught me a lot. But it’s also taught me how to really cherish those things in my life that matter, that bring meaning, that bring me a sense of wholeness. Music is one of those things, obviously. I wasn’t going to be the kind of new parent who says, “Oh, OK, I’m not a musician anymore. I don’t have time for that.” I’m proud of it. I want my kids to see music in a positive light. I want them to see their Papa flourishing in some way and be a model for that. I’m lucky to be with a partner who supports that. He’s a trained musician, so he wants the same thing. We believe that it’s a positive thing, to be creative, to be someone that has a dedicated practice that feels good to them and is, in some ways, kind of insatiable. 

AD: And something that exists outside of the bounds of day-to-day capitalism. 

Danny Paul Grody: Exactly. That’s huge for kids to be able to see the options. Life is not just about getting your homework done and getting good grades, going to college and yada yada yada. From us, they need to see that that there’s a lot that that life contains. It doesn’t matter what it is that gets their heart going, but there should be something that does it. You’re taking care of yourself; you’re doing something that you absolutely love that brings you so much joy and you come home feeling that. That can only be good. You can only be a better person because of that. And your kids are going to feel that too, you know?
