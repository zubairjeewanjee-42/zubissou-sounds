## Body

Bill Orcutt’s existence hinges on precision. This might be a confusing statement, given that his musical history began with Harry Pussy, a ‘90s noise project that feels like the antithesis of that, all clattering of cymbals, spoken word poetry, and fuzzy guitar ringing out into oblivion. Look deeper, though, and you’ll realize that even then, each barbed note coalesced together into a distinct and meticulous structure.

With the band’s dissolution in 1997, he moved to the other side of the country, putting down the guitar in favor of 100-hour workweeks at SF tech companies. But, to our surprise and immense admiration, he picked his signature four-string again in the early 2010s: this time, with an angularity reminiscent of Tom Verlaine or James Blood Ulmer. In the decade since, he’s expanded past genre, throwing blues or jazz or noise into the experimental blender that is his distinct guitar playing. Whether the jagged notes jutting out of his Telecaster, the algorithmic waves made in his open-source synth program, or his layered compositions with the Bill Orcutt Guitar Quartet, he continues to subvert expectations time and time again. Ahead of his live release , Four Guitars Live out March 22 with the aforementioned group, we sat down with Orcutt, talking about Steve Reich and Phill Niblock, improvisation, and using algorithms to find songs to cover. | b arthur

Aquarium Drunkard: What sort of things come to mind when you think about growing up in Miami?

Bill Orcutt: So, in my family, there were no musicians; we didn’t even have records in the house. So I didn’t really think about music or becoming a musician or any of that. I was into painting. And then, at some point, my parents — for no reason, I didn’t ask for it — got me a record player. Then I started buying records and then suddenly became a big music fan. I was probably about 15. I was in high school.

AD: Do you remember the first record you bought? How did you find new stuff?

Bill Orcutt: It was Muddy Waters, I’m Ready. I just started reading reviews to figure out what to buy. So I wasn’t just into the blues. I was also into the Ramones and anything that was getting good reviews back then. And then if you’re listening to the blues and you listen to The Ramones, the reviewer is probably gonna mention John Coltrane. So yeah, it all kind of happened at the same time. 

AD: And then you went off to college?

Bill Orcutt: Yeah, I tried to start a band in Gainesville that was kind of oriented around jazz and experimental music. We jammed a lot, but we never it never quite rose to the level of becoming a band.

AD: What did you study?

Bill Orcutt: I was an English major. My thing was 18th-century literature…Swift and stuff like that. I got my MA, and I taught as a lecturer and adjunct faculty for a while. And then I wound up getting involved in film and making some films and then became known to people in Miami as a film guy. 

AD: And Harry Pussy started around then?

Bill Orcutt: Yeah, I got back and started graduate school back in Miami. I met some people who were doing music and it was really weird and unlike anything else in Miami. They already had a guitar player, Mark Feehan. So I got a drum set and just became a drummer so that I could hang out with these people who I liked.

I wasn’t a very good drummer. [Laughs] I wasn’t a very good guitar player either. But while I was playing with them, as a drummer, I kind of, you know, in my ass-backward way, started writing songs for the four-string guitar. And then another guy was a bass player, but he was a much better fake drummer than I was. So he was like, “We should start a duo and I’ll play drums, and you can do your four-string guitar.” 

AD: Talk to me about the last send off you did with Harry Pussy: this stretched out, fucked up scream of your then-wife and bandmate, Adris Hoyos.

Bill Orcutt: When I was working at the cinema, we had multiple computers that I’d just play around with. And I loved electronic music. So there was some Steve Reich conceptual piece that I had read about where he takes some sound and stretches it. And essentially, Let’s Build a Pussy is a implementation of that Reich piece. I noticed that Sound Edit 16 — this software that doesn’t exist anymore for the old Macs — had a SAS stretch function and I could get that same sound. It was painstakingly done because it would stretch at a certain percentage, so it had to be run over and over and over again to get the longest piece. Because the way the record works is, you hear the original sound and you hear a double. Then it launches into this thing that stretches it over the four LP sides. Yeah, days and days to get the whatever piece of shit Mac we had to stretch the sound out to the right length.

AD: Did you know about Phill Niiblock at the time? Because when you talk about that stretching effect, that’s where my mind jumps to.

Bill Orcutt: Actually yeah. At this theater where I worked, there was this corridor to get to the entrance. And this building that we were in was owned by this wealthy patron of the arts. Apparently, Phill had made some connection and installed a sound installation in this corridor. So I used to hear his music every day I had to work. I remember my roommate had just come back from India, and Phill had these giant handpainted Indian movie posters that we put up in the gallery. And my roommate came in and he was like, “this is the first thing you’ve ever showed that doesn’t suck.” I was like, “okay, cool, man.” I mean, he’s right. There was a lot of trash in the gallery. [Laughs]

AD: Ok, so the band dissolves in ‘97. And you get out of music for, like, a relatively long time. How was that transition? How did it work for you going into the real world in a way?

Bill Orcutt: You know, it wasn’t just leaving music because I still had a job running a non-profit in the art world. So I was really outside of the real world in terms of how I made a living. And then moving to San Francisco and getting what was my first job at age 35, where you had to go and sit at a desk every day. It was the same desk. It was really like high school was the only reference I had. I was like, wow, people have been doing this the whole time. But on the other hand, I made real money and I had real benefits and worked a lot. And also it was like, people didn’t care who I was — not that I had any kind of fame or anything in Miami, at least I had an identity. When you start going into an office, I was just the old guy, you know? I had no identity beyond, you know, the drone at in Sector C3.

AD: And then you get back into music with A New Way To Pay Old Debts, a vastly different venture than your noise stuff with Harry Pussy. Talk to me about that shift; why move to acoustic?

Bill Orcutt: Yeah, I just started with the acoustic because that was the easiest thing to do. I had kids at home. I had neighbors. I was living in an apartment; I wasn’t going to play electric.

AD: Whether right or wrong, people like to associate you with the “Great American Songbook.” Do you ever think about history when you play?

Bill Orcutt: Like I said, I made A New Way To Pay Old Debts and I was looking for some direction to take the my solo guitar thing. When it’s just you on stage, the options are limited. So that’s where the idea came up with playing other people’s songs. And then you have to select the songs. And I’m not going to do like, Neil Young. And that’s actually what led to the idea of thinking about it historically and trying to do a history of music. People say the Great American Songbook, I don’t think I do that. It’s lazy, you know, people tend to write in cliches.

I actually did this weird analysis to come up with a list of songs to do. Somebody created an index of every song title that had been mentioned in American literature and indexed them by frequency. So I went to that list and started pulling songs out to create a list of things to work on. They wasn’t necessarily the best songs or the most popular; well, they weren’t popular in the sense that they’re popular with listeners, but they were popular in the American imagination. So it wasn’t a traditional, “playing the standards” kind of a thing that people might think if if someone said, “oh, he plays the Great American Songbook…” and a lot of the songs I played, were just not that at all. In fact, they were kind of terrible. But I was more interested in where they sat in the American imagination rather than whether they were “good” songs or “worthy” somehow.

AD: Where does the impulse to create come from for you? How did Music For Four Guitars come about?

Bill Orcutt: I don’t know. I know that it’s something I have to do. When I was working so much and didn’t have time to make stuff, it’s like something I need, and it doesn’t have to be music. It could be anything really. I have to be working on something that I’m self-directing. It’s not enough to do work for other people.

I had been trying for years to do a four guitar piece because somebody had asked me to, and it just stuck in my head as a kind of challenge. I had been trying and failing and trying and failing and trying and failing. And then eventually, I figured it out. It was like every other thing that I worked on: in my office, sitting in front of the computer. And just incrementally, it came together, once you have the process, it’s just a matter of piling on and following that. It was interesting: I had no idea what it was going to produce. Usually I have some sense of what it is I’m making while I’m working on it. But in the case of Four Guitars, I really just had the process. And I didn’t know what the process was going to produce. I had to ask myself, ‘Is this what Bill Orcutt sounds like?’ It worked out in the end, though, and people seem to like it, which was surprising. I kind of thought this is going to be some oddball thing that I do to satisfy myself, but it actually worked out for me.

AD: The live album was recorded in Utrecht and Copenhagen — how was touring over there?

Bill Orcutt: It was good. You know, there were so many things that could go wrong, between COVID and flights almost every day. But everything worked out. I was so amazed and grateful. Towards the end of the tour, I dumped half a bottle of orange juice on my laptop, which eventually died, but other than that, nothing went wrong. I was very happy. 

AD: Do you remember what you were listening to? Or reading? 

Bill Orcutt: Yeah, I brought a book, but I don’t think I actually read it at all. I have trouble relaxing on airplanes. And I never bring headphones — I don’t like the headphone experience. I always feel like I’m cut off from the world. I’ll just watch movies that I already know without sound. Because I already know the dialogue.

AD: You mentioned that you viewed Four Guitars as a challenge that you need to complete… what’s the next challenge for you?

Bill Orcutt: It’s all a challenge. I’m working on something right now: a computer music piece and it’s a puzzle again. I had like three false starts — I can’t remember how many false starts I had — but, you know, I have an approach. And then I’m like, “Oh, my God, this sucks. Throw it out.” So I have a process, but it’s still open. You’re making something that’s truly new, instead of like, if I just sit down and start improvising with a guitar, that’s not so much a challenge, you know, I have a specific thing that I’m trying to do. The question of whether or not it’s really going to fly is still unanswered, but I’m working. It’s always a challenge.
