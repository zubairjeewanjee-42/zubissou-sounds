## Body

At the close of the 1960s, Eddie Harris had earned himself carte blanche at Atlantic records. Miles Davis immortalized Harris’ composition, “Freedom Jazz Dance,” on 1967’s Miles Smiles, his early adoption of electronics on LPs like The Electrifying Eddie Harris and Plug Me In put him firmly atop the jazz and R&B charts, and an unrehearsed collab with Les McCann at the 1969 Montreux Jazz Fest, Swiss Movement, moved millions.

For Harris, the artistic freedom couldn’t have arrived at a better time. Ever ahead of the curve, his use of the electric piano and amplified horn beginning in the mid 60s had flowered into full-blown tape delay drenched psychedelia by 1968’s Silver Cycles. Though, while Miles, McCann and even Harris’ own bassist and compositional partner, Melvin Jackson, quickly indulged in album-length headiness (see McCann’s Invitation to Openness, and Layers, or Jackson’s Funky Skull for proof), Harris’ transition began timidly – balancing his typically left-of-center post bop with a whiff or two of stoned freakiness within every LP.

By the mid-70s, that eccentricity went full tilt with Harris’ joyous experimentation spread across both sides of the record. 1974’s Is It In was a freak-fest of drum machines, octave divided horns, chanted vocals and deep, repetitive grooves. A Prince joint that pre-empted the Purple One by nearly half a decade. I Need Some Money doubled down, introduced Harris as a vocalist, and picked up where Sly’s Fresh and Small Talk left off – tighter arrangements, stranger sounds, deeper zones. As he told Soul Train’s Don Cornelius in 1975, “I’m indulging into the vocalizations nowadays because everyone else seems to think they can sing, so I might as well join em.”

1975’s Bad Luck Is All I Have is the masterwork. Expertly performed on an arsenal of hybrid instruments (the guitorgan, Fender’s novel bass VI, brass horns fitted with reed mouthpieces), its six tracks are planets from the furthest corners of Harris’ strange universe – its sun, the gorgeous “Why Must We Part”, with slowly building, endlessly layered vocal harmonies, string synth and delicate, subtly mind-blowing bass.

Middling sales be damned, Harris recorded a few more discs for Atlantic, including two sides of raunchy stage banter collected as The Reason Why I’m Talking S–t, and while they included moments of brilliance, none burned as hot as his previous work.

This mixtape collects two and a half hours of the most adventurous moments from those heady Atlantic days. Sequenced chronologically, building slowly, flowering and flaming out, it mirrors the fascinating journey of one of the music’s most misunderstood eccentrics. | j erwin
