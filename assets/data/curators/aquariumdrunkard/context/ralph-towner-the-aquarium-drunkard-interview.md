# Ralph Towner :: The Aquarium Drunkard Interview

**Tags:** ECM, Ralph Towner, Mixtapes

## Body

Ralph Towner has had the sort of career that you can’t fit into a 90-minute interview. Born in 1940, he developed his musical talent early, starting off on trumpet at the age of 6 and learning to improvise, thanks to jazz records that belonged to his older brothers. “I played in a lot of city bands, municipal bands, they were called, from the time I was 14 or 15, playing with older musicians,” Towner recalls. He then took up piano, and later studied classical guitar with Karl Scheit in Vienna. He moved to New York City in 1968, where he quickly fell in with a cadre of adventurous players who were busy reinventing jazz at the height of the rock ‘n’ roll era. His career took off when he joined the Paul Winter Consort, one of the first bands to blend chamber music, jazz, folk and a potpourri of international styles, injected with a healthy dose of hippie mysticism that eventually veered into a New Age hybrid. (Towner recorded what might be his only vocal number, “The Silence of a Candle,” for the Consort’s 1972 classic Icarus.)

Towner and three members of the Consort – his University of Oregon buddy Glen Moore, percussionist Collin Walcott and woodwind player Paul McCandless — broke off from Winter’s group to form Oregon, which fused classical composition, jazz improvisation and elements of Indian and Brazilian music into a deceptively lyrical and mellow sound that was at its core adventurous and experimental. Vanguard released Oregon’s debut, Music of Another Present Era in 1972, and the band went on to release classics for that label (1974’s Winter Light and 1979’s Moon and Mind, as well as two jaw-dropping live albums), Elektra (1978’s Out of the Woods and 1979’s Roots in the Sky) and ECM (1984’s Crossing). Walcott died in a traffic accident in 1984, after which the band took a year off before deciding to reform, first as a trio and then with Trilok Gurtu and later with Mark Walker. Moore retired in 2015, with Paolino Dalla Porta replacing him on what, according to Towner, may well be Oregon’s final album, Lantern, released in 2017 on the CAM Jazz label, capping a remarkable near-half-century of musical activity.

Somehow, in between all this, Towner embarked on a career as a leader under his own name and as a solo artist, duo partner and occasional featured side man. He accompanied himself on piano and guitar via overdubs for his 1974 ECM solo debut, Diary, and worked with a crew of European heavyweights (saxophonist Jan Garbarek and drummer Jon Christensen of Keith Jarrett’s European Quartet, and charismatic electric bassist Eberhard Weber) for 1975’s Solstice, an album that did much to establish the lush yet wintry signature ECM sound. Solo Concert, from 1980, showcases Towner’s formidable technical skill and commanding presence, two facets of his talent often overshadowed by his compositional prowess and his work as a collaborator. Last year’s At First Light featured several powerful and surprising new tunes, revisits of old Oregon work and a few standards that revealed some intriguing connections to Towner’s oeuvre. In between all this, Towner cut several albums in a duo setting with vibist and fusion pioneer Gary Burton and electric guitarist John Abercrombie, and has worked with a bevy of greats, including but not limited to Weather Report, Jarrett, Kenny Wheeler, Larry Coryell, Charlie Haden, Egberto Gismonti, Elvin Jones, Azimuth, Wolfgang Muthspiel and Bill Bruford. He even cut several tracks of extremely chill library music for the First.com label (1995’s Atmospheres and Impressions). Talking from his home in Rome, where he’s recovering from a recent injury, Towner was voluble and in good spirits, happy to reminisce about the highlights of his incredible career and talk about his love of music. (A late-life discovery of Led Zeppelin, particularly Robert Plant, has Towner particularly excited.) Though the usual infirmities of age have made recording tougher, Towner remains busy. “I’m writing a lot for other people,” he says, including British classical guitarist and popular YouTuber Alexandra Whittingham, who has played several of his other compositions online. “Finally, I’ve started to break into the classical world a little bit,” Towner says, “Which I’m really happy about.” At 84, the maestro is still blazing new trails. | r jackson

Ralph Towner: Well, I fell madly in love on tour about what — 30 years ago with an Italian woman in Palermo, who was an actress, a wonderful person — anyway. But that was the love of my life, and since she was an actress, and had a whole career here, which I also already did anyway, I mean, with all the traveling and concert, touring I was doing. So, rather than have her move to the States, when all my work was already mostly in Europe and around the world, I moved to Palermo, I kept the house in Seattle for a while … someone house sat. That ended up being a good investment.

AD: Seattle, really! I didn’t know you’d lived there.

Ralph Towner: Seattle had a really nice jazz scene going on, and that was really good for me. I ended up working really a lot as a piano player, in my Bill Evans piano period. I got good enough to survive when I finally moved to New York in ’68, I guess, and I survived mainly as a piano player.

AD: Bill Evans was such a huge influence on the ECM cohort of guitar players. Did you ever meet him?

Ralph Towner: Bill Evans was my model, and I got to know him. Unfortunately, he never played any of my pieces. Anyway, Oregon had a concert in Boston. (John) McLaughlin was scheduled to be our opening act, playing solo, and bowed out, something happened and he couldn’t do it. So, the people at Berklee called Bill Evans, who was at a racetrack. He loved to play the ponies or whatever.

AD: He was a sporting man.

Ralph Towner: Yeah, so here we are doing a soundcheck. One of Oregon’s  complicated sound checks with lots of open microphones and, and so I’m sitting at the piano or trying to get a sound and in rushes Bill Evans, who says, “Ralph, Ralph listen to this!” He sits down at the piano and scoots me over and starts playing this unbelievable, totally, almost atonal places, really out, free … anyway, it was just mind blowing, and I’m sitting there and then finally I have to sort of clear my throat and say, “Well, Bill, we’ve got to finish our soundcheck.” So here I’m kicking Bill Evans off the piano. Now, if you told me that would have happened, I would never believe such a thing, you know, before I moved to New York. Anyway, that’s the kind of stuff that was going down in the ‘70s, right? You’re hearing and playing with people that you never would have imagined. It was a dream world for music at that time.

AD: It’s hard to imagine. So, I know you met [Oregon bassist] Glen Moore in Oregon.

Ralph Towner: Yeah, we were college students together. That’s where we met.

AD: So, when you moved to Vienna and then you went to New York, did you and Glen meet up in New York or did you sort of plan to go together?

Ralph Towner: He moved to New York and so I did too, about a year later. I moved there in ‘68 and it was almost like a small town, and I would slowly get gigs as a piano player just because of rumors. Like one piano player would have a gig and they’d maybe get two bookings, and they would ask “We need another piano player, who can I call?” And the other musicians would say, “Oh, there’s this new guy in town, he’s very good,” and it was me, you know, and I kept playing with better and better people and it was kind of a measuring stick to check on how well you were playing by how good the people you’re playing with. Some of them became pretty famous!

AD: Can you talk more about what the jazz scene was like in New York at that time?

Ralph Towner: We all did jam sessions, and it was all that big, wonderful ‘70s thing, where everybody’s writing music in oddball rhythms and time signatures, with influences from Indian music and a lot of other music. Brazilian was strong with me. We all played together, and we’d have jam sessions to work on new pieces. I remember we would go over to [Miles Davis bassist] Dave Holland’s apartment and rehearse there. I remember one year — so we had Mike Brecker playing sax, I was playing the piano, and we were working on one of my piano pieces. a new one. Then there was the knock on the door. And here enters Dave’s apartment this man, very well-dressed in a nice wool suit and carrying a guitar and a tiny little amp, and he walked right in and sat the amp down and plugged in and he started reading over my shoulder this piece of mine. It was John McLaughlin! He’d only been in New York for like a day or two. So, a lot of things like that happened. I mean, Jaco Pastorius. The first time he came to New York, he made a whole habit of going around to different musicians that he liked or admired, and bang on the door, which you’re not supposed to do in New York City, you know, so I open the door and here’s this big guy. Immediately he sticks his hand out with this piece of music, he says, “I wrote this for you,” and he probably handed it to everybody, but it was impossible to play. I mean, he could play it. All those amazing harmonics that he would do. But he barged in the door, and he says, “I’m the greatest bass player in the world. And this is my first record.” And he plunked his record onto my turntable.

AD: Wow.

Ralph Towner: I’ve got a million stories. Playing in the middle of the night, getting calls from George Braith, a saxophone player who played a lot like Roland Kirk. He had a jazz club, sort of a loft, an underground loft, and everybody would go there and play and have jam sessions. But it was like midnight, and so my phone rings, and it’s George, calling to say, “Hey, nobody’s here, except Sonny Rollins, and he wants to play with somebody. Can you come down?” So, I went down in the middle of the night, and played all night with Sonny Rollins, on the guitar and piano. It was amazing. He could quote about six standards in the shortest amount of time you’d ever heard. That’s just the kind of thing that was going on, you know.

AD: And this is the environment that Oregon came out of?

Ralph Towner: Oregon was really a New York group. We got together and sort of started our early rehearsals in the Meatpacking District. And we did little gigs and put on a lot of free concerts in Manhattan, and we kind of had developed a following playing in this thing called the free music store, which, every weekend they had a concert in a church on 92nd Street. It was a great opportunity. It wasn’t a functioning church anymore, it was a radio station, WBAI, and we would go and play. We would go around midnight and play all night until like seven in the morning and we would do it without stopping. So, while we kept the music constantly going, and then one or two people go out and get some doughnuts or something. But the other two people were played duet, and so we tried all kinds of things and did a lot of free music, and we built a little bit of a following there in New York City. It slowly grew, and we slowly got more gigs. It was a wonderful scene.

AD: Did you meet Jim Pepper during this time?

Ralph Towner: He and Glen knew each other from high school, practically. Collin worked a lot with him, and we were friends.

AD: I was listening to your version of “Witchi-Tai-To” the other day. That’s such a classic.

Ralph Towner: Yeah, it is. We played it to death, I think. People are still playing it, though.

AD: It’s still being covered today by new bands, which I love.

Ralph Towner: Yeah, the thing was nice about it is that it’s sort of six bars of changes that rotate over the same melody, but the melody is the phrase in a normal eight bars. So, it works out that way somehow. You keep playing this cycle of changes that’s a little bit shorter but somehow keeps fitting. But that adds a lot to the harmony. Since the original is originally an Indian chant. Rain, I think it was a rain dance or something.

AD: There’s a water spirit in there, at least. And you also played with Tim Hardin, right?

Ralph Towner: Yeah, that was kind of a gig! Glen Moore had gotten some gigs playing bass with Hardin, who hired a lot of jazz players. Tim had this gig in upstate New York. It sounded to me like it was some big folk gig, and he needed a piano player, and so I agreed to play piano with him. And I gotta say, I wasn’t that impressed with him.

AD: He was kind of hitting the skids at that point, right?

Ralph Towner: Yeah, he was, definitely, but he still had money he was blowing at the time. Anyway, what I thought was a simple folk festival — it was Woodstock. Most of the artists had to be picked up in a field and flown in via helicopter. So, here Glen and I are in this helicopter, wondering what the hell is going on. We got over the site and looked down, and we could see in one glimpse 400,000 people! The crowd and the vibe in the crowd that was the biggest thing. I mean, the music was interesting. Then the rain came, and we couldn’t get out for a day and there was no place to sleep. I think Glen found a place to sleep on some plastic pipes piled up or something, and I crawled around hunting for a tent that didn’t have somebody who was screwing in it.

AD: That must have been tough!

Ralph Towner: It was! I finally got one. So, I managed to at least get some sleep and then we came running out the next day, because we had a gig the next day in New York City, and we had to make that gig.

AD: So, this gig the next day, how many people were in attendance? You went from like 400,000 people to what?

Ralph Towner: Yeah, not many! I remember [at Woodstock], they made periodic announcements about the size of the crowd, and it finally go got to the point where they said, “We are now the third largest city in the United States!” It really was unbelievable. Everyone was stoned. This band that Tim put together, it was not very good, and so we played, but I could tell, boy, it didn’t even matter. It wasn’t like playing for a big audience. It was just this sea of people, and you might as well have been a street musician or something. The music was good though. I mean, I was a jazz snob at that point, I hadn’t even heard The Beatles!

AD: It was your life; you were allowed to be snobs about jazz. Did you ever listen to John Fahey at all?

Ralph Towner: No, he was one of those people that I was too busy being a jazz snob at the time to listen to. I think I may have realized that he maybe had his foot in the door when it comes to having influences that were a little more than folk. But I don’t think I ever heard him.

AD: Really! He had a whole Dixieland thing, he was into jazz and classical, and he was really interested in Indian music, too.

Ralph Towner: I’m going to look him up on YouTube. That’s one thing about the internet, you can find anybody playing anything now. If I hear a name, I look them up and see what they sound like. It’s wonderful.

AD: So, back to jazz. When Oregon was really getting going, was there a precedent for what you were doing, even with all this intermingling of styles and cross-pollination?

Ralph Towner: We had a special thing. I don’t think it’s really been quite equaled. I mean, because we were experienced as classical musicians. I’d studied, and of course Paul McCandless, one of the best oboe players in the world, period. He’s such a great player. So here we have our lead instrument, it’s this beautiful – oh, and plus he played great bass clarinet and sax. So, we were able to use a lot of different instruments. I played piano and the guitars, and then I took up the synthesizer, when they came out — probably the Prophet Five with keys — and you had to program it all yourself and I’d come up with some nice sounds, I thought, and just dialing around until I got something I liked and then saved it. And of course, there’s Glen on acoustic bass and Collin with his tablas. He’d studied and gotten his degree as a classical percussionist, and so Collin could play and compose and conduct, and also had studied and been on the road with Alla Rakha and Ravi Shankar for years as the road manager. So, he would study the sitar and the tablas on the road in hotels with Ravi and Alla Rakha. That was our background, our experience with music. The thing is, we didn’t try to play Indian music, but we had enough of the spirit that somehow it got included in the way we played.

AD: Oregon really covered a lot of ground while always sounding like Oregon.

Ralph Towner: We had tons of instruments we were dragging around. I would play an organ when we were doing our free pieces, and I also picked up the French horn. I played all the brass instruments, and Paul played every wind instrument he could find. Glen would find old beaten-up violins or violas and scratch away on those. Collin would try to play trombone, and he would get an awful sound.

AD: Really!

Ralph Towner: We would try anything. We’d play anything, and we’d do all these totally free — really free-form stuff all the time. It got pretty sophisticated, the kinds of things we were doing.

AD: Are there any tapes of this stuff lying around somewhere?

Ralph Towner: Yeah, there’s a lot. WBAI made some, I think. Maybe they have some stowed away of our concerts.

AD: That would be amazing.

Ralph Towner: We played pretty much what you hear on the early records. But we kept being involved with totally improvised sections when we played concerts. We would always do something completely free. And we always improvised within the tunes, too. Different methods of improvisation, ways of improvising, that’s one thing we got from our studies and our work with other musicians. Somehow, it all fit together. We were good friends – you had to be. Somehow, the sum of the parts was really incredible.

AD: There are craters on the moon that are named after your compositions.

Ralph Towner: They were named by these astronauts, one of them was the brother-in-law of the cello player in the Paul Winter Consort [David Darling]. He gave one of the astronauts in Houston the cassette. And so, the guys took that cassette and played it on the way to the moon. People loved that record. When NASA was coming up with names for the craters of the moon, they picked two of my tunes, “Icarus” and “Ghost Beads.” And then they sent me a map, and like an idiot, I lost it. I left it in a drawer in my apartment in New York City someplace. It was autographed, and I lost it! Fuck, what a drag! But anyway, I think they changed all the names of the craters later.

AD: Yeah, I think they changed all the names to scientists or something.

Ralph Towner: It was too… it wasn’t scientific enough.

AD: But the real heads know. We know!

Ralph Towner: Right! Much later, I met some of these guys, and I realized how much they had to do. Some of them were really completely emotionally changed from being able to blot out the Earth with your thumbnail in outer space, just this tiny little blue thing … it really puts things in perspective.

AD: When you met the astronauts, was it an official thing, because of the crater names?

Ralph Towner: No, nothing ever official. They might have come to a concert, because, Oregon, we played every state in the union, and I know we played in Houston. I remember we played in Houston once at a club where Dolly Parton had played the night before, and Dolly came up. It was amazing, the connections you could make, just by being a musician and playing and traveling.

AD: Speaking of Paul Winter, he’s still playing solstice concerts.

Ralph Towner: Yeah, yeah. One of his influences was the circus, and so he became a great self-promoter. When the three of us joined that band – Collin, Glen and I, we were all hired at the same time – we were playing mainly arrangements or covers of different people’s tunes like Paul Simon and some Bach things and Baroque things. Winter had this thing of playing a free piece, but he would have all the lights turned out, almost like he wouldn’t let anyone to see us creating this out set. But we grew kind of impatient with that, and then once I started writing, it became obvious what the four of us could do, something more adventurous and personal. The four of us had really bonded, and we could hear what we could do.

AD: And then, of course, you have your own solo career, which has a heavier classical guitar influence.

Ralph Towner: One of the arts of playing in classical guitar is making it sustain and make it sound even more sustained than it really is. it’s really an art, and it’s very orchestral in that way, because you’re paying attention to dynamics. I would tune all 12 strings to different pitches, have not only just unisons or octaves. I try to make every note have a reason for existing. It’s really fun to put that all together. It’s magical but difficult.

AD: It’s interesting that you did so much work with a band and then have an entirely different solo career that’s equally as distinctive.

Ralph Towner: That was one of the initial things that we made clear to each other when we left Paul Winter. We knew we were a good band, but we didn’t want to put limits on what other projects we could do. We could branch out, like I played with Weather Report, without it being a threat to the other guys. [Walcott played with Don Cherry and Nana Vasconcelos in Codona, as well.] The Solstice group, that was the first big test, for me, it was such a great success, for a jazz record. Or at least it got great reviews, anyway.

AD: People still love it.

Ralph Towner: You know, the way that thing came down in the studio, it sounded exactly like that as we were playing it. It’s almost all first takes. Only a few overdubs, I think Eberhard had a couple. It was a miracle album, and we realized it. And it kind of spoiled us for recording afterward. But that was the first strain, to get back to Oregon afterward.

AD: And you’ve gone beyond classical guitar in your solo work, too. Have you thought about doing another synth-focused record or a departure from your usual thing, like [1982’s] Blue Sun?

Ralph Towner: Well, not now. No, I’ve kind of reduced it now to just staying with the classical guitar. I’ve had a little setback here that I’m healing from. I had a fall and fractured my vertebrae. So, I’m getting over that slowly.

AD: Oh, man.

Ralph Towner: But I’m still writing and practicing.

AD: That’s good to hear. Do you come back to the States very often at all?

Ralph Towner: Well, I did that before the COVID thing, and that canceled everything for about three years, and I managed not to see my daughter [who lives in California] for over two years, two and a half years at least, and so that’s, boy, that threw everything off. But I wrote a lot of music during that period, and I never did try to do a video.

AD: I was going to ask if you’d done COVID videos. A lot of musicians did.

Ralph Towner: Yeah, I just didn’t bother with it. But I still played quite a few concerts, but not that many, fully masked all the time. I think we got, I think my wife and I both got it, but it was mild, and we didn’t have a very heavy thing. It was a week and then it was gone. We had already had our shots and things.

AD: Have you ever really played electric guitar much?

Ralph Towner: No, I never could. I never learned how to play. I wasn’t interested in the guitar sound before all the modern guys, like Abercrombie and [John] Scofield. A lot of players, they all kind of come from Jim Hall. He’s one of the founders.

AD: He has that great album with Evans, Undercurrent.

Ralph Towner: Oh, I just died with that one. That was too great. I used to see him in New York, in little jazz clubs, and Jim could play the longest solos without ever repeating himself, it was unbelievable. It was an honor just to know him. But anyway, by the time I grew to love all the new players, you know, I was totally involved with the classical guitar. I couldn’t relate to the electric guitar physically, with those little strings, and they’re so close together! It’s hard to play all the contrapuntal stuff I do on one. But the electric guitar has a wonderful history and evolution that I admire.

AD: But you’re not gonna play it!

Ralph Towner: No. I picked up one of Abercrombie’s once when we were jamming together at his place. His guitar was lying there on the bed, so I picked it up and played one note, and the ugliest sound came out, and John says, “Never touch that again!” And I never did.
