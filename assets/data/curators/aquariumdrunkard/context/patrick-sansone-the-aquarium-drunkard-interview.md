## Body

The other evening I set out at dusk on a run to the record store, pressing play on “Measures,” the opening number of Pat Sansone’s new album of synth instrumentals, Infinity Mirrors, as I got in my car. The window down and the sky awash in a gradient of blue, pink, and purple as the sun set, I found myself settling into a daydream fantasy, something like a scene from an unfilmed, totally imagined John Carpenter film. Though he’s known for his work with Wilco and The Autumn Defense, Sansone embraces wordless vistas and inner/outer cosmic tones on the album, evoking the work of Steve Roach, Klaus Schulze, Tangerine Dream, and yes, the occasional John Carpenter vibe. Sansone corresponded with us about his teenage synth fixations, and how photography and mindfulness tie into the expansive spaces of his new album. | j woodbury

Infinity Mirrors by Patrick Sansone

Aquarium Drunkard: To start off, I’d love to hear about your earliest synthesizer experiences. What kind of gear did you first get access to, how did you get it, and what kind of music came out of those early experiments? 

Patrick Sansone: I got my first synth probably around 1984 or so when I was 14. It was a Korg Polysix, and I wish I still had it! I also remember having an Arp Axe at some point around that time, but it was the Korg that I really connected with. It was polyphonic, meaning I could play chords, and I remember spending hours in my room with headphones and that synth, enjoying discovering new chords and creating sounds and textures. It was a time of real discovery for me. 

I would also spend a lot of time in the music stores in my home town of Meridian, Mississippi, taking guitar lessons and just hanging out with the instruments—probably driving the staff crazy!—but I remember spending lots of the time with the new synths as they came out. I don’t have any recordings of those early bedroom experiments; I wish I did.

I also remember that around that time I was allowed to sit in on a course on electronic music at the local community college which had a great music department. It was a great overview of all kinds of experimental music and it might have been the first time I heard the term “Musique concrète.” Thinking back on it, that was a pretty great experience for me at that time.

AD: Infinity Mirrors is expansive and thoughtful music. Do you maintain any meditation or mindfulness practices on a personal level? Or does music blur into that zone for you? 

Patrick Sansone: Music has provided a path toward transcendent experiences for me over the years, but it’s complicated because it has become my vocation, so there’s a whole complex of things layered in my mind’s relationship to music. I think the impetus for creating these pieces was to try and get back to a mindful feeling in making music, and to shake myself out of some patterns I had drawn for myself over the years. I think I needed to do something that was more about the pleasure of sound, and less about form and song structure. My photography practice is probably the thing in my life that is the closest to a meditation practice, and I think there are parallels between my photography approach, and the way I approached this project.

AD: I hear a lot of Klaus Schulze and Tangerine Dream in these songs—especially “Jupiter Removed.” What inspirations were you drawing on for these compositions? 

Patrick Sansone: I was not familiar with Klaus Schulze until recently when seeing his name in some early reactions to Infinity Mirrors, but I have since been checking out his records and I am actually listening to him as I type these answers…and I am really digging it. I do love Tangerine Dream. I remember staring at their album covers in our local record store as a kid…I think I imagined their music in this way for some time before actually hearing them. I do love their ’70s albums and their work was in so many films from when I was growing up. 

I do think that some of the music on Infinity Mirrors was probably unconsciously influenced by film scores from the ’70s and ’80s…Tangerine Dream, Vangelis, John Carpenter, Wendy Carlos, etc. Music which imprinted itself in the minds of my generation, for sure.  But I wasn’t thinking of them specifically when making Infinity Mirrors, and I do not profess to be an expert of or a deep collector of ambient or early electronic music. 

I have encountered things from that world over my lifetime that have made an impact on me, Eno, Fripp, Harold Budd, Harmonia, Terry Riley, Eliane Radigue, and I am always discovering things. But wouldn’t say Infinity Mirrors is a reflection of a deep knowledge of this genre. But it is a reflection of a love of these sounds and textures and of analog synthesizers as mysterious and expressive instruments.

AD: I really enjoyed your work with Mellotron Variations—did that project feed into this one? 

Patrick Sansone: I had actually forgotten that I had started the project that became Infinity Mirrors in 2017, about a year before we began writing Mellotron Variations music. But I do feel like the two projects are related for me personally. I think the fact that I had already been experimenting with these instrumental pieces on my own helped me to feel more comfortable collaborating with the other musicians in this way for the Mellotron project.  

AD: With Wilco, you’re often adding extra colors and textures to the songs; how does your work with Wilco inform your work in this more ambient field? 

Patrick Sansone: I wouldn’t say that my work in Wilco specifically has informed it. I have used all kinds of keyboards and other kinds of instruments and production techniques for many years in my arrangements and productions…as a producer for other artists and songwriters, or in my own music with Autumn Defense, or working in Wilco…I think all of these experiences inform each other. In many ways, my role in Wilco tends to lean more toward traditional sounds…though I have kinda carved out a role as the main Mellotron player in the band. 

Mikael Jorgensen expertly handles most of the synth duties in Wilco, and has a long history with synths in his background. But I do think there was probably a growing feeling within me over the years of producing records, and adding abstract textures here and there as embellishments to a traditional arrangements, that it would be interesting to lean into a project that was just about those kinds of abstract textures in themselves.

Infinity Mirrors by Patrick Sansone

AD: Do you and the other Wilco dudes pass projects around for ideas? Or is this more of a solitary thing?

Patrick Sansone: By design, this was completely solitary project. I didn’t share it with anyone until the six pieces were completely finished. 

AD: These songs were the result of spontaneous composition–is that a way you’ve often worked? What’s the key to getting the conditions right for that sort of thing to happen on its own?  

Patrick Sansone: Well, even in songwriting in a more structured style, there has to be those initial moments of spontaneous composition, just to bring the ideas from the subconscious, or wherever they come from. But then that original seed is grown, refined, and shaped into the final work. So I think with this project I wanted to try and capture that initial “conjuring” experience…to allow the final product to be as closely connected to the initial moment of inspiration as possible. My method was basically setting aside an evening for each piece, going down into my home studio with nothing planned, choosing a limited number of instruments to work with (usually four), and let whatever happens in that one evening be the piece…with no significant changing or editing after.  

I would turn on an instrument and try to put myself in a calm state, and then just put my hands on the keys to begin…and whatever happened in that moment would be the foundational element for the piece.  Then the other elements were added as improvisations with the previous elements. And this being done rather quickly and spontaneously allowed me to be both surprised in the moment, and surprised a few years after when revisiting as a listener. I couldn’t actually remember how I had made some of it, and I found this valuable in a way.  That’s why I sometimes think of these as being like sand-paintings.  

AD: I tend to imagine vast landscapes and open worlds while listening—the cover of this record is drawn from John Pfahl’s Altered Landscapes. What draws you to Pfahl’s work?  

Patrick Sansone: I came across John Pfahl’s photography through a friend who gifted me the book Altered Landscapes in late 2022. At this time I was already planning on revisiting the project at some point, to add a few pieces to the work I had done in 2017.  When I saw the image from the book titled “Salt Lake Angles” it spoke to me, as it somehow looked like the music I had been making with the project.  It sort of said, “I’m the cover of your unfinished instrumental album, and now you need to finish it.” So later that week I returned to my studio, directly inspired by this, and made the last two pieces, “Phosphenes” and “Jupiter Removed.”  Pfahl’s work has a sense of suggesting the inter-dimensional with natural and familiar elements, and I find this very poetic.  His work is deeply beautiful and mysterious and I am so grateful that I was able to connect with his estate and was granted permission to use the image as the cover for the album.  

AD: Can you elaborate some on the relationship between your photography and musical approach on this album? Do you usually work with specific images in relation to the music, or is it images in your head, or both? 

Patrick Sansone: I wouldn’t say the relationship is that structured or defined…I do think that the way I approached composing this album has a relationship to how I photograph, in that my photography practice is a form of meditation for me. Taking my cameras on solitary wanderings and letting my eye and my mind be present and accepting of wherever I am. I don’t plan or set up anything for my photography, I just wander and notice.  This is similar to the headspace I was in when making this album.  

AD: What have you been listening to lately? 

Patrick Sansone: I’ve been listening to Nick Drake recently after reading the new biography about him, and he’s been one of my favorites since discovering him in the ’90s.  I’ve been working on several music production projects lately so I haven’t had a lot of bandwidth for music listening for pleasure…but in the last week or so I have found myself listening to some Gimmer Nicholson, Bill Evans…Paul Bley’s solo piano “Open To Love” to wind down in the evening after sessions. The song “Sleep Sweet Child” by arranger Clare Fischer. I have also just recently discovered Eliane Radige which is really wonderful. I’m also always gathering things for my monthly radio program The Baroque Down Palace on WYXR Memphis, which focuses on ’60s-’70s pop/rock/folk/soul music that features orchestral arrangements…so I’m pretty all over the place in my listening.
