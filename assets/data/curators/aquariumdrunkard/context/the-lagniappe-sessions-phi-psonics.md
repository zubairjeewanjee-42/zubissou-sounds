## Body

Lagniappe (la ·gniappe) noun ‘lan-ˌyap,’ — 1. An extra or unexpected gift or benefit. 2. Something given or obtained as a gratuity or bonus.

Earlier this spring, spiritual jazz collective Phi-Psonics released their sprawling, fantastic third album Expanding to One. Led by upright bassist bassist Seth Ford-Young, the Los Angeles ensemble shifted from previous studio efforts The Cradle and Octava to a series of improvisational, organic live recordings that make up the record’s nucleus (and feature eclectic contributors such as drummer Jay Bellerose of Jeff Parker’s celebrated ETA IVet). For their inaugural Lagniappe Session, Ford’s collective delivers a pair of Duke Ellington compositions (including “Fleurette Africaine” from the seminal Ellington/Mingus/Roach triumph Money Jungle), early sixties Sun Ra, and “River Man” from Nick Drake’s debut album. Composer Seth Ford-Young’s detailed words on all of the selections below.

Phi-Psonics :: Fleurette Africaine (Duke Ellington w Charles Mingus & Max Roach)

Money Jungle has stood out as an important album in my development as a musician and bass player since I first heard it back in the early 1990’s . It was inevitable that I would wind up buying a copy— I was obsessed with Charles Mingus and Max Roach was one of my favorite drummers. But it was the elder member and leader of the group, Duke Ellington, who I was about to see in a new light. I had listened to Duke, appreciated his impressionistic yet compositional and often percussive piano playing, and learned many of his compositions as any young musician eager to play the music would.

However the spirit of the man became more clear to me after hearing him in this unusual context. The session was famously not the smoothest, and the tension is palpable even as the three maestros work through a meditative piece like “La Fleurette Africaine”. It comes front and center as the band gets downright pugilistic on the title track “Money Jungle”. The album is a journey through gut bucket swing, Uptown Romanticism, avant wailing, and gauzy minimalism, but all with a distinct Ellingtonian feel. That has as much to do with Duke’s very distinctive (and underrated) piano as it does with his undeniable compositional voice. “Fleurette Africaine” has a particular emotional pull, one of those pieces that sounds familiar the first time you hear it, and it is one of the Duke tunes I’d often tinker with on the piano.

Once, while visiting my parents in the DC suburbs where I grew up, I was playing through some of my favorite Duke pieces on the old family piano. When my mother heard me playing Sophisticated Lady , she announced from the kitchen “You know you met Duke Ellington in the Brussels airport when you were a baby!” Apparently, a few months after my birth and a few before his earthly departure, we met briefly. He pinched my cheek and took a photo with my family. He never knew what an inspiration he would become to me as a musician, bandleader, composer, and human.

Here, I’ve done my best to find an original way pay tribute to the great composer and one of the most inspiring Washingtonians. For Phi-Psonics’ interpretation, I’ve expanded the piano trio arrangement with two horns covering the melodic harmonies. Inspired by a rare live solo piano performance where the composer skips the major lift on the B section, we stay in the minor as well. I chose a different rhythmic approach too, instead of the original feel which is a sort of deconstructed Bolero, we explore a more languid swinging feel that hints at 12/8.

Phi-Psonics :: New Day (Sun Ra)

From “The Futuristic Sounds of Sun Ra”, Ra’s only release on the Savoy label, this album is among his more accessible works. It’s part of an important ‘transitional’ period after the Arkestra’s move from Chicago to NYC but before the freer, often synth-heavy Philadelphia years. I encourage listeners to delve into all of Ra’s deep catalog, it’s so vast and varied there is something for anyone with open ears. Active since the early 1930’s he worked as an arranger, including for Fletcher Henderson’s band, and led smaller combos before starting his bigger band. The Arkestra was among the few who rivaled Ellington’s band (1923-1974) in the length of its tenure. Ra led his band from about 1955 til his death in 1993 (I had tickets for a spring ’93 concert that sadly never happened).

The song ‘New Day’ is notable in the absence of the composer’s keyboard. Ra sits this one out, as Marshall Allen plays a very impressionistic version of the written melody on ‘morrow’ (a Shakuhachi with a Bb clarinet mouthpiece) while the percussion and repetitive bass line provide the backbone of this piece.

For our version, I found a copy of Ra’s original, handwritten score and created an arrangement based on the written melody and bass line. The melody on the recording differs greatly from the score, probably due to the unusual instrument Allen was dealing with, but the bass line was as written. The score, like the recording, has no chords or harmony, so I inferred from what was there and filled out our arrangement adding a harmony below what he had written.

Phi-Psonics :: River Man (Nick Drake)

A quietly powerful classic from Drake’s excellent debut Five Leaves Left released in 1969 via Island Records. This piece has always intrigued me with its mystical simmer, and I’ve thought about how to approach playing it for years. The song writing, performance, arrangement, and recording are all superb on the original, so covering this piece is a bit daunting. I was surprised to see how often it’s been attempted considering the high bar set by Drake, though hearing Brad Mehldau’s interpretation made me feel that it was both possible to do well and also open to another look from Phi-Psonics.

I wanted the piece to expand while maintaining its meditative quality, so I started with the melody stated on flute and alto flute, followed by a few improvised choruses, capped off with Tenor and Baritone saxes on the head out and coda. This allowed us to naturally build the piece without breaking the spell of the inherently immersive composition. The trickiest part was notating the melody for the horns as Drake changes his interpretation on each pass. I wound up distilling his vocabulary down to something I could write down, while allowing the band to find its own swing and interpretation.

Phi-Psonics :: Bourbon Street Jingling Jollies (Duke Ellington)

This is a deceptively named piece from Duke’s masterful 1970 LP, New Orleans Suite. Despite the name, this piece isn’t a terpsichorean second line romp. Rather it’s an impressionistic mood piece that, in the maestro’s own words, is “a rhythmic tone parallel to the excruciating ecstasies one finds oneself in when one is in the throes of the jingling rhythmic jollies of Bourbon Street”.

Like his “Far East Suite” and “Latin American Suite”, “New Orleans Suite” finds Duke using some of the musical language of the location, but largely focuses on his personal feelings inspired by visiting these places. To me, this approach is far more effective than the more obvious choice of leaning into the vernacular of the locale being celebrated. These intimate, impressionistic interpretations are timeless and allow his own voice to deliver new colors through his inspired orchestrations.

The original is a feature for Norris Turney’s transcendent flute work and Duke’s rich arrangement for his long-time orchestra. I decided to instead feature the tenor sax and electric piano in conversation. Otherwise, the piece is largely similar, though our much smaller group setting brings out the intimate reverie of the composition. I can imagine Duke’s wry smile at Mitchell Yoshida’s beautiful Wurlitzer piano solo as he hints at “Aquarium” from Camille Saint-Saëns “Le Carnaval des Animal”, bringing us to an otherworldly place that floats above the titular street.

It feels fitting to begin and end this set with Edward Kennedy “Duke” Ellington, who, while our outward styles of music may differ, remains my biggest influence. I hope through these offerings we are paying tribute to him and his work while encouraging listeners to explore his vast catalog.
