## Body

Nora Brown has been playing old time music since she was six years old. She came up in the folk scene surrounding the Jalopy Theatre, the headquarters of traditional music in New York City. Now at eighteen years of age Brown has developed into one of the premier banjo players and old time musicians in the country, with NPR calling her a “rising star” and Fretboard Journal describing her as “impossibly talented.” Brown has released two full length albums and two EP’s on Jalopy Records, the label associated with the theater. The vinyl version of her latest release, Lady of the Lake, an EP recorded together with her friend Stephanie Coleman on fiddle, was just released last last month.

Gearing up for a European tour this spring, Brown spoke with AD about the banjo, the vibes of old time music, listening to your elders, and the realities of posting both as a professional recording artist and a freshman in college. | c dandridge

Aquarium Drunkard: You’ve just started attending college. Are you studying music?

Nora Brown: No, I’m not really interested in doing music in a higher education situation. But I am able to take some music oriented classes. For example, this semester I took a class about Bob Dylan called “Bob Dylan: A Way of Life”. I actually haven’t listened to much Dylan throughout my life which is kind of why I took the class because I read the syllabus and saw we’d be listening to every one of his albums.

AD: Are you performing around campus while you are in school?

Nora Brown: Not really. But I’m in a Slavic choir at school so that’s been pretty great so far. I’m not Slavic but I’ve been around the choirs a lot. There’s lots of Slavic choirs within folk scenes. It’s a popular kind of traditional music. There’s also this festival called Golden Fest (Balkan border-crossing celebration) in Brooklyn that existed for a long time that I used to go to. It was held in this beautiful ballroom called the Grand Prospect Hall that was demolished during COVID, which is devastating. But I’ve just always really admired a lot of the Slavic and Balkan traditional music.

I enjoy being in a choir and hearing harmonies, especially because I was a vocal major at a classical music high school, but the harmonies sung in a Slavic choir are so unique and beautiful. The intervals are weird and there’s funky stuff going on. The choirs are so much about listening to the others sections around you. In my high school we were always surrounded by people singing the same part as us. But in Slavic choir we learn our parts in our sections, then we scatter when we sing it together so you are with different sections. It feels so much more connected.

AD: You started playing music on the ukulele. How did you end up with the banjo?

Nora Brown: I started learning traditional old time music on ukulele from this great teacher, musician, and historian named Shlomo Pesco. In his house he had all these instruments all over the floor in stands. And once I’d learn a song on ukulele he might fiddle along with me or play banjo. So I was engaging with those instruments and I learned some fiddle, some mandolin, accordion. I dabbled in a lot of things before I really started working on the banjo. So I was really just around it.

AD: Do you think about the banjo as being an African instrument?

Nora Brown: Of course. It’s pretty amazing to be able to hear or to look at different parts of the instrument and be like, “Oh, this came from that instrument.” Like the drop fifth string, which is that short string down the neck, is a key component in looking through the banjo’s ancestry and there’s instruments like the akonting and other West African instruments that people connect the banjo to.

AD: What is the relevance of that drop fifth string?

Nora Brown: That’s a drone string. It’s a pretty unique element of the instrument that’s not really in a lot of other instruments out there. So looking for that when observing some different African lute-like instruments is a key thing. It’s never fretted, it’s just this open drone string. It’s the coolest thing about the banjo because who doesn’t love a drone? It creates this whole other rhythm that you really can’t do on guitar. It’s a way of playing that introduces this constant tone.

And the picking hand of the banjo is played with this clawed shape of your hand that allows you to strike the strings with your nail which also comes from the akonting. So yes, I’m often thinking about the African roots of the banjo and not just the banjo. Old time music is, at its heart, very African American. The banjo itself is African American. And the joining of the fiddle and banjo is also a pretty African American tradition. It’s important to try to be educated on these things but I’m no historian or anything about the history of the instrument.

AD: You’ve put out two full length albums with Jalopy Records and also two EP’s, including your latest with Stephanie Coleman entitled Lady of the Lake. How’d you get hooked up with Jalopy?

Nora Brown: ​​Shlomo was a big figure within the Jalopy scene. Kind of anyone who plays old time music hangs out at Jalopy or at least spends some time there. But Shlomo also taught through Jalopy and we’d have recitals there. Jalopy has always been really good at promoting learning and expanding. There’s this camp called Miles of Music that is in New Hampshire that I’ve gone to a couple times in recent years. But before I had gone, they had a house camp at Jalopy that I first went to when I was 10 or 11 or something. It was always very expanding for me. They had all these workshops in different rooms in the theater. Jalopy’s just great at building bridges for people and exposing people to the music.

AD: You’ve recorded your records in some pretty neat places, like a lagering tunnel beneath Crown Heights. Your last full-length album, Long Time To Be Gone, was recorded in a 19th century Gothic church in Brooklyn Heights. How did you decide to record in that church? I imagine the logistics could be difficult.

Nora Brown: I hung out there a lot because it is where the Brooklyn Folk Fest is held and I was working with Jalopy Records on that project and they’re like, “Yeah, let’s do that.” I’m not so inclined to record in a studio so much. I think I probably prefer something that’s not like a soundproofed room. Maybe this sounds kind of cliche, but it’s sort of dead. A lot of people talk about that, that it’s a dead sound because everything’s absorbed and it’s very clean.

And I love singing in the church, especially. It’s really nice to sing harmony. It’s a difficult thing, though, because you think that big open caverns are the best place to play music because of the acoustics but it’s actually a lot harder to hear yourself without a short ceiling because the sound doesn’t have anything to bounce off of it just shoots up into the sky. So when I was recording there I’d record in different spots of the church. There’s an overhang with the mezzanine so I recorded a lot under there with a couple of mics up on my instrument and vocals and then room mics out catching how things sound in the cavernous space. Then those things got mixed together.

AD: Something that strikes me about your playing is the atmosphere you are able to create out of this instrument that is so often associated with fast picking running up and down the fretboard. Yet much of your music is, as the kids say, more vibes-based. What accounts for that?

Nora Brown: I think that is a quality of traditional old time stuff. When you think about bluegrass banjo, what comes to mind is a lot of agility and moving quick and knowing where to put your fingers. Those are all very good things that I’m not so great at. But in old time music there’s a little bit more of an attention to tone, and I’m speaking very broadly, there is, of course, attention to tone depending on who’s playing bluegrass banjo, but I think there’s just maybe a little bit less of a show-off-i-ness in old time. Like there’s no soloing in old time. You kind of just play the melody and see where it takes you. Whereas in something like bluegrass you’re gonna have your moment to shine where you go crazy and improvise and do stuff. There’s a lot less purposeful improvisation within old time, a lot of more of it is the improvisation that happens through playing it again and again. And because things change, you don’t remember things. It’s just your brain and human error kind of combining to allow for change. And then when you hear it, you’re like, “Oh, that’s cool.”

One of the major differences between old time and, say, bluegrass is the partnership between the banjo and fiddle. In bluegrass the banjo and fiddle are equal. But in old time fiddle leads with the melody and banjo fills in the gaps. A lot of this is because the shape of the hand when picking. The shape of the hand does not lend itself to playing melody that easy. There’s lots of limitations. It’s more funky and rhythmic in many senses.

AD: Compared to other genres old time music places such importance on the lineage or tradition. The genre almost necessitates a relationship with your predecessors. You’ve had great relationships through the Jalopy scene in Brooklyn, but you’ve also studied in Eastern Kentucky with banjo players like Lee Sexton, John Haywood, and George Gibson. You even had the great Alice Gerrard produce your first record. Could you talk about your relationship with your elders?

Nora Brown: I’m so glad that I’ve been able to spend time with masters of this tradition like Alice, John, George, and Lee. There’s something really different about learning from someone in person, not necessarily in a formal way. Like hanging out with Lee I never had a formal lesson. No one was telling me what to do. It was kind of just about hanging around learning. And asking questions but mostly just listening. We’d hang out and I’d be like, “Will you play some Cumberland Gap?” or something like that because I wanted to hear him play. Then I would be playing along while he was playing. And I would maybe ask him a question about if I’m doing something right. But I’m mostly just hanging out and listening. He was also really hard of hearing when we were spending time together so it was kind of an ordeal to communicate. It was easier to just be around him and hear him play.

It was a little bit different with Alice because we also had a professional relationship that was related to my album she produced. So it wasn’t just absorbing music. She really helped me a lot with honing my own voice. It’s funny, I’m really embarrassed of this, but when I was younger I used to sing a little bit with a Southern drawl. There are videos of it on YouTube that are terrible. I hate it. But I didn’t know I was doing it. I couldn’t hear it. And she helped me break through that and to learn to sing the way I talk. Which is some of the greatest singing advice you could ever give anyone. That’s what sounds the best, in my opinion, and can convey the most emotion, is when you sing how you talk.

AD: How do you find new music these days? Are you on Spotify?

Nora Brown: Yes, I am on Spotify. And sometimes it does expose me to new things, not necessarily old time stuff, but music that I enjoy. I kind of hate to give it that credit, but it does, it does. But also friends will send me music that they think I would like. There’s a lot of sharing: a culture of sharing music. For instance Steph is super into traditional Irish stuff so she got me into that a little bit. I’ve been playing a lot of bouzouki recently. (the bouzouki is a traditional Greek instrument that came into Irish folk music in the 1960s)

AD: What is your relationship to social media? You are an artist who has to promote their stuff on a professional platform, but you are also a young woman whose friends are probably posting embarrassing pictures of their weekends. How do you navigate this?

Nora Brown: Yeah, it’s funny, but I do think about, “Could I ever make my music account a little bit more representative of me and a little bit less constant promotion, or at least less music-centered?” Because there are artists who I see running one account that’s personal and professional, and I like that. And I want to do that. It’s so dumb, honestly, because it’s like, why not? Who cares? Just post stuff? It’s definitely not that I put on a persona. It’s more that I feel like I’ve had to be very professional to compensate for being young. So posting kind of silly things, you know, I’ve been a little wary of that. But I mean, I do post silly stuff sometimes. I’m working up to it. It’s a slow process. I’m getting there.
