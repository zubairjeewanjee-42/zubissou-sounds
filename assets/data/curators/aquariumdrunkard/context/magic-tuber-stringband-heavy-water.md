## Body

The Magic Tuber Stringband isn’t interested—or at least isn’t solely interested—in historical re-enactment. The three members play traditional instruments, Courtney Werner on fiddle, Evan Morgan on guitars, banjo and pump organ and Michael DeVito on bass. They are more than capable of turning out a heart-breaking, pre-war-style, country lament (as in the gorgeous “Scintillation” from this ninth full-length). And yet, the three musicians connect that tradition to the present in a variety of ways, from their willingness to incorporate noise, drone and free improvisation into their tunes, to their love of field recordings, to their clear-eyed, unsentimental perspective on the rural southern communities in which they find themselves.

Heavy Water takes inspiration from Werner’s graduate work at the University of Georgia’s Savannah River Ecology Lab, where she studied birdlife in one of the most damaged ecologies of the south. There, on the site of Ellenton, South Carolina (known as “Atomic City”), the U.S. The U.S. Atomic Energy Commission tapped DuPont to build a nuclear materials plant in the 1950s. the project displaced 6,000 residents, many of them black farmers, and dumped massive quantities of waste into the watershed. The area was named an environmental Superfund site in 1991. Werner’s job was to find out if the birds who lived there were radioactive (spoiler: they were).

The area that Werner studied had been abandoned by humans, and so was teeming with plant and animal life. It was beautiful but also deeply toxic. That dislocation seeps into the music, which is lovely but also a bit ominous, shot through with found sounds, including gunfire from a local militia group.

As a result, while Heavy Water has its share of careful rambles and bucolic reels, it also bristles with modernism, as in the squalling, free ranging “Sound of a Million Stars” with its wild, overlapping drones in stringed instruments, more Sun Ra than Old Crow Medicine Show. “Woodpeckers” captures the region’s volatile blend of serenity and violence, with the hammering of woodpeckers intersecting with heavy artillery sounds. Even the loveliest, most light-filled cuts — such as “Tribute to the Angels” — carry unexpected weight, graceful flourishes shading into dissonance, death and heaven intertwined.

Magic Tuber Stringband lives in the same community as Nathan Bowles and in the same general area where Daniel Bachman turned from playing the guitar to playing the landscape. It’s not like they’re pursuing their version of truth and beauty in a vacuum. Still, few bands have balanced tradition and exploration, ideals and human depravity, as deftly as this trio. Heavy Water, indeed. | j kelly
