## Body

Jessica Pratt emerged out of an acoustic, self-recorded, Bay Area indie folk scene, but pristine as her self-titled debut and the luminous On Your Own Love Again were, she always heard more in her head. Indeed, even early on you could catch her approximating a horn section with an overdubbed guitar or a string orchestra in the eddies and swirls of massed vocals.

Now a decade on, recording for the second time at Gary’s Electric Studio in Brooklyn and employing a full band, she’s realized a lush, baroque 1960s pop sound akin to Vashti Bunyan’s work with Andrew Loog Oldham, Dusty Springfield, even Petula Clark.

As a result, Here in the Pitch is a gorgeous slice of baroque pop, but also something gnarlier and more complicated, inhabited by the subversive characters that haunt Pratt’s imagination and full of a manic, scheming dynamic. Of the music, she says, “It’s mellifluous but there’s a creeping energy to it. That’s how I’d describe it. Like a shadow drifting across the wall.” | j kelly

Aquarium Drunkard: I’ve been listening to your music for quite a while, and I feel like you started out with the self-titled with these really beautiful but kind of introspective and small-scale folk songs and with the last couple of records you’ve really been aiming at something larger and more baroque and more pop. Can you tell me a little bit about your artistic journey from where you started to where you are now in terms of this larger pop sound?

Jessica Pratt: It feels like it’s been quite a long time since the first record. It really hasn’t. I guess it’s been over ten years which is maybe a long time for anyone, but it feels further away. It feels like 20 years or something. Maybe that’s how everybody feels about their earliest works.

I’ve never been a person who pictures the future in any sort of concrete way. I’ve always been pretty moment by moment. I think I’ve lived my life that way. I’ve always functioned that way. It’s been a completely natural progression.

I haven’t listened to anything off of the first record in a very long time, but the way that I made that record is probably not how I would have done it if I had decided, okay, I’m going to make my debut album now. It sort of happened in a happenstance way. I feel like it’s even more of a primitive document compared to where things are now.

AD: So maybe you would have always had string sections, oboes, timpani, and whatnot but you just didn’t have the resources?

Jessica Pratt: I think I’ve always pointed towards those things. On my second record, there are certain overdubs that are like mocked up versions of that kind of thing, or maybe even some of the wordless vocals will stand in place of a horn section. I was always kind of doing that stuff a little bit in some form.

But also you can write and record a song and maybe that stuff occurs to you to try, but it might not suit the song. Like the real full-fledged orchestration might overwhelm the song. Honestly, I think it’s just that these new songs, for whatever reason, feel like they’re an appropriate canvas for a certain grade of extra color.

AD: I was getting the vibe of Vashti Bunyan’s work with Joe Meek and Dusty Springfield and Petula Clark, these really baroque 1960s pop artists. Were those touchstones at all? And if not, who were you thinking about?

Jessica Pratt: Yeah, all that stuff goes in. Especially the sort of cross sectional sounds of that era of music. Even if you’re talking about Petula Clark, even stuff that you’ve just heard on the radio your whole life that you don’t even want to get deeper into but it’s just sort of permeated your brain. I think that’s, yes, there’s an era of music that really resonates with me and maybe it just sort of like sonically or energetically, it feels like I’m returning to it.

But yes, I listen to a lot of music. It’s hard to talk about influence because I do feel like it is this sort of uncanny process. It’s not a direct process of, well, I listened to this and it came out this way. Though sometimes it feels like that. But more often, my mind is very porous with influence. I have to be somewhat selective about what I listen to when I’m working on music. I was listening to some jazz vocalists, definitely listening to some folky-jazzy stuff and the Beach Boys and Scott Walker, Burt Bacharach. There are these perennial things that I tend to listen to over the years. It’s getting in at the molecular level.

AD: I’d heard that Pet Sounds was an influence.

Jessica Pratt: That’s true. Mainly in the sense that I had the privilege to work in a studio in New York that is very nice and also has a large and tall live room, so the ability to get a big sense of space in there was really exciting. I got to experiment with that on my last record because I recorded in the same place. For instance, using drums. I’ve never had percussion on a record before. Not in any real way. Maybe one song on my second album has a little click on it, but being able to experiment with atmospheric-type percussion in the studio was really thrilling. It’s very satisfying to experiment with these kinds of sounds.

AD: The percussion is interesting. I have some notes about “Life Is” and “Get Your Head Out” almost having a bossa nova feel. Is that something you listen to or think about?

Jessica Pratt: The percussionist is Brazilian. That, again, was serendipitous. I do listen to bossa nova. It’s a mainstay. But also so much of the music of that era was influenced by that kind of sound. It permeated everything. Even Bacharach. So I feel like again it so saturated the music that those rhythms just came out.

But Mauro [Refosco], the percussionist, was recommended to us along with the bassist, and someone that we know recommended a bassist Spencer Zahn, who often plays in a duo with this percussionist. It just worked out that he was already on that tip. I think he’s at the heart of the record. He had such a knack.

AD: What’s his background?

Jessica Pratt: He’s played with David Byrne and the Red Hot Chili Peppers.

AD: And you had never worked with either him or Spencer before?

Jessica Pratt: I had not. I was not familiar with either of them. Well, maybe I had heard of Spencer’s music, but I was not very familiar. I felt very lucky to find them. Although Spencer was not the only bass player on that record. The engineer Al Carlson plays on a few songs as well.

AD: And you have worked with him before?

Jessica Pratt: Yeah, I recorded the last record with him, and it was basically the same process, just more involved this time.

AD: What is the process? Do you come in with fairly finished songs or do you do a lot of work in the studio?

Jessica Pratt: I definitely try to be as close to finished as possible with the songs. I’m not the type of person who could just roll up to the studio. Potentially I could in the future, but as far as having an objective you’re trying to reach, I always try to go with as many songs as I can. Especially since the studio is in New York.

AD: It’s expensive.

Jessica Pratt: It’s a practical issue. I try to get everything down, the song structure, the lyrics and maybe have an idea of the things I’d like to do production-wise. But it is interesting how your original conception of a song and what it might sound like can change based on what it sounds like when it’s recorded.

AD: Is there a good example of that on this album?

Jessica Pratt: I think so. For instance “World on a String,” the third song. When I first wrote it, it felt like a downer, jammer, almost Beatles-y. Not to flatter myself but a distorted version of that. Really simple and very straightforward. And then once it was recorded, it started taking on these other elements. I was going through an obsessive Guided by Voices phase when I went to record that song. I think sometimes…my obsessions are a guard against reality. They insulate the brain. Maybe you can relate as a journalist who goes deep? But anyway, and maybe it’s not such a jump from the Beatles to 1960s Brit pop stuff. But there’s that instrumental break where there’s a 12-string guitar playing a riff, unamplified, so it has this plinky sound. I was thinking about Pollard and his obsession with that era, all the British 1960s stuff. Even the backing vocals stacked in this distorted way. I was thinking about some stuff on the Bee Thousand album. Completely unexpected and not even necessarily an obvious reference. It’s just that your mind starts making these connections and it just happens to be where you are at that particular moment. But at the end of the day, I’m not sure that anyone picks up on it. That’s what I was trying to say earlier. I think influence is tricky. It’s not always deeply meaningful. It fluctuates like the weather on a particular day. I love music. I love the Beatles. I love all of this stuff that goes into my music, but there isn’t always a ton of foresight that goes into that stuff.

AD: I think with any creative endeavor, it could go in a million different directions, and it’s just sort of happenstance about which way you go. One sentence could change the way a book goes.

Jessica Pratt: Absolutely, and I love that side of it, the unpredictability. It makes it really exciting.

AD: I was thinking about the first single, “Life Is” and it has big, syncopated beat and swirling vocals and strings. It’s got a real swagger to it, in terms of the arrangement. And yet, it’s about uncertainty. And I’m wondering if there are little confessional songs at the heart of everything, underneath all the orchestration.

Jessica Pratt: Yes. I feel like I’ve broken the record down into these thematic sections. The first three songs on the record have this manic quality to them. They’re aggressive, I guess, and sort of defiant, but there’s almost a layer of denial happening. I think this is a character that asserted itself as I was making these songs.

AD: Oh, so not you or sort of you?

Jessica Pratt: I think that that’s the eternal question with art. How much of it is the person making the art and how much is this genuine fictional side of it? Certainly, everything is autobiographical at the end of the day if you’re writing in this way. But I think it’s also true that these characters emerge. They’re a ballooned version of these simmering ideas that you have in your subconscious. They become a vessel for these ideas and also a magnifying glass for them. They can become distorted in their own way and not everything is a true reflection of you.

Think about somebody writing a screenplay. They write some dialogue for a character who is diabolical. That doesn’t mean that the person behind the dialogue is an evil person, but it’s an expression of something in them. You could spend a lifetime trying to dissect that area as writers.

AD: Do you want to talk about the themes? You said that the album breaks down on thematic lines. Is that something you want to explicitly talk about or would you rather not?

Jessica Pratt: It’s a good question. Yeah, I think it’s…this is an interesting thing and I wonder what you think about this. I like dissecting these things and I like talking about them, but then at the end of the day, it’s difficult to know how far to go discussing certain things. The mystery is part of what makes music interesting. Especially, since I feel that my lyrics are a bit more abstract.

AD: And there’s also the thing where when you write a song, it goes out into the world and people interpret it in any number of ways. The song has its own life. After you’ve decided what it’s about, other people decide what it’s about to them, too.

Jessica Pratt: I love that experience. I’m just beginning to learn what things people are picking up on. Mainly through journalists. But yes, there are certain themes that pop up to me. One is paranoia and a plotting or creeping feeling. In “Get Your Head Out” and “Nowhere It Was” and “By Hook or By Crook” and “Empires Never Know” there’s kind of like…it feels a bit sinister and also like there’s some kind of plot being carried out. But also a bit of smugness in some of those songs. And I think it comes down to these characters that are vehicles for these odd emotions. It can be a bit amorphous.

AD: Do you have a visual picture of these characters in your head? What kind of person this is and what they wear and what they look like?

Jessica Pratt: A little bit. It’s very vague. Male or female. One thing I think is interesting and I wonder if other people experience this, is like ….I’m going to go off on a complete tangent here.

There’s a type of synesthesia where you assign gender to numbers and letters. Have you ever heard of this? I think a lot of people do this.I don’t think a lot of people even understand that it’s a type of synesthesia, but I’ve always had that. And I feel like it can be this very vague thing. The character I know is male or female and there are certain attributes, but there isn’t a concrete image. It’s more like a sense of the person.

AD: Do you like writing as a male? Is that fun?

Jessica Pratt: It’s hard to talk about it because it’s difficult to describe it accurately, what the experience is. But I do think I have a tendency to always write in a slightly more …I feel like my creative voice is a little more masculine. Not that I’m trying to make it that way. It just feels like this innate thing, and I don’t know how to describe it. and not every song. Sometimes it feels more feminine. I wonder if this is just some weird part of my brain.

AD: No, I think people do have views about written words and whether they are male or female. For instance, sometimes I get jobs after people evaluate a whole bunch of writing samples, and I’ve had experiences where people picked mine and were absolutely certain that it was written by a guy. It wasn’t like I was writing about football. It was just the way the nouns and the verbs were put together.

Jessica Pratt: How do you feel about that?

AD: You know, it’s a good thing. Men run the world. It’s good to sound like a man.

Jessica Pratt: Yeah, well, that’s an interesting part of it. I wouldn’t want people to think I think men are superior and I’m trying to emulate them. It could even be something as simple as…a lot of the music that I listen to and have been influenced by is made by men. Even if we’re talking about, on my second record, the big song on that, “Back, Baby,” there’s a part of that song where I address the girl by saying the word “girl.” I think part of that was listening to the Wallflowers a lot at that time, and that sound was banging in my head. But it just felt correct to assert that lyric in that way. It’s weird…it’s like…

AD: One of the things that I think is so interesting about this sort of 1960s pop aura your album has is that the original 1960s artists, those women, in general, were not in charge of how their music sounded. Vashti Bunyan, she only could control the folk albums, not the pop stuff that she did with Joe Meek. But, in contrast, you’re doing this because you want it to sound that way, and you have a degree of autonomy that none of those original artists had.

Jessica Pratt: Yeah, it is very interesting to me, and obviously, I think about that. I was thinking about Laura Nyro recently, who is somebody who was very much ahead of her time in writing all her own songs and being very uncompromising about the production and how the songs were received. Not changing lyrics. Certainly to the detriment of her career. And I don’t think people talk about Laura Nyro, which is also interesting.

AD: I haven’t heard that name in forever.

Jessica Pratt: Yeah, and she was a huge influence on Todd Rundgren. Like so much of his musical sensibility comes from her. I love Todd. I’m not hating. But yeah, I don’t know. I’ve never talked about this with anyone before. It’s very interesting that it’s come up. Gender stuff…

AD: (laughing) I’m so sorry.

Jessica Pratt: No, I’m very happy when novel conversations spring out of interviews. There are certain relevant questions to a release, and you’re going to end up talking about those things, but if you can kind of veer off into a related but unexpected topic, that’s great. I almost want to talk to my musician friends to ask if they can relate to this experience at all.

AD: Here’s a question I ask almost everybody. Do you have any favorite sounds or moments or lines? I’m not asking what’s your favorite song on this album. Just little things about the record that pop for you.

Jessica Pratt: Yeah, there are many. I think for whatever reason, “Get Out Your Head” is kind of my favorite song on the record. It’s more about the feeling that the song gives me. The production and the instruments on that song – it feels like it is very exemplary of this sort of abstract thing I was going for. I don’t know if I can put it any more directly than that. It gives me this…it’s mellifluous but there’s a creeping energy to it. That’s how I’d describe it. Like a shadow drifting across the wall. I was trying to tap into that feeling, and I feel like I got it on that song. And that feeling is the major essence of the record.

AD: Mellifluous but with a creeping shadow. That should be on the sticker. I saw you play live a couple of years ago. It was the On Your Own Again tour. And it was just you and another person, a guitar player, I think. Will it be more elaborate this time?

Jessica Pratt: Yes, it will be. There will be a percussionist, who is playing mostly hand percussion but also a very stripped down drum kit, a really great bassist, my husband Matt who plays keys and me.

AD: Will you be reconfiguring some of the older songs to fit the new line-up?

Jessica Pratt: Yeah. The whole point of the band is sort of like…There are many instances where a previously solo artist will scale up and get a band and everything goes through the band filter and it’s not always good. I’m really trying to avoid that.

I wouldn’t call this record sparse. It doesn’t feel like the atmosphere has changed drastically from the last record. It’s in the same sonic world but there’s a little more happening. I want it to feel that way live. So the songs will stand on their own and there will be extra instrumentation but there will still be a sense of space. The spirit of the songs won’t be completely augmented to fit the band structure. It’s going to be the other way around. The band is trying to support the existing feeling of that music.

AD: I know you’re probably super busy with getting this record out and planning your tour and all that but are there any other side projects or collaborations or soundtracks or anything like that that you’re working on that you want to talk about.

Jessica Pratt: No, there’s nothing like that in the works. I’d love to do a soundtrack. I feel like they’re using songs…with words and lyrics, you know? They’re not really doing soundtracks anymore. But yeah, I have the tours coming up. That’s the main thing.

AD: What are you reading? Anything interesting? I just read the oral history of the Village Voice [The Freaks Came Out to Write by Tricia Romano], which was phenomenal. So good. It makes you feel so terrible for having missed all that.

Jessica Pratt: I’m reading right now a book called The Ghost Map by Steven Johnson. It’s about the cholera outbreak in London in 1854 and the guy, John Snow that figured out that it was communicable by water.

AD: Oh wow.

Jessica Pratt: Yeah, Victorian England seems like a made up thing.

AD: I think it was considered very real at the time.

Jessica Pratt: It seems to be verified at this point. But it’s so insane to read about it now. It reads like science fiction to me. When I was making the record, I read a couple of Neil Gaiman books. One of his biggest books, Never Where. I don’t know if you’re familiar with the book. It’s about an unseen, subterranean version of London beneath the existing one. It’s kind of like science fiction but also magical realism a bit. There’s this underworld with its own society and it’s very interesting. And then reading about the gritty details about Victorian London in this book. And also, I read Great Expectations in between those two, and obviously, I think both of those things, the real history and Dickens, influenced Neil Gaiman. All of this stuff feels very palpable to me.

AD: Very Victorian.

Jessica Pratt: I wasn’t intending to do that, but it ended up being what I was reading.

AD: What do you think makes a great song a great song?

Jessica Pratt: I think it’s like an uncanny layer that you can’t necessarily pinpoint. It has to do with melody. It has to do with structure. But I think it mainly has to do with a momentum and feeling that is very instinctual. And, sure, somebody could sit down and write out what everything means, like the music and all of that. You can try to approach it with logic. But I think if you then take that logic and try to apply it and make something of your own based on that exact framework, it might not work. I think it’s a very human thing. Why do certain sounds elicit certain feelings? I guess it could be the difference between an acted scream that you’d hear in a studio and a scream that you’d hear that is actually born out of someone being in trouble or afraid. You can’t necessarily outline what the difference is but you can feel the difference.

AD: Yep. You know it when you hear it.
