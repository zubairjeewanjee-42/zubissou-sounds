## Body

Genre often has more to do with the ear of the listener than the intent of the artist. But as writer and musician Julie Beth Napolin points out, that is precisely how resonance works. At its best, art develops its own associations as it moves through the world. With Only the Void Stands Between Us on Silver Current Records, Napolin delivers a singular debut, one that cuts new trail through the cosmic and the colloquial wilds while adding to the amorphous canon of psych-folk. The author of 2020’s The Fact of Resonance: Modernist Acoustics and Narrative Form, Napolin isn’t merely content to explore the unknown, she is resolute in facing it. Only the Void Stands Between Us proves a formidable soundtrack for traversing great distances, from mantras to minimalism, Paul Celan to Daniel Higgs, from where we find ourselves to where we’re heading–from one side of the void to the other. 

On a quiet winter afternoon, Julie Beth Napolin and I shared an expansive video call, discussing Only the Void Stands Between Us, The Fact of Resonance, the ontic and ontological, and the fertile soil of nothing. | n lekas

Aquarium Drunkard: Has the void taken on new meaning post-pandemic–both in the social nothingness of it and the spiritual depletion that so many of us endured? Has the idea of the void evolved since you began working on the record? 

Julie Beth Napolin: I wrote the record prior to the pandemic and sat on it for quite a long time. That expression [Only the void stands between us] in particular took on new meaning after the pandemic with the phenomena of social distancing. You called it “social nothingness” which I think is really great because suddenly time and space that had been constantly filled became very void-like, but also because things that had filled our time before suddenly seemed quite meaningless. The title is from a poem by Paul Celan that’s colloquially called So Many Constellations. I am not the author of that line, I’m kind of riffing on Celan. Celan is a Holocaust survivor. It’s hard to separate the milieu of the Holocaust from his writing even though he doesn’t mention it directly. When he is writing that poem, or someone of his history is writing that poem, it’s hard not to interpret this apocalyptic sensibility to what he means by the void. But the poem begins with constellations so it’s also very cosmic and there is a sense of totality about it. I think I’m drawn to this idea of the void because rather than the nothingness maybe it’s actually a totality if that makes sense. And I love how the next part is “We get all the way to each other” and I include that line in the song, so it’s not insurmountable. In getting to each other you have to traverse something almost incomprehensible, getting to each other is not some easy feat, it’s daunting and maybe on the verge of impossible, but you get there nonetheless.

AD: I remember in college reading a lot of literature of the Holocaust, Charlotte Delbo, of course Elie Wiesel, but I can’t recall if I read this Celan piece.

Julie Beth Napolin: You may have read Todesfuge, it’s a famous poem by Celan that’s often included in Holocaust Lit. To answer the other part of your question, I think that that song [Only the Void Stands Between Us] is the first song that I wrote for that record. Originally I wanted to call the record “Changing Shapes” because another theme across the record is transformation and things changing shape, which I take from two different sources. One is Dan Higgs and Lungfish. He talks a lot about changing shape. But I’m also interested in the philosophy of Hegel, transformation and synthesis is huge in his philosophy. He often talks about shapes, where things are kind of indistinct and moving towards their form. I don’t know if Dan Higgs read Hegel, but they are of a piece. So originally I thought about that, but then I came to realize, Only the Void Stands Between Us was really the thesis statement if you will for the whole record. I don’t know that that concept evolved over the course of writing the record. It was only later that I came to realize every song was touching on that theme, trying to reach something or somebody across a distance. At the time that I worked on the record, I experienced a ton of loss through illness, people I love, and death–so I think the record is working through reaching people and experiences that are void-like.

AD: Do you differentiate between the physical and the spiritual void?

Julie Beth Napolin: There is another dimension of the record that I should touch on. This has to do with the genre of the record which I don’t really know how to describe. I suppose it’s folk music, but I never really figured out a place where my music fit in. I never quite fit into the various scenes as they were unfolding in the different places where I lived. Maybe my music was a little too droney and electronic for the various folk scenes, but not quite experimental enough for some of the noise scenes. When I wrote the record I had the sense that I didn’t know where the music or where I as the musician belonged. I think that desire for belonging is palpable in the songs. The desire to reach out and be a part of something. There is a chant-like quality to a lot of the lyrics. We know through religious practices that chanting is a way of materializing, so there is almost this sense of trying to bring something into existence. That is one element of the social dimension. Of course this sense of where we belong was really heightened in the pandemic. People really reevaluated their relationships and social ties, and in many ways people’s worlds got much smaller, never to really expand to the way they were prior to the pandemic. Spiritual nothingness I think is actually different because there is this sense of wanting to empty things out, or void things out in order to find a kind of tranquility in a world of anxiety. Heidegger has this distinction between the ontic and ontological. The ontic is kind of our existence as we live it but the ontological has to do with being. It’s prior to and exceeds our individual existences. So, I would say that the social is more the ontic, Heidegger would call it the “They”. The people you care about who occupy and chatter away in your mind. The social anxieties you may have. The day to day worries and moods that you may have. The ontological is almost this all encompassing backdrop that far exceeds any individual existence. That’s probably how I’d be inclined to think about the difference. Through resonance and through music I do think that we touch that background if you will, through resonance and through vibration we start to exceed ourselves. That’s why I’m so drawn to resonance. Making the guitar, through electronics, really just resonates as much as possible.

AD: Some say we are in a post-religious society, or at least entering one, if so, it doesn’t seem that our desire for resonance has diminished with the dismantling of any of those systems. Desire for the congregational element of resonating doesn’t seem to have diminished at all, we’re just looking for it in new places. During the pandemic I started listening to ambient guitar music and things like that in a way that I never had previously, I started looking for that resonance in records where maybe I hadn’t needed to before. Do you think that “social nothingness” as we were calling it has changed our listening or made us more open to ambient or resonant music?

Julie Beth Napolin: I don’t really know how to diagnose or theorize why there has been an ambient turn. I think it has been going on for quite some time but I would agree there has been a sharper turn in the last few years and something about the pandemic probably does explain that. Maybe also, aging, getting older and our relationship to our domestic spaces and ways of listening–where and how we listen to music. I’ve always felt pretty strongly that I don’t want my music to be ambient. I want people to listen in an engaged way. We’re living through times that feel apocalyptic to many, so I don’t want to write music that turns away from that. I don’t want to write music that a listener feels like they can escape into. I want the music to feel very confrontational–even if it’s a soft confrontation–still very present and not trying to transport me or anyone else away from where we are.

AD: We are living in a weird time, one where ambient also means Ai-generated soundscapes. The perspective and the intent separates craft from computers. Ambient is a particularly strange word as it’s more about the listening experience than the sound. If you walk into a store and Green Day is playing, isn’t that ambient music? I was curious, when you are writing, do you start with guitar or lyrics?

Julie Beth Napolin: I start with the riff. Usually it’s a riff that just won’t go away. Ben Chasny had this interview many years ago about the riff. He said that his songwriting practice is to write a riff and then leave it alone. He actively tries to suppress it and it’s only if the riff gains a kind of agency of its own and keeps returning then he’ll allow it through the gates of consciousness to be a song. I remember reading that and thinking that sounded familiar to me. Each of the songs had a riff that wouldn’t go away. Usually when I start writing lyrics I murmur to myself and I almost experience it as a kind of channeling. I’m murmuring and the sounds start sounding like a word, then I have maybe one or two words that have surfaced from what started as a sound. Then I try to think about the significance of those words. What do they mean? What could then mean? Then I try to craft a larger concept around them. It starts in sound. I hear my infant son starting to grasp towards language, how he was making these sounds and they start to sound more like phonemes. Eventually there will be a word in there, so I do think that the way humans acquire language is there in the songwriting–at least for me. I’ve always had a lot of envy for songwriters that can tell great stories. It’s almost like they willfully and with a lot of intention take an experience, whether it’s lived or fantasy and then they go tell that story. I’m so envious of that because I think it’s such a wonderful way to write songs, it’s just never been my experience.

AD: Fundamentally I think of creativity as making something out of nothing. Whether in your approach to songwriting, or the child finding their way to language, or a painting, I think all creativity shares that act of finding something in nothing.

Only the Void Stands Between Us by Julie Beth Napolin

Julie Beth Napolin: I think that is a perfect way of describing my relationship to “void” that Celan shares with us. It’s out of nothing that something comes to be, so that nothingness is actually the ground, it’s the beginning. Heidegger in the Introduction to Metaphysics, he begins with a question, “Why is there something rather than nothing?” That’s the question, why is there something? Why is there something?

AD: I like that idea of nothing as the beginning, the ground, the soil. The song “Heaven and Earth” has a William Blakian element to it, like in a press release maybe I’d write it sounds like if William Blake was in the Velvet Underground.

Julie Beth Napolin: That song is a good example of that practice I was describing of murmuring. When I was writing I just started thinking “Heaven and Earth.” I like that it’s not Heaven and Hell, it’s Heaven and earth. Here we are on earth, this is where we find ourselves. I like the idea of something kind of lofty but also terrestrial–back and forth between those. “In drowning waters” was another phrase I kept hearing and then I heard “we” and then I heard a phoneme and I couldn’t be sure what the word was. I remember talking to a friend about it who is a fellow writer and I played him the draft of the song. I said, “What do you think it is? What do we do in drowning waters?” He said, “I think we write.” I remember at the time he mentioned Byron and didn’t say more about that so I don’t know which particular poem he was mentioning. I know that Shakespeare says that men’s virtues, we write them in water–meaning that they are ephemeral. You can do a great thing but don’t expect people to remember it later. That’s not necessarily a strong reference for the song, because words come to me and their associations come later. The associations aren’t a predetermined fact, they are an after effect. That’s actually what resonance is, something keeps continuing on and accrues meanings and associations as it’s moving along. It doesn’t matter whether or not it was the intention. So that song for me, it does touch on some of the climate catastrophe, that the world can be sinking around you and still we have a desire to write. We still have a desire to create. I did record that song during the pandemic. I can’t remember if I finished the lyrics before, but I remember as a writer during the pandemic really thinking through what’s the point of writing anymore? What’s the point of making music anymore? What’s the value of creating when so many people are dying? The song ends up being kind of declarative, “In drowning waters we write.” We have to write. We have to create.

AD: Daniel Higgs came up a couple times already, what is the connection to Lungfish for you?

Julie Beth Napolin: Lungfish for me unfolds as part of my interest in minimalist music. I would contrast minimalist to ambient. I think minimalism has to do with the relationship to duration, where through repetition the consciousness of the listener changes through what is unfolding sonically. You start hearing things that you weren’t able to hear before. Marcus Boon, a writer that I am pretty interested in, says that in drone music we exist as finite beings within eternity. What I love about Lungfish is they take the conceit of a punk band, and like a three-minute punk song, but they bring eternity or infinity into that shape through the power of the riff. But it isn’t like a power chord, it’s often a very delicate exploration of an idea, and they just let that idea unfold. Daniel Higgs just sings around it and explores the ideas. I love the guitar playing in Lungfish. “Pray for the Living”, I had actually done a cover of this song with Trevor Healy. We listened to Lungfish a lot when we were doing The Meridians music. We did two Lungfish covers and this was one of them. I continued to play this song and explore it and its significance for me. I’ve said before, and I said to Ethan (Miller) when I play “Pray for the Living”, I play it as a woman. I’m not imagining that I’m Daniel Higgs. I’m not channeling his personality even though I really love Daniel Higgs and sure I’d love to be him or I’d love to experience the world as he might experience it, or at least as he tells us about the experience in his lyrics. I’d love to be able to touch that. But it’s not like a drag or an impersonation as covers often are. I’m trying to bring my experience as a woman to that song. The lyric, “Pray for the never born and pray for the unmade.” That line is important to me in relationship to abortion and terminating pregnancies, as a woman thinking about the potential for giving birth that kind of like marks your being, socially it marks your being from the beginning. Then it’s sort of this mysterious capacity that is always in the background of your mind. I love that idea of “Pray for the never born” and also, “Pray for the unmade” because there may be people who decide never to have children, or could never have children, maybe they have a completely different relationship to their sex or their gender than is normatively determined or demanded, and it also really touches on that idea of nothingness. There are some things that were never made and they are no less. The things that were never made are no less a thing if that makes sense. They may have this status as only an idea or a wish, or even something completely unthought, they are nevertheless unmade. So I just love that lyric. He also says “Pray for the demented and pray for the sane.” When I started covering that song a person in my immediate family had adult onset schizophrenia. So I just love that idea of making a place for what Higgs calls “the demented”, that they are part of our world. And so there is a prayer for all of these things in that song, that’s why I wanted to sing it.

AD: The idea that if you play a riff and can’t escape it, opposed to the chasing the muse approach, it’s all about not feeling obligated to make every idea exist. The difference between obligation and inescapability is an interesting idea as an artist. I’ve always been fascinated by the innate rhythm of the written word. How did you get interested in the resonance of language and sound as a topic and focus for The Fact of Resonance: Modernist Acoustics and Narrative Form?

Julie Beth Napolin: The book began in my own experience of writing. I don’t write poetry but I would say that my writing tends towards the lyrical, and just this sense that I have when I’m writing that I know when it’s right. You call it the rhythm and I would agree with that, but there is something about the pacing and the effect of the word choice, it’s like when you are writing a song, you know when it’s right and you know when it’s wrong. I never have any confusion when I’m writing a song. I know when I’ve found it. There is never any confusion about that. I have the same feeling when I write a sentence. I am a writer who works at the level of the sentence. I craft each sentence individually so that it communicates exactly what I want to try to communicate–not at the level of concept but at the level of feeling. I know when it’s right. Eudora Welty talks about the Writer Voice, this voice you hear in your head when you’re writing, she says you hear it and you know that it’s right. It doesn’t matter whether anyone “likes” it per se, including me–when it’s right, it means I can play the song with the courage of conviction. Studying for my exams for my PHD, I started reading Joseph Conrad who I’d never really read before. I read Heart of Darkness for the first time–there were sounds all over it. The characters hearing sounds and the narrator hearing sounds, but also the experience of reading Heart of Darkness was itself incredibly sonic. I felt, this was a writer who was hearing something. I learned after reading it that English wasn’t his first language. That it was actually his third. That blew me away. So that relationship of language as an outsider, his struggle to find a voice on the page that could reach the reader without any ambiguity, that would impact the reader with the feelings he was seeking.

I was just so drawn to that and had a lot of sympathy for him as a writer. He struggled a lot with depression and anxiety. He touched darkness I think–especially participating in the colonial exploits he saw the most horrific things that you can’t unsee. Despite his own racism and his own colonial mentality, I think he tried to write through those experiences. Then I discovered that nobody had written on this aspect of Conrad, and I just felt like it was something I wanted to communicate. The project was dual in that way. I wanted to communicate something about writing that is resonate but using Conrad as the vehicle to do that.

AD: Those themes of changing over time, developing over distance are very present in both your and Conrad’s works.

Julie Beth Napolin: Conrad is so much about transfiguration. Heart of Darkness kind of takes place as the sun is setting, when the narrative comes to an end it is dark. Many of his narratives have that same passage of the sun from sunset to dark. By the time you get to the end there has been this transfiguration within the narrator and I think also within the reader, but also the elements themselves are transfiguring, light is becoming dark, and sounds will become silent, so transfiguration and transformation is all over his work.
