# Steve Tibbetts :: The Aquarium Drunkard Interview

## Body

Steve Tibbetts makes albums slowly and rarely tours. His methods can be esoteric — he uses samples, tape manipulation and electronics to give his guitar compositions depth and nuance, and several of his songs have Sanskrit names. His music teems with non-Western textures gathered during his extensive travels, particularly In South Asia – he’s sampled gongs and gamelans in Bali –and played bouzouki and kalimba. He’s recorded albums with a Buddhist nun and a Norwegian fiddle player. All this makes for a certain mystique, and it’s easy to picture Tibbetts as some kind of reclusive, guru-like figure. But Tibbetts, for all his enigmatic vibes, is an approachable Midwestern musician. He’s covered Led Zeppelin and named a song after a Savoy Brown album. He’s lived in Minneapolis for much of his life, where he’s worked with several longtime collaborators, most importantly percussionist Marc Anderson. He has a website where you can find personable blog entries about his life and practice, and a contact email for his label, Frammis Rekords, on which he released his first two albums, his self-titled college project in 1977 and Yr in 1980.

From 1982 on, ECM has put out most of his work, with his eclectic, evocative style seeming right at home on the storied jazz label while carving out its own distinct space. His latest album, Close, builds on the somber acoustic richness of 2018’s Life Of, with an emphasis on floating, transitory tunes built from carefully constructed layers, but makes room for the occasional bursts of electric wildness, a la 2002’s A Man About a Horse. At times approaching ambient, it can also flirt with the dense builds and unpredictable intricacies of post-rock, but all from Tibbetts’ singular, sprawling perspective. In conversation, Tibbetts mixes careful consideration with friendly inquisitiveness, mirroring the open yet occult energy of his music, with new ideas and changes in direction coming quickly and often overlapping. Aquarium Drunkard talked to him via phone on Oct. 23, the day Close was released, with some texts and a follow-up call over the course of two weeks. This interview has been edited and condensed for clarity, concision and flow. | r jackson

Aquarium Drunkard: I thought I’d start by saying I’ve never actually talked to someone on the day their record was released. How does it feel, release day?

Steve Tibbetts: (After a long pause). It feels good. It feels like a birth and death happening on the same day. It’s really over now. It’s out.

AD: How long had you been working on it?

Steve Tibbetts: On and off for about six years. I’ve been doing other things in between and taking time off and doing some traveling, and I’ve had to work another job too. I work as a Licensed Practical Nurse, so I went to school for a while. I’ve always had to work other jobs.

AD: That was going to be one of my line of questions …

Steve Tibbetts: Do you work another job? I bet you do.

AD: I’m on my break from my other job right now – I’m an editor for a newspaper syndicate.

Steve Tibbetts: I used to work at Minnesota Public Radio, and one of my jobs was to keep the AP and UPI Telex machines going all night. They were just typing like crazy, rolling out rolls of paper.

AD: Oh, man.

Steve Tibbetts: It goes back. In fact, my first night there, I forgot to reload the paper, and so I was confronted in the morning by a purple-faced newscaster who had nothing to read.

AD: So what happened? Did you still have a job after that?

Steve Tibbetts: Yes, I did, I stayed there for quite some time. It was a great job, I loved working nights in a radio station, and between that and working in a record store, I learned a lot about media dissemination and the world of music and getting your stuff out there.

AD: I was going to ask you about the record store, too actually. Is that record store still around? It was Wax Museum?

Steve Tibbetts: There aren’t any record store still around.

AD: (laughing) We’ve got some in Kansas City, there’s one right by my house…

Steve Tibbetts: Which one is it?

AD: It’s called Sister Anne’s.

Steve Tibbetts: Yeah! I’ve heard of that. We have the Electric Fetus in town, and some of my friends moved [there] from the Wax Museum, and they’ve survived. The Wax Museum is now an Ethiopian restaurant, and a pretty good one. I had dinner there with Joe Boyd, of all people. Somebody you should have on your podcast … [But] the Wax Museum, that was just Mondays and Tuesdays. That was enough to get current with everything that was new, listen to music that influenced the music that I did. When I had a manager there that was deeply into metal, into Motorhead and Girlschool, crazy bands like that, I ended up with Exploded View, a lot of electric guitar. That came from the Wax.

AD: How did you get to know Joe Boyd?

Steve Tibbetts: When I came back from Kathmandu in ’93 or ’94, with a rough recording of [Buddhist nun] Choying Drolma singing in a friend of ours’ small room … I made a gift tape for her and Rob Simons at Rykodisc and for the writer John Diliberto in Philadelphia, and I said, “I hope you guys enjoy this … I’m just seeing if I can put samples around it and make anything out of it.” And Rob got back right away and said, “You should put this out, this is great stuff.” … And Rob said, “We just bought [Boyd’s label] Hannibal Records. … Joe’s in town, let’s go have dinner with him.” And [Joe] said, “I like this thing, I think we should put it out.” And then a couple of days later, I received sort of a startled call from somebody at Rykodisc, who said, “Joe Boyd has instructed me to give you the highest possible royalty, control over the cover, the booklet and the mix, and a $16,000 advance.” He sounded a little incredulous. And this was a little bit startling to me as well. But I then I got to know Joe really well. … He’s been a great friend. He lets us stay at his place when he’s not there. If I say I’m passing through London, he’ll say “Here’s where the key is. I won’t be home for a month. This was years ago, but he said, “You’ll be happy to know if there’s a depression from the bed, it was from Anna McGarrigle, she just left a week ago.”

AD: How long have you been a nurse?

Steve Tibbetts: My mother, as she was dying, I was down in Madison, Wisconsin, this was eight years ago, and I was wondering who these people were that were in my parents’ apartment. My sisters would say “The CNA will come, and then the LPN will come in for a check, and then the RN will stop by.” And I wondered what these acronyms stood for. So I asked one of the CNAs “What is that?” and she said, “Certified Nursing Assistant.” And I said, “Do you like the job??” and she said, “Well, sort of.” I thought, well, since there isn’t a record store, since the Wax Museum is dissolved, maybe this is something to look into. Moreover, my job in Kathmandu and in Ubud in Bali was ostensibly a director of health and welfare, which meant nothing. It meant that if people got bacterial dysentery, amoebic dysentery or giardia, I knew what to do. … So I had some experience with care, but it was bogus. I didn’t have a license. … I thought “Why not?” This is just a six-week course, and one of the instructors pulled me aside and said, “You have a degree. If you went to community college for nine months, you could work anywhere you wanted, as long as you wanted and go anywhere and work.” And that sounded good to me.

AD: And do you like it?

Steve Tibbetts: I like it when the night’s over and I haven’t screwed something up. It’s a little scary, and I don’t have the mental firepower that a 25-year-old nurse does. So I have to be really careful. On the other hand, sometimes I’m taking care of people who are younger than I am.

AD: Has anyone ever known who you were?

Steve Tibbetts: They’re not interested. … My co-workers are 99% West African, so we listen to their music. … They were astounded I knew Fela was. … I was playing Talking Timbuktu by Ry Cooder and Ali Farka Toure. The nurse from the other side said, “He’s singing on the language of Sahel, that’s my language.”

AD: And you also worked for the Naropa Institute?

Steve Tibbetts: The Naropa Institute was a job I picked up simply because I’d been there as a student in 1975, and I wrote a short letter to the music director, the guy in charge of the music program, and I said, “If you ever want somebody there to teach guitar or recording, let me know.” He said, “I would but we have the entire band Oregon coming this summer, so we’re filled.” But then [Oregon percussionist/sitarist] Collin Walcott died that November. So [composer and Naropa teacher] Bill Douglas called me up in tears and said, “Can you come out this summer?” And that led to me working for their study abroad programs in Nepal in the autumn and Bali in the spring.

AD: When you were at Naropa, did you cross paths with Harry Smith at all? He was there at about the same time, right?

Steve Tibbetts: He was. I was on faculty then. A lot of the faculty intimidated me, a little bit. My roommate – he was in the Fugs for a while – Steven Taylor. He was instrumental in bringing Harry Smith there. He said once – and this is a morning when I’d made myself a boiled egg and run off to my class, I was late … “I haven’t smelled burning aluminum since I lived in the West Village, and I said, “Oh, I’m so sorry, I forgot to turn of the burner,” and he said, “Do you want to go have tea with some of the other faculty, and I thought ‘It’s the least I can do.” And there I am in a room with Marianne Faithfull, William S. Burroughs, Allen Ginsburg and [Anne Waldman]. … I just thought “I’ll sit here and not say anything, and maybe I’ll be OK.” … And I think Harry Smith was there … they were discussing the second semester that was coming up and whether somebody who insisted on keeping their name in lower case was in accord with Strunk & White. This is how sophisticated they were.

AD: Are you still a practicing Buddhist?

Steve Tibbetts: Yeah. I mean, it also depends on what …  you mean by that. I just came off a couple weeks of solitary retreat in southern Colorado at place called Dorje Khyung Dzong, so I guess, yes, that makes me a Buddhist.

AD: Yeah, I think that qualifies.

Steve Tibbetts: Well, you can ask people what do you mean by that, and they’ll say, “Well, you meditate. But most of the world’s Buddhists do not meditate.

AD: Really!

Steve Tibbetts: You go to Cambodia, Sri Lanka, Vietnam, Laos, you have monks in monasteries and nuns in nunneries, but … they don’t do a lot of meditation, and the lay population is mostly involved in making offerings to the temples and hiring monks and nuns to come and do pujas in their houses to keep everything copacetic. And that was true in Tibet, too, to some extent. So, you can ask “What do you mean by that?” but it’s kind of a snarky way to begin a conversation.

AD: When I think of practicing Buddhists, I think of something like regular temple attendance or something.

Steve Tibbetts: My world is solitary practice. I try to avoid religious groups as much as possible. But there’s a certain point where if you’re going to proceed, especially in the karma kagyu school of North Indian and Tibetan Buddhism, you need to interact with teachers, temples, empowerments and instructions manuals and sadhanas and retreats and so forth. But I have three uncles and two grandfathers that were United Church of Christ pastors, so there was a little hesitancy to being part of anything, any religious groups whatsoever. They tend to just rot, like bad vegetables in the crisper, you know something’s not quite right.

AD: I went to a UCC church for several years.

Steve Tibbetts: My parents said you can come to church with us, or you can go to this evening group … It was a Sunday night group, and this was in the late ‘60s, it was run by a youth minister named Ray Gillies, and that was fantastic. We had Hare Krishna people come and talk to us, prostitutes, tattoo artists, we would spend an evening and watch the film Billy Jack and talk about it, we’d go into retreat and listen to all three sides of “Tommy” and have discussions about that. So those days were great.

AD: I didn’t know you had a religious family.

Steve Tibbets: Well, they weren’t, really. They were more, I guess you would say intellectually involved with it. My father was absolutely not interested at all after World War II. He said that that convinced him that the ministry was no place for him.

AD: So, you said you were just back from a solitary retreat. I assume that’s just you in a shack? What’s going on there?

Steve Tibbetts: It’s a cabin. It’s a closed retreat, which means I don’t see anybody as long as I’m in retreat. Usually I do a month, this was just two weeks. And I’m used to it. I like it. Actually, it’s not so much that I love retreat as the alternative is worse. That’s not really a great endorsement, is it? But it’s a good thing, if you have a practice to go do it in a solitary environment and get used to being with your own mind. It’s hard to recommend to people because … it’s a real tsunami.

AD: Do you consider your music a spiritual practice?

Steve Tibbetts: No. I considered it a way to get attention, as a 13-year-old. In those days, when somebody picked up a guitar, everybody would stop talking. So – it can have those overtones, of course, but when you start, it’s like trying to start on Park Place instead of at the beginning of the Monopoly board. It’s not going to work so well. … You gotta start at Baltic Avenue.

AD: So, if you’ve used music as a way to get attention, has it worked?

Steve Tibbetts: Oh, yeah. I’ve been very lucky, too. I was lucky to be in a musical family, and to start playing electric guitar when it was mildly subversive and seen as a revolutionary act. … Then to find a job with the Naropa Institute, where they say, “We’re going to fly you to Bali … and we’ll pay you.” That sets you right in the middle of a whole new culture and their music. The same thing with working with Nepal. So that’s an extraordinary stroke of luck on my part. And being on ECM has also been — more than lucky. I always wonder when they’re gonna wise up and say, “Wait a minute.”

AD: I think they’re pretty happy. Even though you’re younger, compared to a lot of the ECM guitarists — you’re a part of a newer set … you’ve defined one realm of the ECM sound yourself.

Steve Tibbetts: Once I was cleaning up here at the studio, I was working with a woman named Suzi Katz. I put on an album, probably a cassette tape, by Bill Connors, Theme to the Gaurdian.

AD: I like that one.

Steve Tibbetts: And she said, “Wow, that guy was really influenced by you.” I said, “No, Suzi.”

AD: The other way around.

Steve Tibbetts: “I got my vibrato and everything from him, his whole approach to space and chording and everything.”

AD: His work on the Return to Forever album [Hymn of the Seventh Galaxy] is phenomenal.

Steve Tibbetts: Before I worked in a record store, I was trying anything to break into the business side, and I worked … as a [concert] security guard. Now I’m 5’6’’ and 120 pounds sopping wet, so I’m not exactly a rugby player. But they would put me at the soundboard. And I was there for a Steve Miller concert, and the opening acts were Luther Allison and Return to Forever. Luther I knew, because he would come from Milwaukee and play at a place called the Stone Hearth. … But I stood there with my mouth hanging open watching the sound check with Chick Corea, Stanley Clarke, Lenny White and especially Bill Connors. … And then I saw … Theme to the Gaurdian, and on the back cover, he’s playing a blacktop Les Paul, and I thought “Perfect, a solo electric guitar record. Nope. It’s acoustic. But it was even better. And … this is one of the first things I mentioned to [ECM boss] Manfred Eicher … when we’re having lunch at one of our first recording sessions in Oslo. He said, “You noticed that?” and I said, “I noticed that and that you also misspelled the word “guardian.”

AD: I noticed that, but I didn’t notice the guitar.

Steve Tibbetts: It may have just been Connors messing with them. But people often flip those two letters.

AD: Did you ever talk to Bill Connors?

Steve Tibbetts: No. I wish. That’s a question I always had. Why, after Swimming With a Hole in My Body didn’t you do more work with them? There was just really no answer forthcoming. But guitar players know about him – I heard Carlos Santana talking about him.  … Where is he, why isn’t he out playing more?

AD: He did a couple more albums, later on, but not for ECM.

Steve Tibbetts: They were kind of Jeff Beck-y Blow by Blow type things. Like many artists, he may have had a run-in with ECM that soured him on the whole deal. That hasn’t happened with me. I’ve gotten along with them very well. And that’s another miracle.

AD: You and Ralph Towner.

Steve Tibbetts: You know the live [Ralph Towner album], just called Solo Concert? Can you believe that record? He’s just there with a guitar! … I gave a copy of that to Leo Kottke, and Kottke held it up and said, “Boy, it’d take a lot of moxie to do something like this.”

AD: Kottke came through town recently, and I regret to say that I did not go.

Steve Tibbetts: We usually have lunch once every seven years. And now I have an excuse to get him out again. I’ve got something to put in his hand.

AD: Have you thought about playing concerts at all, for the new album?

Steve Tibbetts: Sure, if it were the right situation. If the gig is sufficiently weird and Marc wants to do it, we’ll go. We were asked to play in Singapore. … It was wonderful. Or the festival in Kristiansand in Southern Norway called the Punkt Festival. … The idea was that musicians would play and they would be simultaneously sampled, and at the end of the evening, there would be a live exposition or concert or reordering of things played by the people who did the sampling.

AD: How did that go?

Steve Tibbetts: Well, one-off gigs are always … it usually takes two or three gigs before you really pull it together. But I trust Marc, he said it was good. “Relax, Steve, it was fun.” And they seemed satisfied, too.

AD: Is there a recording of that anywhere?

Steve Tibbetts: No.

AD: I hate to hear this answer. There were no tapers?

Steve Tibbetts: No, I don’t think they did. But I have been going through – after you put a record out, you tend to go through your “what’s left over,” and I have found some good live board tapes, and maybe they’re worth doing something with.

AD: Please do that!

Steve Tibbetts: When I put out my first record, Steve Feigenbaum [of Cuneiform Records] when I was starving to death, he would order a box of records and send me a check for $75. And in 1976, that meant a lot. That meant food for two weeks. … What a wonderful man. … He eventually put it out [as a reissue]. I’m not sure how I feel about that record, I was stoned out of my mind for most of it.

AD: I think it’s great.

Steve Tibbetts: I thought, well, the Dale Warland Singers used to put out a Christmas record. So, there’s gotta be a place that presses your record. You send them a tape, and they send you records. And that was Wakefield Manufacturing in Arizona. … So, I sent them my tape and a few hundred bucks and ended up with four boxes of records, wondering what I was gonna do. And Steve was kind enough – and also Yale Evelev at the New Music Distribution Service, Carl Bley’s company. They were great. They supported me for three for four years on sales of those records.

AD: Well, I would love to hear some live tapes. 

Steve Tibbetts: It’s live stuff, and it’s stuff that wouldn’t fit on ECM records, that I had programmed into the mix and then at the last moment, Marc or someone with some brains would say, “You know, that really doesn’t fit.” It’s nice that you’re covering “Villanova Junction” by Jimi Hendrix or “Bron-yr-aur” by Jimmy Page … but it just doesn’t’ work.” That’s why I need Marc’s brain and other people’s minds. You get a little inbred working on your own.

AD: What happened your old bass player, Bob Hughes?

Steve Tibbetts: Well, I have to get in touch with him because I’m mixing some stuff that has him prominently featured. He was a great bass player on the road. He played bass like Jack Casady, and was a great musician too. When I had trouble explaining what I wanted, he would say, “Just play it, and I’ll write it out,” and he was able to do that.

AD: I hope you get hold of him.

Steve Tibbetts: I will. We have mutual friends.

AD: Do you still listen new stuff? Are you still plugged in like you were at the record store?

Steve Tibbetts: I try to. My daughter and my son will hand something to me, or, as they do these days, they will link me to something, and say, “You should listen to this.” And also, being on ECM, I do try to stay up to date with what they’re doing, so I can have reasonably intelligent conversations with them when I’m at the office. But you’d be surprised, a lot of staff in the office listen to all sorts of music. I had a long conversation with one of the staff members about Rammstein. … You think they’d be tonsured monks listening to Arvo Pärt on infinite repeat, but there they are in the second row at a Tool concert.

AD: There’s a certain vitality to their stuff …

Steve Tibbetts: You should check out the latest Anouar Brahem record [After the Last Sky]. It’s a classic ECM record.

AD: Do they send you stuff?

Steve Tibbetts: No, I buy it.

AD: No perks, huh?

Steve Tibbetts: Well, I always write down a list of demands when I go over there. And I always do go over there. This is something I learned working in Bali and Nepal – it’s good to make personal contact with these people. So, I do wave my list of demands around, and one of them was “You send me everything, all the new stuff.” But that dropped to the bottom of the list, and I don’t’ even bring it up.

AD: I hope this interview gets them to send you some new stuff at least. … Have your kids given you anything recently?

Steve Tibbetts: My son wants me to listen to Foo Fighters more.

AD: Hmmm. OK.

Steve Tibbetts: But I like Dave Grohl as a drummer.

AD: I gotta agree with you there.

Steve Tibbetts: My daughter and I are going tonight to see Väsen, the Swedish group. [Minneapolis] is a hotbed of Scandinavian music.

AD: Does your family like your music? Do they listen to it?

Steve Tibbetts: No. I think my father was proud. He told me once that he went into a record store called Rose Records on State Street in Madison and wrote a check for a Johnny Cash record and the clerk said “Tibbetts!” and he liked that. My daughter … likes that I’m online and she can find me on Spotify. … [She] got a text saying that the record you preordered is out, and she sent that text to both my wife and my son.

AD: I did have a few questions about the new album, believe it or not Is it Close [as in near] or Close [as in shut]?

Steve Tibbetts: Your choice.

AD: What do you call it in your mind?

Steve Tibbetts: I don’t. I titled a record once that I couldn’t even pronounce the name of, the one with [Norwegian fiddler] Knut Hamre.

AD: I was going to ask you about that one too.

Steve Tibbetts: How do you pronounce that letter?

AD: I did not even think about it.

Steve Tibbetts: It’s an “A” with a circle over it. It looks cool, though. … I love that record, and I love Knut. He’s a shaman of the hardanger fiddle. But I think that instrument is meant to be solo. We did well with a couple of songs, but having been over there and being part of bits and pieces of Norwegian culture here, I understand how that instrument is meant as either an instrument you’re going to dance to the music of, or you’re going to go into a trance.

AD: Back to the new album: Am I wrong in thinking that this is the first one with multi-part compositions since Big Map Idea?

Steve Tibbetts: Yeah, I didn’t know what to do. I could have strung them all together, but it seemed to make more sense to title them separately. But then I end up with 20 different titles – that’s a long string on the back of a CD booklet. So, again, what to do? I didn’t really know. Marc is a good person to run these things past. For the record before this, Life Of, I had a set of palindromes I was going to use, and he said “Ehhh … sounds too much like Frank Zappa. I have 13 records of instrumental music, you’re going to run out of the names of Sankskrit charnel grounds.”

AD: Do you feel like the Sanskrit material is more – spiritual, for lack of a better word?

Steve Tibbetts: No, I just like the rhythm of the words, that’s all. That was for Natural Causes, and I was stuck for titles. Honestly, there’s so much that happens at the last minute.

AD: Do you still put out stuff on Frammis at all?

Steve Tibbetts: Maybe I should. Maybe the live thing. But that’s a lot packing up records and a lot of paperwork.

AD: I noticed that you have a Frammis contact email address on your website. Do you still get emails at that address?

Steve Tibbetts: I do. That’s my only public address.

AD: What kind of stuff do you get from people? What do they want to know?

Steve Tibbetts: It’s the same thing I want to tell people I’m fans of. I wanted to tell Havey Mandel how amazing he’d been to me. … I like hearing from people. Years ago, the Grateful Dead put up a notice on the back of their records: “Who are you? Where are you? What are you doing? Write to us.” And that’s on my website. So I get postcards from all over the place. It’s really fun.

AD: Were you ever a Deadhead?

Steve Tibbetts: Only by osmosis. Many of my roommates between ’68 and ’76 were serious Deadheads. So, it took me a while, but I think Live Dead is one of the 20th century’s masterworks. I like what [John Oswald] did with Grayfolded, but I like the original better. And working in a record store, I was probably the only one to play Terrapin Station. Live Dead really sums up a whole era; you can get it all right there.

AD: Are you working on new stuff? Will there be a new album coming seven or eight years down the line or sooner?

Steve Tibbetts: That would put me at 78 years old.

AD: That’s not so bad!

Steve Tibbetts: Marshall Allen just put out his first album at 101 … But I’ll just say that if this is the last album, I think it’s a great place to end up.
