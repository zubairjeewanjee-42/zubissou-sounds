## Body

Another place, another time. Raised in Puerto Rico and based in Los Angeles, dub master Pachy “Pachyman” Garcia evokes both across the expanse of his latest platter of tricked out riddims, Another Place. His sound is undeniably rooted in the classic dub techniques of King Tubby, Scientist, and Lee “Scratch” Perry, but with the new album Garcia pushes things into new territory, doubling down on the synth textures of 2023’s Switched-On, incorporating hazy lead vocals into the mix, and even crossing into post-punk territory with the protest song “False Moves.” Cut in his basement studio 333 House, it retains a homespun feel while nonetheless expanding what the Pachyman sound might be. We spoke with him via Zoom to discuss the record’s feel, how being in the studio inspires its sound, and reflect on the relationship between reggae and punk. | j woodbury

Aquarium Drunkard: Another Place is really fresh sounding. As your discography grows, the work becomes more diverse, more expansive. There are some minimal moments on this one, some post-punk, some Krautrock. 

Pachyman: It’s definitely a bunch of my influences all mixed together with me trying to figure out how to incorporate it all in a way that makes sense in this project.

AD: You obviously exist in a classical dub framework. Is it a challenge sometimes to not get stuck in a purist mindset?

Pachyman: Yeah, absolutely. When it comes to that sound, I am really obsessed with the ’68-82 era of reggae and dub. That’s what I wanted to do with this project. To me, it’s important to be a purist, to understand the foundation of that sound and where it came from. I’m always asking  “How do these people see music in order to create something like that?” But I’ve always wanted to take the genre forward too, to take it to the next place until the next generation comes. So instead of just parting ways with the genre completely, I’m using it to my advantage to just kind of work with it. I’m still trying to really hone in those skills. But I feel like I did my job. I did four records that were solid, full-on dubwise, Scientist, King Tubby, very, very pure stuff. And I feel like now I feel like I’m like, “Okay, I paid my dues in this genre.” I did my quote unquote 10,000 hours. [Laughs] 

AD: It’s funny, because classic dub still sounds like it’s from the future. 

Pachyman: It’s true, classic dub still sounds like it’s from the future. 

AD: Those guys were masters of the studio as an instrument. You did this one in your basement studio, correct? 

Pachyman: Yeah. I love the sounds that I get there. I’m a big proponent of you don’t need more stuff. You just need other ideas to hash out, other ways to bring songs to life. I’m not saying that you don’t need to buy new instruments or new microphones. That might make life easier. But you get creative when you’re stuck. I really like the sounds that I’ve discovered in the last six years. I wanted to see, “Okay, now that I did all that, how can I use these sounds that were used in that era and make new music with it?” 

AD: In addition to all your instruments and stuff, do you have a lot of art in the place? 

Pachyman: Yeah. I bring posters that I found or I’ve acquired from friends. Pieces that I’ve acquired. My wife makes a lot of art. I love her pieces, so I put ’em down there as well. Bottles of mezcal and tequila that I love that are beautifully designed. I kind of like the vibe out the spot. I recently put a Sister Corita 10 Rules poster up. It’s nice to see when you’re disconnecting from the outcome and actually putting in the work. I feel like the art brings a lot of energy into the spot and being surrounded by the things you like, you work better. 

AD: What kind of art does your wife make?

Pachyman: She’s an illustrator. She does a lot of sketches. She recently did a big piece for a friend’s party that was just kind of like a face cutout thing and for people to take pictures with, and she does all sorts of different stuff. She’s been into making ceramics lately. She does a lot of cross stitching, but she’s mainly an illustrator. She also does all the layouts for my records, all the typography and all the layout. 

AD: Any other good one posters up you might want to mention? 

Pachyman: There’s a poster by Madalyn Stefanak. She actually did a couple of my t-shirts and some flyer work. She has a poster that I acquired recently that says, “I am free to make bad art.” I’m like, oh my God, I needed to hear that. That is so liberating. It’s next to the 10 rules, they go hand and hand. You got to be able to experiment and fail. A lot of the things that I’ve done that have been successes  have come after failures, all these other failures. So many artists feel pressure when you are in the public. But we’re just humans, and we’re trying to evolve with ourselves. 

AD: Where do jams usually start for you? Guitar? Drums? 

Pachyman: I think it changes from time to time. Originally, it was always started on drums. With the first two records, I feel like everything started on the drums and I would layer on top of it. But when I wanted to explore more harmonic patterns, more keyboard melodies, more synthesizers, and eventually more vocals while I started writing on piano and guitar, so I changed it a lot. I feel like I like to sit down on the drums a lot and play rhythms and just kind of build on those, but it moves around a lot. I hardly ever write on bass, but funny enough, bass is the instrument that I feel the most comfortable playing, so I can pick it up at any moment during the process and be like, lay down a baseline, feel comfortable about it.

AD: You played in rock bands, reggae bands, all sorts of configurations. In your garage band Prettiest Eyes, you often sang and played drums, right? 

Pachyman: Yeah, I was the lead singer/drummer for that. We toured for six years, I feel like it’s the most touring that I’ve ever done. So it’s always been the most comfortable seat to be in a live setting for me. I am putting a quartet together. I’m going to be the drummer lead singer of that. 

AD: “False Moves” from the new record has a post-punk feel, and it’s about the genocide in Gaza. I can imagine a lot of people coming to your music for a sense of escape, but it must have been important for you to reflect on that. 

Pachyman: Exactly. We all react differently to all these world situations. I have a studio and music to express myself, and it can go both ways. I can go in the direction of creating an escapist utopian moment as a healing force—which I feel like I’ve done for all these records. But that was a really tough moment, when it all started happening. I was writing the record around that same time. It was a really reactionary moment of saying “this is how I feel right now.” And these lyrics are kind of all over the place, and they don’t really say anything in particular, but it’s just more about a really intense feeling that was happening in the moment. I’ve done very escapist lovers rock utopian moments. I love those kinds of songs as well. But I felt like I needed to change it up a bit. I needed to introduce a little bit of edge to the project. I felt like it was getting so soft; I was being disconnected from my own music and it shouldn’t be that way. There are a lot of moments that are intense on this record, dark feelings, gnarly, militant feeling music.

AD: For so many listeners, punk and reggae feed that need. As a kid, were both punk and reggae in the mix for you?

Pachyman: Absolutely. I grew up on an island in the Caribbean. Puerto Rico had a big hardcore scene in the ‘90s. But we also all saw reggae bands. There were a lot of skinheads, and reggae bands. It’s a small island, a small music circuit, so a lot of the same people congregate in the same shows, and a lot of the same shows happening in the same place. I used to go to punk shows and there was a band that was just a traditional ska band, and there was very classic for those two to play together. There was a lot of crossover. The thing is, I didn’t understand how deep that connection had been made in the ‘70s until way later, until I got into The Slits and Bad Brains and all of that. I was just going to my local shows and seeing those two genres participate from one another and it being totally normal. It  made sense to me. It’s revolutionary, it’s sufferers’ music, very about social issues. Working class music for working class musicians. It makes sense that it all goes together.

AD: How did you get music as a kid? Record stores? Online? 

Pachyman: There were record stores, a lot of CD stores that were in the mall. We had Hot Topic, Borders, places to find new and old music. I grew up at the start of the blogspot era. So people were just ripping records from the internet, and it was just the beginning times of MP3 players and the iPods. So people were just finding ways to rip each other’s music and share music. 

AD: You did a Lagniappe Session for Aquarium Drunkard. You covered Scientist and wrote something along the lines of how “the feeling his music inspires is a feeling of being stuck in some space station at the edge of Earth or whatever.” Have you ever crossed paths with him? 

Pachyman: I met him once at In Sheep’s Clothing. He was doing these listening events and was interviewed by Frosty from dublab. He shared all these incredible facts and cool stories about the golden age of dub. And then during the era of Vans Channel 66 streams, they were doing all these DJ sets. Frosty got me and Scientist to do a soundclash. We set mixers right in front of the other, and he was playing his mixes. I would go and play my mixes. It was just a back to back. And he had an MC singing over stuff and toasting over the songs and toasting over mine, he brought out the melodica, and it was such a good vibe. At the end, he was like, let’s go super fast. And we were just playing 30 seconds and I would dub and he would go 30 seconds, back to back. It was such a fun time.
