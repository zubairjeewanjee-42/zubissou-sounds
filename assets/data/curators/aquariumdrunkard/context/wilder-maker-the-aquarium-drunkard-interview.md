## Body

Songwriter Gabriel Birnbaum’s latest with Wilder Maker, 2025’s The Streets Like Beds Still Warm, leans into sophisto-pop shaded indie pop noir, careening from rootsy swagger into bursts of digital space jazz and ambient funk. The progressions rumble and Birnbaum narrates like a streetwise-type who’s been up way too long, finding warped nocturnal avant-pop tricks, tricked-out Broadcasty serenades, and engaging hooks around every corner.

Wilder Maker’s music has always been prone to left turns, but this set is his most expansive yet. It turns out it was drawn from sessions that found Birnbaum working in entirely new ways. He joined us to explain this new approach, his attraction to the seedy side of life, and why, trendiness be damned, Tom Waits is a “cool motherfucker.” | j woodbury 

Aquarium Drunkard: The Streets Like Beds Still Warm is a solid mix of drifty, ambient soundscape zones and a more songwriterly focus. Did finding balance between those different approaches take a fair amount of time? 

Gabriel Birnbaum: Yeah. definitely takes some sound inspiration from my ambient project, Nightwater. But the process for this album was totally different for me. I usually write on guitar, keys, and for voice, then I bring the songs to the band and we workshop them. It’s a fairly traditional process. We record with the band, live in the studio, and then do some overdubbing.

This was completely different. It was partly a creative choice, intentionally, and partly, it was logistics. It was so difficult to get the band in the studio. I had songs ready and we had dates booked to record, but no time for rehearsals. So that put me in mind of the International Anthem folks; I’ve worked with bands in the jazz and improvised world, and I was like, “I’ve been interested in that process for a while. Maybe we could do something like that?” We did those sessions fully improvised and I ended up with six hours’ worth of material. I shifted through it all, and edited it into structures. That process involved lot of dead ends and walls I hit, but ultimately, I ended up with this record and enough for two more records. 

AD: Joseph Shabason adds really marvelous, effects-laden sax on this record. How did you two link up? 

Gabriel Birnbaum: I’ve been a fan of Joseph’s playing forever. I love his solo records, and his saxophone and pedal work really inspired me to explore that (along with Josh Johnson), and his records with Nick Krgovich were definitely an inspiration for the vocal approach on here. I think I started tweeting at him at some point many years ago just trying to make contact and he was quite nice to me. Then I went to see him play a solo set at Le Guess Who when I was there playing in Debo Band and introduced myself. Now we’re labelmates on Western Vinyl so Brian from the label was kind enough to connect us and Joseph was totally game to join my process—he even ran his parts through an additional “weird” delay that he thought would be a nice fit. He makes appearances across all three records, so there’s more of that to come. I also connected with Chuck Johnson the same way, who added pedal steel and guitar to LPs 2 and 3. 

AD: There’s a film noir quality to it too, in addition to that ambient/jazz sensibility, where we start with a character and by the end of it, he’s been put through the ringer. How did the narrative come together as you sculpted the sounds? 

Gabriel Birnbaum: The three records were sequenced intuitively; I listened to each improvisation and decided what “time” it felt like, and then I put the song at that point, and then I just laid out the whole thing out hour by hour, from sunset to sunrise. So that’s across all three records. As I was writing, I was creating a chronological order with the lyrics. I had different themes I wanted to touch on, moving through time. At one point, each side of the six sides had a theme to it, which I then stretched out and changed a little bit, but that was a guiding principle. But I also wanted to tell the story in a more abstract way, thinking about David Lynch. He loves twins and doubling, and his stories aren’t always quite linear. You can find a way to justify it all and figure out a clear plot arc, but that’s usually not the best way to enjoy his films. 

AD: It’s much more a thing of recognizing the tone and texture of each part of it, the primacy of it. 

Gabriel Birnbaum: That’s also something from therapy that influenced this. With therapy, you can talk about whatever: problems, emotions, you can talk about what’s going on and what’s happening. But sometimes there are moments where you get away from words and the nice intellectual conversation about feelings and thoughts, and you somehow strike into this livewire of feelings. You tap directly into it. And my goal in making this music after experiencing that as a therapist. I want the music to work more like that and less like, “Here’s a narrative here we’re talking about.” 

AD: It does feel like culturally, we’re at this point where people really want to know what things mean definitely. But part of the attraction to noir is the inherent mystery. Did that inform the narrative remaining somewhat hazy with these songs? 

Gabriel Birnbaum: Yeah. And part of the fascination with the noir thing for me is how noir is usually set in an inherently corrupt world. The protagonist is struggling against that corruption and being corrupted by it. I think all of us are experiencing that on a daily basis. Right now, we’re living in a really corrupt, fucked up world that is trying to corrupt us at every corner and to make us part of murder and genocide. It’s very hard to know how to live with that guilt or to live without participating in those systems because it’s basically impossible. So I think that on the one hand, I get drawn to film noir because it echoes and mirrors that experience I have in my life.

I like playing around with that, the criminal underworld, the seedy underbelly of the city. I was talking to Will Stratton recently, and he was just like, “I realized all of your music is about the individual interacting with the city.” And I was like, “Damn, you’re right.” That’s what it’s always about. But to me also, this underworld thing is connecting with, for whatever reason, I guess it’s a language trick, but it’s like connecting with the actual underworld, the world of the dead also in my head. 

AD: In “Turn Signal,” you sing, “I stalk my future, it’s stalking me too/It’s after me too.” Does that tie into the non-linearity of things? What inspired that line? 

Gabriel Birnbaum: Power and control are big themes across the three records—that line just sort of came out when I was singing, but looking at it from here I think it balances the seductive idea that we’re boldly colonizing time as we live, carving out our lives like explorers, and the reality that we might not be in control of anything. It’s soothing to think that we are, but it’s a hard myth to sustain when you look more closely. And looking at that gives me a kind of vertigo that can be exciting in a perverse way. 

AD: On the topic of exploring strange territory, I really love how you reference the hollow moon on that song. Have you gone down the rabbit hole about the hollow moon or the spaceship moon conspiracy theories? 

Gabriel Birnbaum: No! Holy shit, I had no idea about this. I love conspiracy theories and mass delusions though. Have you heard of the one from the 1600s where tons of people became afraid that they were made of glass and were going to shatter into pieces? It’s so interesting to think about why these things happen when they do, what they mean. It’s also an example of what to me is a beautifully poetic and creative expression from people who generally don’t think of themselves as artists. People are really out here just world-building for their own enjoyment. 

AD: There’s a song on the record called “Fixing The Past is a Sucker’s Game.” It seems like it’s pretty illustrative of some of the themes on this record. 

Gabriel Birnbaum: I have to credit my dad. That is a direct quote from an email from my dad to me about some family stuff. We were talking about some family stuff. As I get older, obviously, I’ve got more interested in family lineage. Especially on my dad’s side—t’s just a bunch of fuckers. You get past my folks, and it’s just like, man, all Jewish mobsters, philanderers, cheats, wife beaters—not good people. 

AD: Not your crew. 

Gabriel Birnbaum: No, but that’s who I come from. It, feels the wrong word, but I’m just very “tickled” by that. I’m like, “Wow, I come from a line of criminals. That’s so interesting.”  

AD: And a record is a good place to explore those darker motivations. Those complications are wired into the human experience. In the bio, one of the artists cited in relation to this album is Tom Waits. Now, you don’t sound like him, but I feel like right now it’s a little trendy to dunk on Tom Waits. 

Gabriel Birnbaum: For sure it is. And I was very nervous including that in the text because I was like, “Everyone hates on Tom Waits right now.” 

AD: Well I want to give you props for planting a flag for him, because I think that it’s really easy to see why people dunk on Tom. Maybe it’s also semi-understandable, because he’s a distinct flavor, but it feels shortsighted. I do not think it will be the prevailing take in the future. 

Gabriel Birnbaum: I agree with you. I think history will redeem Tom Waits. I remember watching Down By Lawback in the day and being like, “This motherfucker is so cool.” Unquestionably. 

AD: I think he’s outlandish and playful in a way that isn’t necessarily fashionable. I saw Ethan Hawk talking about having to be willing to play the clown in order to get somewhere emotionally resonant. You have to wander into pretension and you have to wander into senses. I mean, did you feel that with this record and this thing coming into play at all? 

Gabriel Birnbaum: I think this is a constant battle for me to try to make myself more willing to look stupid in order to get to the places where I want to get with creating.
