## Body

Lagniappe (la ·gniappe) noun ‘lan-ˌyap,’ — 1. An extra or unexpected gift or benefit. 2. Something given or obtained as a gratuity or bonus.

Earlier this year saw the return of Rich Ruth via the Nashville based artist’s third long-player, Water Still Flows, an album we described as “absolutely audacious in its musical fusions and amalgamations… a woozy kaleidoscope of spiritual jazz, post-rock, chiming minimalism, Berlin school synth sequencers, metal and drone.” For this installment of the Lagniappe Sessions, Michael Ruth and co. reimagine the Boognish via a cover of Ween’s “A Tear For Eddie,” and take on Black Sabbath outlier “Planet Caravan,” via the band’s sophomore effort, 1970’s Paranoid. 

Rich Ruth :: Planet Caravan (Black Sabbath)

What can I even say about Black Sabbath? I find myself returning to them throughout my life more than any other classic rock era bands. The synergy of those original four members is mystical and unlike anything else in the universe. “Planet Caravan” has always been an all-time favorite. After recording this I started digging and realized there are so many other cover versions of the tune out there, no two alike. It feels like a jazz standard in that sense, open to infinite interpretations around those two meditative chords. As I built the recording, I kept hearing more voices and enlisted the majority of collaborators who help make up the Rich Ruth universe – Spencer Cullum on pedal steel, Sam Que on soprano sax, Allison De Groot on banjo, Patrick M’gonigle on fiddle, and Mikaela Davis on harp. Everyone found their spot so naturally and it became a haunting rendition of one of my favorite pieces of music. Long live Geezer, Bill, Tony, and Ozzy.

Rich Ruth :: A Tear For Eddie (Ween)

In 7th grade I downloaded each track from Ween’s Chocolate and Cheese from a sketchy file-sharing site and burned them onto a CD. I’d often listen to it in my room while I went to sleep at night. I distinctly remember waking up from a dream while “A Tear For Eddie” was playing and it felt transcendent. Perhaps a feeling I’ve been chasing for many years with my own music. Since then that particular song has always resonated from Ween’s extensive, beautiful catalog. In college my friends and I would listen to it on repeat, stoned in our dorm rooms. I’ve always been partial to the odd instrumental song on a long, weird 90s album. “A Tear For Eddie” is but one beautiful example of Ween’s steadfast devotion to doing whatever they want at all times.
