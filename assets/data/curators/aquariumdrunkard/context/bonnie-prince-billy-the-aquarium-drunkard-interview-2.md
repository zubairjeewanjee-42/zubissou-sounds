## Body

“It’s like you’re locked in a room with a wild animal.”  

Will Oldham has known Daniel Higgs for decades, first in Baltimore in the late 1990s, later putting up the Lungfish auteur whenever he passed through Louisville. So when his friend, musical collaborator and Louisville neighbor Nathan Salsburg suggested covering a Lungfish song that he’d been singing to his infant daughter, it made perfect sense to Oldham. “It’s always part of the enigma and part of the experience of listening to Lungfish and Higgs music, this sense of looking to the future for where this music will fit in, even as you’re listening to it in the moment,” says Oldham.“ There’s not a better metaphor than sitting with a child and singing it.”

The two musicians, along with fellow Kentuckian Tyler Trotter, set out to explore and interpret two Lungfish songs, “The Evidence” and “Hear the Children Sing.” The three of them extended and elongated both songs to side-long length, as they probed multiple layers of meaning in Higgs elliptical lyrics. The music is soft, ruminative, grounded in acoustic folk songs, but made hypnotic through repetition. Oldham and Salsburg brought their two daughters in to sing on “Hear the Children.” You can hear their soft, lisping voices navigating the revelatory line, “Oh, the devil is a flower plucked from a cloud.” 

By chance, our interview occurred on the day that Steve Albini died, and Oldham was clearly shaken by the loss. Their partnership went long and deep, on both a personal and professional level. The two met after a Big Black show in 1995, when both Oldham and the band stayed at the same house in Northern Kentucky and struck up an accord which led to Albini engineering a string of 1990s Palace Music albums, including Arise Therefore, Viva Last Blues and the 7” Mountain. Oldham, a Justice of the Peace, ended up officiating at Albini’s wedding. We were out of time before we really got into the topic, but you can read more about the pair’s history and relationship in this touching tribute in the Guardian.

There was plenty of time, however, to ask Oldham about the gorgeous Hear the Children Sing The Evidence, his relationship with Daniel Higgs and Salsburg and the mystic thread that connects them as artists, whether they are recording music or interpreting old songs or just sitting around talking about Star Trek. | j kelly

Aquarium Drunkard: I was reading about how Nathan used to play “The Evidence” for his daughter when she was having trouble getting to sleep. Do you have a similar connection to this music?

Will Oldham: Yes. Similar insofar as when he presented the idea, it seemed very logical and natural and appropriate. Similar, also in the way that Lungfish songs and Higgs songs seem just rife with potential application. It’s always part of the enigma and part of the experience of listening to Lungfish and Higgs music, this sense of looking to the future for where this music will fit in, even as you’re listening to it in the moment. There’s not a better metaphor than sitting with a child and singing it. I’m thinking that maybe in this lifetime I’m not going to figure out how to midwife these songs into significance, but I’m at least doing the work right now to pass it on to another generation. 

AD: He’s a really interesting figure, Daniel Higgs, who combines music and poetry and movement and other art forms. Is that something that appeals to you? The way that his art participates in all these different disciplines. 

Will Oldham: It absolutely does. I had a wonderful series of introductions to who he is and what he does. In 1998, I moved to Baltimore to live closer to my older brother and his family. Higgs was living there at the time, and he was doing a lot of visual art. I would find myself at his apartment. I had a friend who was involved with him on a visual art level. At that time, Lungfish seemed a little bit up in the air and Pupils was sort of happening. So then I saw a couple of performances during the years that I lived in Baltimore and understood that his relationship to his creativity was diverse and multifunctional. Art is life, but he applied art to life and life to art in ways that I felt are crucial and necessary but not something you would witness all that often in creative practitioners. 

It’s exemplified by the eyeballs tattooed in the palms of his hands. Which were his tools. His hands. And being able to communicate with him on banal levels that were immediately elevated.

AD: I was wondering about that. Does he communicate on banal levels? Could you watch Lost with Daniel Higgs? 

Will Oldham: Well, I’m not sure what his relationship to Lost was but he was a Trekkie. 

When I recorded a version of a song I wrote “I See a Darkness” with Johnny Cash, and Johnny Cash was on the wagon, and he put some sort of Singers Saving Grace throat spray in his mouth before singing. And he got this devilish, impish look in his eye because that was the only time he could have alcohol. That was the same look that Higgs would get in his eye when he would talk about Star Trek. It seemed like it was a mythology that he took very seriously, even though he knew there was a ridiculous level to it. 

But yeah, there was a period of time when he was beginning to explore performing solo. He made his way through Louisville a few times. It was a time when I was exiled here because I was trying to oversee the care for my mom, who had Alzheimers. So I couldn’t travel for fun. I only travelled for work. I was very reliant on anybody who came to town, and when he would come to town, he would stay with me. 

I have musician friends who I am friends with because I love their music so much, but outside of the musical experience, I’m sometimes grasping at straws. And then sometimes it’s the opposite. But a wonderful thing with Higgs is that everything is interconnected. So sitting on the front porch and talking about Star Trek informed what I get out of his music and what I get out of his visual art. I’m like, I’m so glad I have this conversation with you about Star Trek because rather than weakening my relationship it strengthens that. 

AD: I was listening to the two originals this morning as I was getting ready to do this and your covers are reasonably faithful except for one thing. They’re way longer. You almost never hear really long tracks with vocals and lyrics on them. 

Will Oldham: That’s working on the lullaby idea. Recording these was such a fantastic experience. We planned that we would go through the forms at least three times and potentially more. Each time is warming up for the next time through the lyrics. And I know I’m going to be strict with the lyrics. I’m not going to improvise any lyrics. If my mind is changing and my perspective is changing about what I’m saying, I always have to go back and find it in the lyrics. I can’t do anything else. Melodically, I’m going to go somewhere, but lyrically I’m going to stay right there. 

Dan’s words are words that you can do that with.

AD: They’re pretty open-ended. 

Will Oldham: Yeah, they’re open-ended but there is so much in there that they really reveal themselves to you upon repetition. It’s like you’re locked into a room with a wild animal. I know I can’t leave this room until I’ve tamed this wild animal. I’m still fighting or cajoling or massaging these words and trying to own them. 

If you’re singing somebody else’s words, the only reason to do it is you think you have a reasonable chance at creating some authority with your voice with that. And Dan’s words are intimidating because they’re so unlike anybody else’s. No one else writes like that. With Tortoise, years ago, we did “Love Is Love” and “Love Is Love” is probably one of the most accessible lyrics in the Lungfish catalog. If we’re going to get into Lungfish, we can start with “Love Is Love.” But with anything else, it kind of requires that mastication that we were able to do with these long forms and also building in the spaces between the lyrics allowed for a regrouping. The length was a crucial part of the identity of this project.

AD: How long did it take you to get these pieces to where you thought they should be? Did you have to go through it a lot? Or were there only a few takes? Maybe one take?

Will Oldham: The way that I like to work in general is for everyone to do as much preparation as possible separately. Then one of the things that the recording engineer has to do and gets to do is capture the interaction between people. So it wasn’t one take. Each one, we probably did three or four takes, and I don’t think we were working with the final takes in either case. But it’s part of the process. And they’re long, so yeah. But at the same time that everything else is happening, you’re listening to the other players, and that’s a part of the experience, part of discovering. And that’s what I think is special about making a record, the opportunity to capture something actually happening.

AD: Tell me about your relationship with Nathan. I know you’ve done some recordings before and you live close to each other. How did you meet and what connects you musically?

Will Oldham: I think we met when both of us were living in New York City in the late 1990s. I moved from New York to Baltimore. He was in New York at the time. He’s somebody I could dive into musical conversations with pretty easily, no matter where we are or what we’re doing. Those kinds of conversations for me are fewer and further between than one might hope or expect. At least to that extent and at that level. 

Over the years, we’ve found different ways of collaborating. I think maybe the first time was in 2006. He was putting together a show or a series of shows at Joe’s Pub over a weekend. He asked me to open for Hazel Dickens who is a protest folk singer from West Virginia. The occasion was celebrating the centennial of Alan Lomax’s birth. So he gave me a couple of hours of Lomax recorded songs and ballads and said, “Why don’t you put together a set from these songs?” That was our first collaboration. Then I think he put together a comp of E.C. and Orna Ball songs, and I contributed a song to that. Then gradually here and there, and especially when he moved back to Kentucky, he started renting office space in a room in my house in probably 2006 or 2007.And then more in the past decade, we’ve found other projects. There’s an important waterway in Louisville called Beargrass Creek. There was a guy who made a documentary about it, and asked us to write a song, and we did that and recorded another song about a creek for that. 

During lockdown, we recorded a couple of Jerry Jeff Walker songs, around the time Jerry Jeff Walker died. As well, we did these three great murder ballad songs. And then he and Joanie played on the Bonny Prince Billy record I Made a Place.

For someone who’s introverted—myself—and sometimes socially awkward—myself—I find that music can be a huge, huge, huge vehicle for human connection. There are people I want to spend more time with and I want to get to know more and I want to get deep with, and music is the way to do that. To say, would you work on this piece of music with me. Thankfully, he’s somebody that we continue to find ways to do that. 

AD: I spoke to Joan a couple of years ago, and they were living in a converted tree nursery. It sounded idyllic. 

Will Oldham: Yeah, they were living on a converted nursery. It was completely idyllic. We would visit them with our daughter. We would visit them during lockdown out there, and we were some of the only humans they saw for weeks at a time. And they said that seeing us with our daughter inspired them to take the plunge. 

Then after having a daughter, living out there in the sticks, then they realized that life was hard when you’re living out in the middle of nowhere with nobody around. And then they were trying to decide if they should move to Michigan where Joan has roots or move into town. Fortunately, they decided to move into town, and they lived about a half mile away from us. But then they had all this mold in the house, so right now, they’re in Michigan and they’re trying to decide if they can live in that house. They have a place to stay in Michigan so I think their future is up in the air. They’re not out in the sticks anymore, though they’re in the sticks in Michigan.

AD: He’s got an interesting background. He’s got almost a musicological knowledge of American folk music and also this real mystical Jewish thread to what he does. And I think that your work is like that too. You draw on this vernacular tradition but also there’s a real undercurrent of spiritualism.

Will Oldham: Yeah, and we come together with Higgs and Lungfish there. That’s really doubling down on the mysticism bond we share.

AD: It’s interesting because music and religious experience trigger the same parts of the brain. It’s very linked.

Will Oldham: I have always had that. But maybe two weeks ago, I ran into a woman. I probably haven’t seen her since we were both about ten years old. We were in fifth grade together. She said, one of the main things I remember about you from when we were ten years old is that you were the first person I met who identified as atheist. I didn’t remember that. 

But especially in the 1990s when I was starting to make records and write songs, I felt compelled to try to wrestle with my lack of affiliation with publicly recognized theism of any sort. Understanding what I felt through and from and with music was what I was observing. Sometimes I would observe some of the more powerful applications of music in a religious context, which I still won’t get down with. I never will. Especially now, I know more than ever it’s not right, and it’s not good, and it’s a human weakness to have constructed religion to try to understand these needs and these thoughts and these passions and these drives. It’s something that we should have discarded at some point. 

Still it was important to me to try to use the word god. I was trying to not feel alienated from those who would find uplift and catharsis and revelation in music. It’s there. But also musicians have done a fairly poor job of assembling emotions and thoughts into something cohesive the way that religious entities and institutions have.

So, there’s something here. There’s something here that’s between all this. But god is always a safe word because it’s a shared word, but it’s still ultimately triggering and alienating to a lot of people.

AD: I did want to ask you about Tyler because I’m not familiar with him. Who is that and how do you know him? 

Will Oldham: Tyler Trotter and Nathan grew up together. They knew each other before their voices broke, I’m pretty sure. I got to know Tyler on a plane. We ended up sitting next to each other, weirdly, on a plane going to Europe. I think there was a fire on the plane, so then we had to force land in Frankfort Germany. So we had a long time to talk together and realize that we had a lot of mutual friends in Louisville. At the time, he was mostly doing sound for a group called the California Guitar Trio, which an instrumental, acoustic prog group. He asked me, do you want to sing a couple of songs? So I think I sung a couple of songs on one of their records. 

He was at the beginning of getting involved in a really exciting time in craft beer in the world. And for me, I got involved through him, it was exciting. Bookstores were on the decline. Record stores were on the decline. And I was like, what do I do in these cities? And craft beer was intriguing. People were experimenting really heavily and there was this really intense and wonderful community that was bubbling with ideas and creativity. Tyler was my principal guide through that world.

AD: Is he a beer maker?

Will Oldham: He opened first a store that sold esoteric beers to Louisvillians. And then opened three or four stores that each approach selling of alcoholic beverages in wonderful and creative ways. He then started playing in a band called Watter with a guy named Zak Riles who is also in a group called Grails out of Portland, Oregon. And the drummer was Britt Walford who was in Slint. So they played. 

There’s a brewer called Stillwater and at one point we wrote and recorded a song, and Brian Strumke created a beer and we put the song and the beer out together We called it Bonnie Stillwatter. At one point we did a tour of New York City where we played eight beer pubs where this beer was being served, and we just set up and played two songs. That song and a cover of Aphrodite’s Child, the song about the Four Horseman of the Apocalypse. 

AD: Did you have a beer at every pub?

Will Oldham: Oh yeah.

AD: So you were pretty ripped by the end?

Will Oldham: We were in the back of a van and Jeremy DeVine who runs Temporary Residence drove us around all day. It was so much fun. 

So Tyler is deep into music. Now Tyler’s in Om. He has been for the last two or three years. 

AD: I wanted to asked you about having the kids on this record and what they made of it. Especially that lyric “The devil is a flower plucked from a cloud.” Were they freaked out by that or did they think it was cool or what?

Will Oldham: They thought it was cool. Nathan and Joan’s daughter is younger, so for her, it meant less. Our daughter was probably four when we tracked. She probably shared a little bit of that glint that Johnny Cash and Dan Higgs had. She loved rehearsing. We did rehearse that a lot in the car leading up to it. Joan and Nathan’s daughter couldn’t quite get it when we tried to track them together, so we had to wait and retrack her voice. She had microphone shyness as well, and our daughter was trying to coach her. 

It felt wonderful. You know, I trust Higgs. I trust the words. Like I was talking about in the beginning investing in…putting these seeds in their minds. I trust where these words came from and where they’re going and I want them, when they’re older, to be able to think about how they had those words. 

AD: With kids that age, you can give them a phrase like that, and it goes into their head and it can go into all these weird and wonderful directions. They have the most amazing take on things. I can imagine my son, when he was little, really fixating on a line from a song and making all these wild pictures that you wouldn’t anticipate as a grown-up.

Will Oldham: No exactly, I’m so grateful that my folks had an interesting record collection. They would make interesting mix tapes for long road trips. And I know that the lyrics that I heard, that I memorized, without even thinking about it, I’m so grateful that those are part of the infrastructure of my brain.

AD: You just have the one daughter?

Will Oldham: Yep. 

AD: Is she musical? Do you think she’s going to want to do what you do?

Will Oldham: I don’t even know what I do. And I don’t know that what I do will even be recognizable by the time she’s ready to do it. She’s very physical, so she likes to dance. I appreciate her relationship to music, but it’s so hard to say what music is now and what it will be in 10 years. But I don’t even know. At worst, filling her life with thoughtful music will only be good for her. 

AD: She may still end up liking some very popular music. That was my experience. 

Will Oldham: She’s into the Trolls soundtrack, which is really hard to listen to.

AD: I remember when my son came home from daycare the first time, and he was all about Pokemon. After a 100% organic infancy. But what can you do? They have to confront the world.

Will Oldham: Yeah. She’ll mention…I have faith by the time it matters that nobody will care about Taylor Swift, but she has mentioned the name once or twice. I have a feeling that…I don’t think Taylor Swift has that kind of reach or that kind of legs, but I could be wrong. 

AD: But someone else will come along.

Will Oldham: Eventually, someone else will come along.

AD: I wanted to ask you what you’re going to do with this music besides releasing it on record. Are you going to tour it?

Will Oldham: That’s a great question. We haven’t discussed it. I’m in the process of deepening the relationship with No Quarter. We’re having a really good time with this record and potentially some more things coming down the road, so that’s cool. Pinning down where Nathan and Joan are going to live will be a big deal. I can imagine finding the right scenarios for presenting the music. It’s a strange repertoire. So finding the right scenarios is something that both Nathan and I are open to.

AD: You can’t just slip a 20-minute track into the set list. 

Will Oldham: Yeah. And you wouldn’t necessarily want to. You might want to. When I made this record with Bitchin’ Bajas and the lyrics were all cookie fortunes. Live we could make them a two minute song or an eight minute song. And also, we would try to make a point of eating Chinese when we could and getting new fortunes and then at soundcheck trying to come up with a new song or two. Plus I had my library of fortunes which I had collected. So I can imagine that if we made a tour specific we would maybe try to learn two or three more songs in this way, ideally Higgs or Lungfish or Pupils songs, and treat them similarly and try to explore a live performance in that way, where we have these but we would do a couple more and the whole experience would be about exploring these lyrics and these melodies together. 

AD: Just to wrap up, I know you’re doing a lot of things. Are there any other projects you want to mention on the burner about to come out?

Will Oldham: There’s a record that’s just finishing now. But awkwardly having a release ready now means releasing it in the fall and everyone’s concerned that the U.S. elections will significantly handicap people’s bandwidths. Individuals’ bandwidths and media bandwidths. People feel that it might be a disservice to the recording. We might wait until the top of the year, unfortunately.

Over the past six to nine months, I’ve started to think about a record that’s Nathan related. There’s a bunch of pre-war Kentucky recordings where there are 100s of amazing songs that reveal a vibrant musical culture in Kentucky, especially in southeastern Kentucky, that was essentially annihilated by the coal industry. The bright side is that there are these recordings and the songs are undeniable. And so I’m thinking that over the next couple of years, of trying to arrange a group of songs in such a way where they’re not …I’m not trying to present these songs as public domain material. I’m trying to present these songs in such a way that most people would hear them and it wouldn’t matter where they came from. It does matter where they came from. But for the audience experience, it doesn’t have to matter. They won’t necessarily sound like old-time music. It’s in the early stages, especially with the delays. But it’s such a crazy world and getting crazier all the time. 

AD: Is there anything else you want to talk about?

Will Oldham: A cool thing. So I do have this new record, there’s a song I’ve been playing over the past year and a half and it’s a song that I’m now calling “London May.” Do you know who London May is?

AD: No.

Will Oldham: He’s a drummer, and I got to know him in the 1980s, before I played music. All my friends were musicians. We were all huge friends of Glenn Danzig and the Misfits and Samhain which was his band at the time. At one point, he invited a Louisville band to do a week of opening slots for Samhain. I was invited along as sort of a roadie/photographer because I took pictures at the time. The person that we became closest with was London May, the drummer. He’s from Baltimore. And I’m still friends with him. In the late 1980s, he gave me a 7” of his band, Reptile House. Higgs was the lead singer in Reptile House on Dischord. It’s cool that these relationships have continued. I was talking to London on the phone just the other day. He made a horror movie recently and asked me if I would write a song for it. So I wrote a song in which the lyrics line up, it’s an acrostic lyric, so the left hand column of the lyrics spell his name three times. It’s a neat tie-in.
