## Body

Dark Canyon is the nom de plume of Chicago-based multi-instrumentalist/producer/engineer Mike Novak. In September of 2022, Dark Canyon released their debut S/T album via Nudie Records. It’s a sprawling work of maximalist musicality, grandiose and imagistic, paying homage to spaghetti-western scores and library music from the sixties and seventies. Apart from haunting baritone guitars and sweeping marxophone flourishes, S/T features string arrangements, horn sections, and choir ensembles — all tracked with vintage outboard gear and recorded to analog tape. In June of 2023, Dark Canyon followed up S/T with a Lagniappe Session for Aquarium Drunkard, featuring Novak’s reimagining of some Lee Hazelwood classics. 

Long Days, Pleasant Nights is Dark Canyon’s sophomore record, and it continues to sonically explore the same western terrain of S/T, the kind of desolate topography one might find in a Sergio Leone film. But with Long Days, Pleasant Nights, Dark Canyon takes a step back from the elaborate orchestration featured on S/T, instead taking a bare-bones approach to instrumentation. The result is a far more contemplative record, decidedly sparse and pensive. 

While Long Days, Pleasant Nights may be the most minimalistic offering from Dark Canyon yet, it doesn’t sacrifice the inherent cinematic quality of Novak’s music. If Dark Canyon’s S/T was the soundtrack to the climatic saloon shoot-out from some lost spaghetti-western film, then Long Days, Pleasant Nights is what you’d hear in the quieter scenes, the ones where gunslingers post up camp for the night, gazing at a canopy of stars while campfire flames dance across cactuses.

We recently sat down with Novak via Zoom to discuss writing and recording Long Days, Pleasant Nights, becoming a father, the art of improvisation, Stephen King’s The Dark Tower series, and much more. The following is a transcript of our conversation, edited for clarity. | e hehr

Aquarium Drunkard: Compared to the last Dark Canyon album, Long Days, Pleasant Nights is much more meditative and minimal. Was that a conscious choice on your part in writing and recording it, or did the songs organically develop as such?

Mike Novak: My intent with Long Days, Pleasant Nights was to make it more guitar-centric than the S/T album. Guitar is my primary instrument, and I wanted to use it to explore the same sonic realm of the first album but in a more stripped-down way; I wanted to have fewer moving parts in the arrangement and keep the guitar in the foreground. Many of the songs on Long Days, Pleasant Nights came about from experimenting with playing through two guitar amps with a stereo delay, which inspired the record’s sonic quality. One of the primary amps I used on the record was a vintage Magnatone amp from the early 1960s with this super lush vibrato. Embracing that amp’s signature qualities helped shape the record’s palette. I wound up using that setup for all the electric guitars on this album. But in general, I was hoping to get to a position where I had material that was composed and recorded in such a way that I could pull off playing it live without having to hire over fifteen musicians.

The meditative aspect mostly has to do with where my headspace was at while I was writing and recording. I started working on the album around November of last year, which was right around the time that my wife and I found out that we were going to have a baby. In some ways, the music is reflective of that. The title track, “Long Days, Pleasant Nights,” was totally unplanned; I stumbled into those chords while thinking about the changes happening in my life, being a new dad, and what that would mean. The response to those thoughts was this sudden burst of improvisation that I was able to capture while the tape was rolling, and that wound up being the song.

AD: So far, Dark Canyon has primarily been a studio project. Now that you have recorded material conducive to a traditional live band arrangement, are you hoping to start playing out more?

Mike Novak: Yeah, the live iteration I have right now is a trio: drums, bass, and guitar. We played last month in Chicago, and it was awesome. The response from the audience and the venue was super positive, so that was really encouraging. I want to start transferring the studio material into the live arena more in the future.

Dark Canyon is playing in Chicago next month at Maxx McGathey’s record release show. He has a new album Nudie Records is putting out called Imaginary Eyes, which I worked on. Maxx arranged the flute, horns, and string parts on Dark Canyon’s S/T. He’s one of my oldest collaborators, so it feels like a full-circle moment to be playing a show with him where we’re both playing new music.

AD: Your last record was both an homage and an update on the classic film scores of composers such as Ennio Morricone and Luis Bacalov. For Long Days, Pleasant Nights, what were your main points of inspiration? Were you continuing to draw from the same source material as S/T, or were you referencing different material?

Mike Novak: There were a few soundtracks I found myself revisiting a lot. One was Bruce Langhorne’s score for The Hired Hand (1971), which I believe Aquarium Drunkard previously covered in the Videodrome column. That record was on repeat quite a bit while working on Long Days, Pleasant Nights. It helped inspire the flute parts  — all that super trippy, delayed-out stuff. The general pacing of that soundtrack is very cool.

The other soundtrack I kept coming back to was Neil Young’s score for Dead Man (1995). That was a huge reference point for Long Days, Pleasant Night. I saw these videos on Criterion Channel of Young performing the soundtrack live on a soundstage along with a projection of the film, and that inspired my mindset for writing the new album. For some of the songs, I had an idea of an arrangement, and I knew which sounds I wanted and where. But a lot of the songs were improvised based on a general musical motif.

AD: That’s interesting because the arrangements on S/T are so dense and carefully sculpted. What was it like recording a record that was largely the result of spur-of-the-moment improvisation? Did you find it to be more freeing or more difficult?

Mike Novak: I had to trust myself more and commit to whatever happened in the moment. With the S/T album, it was easier to hide behind the arrangements, and there was less space for spontaneity. Because Long Days, Pleasant Nights is so raw and exposed, I had to trust my instincts more as a musician. It was more about focusing on the feeling of the moment and less on performing a composition.

AD: You had a few other musicians perform on the album. Were they allowed to improv as well, or did you give them some semblance of direction?

Mike Novak: Yeah, I live in Chicago, where I record and mix all the Dark Canyon material. There are a handful of Chicago musicians that I really enjoy working with, and I brought them in for a few of the songs on Long Days, Pleasant Nights. I’m not an overbearing arranger, so I like to work with musicians in the room and elaborate on ideas to a point where it feels like it’s more of a collaboration rather than like, “Play this part exactly as written.”

AD: Although Long Days, Pleasant Nights is far sparser than the S/T release, they’re both intrinsically cinematic. For me, they conjure up images of dusty desert towns, outlaw cowboys, and Old West saloons. 

Mike Novak: The visual landscapes are definitely similar. I pictured Long Days, Pleasant Nights as the soundtrack for a desert stroll or voyage—whatever you want to call it. I was less concerned about cowboys and southwest border towns and more interested in exploring a metaphysical world that still operates within the framework of western archetypes. 

When I was working on the album, I had just finished reading The Dark Tower series by Stephen King. Those books have so much nuance; there’s so much to talk about there. But in short, if you haven’t read it, the series revolves around a lone gunslinger who experiences interdimensional travel. I was trying to express aspects of The Dark Tower series through the music on Long Days, Pleasant Nights, something adjacent to the world King created that builds on similar themes in a musical way. Apart from the music itself, I included a lot of subtle references to The Dark Tower series in the song titles and the album artwork.

AD: Right on. So there’s a bunch of easter-eggs hidden throughout the album for fans of The Dark Tower series?

Mike Novak: Yeah, sort of. I guess you could say that.

AD: I heard this album actually got to Stephen King. Is that true?

Mike Novak: Yeah, it’s pretty wild. I had hopes that I could somehow find a way to get Stephen King to listen to the album — just to let him know he inspired me and that his work is still inspiring to so many different types of artists. That was my far-fetched goal: get the album to Stephen King! And through a lucky series of circumstances, it happened. I feel super honored. Not only did he listen to it, but he gave me a very nice and very concise piece of feedback, which was something along the lines of, “That was actually quite good.”

AD: Amazing. There’s your hype-sticker for the hard-copy release: “It’s actually quite good.” Stephen King. 

Mike Novak: Yeah, man, it’s still pretty surreal to me.

AD: On that note, are there plans for a hard-copy release of Long Days, Pleasant Nights?

Mike Novak: The album will be released on cassette by a Texas-based label called Hippie Scum. I’ve never released music on cassette tape before.

AD: How do you feel about releasing an album on cassette for the first time?

Mike Novak: I think it’s pretty cool. I have a cassette player in my Saab convertible, so I’m stoked to blast it on that. 

AD: Any plans for a vinyl release?

Mike Novak: Not yet, but I’d certainly be open to it if there was a demand for it. 

AD: I know Long Days, Pleasant Nights is just coming out, but are you already thinking about future Dark Canyon plans?

Mike Novak: I’m interested in collaborating with more musicians, specifically singers. Dark Canyon has primarily been an instrumental project since it began. Moving forward, I’d like to explore more “old-trail” song-type music with lyrics and vocals. I guess you could call it “gunslinger ballads,” something in the vein of old Marty Robbins recordings. But for now, I want to keep exploring this trio sound. The energy of the trio in the live setting has opened doors to a different side of Dark Canyon that feels really good. I think there’s a lot there to further hone in on.
