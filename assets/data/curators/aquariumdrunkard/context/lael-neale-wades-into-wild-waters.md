## Body

“I once followed blindly but now I can see,” Lael Neale intones on her fourth record–and third in the space of a fruitful five-year period–Altogether Stranger. This is one of many revelations and reflections on her return to Los Angeles having spent several years away from its stifling chaos in the respite of her family’s farm in rural Virginia. The songs astutely capture this internally fraught period with colloquial eloquence. Altogether Stranger simultaneously confronts the relentless noise of city living and provides a much-needed sanctuary away from it through its comforting intimacy. Neale’s latest recalls the scope of Lou Reed, Connie Converse and Suicide, resulting in her most tonally dexterous body of work.

Gaining clarity in how she perceives her surroundings, noted in the aforementioned lyric from her single “Wild Waters,” parallels Neale’s pursuit to find her voice, in her musical expression, which she deftly cultivated on her 2020 LP, Acquainted With Night. Aided by musician and producer Guy Blakeslee in finding her sound on that record, the pair continue to successfully expand upon her wondrous sonic foundation of omnichord-led dream-pop with delicate flourishes of mellotron and pulsing Motorik beats that immediately feel at home in the inviting celestial world. It’s a world that feels timeless and contemporary, ethereal and menacing; Altogether Stranger further demonstrates Lael Neale’s enduring artistic vitality. Speaking recently with Aquarium Drunkard, Neale expands on eerie foreshadowing in her lyrics, embracing playfulness in painting, how she grounds herself on tour and more. | z hedderman

Aquarium Drunkard: Hey Lael, are you having a nice day? 

Lael Neale: Yeah, I got back to my family’s farm last week, so I’ve just been laying low in preparation for the upcoming tour. We’re leaving in two days.

AD: Yeah, you’re heading to Europe soon, I’m hoping to catch your Dublin show. With each album, I’ve enjoyed how your sound is steadily getting bigger. How was it making Altogether Stranger? 

Lael Neale: It was good. It’s almost like each album is a book, or lives in its own environment. [Altogether Stranger] was bookended by me and Guy [Blakeslee] moving to Los Angeles and now upon its release, we’ve moved away from Los Angeles. In that way, it’s very much a product of having returned to the city and processing what that’s like. It was all written in our home that we had just moved into in October or November of 2023 and then the recording  happened pretty quickly. We did it all in the span of a few weeks, in June 2024, I guess. It basically filled-up our entire time living in Los Angeles. Then just last week, we drove all the way across the country back to Virginia. 

AD: Did writing these songs help with the adjustment of being back in Los Angeles? Or did it intensify feelings of isolation or longing to be back in rural Virginia? 

Lael Neale: Yeah, it’s funny. It’s sometimes hard to tell if it makes it worse by focusing on it or if it makes it better by helping to process it. Focusing on things that you don’t want to experience probably isn’t a very good thing to do but it actually helps me when I’m writing because it allows me to step outside of myself a little bit and be more of an observer. In that sense, it makes me detach a little bit from feeling such acute emotions. It does help because singing alone, there’s something about the mechanism of your voice working that can calm your nervous system and be cathartic. Ultimately, yeah it definitely helped but it did also reveal to me that maybe I didn’t want to be in the city for much longer. 

AD: With that sense of detachment and being in the role of an observer, did that make you acutely aware of things that had changed within the city that you had maybe forgotten about while you were away that made you engage with your surroundings differently this time?

Lael Neale: Yeah, definitely. I was much more focused on some of the issues I was seeing that I just kind of let go. I just didn’t let them get to me when I lived there before. In some ways, I feel like it got worse, too. I can’t tell if I got more sensitive or it seemed worse. It just seemed more chaotic and I think people seem to be paying less attention and are kind of zonked out. I don’t know whether it’s phones or weed or what! When you’re observing something, you’re paying attention to every single detail, so of course you see all of the blemishes and wrinkles and issues with where you’re living. Things like seeing trash on the sidewalk, I would try to pick it up when I saw it. I did things to help mitigate some of the issues that were going on in my tiny, minuscule little way. You know, picking up a plastic bottle here and there. It makes me feel better.

AD: In the press materials, you mention different conflicts between city vs country, humanity vs technology and isolation vs society. I was thinking about those groupings and the association with them; one is rooted in a sense of peace or comfort while the other conjures feelings of uncertainty or being overwhelmed. At this stage of Altogether Stranger’s life cycle, over a year removed from the writing and recording, revisiting them as you prepare for its release and playing the songs live, are you seeing things in your writing that came through on a subconscious level that’s only revealing itself to you about that period now?

Lael Neale: Yeah, that’s a cool question. There are some things that almost felt precognitive, in some ways. Even, say on “Tell Me How To Be Here,” the line “the choppers casting shadows above brave houses,” definitely felt eerie when the fires in Los Angeles were happening because that’s what people were experiencing. There were helicopters hovering, trying to spread water and saving places. That felt kind of precognitive or something. Then, of course, the fact that we’re moving away and “Down On The Freeway” being like “Get me out of the city.” All of these things, I think songs are always working with your subconscious. I’m sure on some level, I was telling myself what I wanted my future to look like through the songs and they help create your future. 

In terms of the city changing or my relationship to it changing, they felt more and more true as time went on. The issues I was having felt more acute when we left and I felt a lot of relief driving away. 

AD: Sleep and the inability to sleep are a recurring aspect to the album, as well. I believe you wrote a lot early in the morning. The last couple of years since Acquainted With Night have been super busy for you with writing, recording and touring. I wondered if part of that inability to sleep came from a busyness of the mind because you’ve been so immersed in all aspects of your work. Is that fair to say? 

Lael Neale: I think the writing actually really helps me with that. To me, sleeplessness is kind of like wasted creative energy. When I’m not creating, I will have excessive anxiety and racing thoughts and thoughts with nowhere to go. If I’m in the act of writing or even painting, then all of those things have an outlet. Then I’m released from those nighttime ruminating thoughts, you know. Sometimes I think that’s when anxiety is knocking on someone’s door, it could be that it’s just creativity trying to break through. If that makes sense? It’s something knocking on the door of your mind wanting to be expressed. For me, at least, it always helps. It’s like a release valve when I’m writing. Honestly, a lot of the traveling and other parts of making music now have really nothing to do with creating. That can help be a helpful kind of distraction also, but I think the writing actually helps. It keeps me up in the sense that that’s what’s maybe trying to come through or be expressed.

AD: With the differences of writing music compared to painting for you, I’d love to hear about the differences or the similarities in how you’re able to express yourself with your visual art and music.

Lael Neale: The painting is a relief and a release from caring so much. It’s very lighthearted. It helps me get back in touch with the way that I did things as a child. It’s very pure, I’m not trying to show work for thousands of dollars in a gallery situation, you know, so it’s very lighthearted. Whereas, with the music I tend to take it a little bit more seriously for better or worse. I put more pressure on myself with the music, and so it’s not as lighthearted. I also just think that the expression of writing and singing is more cathartic or more intense with emotions than the painting is. The painting is just playful and fun and the music can be a little bit intense or carry some weight. 

AD: Especially because with your music, it has a voice and people are listening intently to your words.

Lael Neale: Exactly, and I really care about the words, so much. There’s a lot of attention and care and putting together puzzle pieces with that. 

AD: Do you have particular albums or artists that you listen to while painting? 

Lael Neale: When I’m painting, I don’t really listen to any music but if I do put it on, I always listen to Emahoy Tsege Mariam Gebru. She’s an Ethiopian nun who makes instrumental piano music. I love it because I can’t really listen to any words when I’m painting, it can kind of feel distracting. I’m very much someone that can do one thing at a time. 

AD: I’ve always really loved how your work, in a musical and lyrical sense, is so intimate and evocative. There’s a timelessness to the things you sing about and I enjoy how the songs can sound so otherworldly and as though they’ve been discovered after being hidden away for 50 years or so. This album continues to see you expand the tonal palette, there’s more Mellotron and motorik beats incorporated throughout the arrangements.  I’d love to hear about working with Guy and the conversations you have about what tones and textures you want to experiment with and introduce to the songs. 

Lael Neale: Yeah, I mean, that is definitely the realm of Guy. He’s the sonic wizard. I have much less understanding of that, so really my job is to write the lyrics and I usually have my main instrument that I’ve written the song on. I’ll record that straight as it is; me and the Omnichord or me and the guitar. We’ll have that recording and then Guy will start putting layers over it. His obsession, this past record, was definitely the mellotron and it just seemed to suit the songs so well. The album, and these songs, would not be at all what they are without his contribution. He’s able to take them to dimensions that I would have no idea how to even do that. But when I hear it, it sounds so natural. It’s how the song was meant to sound. That’s what it seems like our evolution in making albums together is, I trust him more and more to add on to the songs. But then, you know, we are constantly in conversation and I definitely don’t hesitate to say, “Hey, hold back on the guitar jam-out solo!”

AD: I’m thinking about what you said about creativity and anxiety and needing to allow the ideas out when they arrive. When you’ve been on tour, do you find yourself being particularly inspired by experiencing new places and cultures? Or you find yourself during that time longing to return to an anchor to focus on writing?  How do those periods of flux affect you?

Lael Neale: I’m sure it does, because everything in my life impacts what I do. The touring part really helps me, though. It’s like an arrow drawing back in a bow or something. It fuels my desire to make more music because it reminds me that that’s what I really love to do. Touring and travelling is definitely a challenge, I’m much more comfortable, or content is the word, to be at home writing every day. That’s my ideal state but if I spend too much time at home, I can fall into these apathetic days that just run into each other and I don’t really have the tension to create stuff. So, I think being on tour really helps remind me that the writing part is something I want to get back to. And, of course, I get inspired by travelling and it’s often maybe the more unpleasant situations that are even more inspiring than the really nice, perfect experiences. Like say, ‘There From Here’, that song was born of being trapped in airports and the air conditioning and smells and sounds that I don’t want to be around. The kind of cattle prodding nature of being in mass groups of people herded through lines. I’m very inspired by unpleasant situations! 

It comes to mind when I think about touring and how it feeds the art. On the other side of that, though, we spent a couple days in Dún Laoghaire the last time we were in Ireland. That was one of my most memorable places that we toured. We stayed by the water and it was drizzling foggy rain. That was towards the end of that tour, we had a couple days there and that was a really dreamy time. That really feeds the romantic in me and I think on this tour we’ll have a little bit of time to spend some time in each of these places and so I’m looking forward to that. 

AD: Nice! On “Tell Me How To Be Here,” even though that’s about being back in LA, there’s a line: “It’s just a little lonely without the ground below me” which made me also think of not having a solid base while on tour. What are some of the rituals you have while touring to maintain a sense of grounding and sanity? 

Lael Neale: Yeah, I mean you’re reminding me that I probably should! I do Qigong every day which is a breathing and stretching exercise that you can pretty much do anywhere because I normally do yoga but you can’t really do that on tour. I do that and I intend to meditate every day but that can kind of drop-off during the busy periods but our main ritual that really helps centre us in each of the places is finding the best coffee shop that we can! We’ll even pick a hotel room based on how close it is to the fancy third wave coffee shop. That really helps because we write in the morning and that’s all really grounding for me but yes, the loneliness of not having your feet ever really on ground for too long is a very real thing. 

AD: In the videos for Altogether Stranger’s singles, you’re wearing an incredible mirror suit because you describe your character in the album as being an “alien stranded on earth.” Where did you get that suit? Did you make it yourself? 

Lael Neale: No, but I wish I could say I made it. I bought it from this really shady website and I was worried that they might steal my credit card information and never send anything! I really thought that because it was pretty inexpensive but then I got it and the entire thing has real glass all over it,  glued onto it. It’s thin glass, but it’s glass! Every single piece of the glass had a piece of plastic covering it to protect it so I had to peel hundreds and hundreds of plastic things off, so I feel like I kind of made it in a way! 

AD: At what stage of an album’s development does the visual representation of the world come to you? Was it mid-writing the songs that this theme occurred to you or was it a bit afterwards when you were getting ready to create the music videos? 

Lael Neale: It was all probably after it, I think. We had collected the songs and chosen the nine songs and then we were wondering what to call the album. I’d had this phrase that I kept that I used for an art show and it was just this hovering phrase because I had been working on a song that I never finished with the chorus that was, “I’m an all together stranger,” and so much it really encapsulates how I felt in Los Angeles at the time. It really felt like an appropriate name for that group of songs. From there, I already had the mirror suit, so the mirror suit really informed all of the videos! We basically built the whole concept of the album of the music videos around this suit and this character of this alien being an altogether stranger. The ideas for the music videos came from me going on long walks or meditating and I would just start getting visuals and seeing the videos in my mind’s eye and then talk to Guy about them a lot. We’d go back and forth and work out a loose concept and start shooting. 

My good friend does movement direction in choreography so she’s in a few of the videos and that helped create pillars of the films. We made a music video for every single song on the album and they all fit together in this very loose storyline. It’s pretty abstract, but we made a 34-minute film of separate music videos.

AD: In your newsletter, you wrote “I love doing things the wrong way.” The value of embracing the process of trial and error and how mistakes teach us more about how to get the results we’re looking for and also maybe guide us down certain unexpected paths. How long did it take for you to get to that realization and what was the experience like getting to that point?

Lael Neale: Yeah, I mean I still struggle with it. That’s why I talk about it a lot because I think it’s really important to remember. I have historically been a bit of a perfectionist and I think through the first songs I was recording years and years ago, I was working with people who were doing everything by the book, like, ‘You use this microphone, you put the drums here, you put the bass there, you do this and we have it all through this like these fancy preamps and you use X, Y and Z’. All of these technical things and ‘You have to do it this way’, and I was kind of like, ‘Yeah sure, whatever, we’ll do it the right way. The way that everyone else does it.’ Consistently, I was disappointed in the results and it sucked the life out of the songs and sucked the life out of my voice and the emotion in it. 

That all switched when I started recording with Guy and he brought over the four track tape cassette machine and just hit record and you can do all these different things like put layers of your voice on other tracks and it was so simple. I was able to just play and the funny thing with Acquainted With Night was that we made it and he was in charge of putting all the tape cassette tracks into the computer and he accidentally put them all in through this amp effect on Garageband so the entire album went through this crazy effect that is so wrong. If a professional engineer or producer saw that, they’d maybe think it was dumb. When I heard the songs, because I didn’t know it was from an accident, I loved them and felt that it was better than anything else I’d ever made. That was the first proof to me that sometimes the utterly wrong way is the way that I want is the best. 

From there, you don’t want to intentionally force doing things the wrong way but I’m constantly making accidents or on some of the songs there’s a crazy loud hawk screeching in the background. That’s definitely imperfect but it adds to the ambiance or the environment of the songs.

AD: It brings you into the world of the song and lets listeners in on what was happening while it was being made. It’s a very authentic representation of where the song was captured. On another note, I was reading an interview you gave around the time of Star Eater’s Delight’s release. You talked about ‘In Verona’ and how it came from watching Letters to Juliet with your mom but you only lasted 10 minutes into the film before you left the room. Is that the most unlikely source of inspiration you’ve ever had for a song?

Lael Neale: Yeah, that was a shocking turn of being like, “Wow, I’m actually so glad I watched the first 10 minutes of that horrible movie!”

AD: Going back to airports, I think most people don’t particularly like them but is there something about them that, apart from the bad smells and air conditioning, you enjoy about them that you can’t experience anywhere else?  

Lael Neale: I love watching people and eavesdropping on people! I love people so that’s a really positive part of airports for me. I love looking at trashy magazines and wedding magazines, I love looking at things that I would never look at because boredom is not a bad thing. The airport is a great teacher in that way; you have time to be a little bored and to let your mind wander or watch people. It’s like you’re off the hook because you’re traveling. It’s like a commons, there’s a bunch of different types of people all coming together. The things I was pointing out in the song like the perfume stands and, you know, I could be in a really bad mood and see all the depressing aspects of it as a place but there’s definitely positives.
