## Body

Three years and as many live albums have passed since Setting’s debut album. In that time, the trio of Nathan Bowles, Jaime Fennelly, and Joe Westerlund have sharply fine-tuned their form of exploratory electronic Appalachian drone music, and their new, self-titled album finds them on a heightened plane. The chemistry built between these three players over the last few years comes alive here in an even richer hue of their cosmic arboreal vision. Across a gamut of keyboards, banjos, synths, zithers, tape manipulation, and various forms of percussion, and over five longform improvisational tracks, the trio merges and separates back again into overlapping musical landscapes — verdant and kaleidoscopic worlds existing together in warped, rustic harmony.

Nathan Bowles’ banjo sets the tone on album opener “Heard a Bubble,” strumming over Joe Westerlund’s rattling percussion while Jaime Fennelly’s synth glows in an undulating arpeggio. As a zither begins to rake across the rhythm, the layers blend into one another, and the trio clears for takeoff. They embrace freeform acid jazz and sun-kissed psychedelia on “Gum Bump,” with oozing synths and tinny percussion clanging against one another while surges of keys emanate in quick succession before ultimately getting tangled in a gossamer web of tape manipulation. Vast synths stretch and contract across gentle metallophone tumbles on “What Kind of Fish is a Turtle.” The group’s rapturous sound almost evokes a Tangerine Dream score — if James Caan were taking down scores on the moon — but they ultimately move at their own stargazing pace, ensuring a proper amount of earth remains baked into the cut.

Bowles’ forlorn banjo stumbles across pulsing synths and muted, crashing drums on “Ribbon of Moss,” with the track possessing an almost funereal tone before getting channeled through shimmering cymbals and blurry effects. The banjo takes on a more fervent stride and Fennelly and Westerlund fall in step — unified sonic explorers deftly navigating their way through suddenly emerging terrains. Over the eleven-minute closing track, “Derring-do,” reedy, ominous synths, restless banjo, and propulsive drums build up into an unrelenting Old West standoff, the tension locked in an eternal loop perpetually simmering just under the boiling point. Eventually, the action becomes transcendent, the listener lost in the telepathic performance. And while this is, indeed, music to get lost in, our guides leave ample room for discovery, intended or otherwise. | c depasquale
