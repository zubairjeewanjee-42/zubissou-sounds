## Body

Proving to be a restless sonic chameleon, Adeline Hotel (the moniker of multi-instrumentalist Dan Knishkowy) never makes the same record twice. Inspired by the likes of Jim O’Rourke’s transmuting discography, Adeline Hotel’s recent records range from fingerpicking guitar, jazz-tinged atmospheric compositions, and orchestral art pop. The starkly personal new record Whodunnit feature another new configuration of the ensemble cast backing band, yet comes across as Knishkowy’s most calculated vision to date: a piece of art channeled through a vortex of recent personnel challenges (and the concluding joy and healing optimism on the other side). With nods to the likes of Gillian Welch and John Martyn, Whodunnit is a kaleidoscopic autumnal tapestry that brings Knishkowy’s precise lyrical talents to the forefront. 

From his home in New York, Knishkowy joined us for a comprehensive discussion about the deeply personal experiences that went into the creation of the new record. We also discuss the guiding light of Phil Elverum, the collaborative community around the record and his own record label, his shapeshifting approach to making different records and much more. | m neeley

Aquarium Drunkard: Whodunnit is obviously a starkly personal record. How would you briefly summarize that experience to the listeners?

Adeline Hotel: I got the seed for the record because I was actually gifted a songwriting class from Phil Elverum of The Microphones. And I wrote this song, “Egg On My Face”. Kind of the way I always write is … there will be a song that clearly to me feels like a thesis to write towards and then I write toward that thing. So I sort of had that in mind, and I kind of wrote half the songs sort of leading up to the central narrative of this record, and then half as a response to it. Which is kind of interesting to me, I feel like songwriting brings a lot of things to consciousness that maybe you weren’t ready to look at in your waking life.

Subject matter-wise, it’s tough. I’ve been kind of torn about how to present this record. I feel like, there’s no getting around it in a sense that it’s definitely a record about the dissolution of a relationship, of a marriage. But on the other hand I feel like it’s a lot more about the reclamation of self. And those are intertwined but not always the same thing, I suppose.

AD: That dichotomy is challenging, I’d imagine. 

Adeline Hotel: I think it’s tough in terms of how direct to be about it, because there’s a lot of things going on in that decision. On one hand you’re pulling the strings of how people are going to perceive the record, which is important. Giving people enough framework, but maybe not so much that it becomes so concretely about one thing. Being honest about the darker aspect of things that occured might be good for personal healing, but making those things public can be quite difficult as well. So kind of trying to use the process of making and sharing the record to understand my own story but not being so public that it’s actually double harmful. It’s an interesting balance that I didn’t expect. But everyone I know who has heard it seems to understand that balance in a nice way, so it has been good.

Coming out of that, there’s a lot of, I think sort of loss of reality in a sense. You ground yourself in a certain environment for a long time and it’s very strange to figure out who you are outside of that reality. Making this music really helped me figure that out. There’s a concept to it, in that I was writing to build this world, but at the same time I don’t think I really knew what I was aiming for. It wasn’t so much that I wanted to get from point A to point B, there was an urgency to get out of point A, so wherever this record takes me, that’s where I’m going. That urgency differentiates it from Hot Fruit, which was more meticulously overseen. This one was more in the moment, living it, and that felt really nice.

AD: Could you expand on that Phil Elverum songwriting exercise? What was that like?

Adeline Hotel: Yeah, he taught this class at this thing called School of Song. It’s kind of funny because I wrote this sort of seed that became a song that ended up being central to the record. In a way, hearing him talk about how he wrote and digging back into some of those records, I felt kind of hopelessly addicted to Lost Wisdom pt. 2, which is a divorce record. I was obsessively listening to it on the precipice of about to be experiencing similar things, albeit very different circumstances. Obviously, he was dealing with the death of a partner in that process which is a very different thing, but I think he’s talking so much in that record about completely having his life pulled out from under him. And then in the end, on the last song of the record, the first lyrics are “I believe in love and I still do”, and I think that message was kind of guiding Whodunnit. I love Blood on the Tracks, but there’s such a bitterness to that. I really didn’t want the record to sound like that, because I didn’t want to feel like that. It was kind of nice to use Whodunnit as a sort of companion to figure out where I wanted to end up at the end of this.

AD: When it comes to the lyrics on this record, how much of it was stream of consciousness or improvised versus planned out?

Adeline Hotel: It was pretty much all stream of consciousness. On the first track “How Did I Get So Lucky”?, I feel like I wrote that the way that I wrote songs fifteen years ago when I was just starting to write music. It just came out in one go. I felt like I hadn’t been able to access that in some time, so that felt good. Then the longer tracks like “Whodunnit” and “Isn’t That Enough?”, I feel like I often overwrite. I write a lot of verses, then edit them down. With this, they all felt important to the story. I almost felt like giving too much and kind of pushing the limit to see how far I could go with the number of verses. 

That felt like a strength more than a weakness, because when you’re reflecting back and asking, how did something end up how it did? You’ll maybe think of a central theme or memory. I felt like that was sort of what I was experiencing so it felt more respectful to the record to just leave it that way. Some of the verses were sort of restructured to make the narrative make sense to me, but didn’t really edit with any listener appeal or packaging of the track in mind. For me, I then called it a day. 

AD: Let’s talk about Jim O’Rourke. That influence is definitely very evident on all of your albums, and I think it specifically comes through on the vocals and guitar here. 

Adeline Hotel: I feel like the way that he approaches music is kind of like the guiding principle a little bit, in terms of not being interested in making the same record twice. I grew up Wilco-obsessed and so that was sort of my gateway into him, with mixing Yankee Hotel Foxtrot and A Ghost is Born, and then Loose Fur. So from there, the Drag City song records, and then Tweedy was very big on Bad Timing. So that was the gateway for me. When I released Good Timing, everyone seemed to write it as some sort of homage to him, which it kind of was, but I didn’t know that was going to be so obvious. 

I think with this, I kind of think of that track on Eureka called “Movie on the Way Down”, which has an ambient, almost field recording vibe. And then, he shifts into this very simple, short phrased beautiful plainspoken song. And that’s kind of what I was trying to do with “How Did I Get So Lucky?”, but that’s kind of the world I’m trying to build with doing these instrumental records and then song records. I feel like there’s not that many people who are doing things in the way that he did. The Visitor was a big influence on Hot Fruit for sure, with the arrangements. Yeah, I kind of see this record, even though it’s very lyrical, as sort of a bridge between the two different things I’m doing. Arthur Russell maybe also comes to mind, where he’ll have these very sweet, naive, wide-eyed folk songs but then he’ll do the instrumental records.

I feel like those two, in their way they sort of restlessly made the same thing twice is really exciting. In our current musical climate, it’s not the best move for having people easily make sense of what you’re doing, but it feels good. I honestly had a different record that is like 90% done that I abandoned for this one, because I couldn’t really get into that headspace anymore. So I didn’t really know that this would be the next chapter, but I’m happy that it was. I would love it if Jim O’Rourke made another songs album, but I suspect he may not for many years.

AD: I’ll have to recheck where I got this, but I remember a few years ago hearing that there actually was a completed third Loose Fur album.

Adeline Hotel: Oh yeah! Also, I actually just remembered that I do slightly reference him on this record. I always sort of take lyrics from one album and put them on another, sort of like throughlines between the records. So, on “Isn’t That Enough?”, I slightly reference my own record, The Cherries Are Speaking, but I also use the lyric “dripping cherries”,  and he has that verse on “Ghost Shop”, “It’s just my luck/I get hit by the car/while carrying a cake/dripping cherries on the pavement”. So the dropped wedding cake, and that lyric that I use here and maybe something else. I kind of forgot that that was a nod to him as well. 

AD: It seems like you tend to collaborate with new musicians on every release. Do you consider Adeline Hotel a collective in that way? How calculated is putting the musicians together?

Adeline Hotel: I think it’s always rotating to an extent. There’s always people who are in the orbit, but I think I’m always sort of fascinated by the Will Oldham or Jason Molina vibe of whoever is in the room at the moment is the band. It doesn’t have to be the band that’s playing live or on the next records. I really like being surprised, I don’t like feeling like I need to tell people what to play. I just want to be around people who buy into the vision and bring certain things into it. For this one, I kept it a little bit smaller. I definitely wanted to play all the guitars myself, I think that felt more true to the subject matter in a way. For the next thing I plan on actually trying to play everything, just to see if I can do it. I find it interesting sometimes that I picked some kind of moniker. I’m really fascinated by the distance between what your name can give you in terms of playing with a persona or something. 

I think when it’s your name, you sort of have to be on all the time, but when it’s some sort of fictional collective, there’s a different pocket of creativity that’s made accessible there by not feeling so tied to yourself. At the same time, I think that it can be kind of difficult to figure out what world you’re inhabiting at times. Maybe the grass is greener both ways. It’s probably more straightforward to have a set band and identity, but there’s something liberating about doing it this way. I will say, the one person who is always a part of the project is my friend Winston who plays keys. His band is called Office Culture and they have a great record coming out this fall as well. 

AD: How did the collaborative efforts influence the creative process on Whodunnit? 

Adeline Hotel: With this record, I found so much vibrancy in music and in collaboration. Really connecting with the community I was making music with. Everyone on this record is an amazing artist in their own right, I think all of them are also solo artists on Ruination, the label that I own. I really leaned on this, and it felt really good. The day before we recorded the album, I found out about a family member being diagnosed with cancer. I was riding this insane high of life, and then in this “oh shit, we’re back into this crisis” headspace. That was how I made the record, in this really confusing space around mortality and family and connection. Which was not necessarily what I put into the record in terms of lyrical content. 

To me, the record has these kind of distinct flavors of devastation, but almost intoxicating relief and joy. And then this creeping fear underpinning the performances. That’s one of the reasons I really loved working with Katie Von Schleicher who engineered and mixed the record. She’s very good at trusting your instincts and takes. There was never really a moment of thinking oh, this sounds raw, should we re-do the take? That always ended up being the take.

AD: I wanted to ask about that label you co-own, Ruination Record Co. 

Adeline Hotel: I basically started the label with my two lifelong, childhood friends. We’ve been playing music for over twenty years now. Like most labels, it started as a vehicle to put out our own music, and then for friends we really care about. It kept growing, but I feel our ethos is very much this kind of community, rising ride kind of thing. People who really want to support each other. Most bands on the label are made up of people who play in each other’s bands. There’s people outside of New York, but there’s also a Chicago contingent and it’s also like that with swapping musicians. It’s very egoless in that way, people rooting for each other. That’s why I really love playing with the people on this record.

AD: I wondered if there was any symbolism in Hot Fruit and Whodunnit both having album covers with floral photographs, yet the latter is very dark instead of the bright vibrancy of the former. 

Adeline Hotel: Jackie West took the photo for Whodunnit. It was definitely intentional, when I saw that photograph it both felt like the songs I was writing but it immediately spoke to me as the inverse of Hot Fruit. They’re being released almost exactly a year apart, and they really are two sides of the same coin, both in terms of the feel they gave off but also in the approach of making records in different styles. For me the art has always been very important in how to present that. My friend Hannah Sellers designed the cover and packaging. It has a maroon vibe, sort of in between the vibrancy of Hot Fruit. The song “I Will Let Your Flowers Grow” felt like a musical representation of how those covers evolved. 

I love the refrain in that song because I think it has a dual meaning: kind of losing yourself within the confines of a relationship, where you folded yourself into it too much in a negative way. But in a more positive light, I can let myself walk away from this and that’s okay. And I can let you flourish and let your flowers grow, and that’s okay too. This fear of not being tied to someone in this codependent way where you have to grow in alignment is pretty deep, but it’s okay to grow separately on these vines and still stay tied together, or start growing in a way where it’s clear that that should be split. I was trying to explore this both in the songs but in the visual representation of the albums too.
