## Body

On January 17, 2026, the world lost the great Tucker Zimmerman and his wife of more than fifty years, Marie-Claire, to a house fire in Liege, Belgium. He was 84 years old. Though born in the United States, he had been a resident of Europe since 1966. A novelist, poet, folk singer, classical composer, & electronic musician, Zimmerman had a deep and restless career with more than a dozen albums to his name. However, because of his status as an expat, he was largely overlooked in his birth country for most of his lifetime. In 2007, Jeremy Rendina interviewed Zimmerman about his second (and arguably greatest) album for the now sadly defunct Stop Smiling magazine for an issue focused on expatriates.

Though he’d had a somewhat successful career in Europe, by 2008 the world’s attention had wandered elsewhere, and he’d yet to have any of his records reissued. In their initial correspondence, he told Jeremy that it had been a number of years since he’d been interviewed, but provided him with his Autobiography, from which Jeremy crafted a handful of follow-up questions. 

His records were incredibly difficult to find  at the time of this interview in 2008. Thankfully that situation has changed in 2026. You can now find all of his released albums on the streaming services (though there is still a large gap missing regarding his classical compositions, film scores, and Philip Glass inspired electronic music), and a new generation of people are aware of his work via the heroic efforts of Adriannne Lenker & Big Thief, who produced and performed on Zimmerman’s marvelous 2024 album “Dance of Love”, for 4AD records. | Michael Klausman 

The following is Rendina’s original 2008 article for Stop Smiling, with the unpublished (and lengthy) Q&A after.

In April of 1966, Tucker Zimmerman’s draft papers arrived. In them, he found instructions to report to Fort Ord for basic training. Fort Ord, pocketed in the gorgeous Monterey Bay peninsula of California, was the major hub on the west coast responsible for getting young Americans combat ready for deployment to Vietnam. That very same month, Tucker received news that he had been granted a prestigious Fulbright scholarship to study composition in Rome. After years of immersion in classical music studies at San Francisco State, his cello sonatas and string quartets were gaining some acclaim. At this time he decided to pick up the guitar.

The State Department (custodians of the Fulbright) and the Selective Service would wrangle for possession of his body and soul. According to Tucker, “The era was underlined by an impending sense of doom, a feeling that something terrible was going to happen. I didn’t want to be a part of it.  Never.”  He was granted permission to study in Italy on the condition that he return to the U.S. and fulfill his military obligation immediately at the end of his scholarship. But as for coming back to serve, he never did.

Born on Valentine’s Day, 1941, Tucker Zimmerman spent the better part of his youth in Healdsburg, California, where the Russian river runs through a quiet community of orchards and vineyards. Raised by a grandmother with a proclivity for the occult, he showed early musical promise on the piano and trombone, but it was his discovery of Leadbelly and Moondog at the age of ten that had a most profound effect. Tucker joined a rhythm and blues band and performed at local dances and weddings, getting paid in wine. Eventually he migrated down to San Francisco and enrolled in music theory class at City College and began work on some of his first compositions. He roamed North Beach on the weekends with a leather flask full of spirits and his head full of Patchen and Huxley.

The time had come to leave for the eternal city and begin his scholarship.  Italy proved to be most fruitful for Tucker and was a welcome retreat from the drug-bombed daze of Haight Ashbury. While there, he wrote some 300 songs. And his poetry flowed. He met his lifelong companion, Marie-Claire who was working at the Belgian embassy. But the scholarship would run its course. It wasn’t until his final weeks in Rome that it occurred to him that he should be the one performing the songs.

Leaving Rome for London, Tucker was now officially a draft dodger to be arrested and jailed the moment he returned to the states. He was forced to go underground. Another exile, Tony Visconti, the famed producer of T-Rex, had been living in London and would ultimately produce Tucker’s first album, Ten Songs. Visconti brought in none other than Steve Winwood, Aynsley Dunbar, and Ric Wakeman to lend a hand. He also helped Tucker to get session work as a “Canadian” since Americans needed work permits. His record yielded one single (The Red Wind b/w Moondog) that was met with marginal success, but with a visa that could no longer be renewed, the British government grew suspicious. After catching up with him, he was given a week to pack his bags and get out of England.

On Christmas Eve of 1969, Tucker and Marie-Claire fled to Belgium.  Nearly forty years later, they still call it home. Settling in Liege, Tucker entered into sonic seclusion. In their apartment, he hung rugs on the walls and baffled the windows with pillows and blankets and began to unravel some of the songs that he developed while in Rome. The songs seemed to write him. It was fortunate that the bleed through on the run down two-track tape machine enabled him to synchronize his guitar and vocals on the left with organ and piano on the right, before playback as full track mono.

The songs are deliriously unhinged, closely navigating the delicate edge of sanity.  Alarmingly personal lyrics are delivered in a falsetto like none other.  He said, “My heart is with the poets of old Japan and China.  I always feel that I am touching a vibrant source when I read their work.  I am always moved by the image of the Chinese poet sitting by the riverside and composing his quatrain, then casting it into the river to watch it float away.” 

Everything I see turns to fantasy
Everything I hear goes out the other ear
Everything I taste goes to waste
Everything I touch turns to dust
And I can’t tell you why

Sometimes mistaken as the brother or cousin of the other Zimmerman, Bob Dylan, Philips Records in Europe offered Tucker a recording contract with the stipulation that he change his name. He agreed — as long as the label would change theirs as well.  No contract was signed, but Tucker would go on to record another half dozen albums and is still actively playing today.

In an attempt to mend the wounds of war, the US offered unconditional amnesty for deserters.  This allowed Tucker to return to California with his family after years in exile.  “I still didn’t go back until 1986.  A 20-year absence.  We stayed for three weeks – and I was glad to get back to Belgium.”

March 22-26, 2008

Jeremy,

Here are some answers to your questions. I see the answers are a lot more elaborate than I expected, soI’m probably giving you a lot more than you’re asking for. Take what you need from them for your interview. I’m also aware that my answers might provoke a few more questions. So be it. I like to talk. I like to tell stories.

Jeremy Rendina: Do you have a copy of the Belgian television (RTB) documentary? (a short film made in 1969 for Belgian TV, unclear if still in existence -ed.)

Tucker Zimmerman: No copy of the documentary. I doubt if the RTB has kept a copy. It was in a series of shows called “Nous, Les Wallons.” Not very good. They tried to make something sensational about me and Marie Claire living together and not being married. It’s so common today that nobody would blink. I think they made me out to be a hippie freak more than the serious musician I considered myself. Stirred up a lot of useless dust. I don’t think you’d like to see it. I wouldn’t.

JR: What about the interview in the Oxford newspaper or the radio interviews by Marc Moulin? (Marc Moulin was a Belgian radio personality who went on to form a number of ground-breaking groups, including Placebo, Telex, and Aksak Maboul –ed.)

The Radio Luxembourg interview? I was on Cap de Nuit with Marc Moulin (RTB in Brussels) several times but I doubt if those shows were ever taped. I never thought to ask. Anyway I’m pretty sure they didn’t keep those kinds of documents then. I must say that Marc also helped me find a few of my first gigs in Belgium. He’s still around, making music – up in Brussels – but I haven’t seen him in years. Same story with Radio Luxembourg. Kid Jensen was his name. The Prince of Darkness? Is my memory accurate on that? Is that what he called himself? We talked for about four hours on the air –between tunes – and he ended up playing all the tracks of my first album. Tony set me up for this interview as I was leaving England – late December of 1969. We had done a single for EMI called “The Red Wind” and by some fluke it got into the charts at Radio Luxembourg – number 38 on the top 40 or something like that. I think it was the only radio in Europe to play the single. It stayed on the charts for one week. I was not disappointed. I hate to think that if it had become a hit I would still be singing it today – on sixties revival shows – and absolutely nobody would be interested in anything I’ve written since. What a horrible thought.

JR: Please describe the climate of times when you decided to leave the US and if you could provide any anecdotes about the draft board and the pressures that they may have put on you.

Tucker Zimmerman: From my personal point of view the era was underlined by an impending sense of doom, a feeling that something terrible was going to happen. It was as if those in power had taken the abstraction out of the communist threat of the 50s and beefed it up with the very visible hell of the Vietnam war. I didn’t want to be a part of it. Never. It was obvious to me for the first time that our government was lying to us. That was a big shock because like most kids I grew up believing in our benevolent, “democratic” government who stood for world peace and equality (and I think there was something about “freedom” in there too). Everything was suddenly turned upside down and I felt like my legs had been cut from under me. I speak of myself, but I am sure I was not alone in feeling this way. It’s not such a shocking thing today – what else can we expect from the powers that be but lies and treachery? But we’ve had 40 years or so to get used to the shattered dream. I was in my second year of graduate studies and working on my thesis when I got my draft notice in the mail. It was the ordinary horror show I’d been waiting for. It had to be a mistake tho. I’d just been approved for another deferment. I went to my draft board on Taravel Street. They looked up the records. They said sorry we made a mistake BUT – too bad, as soon as you finish school you go to Fort Ord for basic training. About the same time – April -I received my Fulbright Scholarship which would start in the fall. They’d already booked my flight to New York and the ship sailing for Naples on September 23rd.

In the end I managed to get lawyers from the State Department (Fulbright) to sit down with the Selective Service in Sacramento and work out an agreement. They’d let me have a passport and leave the US on the scholarship, but when it was over – in a year – they wanted me back in California for the army. The decision didn’t come down until the very last minute. I’d intended to fly to New York a week earlier to stay with friends and see the city. But I didn’t get the phone call for the OK to leave until about an hour before my rescheduled flight to New York. It was literally a last moment, cliff hanger. I was fully prepared to take off for Mexico – or Canada – in case something went wrong. 

As for coming back, I never did. I got a renewal on the scholarship for another year and then took off for London and a new life. I figured if they wanted me they’d come and get me. I experienced a nervous week in London when I took my passport into the embassy (or maybe it was the consulate in Grosvenor Square) to be renewed. Then we had a three-year passport that could be stamped for an additional two years. I didn’t know if they were going to arrest me when I went back to pick up my passport. The Marine guard was very visible and somewhat menacing. However I walked out of there with the two-year stamp with no trouble at all. From then on I had a much calmer view of the matter. It was clear that they didn’t want me. I was 27 years old and would have been a misfit in the army, probably a bad influence too around all those 18-year old draftees. More trouble that I’d be worth. 

Then Nixon pardoned all the unpatriotic scum in 1974 and I was off the hook. But I still didn’t go back until 1986. A 20-year absence. We stayed – Marie Claire, our son Quanah and I – for three weeks in Concord, Visalia and SF – and I was glad to get back to Belgium. But back in 1963 when I first started hearing rumors about the “conflict” in Southeast Asia (I was already getting regular student deferments from the draft) and from then on the pressure seemed to increase daily. I was frightened most of the time, really scared once in a while and apprehensive at the best of times. My mother thought I was going crazy so she had me committed to Langley Porter for psychiatric evaluation. I dodged a bullet on that one, for as we all know (I hope) that once those clinics get you behind their locked doors they can do anything they want, turn you into raving maniac if they’re in the mood. I got shuffled off to the outpatient clinic – once a week visits to a shrink (who curiously enough was an ex-navy doctor) who couldn’t understand why the idea of being drafted and sent to some far away place to be killed should be driving me nuts. We reached a stalemate and he gave up on me and released me back into the population. In his terms I remained crazy for the rest of the time I stayed in the US – and no doubt I am still crazy by some other authority’s definition of insanity. So be it. I’ve had a lot of time to think about insanity, psychiatry and the whole mental health issue and I’ve concluded that all of us are crazy to some degree and I’d be better off avoiding that particular institution at all costs. (All institutions should be avoided if possible – but that’s another branch of the tree and I don’t feel like climbing out there right now.)

I’ve written about the draft and the war in two books – Up on Downey Street which gives a limited social slant to the whole thing, and in Song Poet which is much more personal. I could send you excepts from these books if you wish. As for anecdotes, I recall sitting around with other potential pieces of meat for the war machine in 1964 and 1965 and talking about ways to beat the physical exam for the draft, discussing such things as cutting off toes to declaring an affiliation with the communist party. Only one guy I knew got out of it – by standing up in the main room when you first come in and throwing one of his boots at the sergeant’s head. He missed I think. But he followed it by standing on his chair and raving and ranting about who knows what. They kicked him out. Too much trouble. I passed my physical and got lucky with the Fulbright. I had a couple of friends who were not so lucky. They’ve been dead these 40 years.

JR: How did you meet Tony Visconti and what led to your working with the Butterfield Blues Band?

Tucker Zimmerman: These are two questions, unrelated. Two different stories. Paul first. Summer of 1966 I was still in America, just finished my masters at SF State. I got involved with the Ann Halprin dance group over in Marin County and there I got to know Kathy Peterson. Towards the end of the summer I was in LA. Had a fight with my girlfriend on Sunset Boulevard, jumped out of the car at a red light with my knapsack and guitar and walked into a movie theater showing Fellini’s Juliette of the Spirits. I’d already seen the movie about 4 or 5 times. I went in mostly because they said the room was air conditioned. I got a seat in the back row and went to sleep. I don’t how many times the movie looped around but when I woke up there were four people standing in the row in front, looking down at me. Kathy Peterson, her sister Nancy, and her mom and dad, Doc and Dottie (I hope I’m remembering “Dottie” correctly). Kathy was surprised to see me in LA. She asked where I was going. I said no place. I’m on the street. They invited me to their house up in Mulholland Drive. Fancy place with a swimming pool. I stayed there about two weeks – this was just before I was supposed to leave for Europe on my Fulbright (I was also avoiding the draft board, but that’s another story) One day Kathy asked me if I’d like to drive to the airport and pick up her boyfriend who was flying in from Chicago. I went along and met him. His name was Paul. We drove back to hills and hung out for about a week. I didn’t know who he was. One night we went down to the Whiskey-a-Go-Go to hear the Byrds. Bad gig. Paul introduced me to Roger McGuinn. For some reason I was not surprised that Paul knew him. Back in the hills I was sitting in my back bedroom with my guitar a lot of the time, working on my songs. I ended up singing a couple to Paul. He asked me if I had something for his band. I still didn’t know who he was. He brought out an advanced copy of “East-West” and we listened to that. And here’s the part that will probably surprise you. I thought the album was terrific but I’d never heard of Paul Butterfield before.

From this you can surmise that I did not have my feet planted very deeply in folk soil. I was far more concerned at the time with my String Quartet and my Cantata on texts of Thomas Wolfe (my master’s thesis at State).  It was actually a good way to meet Paul. There was no baggage, or garbage, in the way. He was just a very decent guy who was good to be around. He could also play harmonica like I’d never heard before. So we sat down by the swimming pool a couple of days before he left – back on the road with his band – and we put together the song “Droppin’ Out.” I had the melody and chords and most of the words. Paul had a few suggestions about phrasing and tempo. I wanted it slow. He wanted it up. I played guitar, he sang and played harp. We went thru the tune about a dozen times and he wrote the lyrics down on a napkin the morning he left, saying he’d see me in San Francisco in a couple of weeks. I didn’t think too much about Paul or the song after that. I had a whole pile of crap to deal with anyway (back to the draft board story). As it turned out I didn’t see Paul in SF. I left the city – and the US – on September 22 and one of the last things I saw as I was leaving the Haight-Ashbury (and that’s another story in itself) was a psychedelic poster announcing the Butterfield Blues Band at Winterland. on the 23rd – I missed him by one day.

A few months later, a friend in California sent me a copy of the Pigboy Crabshaw album in Rome. I was surprised to find our song on it. There was also a song that he’d worked out with Kathy. Then, again, the whole thing slipped my mind – until about a year after, when I was living in London. I hooked up with Paul again. He sent an urgent message to my record company saying he had to see me. I dropped by his hotel. He said he had a stack of money for me and hadn’t known where to send it. We spent a night on the town, had a few drinks, and then I never saw him again. He was a real straight shooter. Down to earth, absolutely no bullshit. He was also the most honest of people in the music business I’ve ever encountered. I still get royalty checks each year for that song. Believe me, at the time in London I was living from hand to mouth and the check he sent me was like a gift from heaven. I can’t say that I really knew Paul. We didn’t spend that much time together. But I always get a warm feeling when I think of him. There is no doubt in my mind that he was one of the Good People who walk the earth, that keep everything in balance.

Tony Visconti is another story as you might imagine. I’m sure that to some degree, lesser or greater, we are all influenced by everybody we meet. In most cases the influence is slight, even imperceptible. Tony was one of the few people who really changed the direction of my life – not that I was unwilling. I left Rome in the summer of 1968 (the second year of my Fulbright had run out) and there was only one place for me to go if I wanted to record an album. New York or LA were out since the army still wanted me for cannon fodder. So Marie Claire and I went to London. I was out on the street for the first time in my life. Out from under the protective umbrella of the academy. I knew I would make a lousy college professor of music, and the very thought of spending the rest of my life in such a place horrified me – deep down. 

So, as I say, I turned my back on the academic world and set off with my guitar and songs to see if I could survive as a songwriter. I was 27. I had about 300 songs in my knapsack. We spent a lousy year and a half in England. Nine months in London, another nine in the countryside near Oxford. We would not have survived without Tony. I met him in my second month in London. A young English kid, Nick Jones (son of Max Jones, who was a senior editor at Music Maker), and who knew just about everybody on the music scene, suggested I go see Denny Cordell at Regal Zonophone. I went into the Regal Zonophone offices on Oxford Street and learned that Denny Cordell was off in America working with Joe Cocker. Tony was there so I talked to him instead. Nick had mentioned Tony as the producer of Tyrannosaurus Rex (I’d seen their first album in a record shop window on King’s Road but had never heard their music). I sat down with Tony, took out my guitar and sang a few songs. Next thing I know I was in the demo studio beginning what was to be a long preparation for an LP. 

Tony and I spent a lot of time in the Regal Zonophone demo studio and we recorded something like 80 songs before we thought we were ready. The album from the start, I knew, would not be a “solo” album. Tony was very much a collaborative part of it. It was a very small room that had Procol Harum’s “Whiter Shade of Pale” organ in one corner, a wine red upright piano with baroque scrolls and candle holders in the other and two Brennell 2-stracks on the floor. (I’ve got a good photo of this I’d be glad to send you a copy of). We bounced tracks from one machine to the other until we had a decent support for each song – 12-string, organ, piano, voices, percussion and Tony always put his electric bass on last (which by the way was a Hofner discard from Paul McCartney – and who knows what kind of history that guitar could have told – but for some reason it was just laying in the studio all the time we were there, so Tony used it – and by the way to give you some idea of how good a musician Tony is, the strings on the bass were reversed for a south paw, and Tony never got around to changing them – so he always played right-handed with the strings upside down. I never heard him pluck a wrong note).

Cordell was away all this time – well into December when Tony got the OK to start taping the album. We finished the tracks in December, working at various 8-track studios around town. Olympia was the first, there was one called Pye, another called Regent – and we ended up at Trident on St. Anne’s Court. We did most of the tracks there and all the mixing. Tony titled the album – “Ten Songs ” and took the photo for the front cover. We listed only the two of us as the musicians on the back, which in retrospect was a mistake. We should have given credit to Shawn Philips for his sitar work, Aynsley Dunbar for the drum tracks, Steve Winwood and Rick Wakeman for keyboards. The basic tracks were my voice and 12-string – we followed the routine we’d picked up in the demo studio. I added harpsichord, mellotron and harmonica. Tony played all the electric bass tracks, four recorders (multi tracked and bounced), Spanish guitar, electric guitar, various percussion and backing vocals. Then we sat back and waited to see what would happen. Not much. But that’s another elaborate story for another time.

Tony and I spent the rest of the winter together in London. I saw him almost everyday, usually at his flat on Lexham Gardens. We became a part of each others’ life. We were both starting out. Both self-exiled Americans and thus spoke the same language – more or less (Brooklyn and California are a continent apart but a lot closer to each other than Great Britain). Marie Claire and I moved up to Oxford in the spring and Tony came up to visit almost every weekend. He set us up with a tape machine and brought copies of the latest stuff – some of it his work with T-Rex and Joe Cocker and David Bowie. I went down to London about twice a month, stayed with Tony – slept on his living room floor. He got me work as a studio musician playing piano on several projects he was producing, slipped me the money “under the table.” I was on the waiting edge the entire time I was in England – the Home Office would not give me a work permit.  I don’t know how many hours Tony and I sat up late at night talking and talking. I guess I helped him form his view of the world somewhat (how could it have been otherwise?) but I only know for sure what he gave me – ideas, thoughts, definitions. We shared a love for soul music and comedy. He introduced me to TV (never had one in my house until 1976). I remember we laughed a lot. Tony has a refined sense of humor that goes deep and turns everything about him into a warm cloud of comfort. Several times he just handed me money from his pocket – to pay the rent and for food. He rode a motorcycle around London and was just starting to study the martial arts. An amazing man. I love him profoundly. Which reminds me I’ve owed him a letter for over a year. He regularly keeps in touch, keeps me up to date with his life.

JR: What was the Watkins’ bookstore like? (London’s oldest esoteric & spiritualist bookshop, established in 1897 – ed.)

Tucker Zimmerman: Let’s keep this question with the London scene. Watkins was on Cecil Court and the strange thing is that recently I tried to locate it on three different city maps and it was on none of them. Has Cecil Court – and Watkins along with it – vanished into thin air? I wouldn’t be surprised if some regulars at the store got together, worked some arcane magic and had it transported to a distant galaxy. It was strange place to me – simply because I have never completely trusted the occult world. I’m fascinated by it. I’ve read a lot of occult literature. I’ve talked to many people about it (Tony in particular) and formed some basic ideas, but I’ve never had both feet into it. I’ve never been a real “believer.” Nor can I say I’ve ever been too enthusiastic about science, tho I’ve delved into that area of the world somewhat. I guess there’s just a basic conflict with poetry there – as I see it, feel it and live it. It was dark place. Dusty, but with no dust visible. The vibrations were strong and not all of them were agreeable. I bought a few books there – astrology and tarot and the Hardbound copy of the Wilhelm I Ching (which still sits by our bed after all these years – Marie Claire being its most frequent visitor. I toss the coins once or twice a year and usually ignore its advice, but I went to Watkins because it was the only bookshop in London that had those books. Indica opened up about this time, but I didn’t go there but a couple of times. There was a sense of no-nonsense about Watkins. Very serious. Who knows who had been in and out of their doors over the past century. Mixed vibrations. Sometimes I shuddered at the thought of the black magicians who I was sure were lurking in the corners.

Footnote: Marie Claire just checked the internet and tells me that Watkins does indeed still exist on Cecil Court, so disregard my previous comments about its mysterious disappearance – tho I would not be surprised to learn that it has been levitated to another dimension and replaced by something that only looks like Watkins. As I’m slowly and painfully finding out with other things, this happens all the time.

JR: Did learning how to cast a chart influence your perspective on music at all?

Tucker Zimmerman: This question belongs to London too. Right alongside Watkins bookstore which is where I bought a copy of The New Waite’s Compendium of Natal Astrology, from which I learned how to cast a chart. It’s a very simple thing to do. Very easy. Basic bonehead arithmetic. The hard part is finding out what the casted chart means. I never discovered an innate talent for that. I just don’t think it’s in me. I know that the chart – the physical chart – can function as a talisman for some – that by touching it one can get an inkling of the esoteric secrets it contains. But that never worked for me. I read a few books on interpretation, but they all seemed to contradict one another. This didn’t stop me from being a quack. I cast a few charts simply because I needed the money. I also discovered that you can tell a whole lot about a person if you sit down in front of them and just take a good look. Those who came to me were “believers” so I was more than half way there with my quackery. I told them what they wanted to hear – which is not a bad thing in itself. I gave it up for another reason tho – I saw how much power I had over these people – most of them young, flipped out on acid, ready to believe ANYTHING, just begging for some guru to come along and give their lives a meaning and direction. Too much responsibility for me. I got out. 

How does astrology touch my work? Here and there. As poetry. I like the structure and thought-system behind astrology. It’s more colorful to speak of the signs of the zodiac than the months of the year. References sneak into my song lyrics once in a while. But I do not take it seriously. (For that matter I don’t take ANYTHING very seriously). My heart is with the poets of old Japan and China. I always feel that I’m touching a vibrant source when I read their work. They never took the ups and downs of their lives too seriously. It’s difficult to find a belief system behind their work and that appeals to me. I am always moved by the image of the Chinese poet sitting by the riverside and composing his quatrain, then casting it into the river to watch it float away. Of course there’s a contradiction in me here. If I were around when Li Po or Tu Fu cast their poems into the river, I’d be the fool around the bend fishing those poems out of the water to see what they had to say to no one. Lao Tzu has been another source of balance for me, but I’ll be getting off the track if I start talking about the Old Man. 

Back to astrology. I’ve sketched out a couple of composition projects using the structural elements of astrology as a frame, but I’ve never got around to taking those projects further. It’s been years since I looked at them. And I still don’t think I’ve answered your question. Casting a chart and how I see my music. I’d have to say no influence at all. But what do I know? There are forces at work in the background that are only occasionally visible. I cannot say flat out that there has not been some influence. Of course I say that about almost everything. Of the occult, only the I Ching and the Tarot have consciously become a part of my work. One song on the last album – “Turning Point” – I wrote after tossing the coins and coming up with Hexagram 24. It fit right in with all the other turbulences of my life at that moment. (another long story). In my “Song Poet Cycle” (another story for later) I’ve used the Fool card – the first in the deck (or as the adepts say – number 0). The Fool card floats in and out the narrative and ties several scenes together. I have not studied the other cards of the deck and I have never used them for divination. That kind of thing does not interest me. My grandmother took the occult world seriously in the last years of her life. But I’ll talk about her later.

JR: How did you become connected with the Autogram label? (German folk music label established in 1959 – ed.)

Tucker Zimmerman: I was playing a gig at a folk club in Antwerp in 1970. Willy Schwenken came up to me afterwards and asked me if I’d be interested in touring in Germany. He operated the Autogram label In Germany. He also ran the Osnabruck folk festival which was something new to me. Apart from a few songs we sang in the family when I was growing up, I knew absolutely nothing about so-called “folk music.” Even the pop music scene was new to me when I first went to London. I knew something of the top-forty in the Bay Area up to 1966 and I had a couple of LPs – the first Jefferson Airplane and Dylan’s Blonde on Blonde. But the domain of folk music was uncharted territory. What I’d heard, here and there, I didn’t like. Not until I met Derroll Adams and got to know him and actually started performing with him did I begin to understand what the folk music world was all about. I eventually wrote a few songs for Derroll – in fact I still do, tho he’s been dead now 8 years. “Turning Point” (mentioned before) was a song for Derroll a year or so after he’d died and while I was up in Denmark performing as part of a tribute project for him. After reading the books of Greil Marcus who guided me into the Harry Smith Anthology I have a different perspective on folk music. But I must say they were never the roots of my songs. I was influenced by recordings of Leadbelly and Moondog in the 40s and 50s. The rhythm and blues records of the early 50s broadcast on KWBR (Oakland) really hit me hard. Dylan turned my head around with “Another Side” and made me realize that all the poetry I’d been writing the past 4 or 5 years could be set to music with a guitar and harmonica. That’s about it for influences. My education was in old European music. I hardly if ever use my education.

JR: Can you describe the recording process of the BLACK ALBUM? (Tucker’s second album, first released by Autogram in 1971, then reissued the following year by Village Thing in England. First U.S. release by Numero Group in 2016 -ed.)

Tucker Zimmerman: Soon after I did my first tour in Germany Schwenken wanted an album. I was free to do what I wanted as long as it didn’t cost him any money. I asked for a two-track recorder and he delivered an old Telefunken to my doorstep in Liege. A friend loaned me a Philicorda organ and another friend loaned me an electric piano. I recorded the whole album at night in our third-floor living room, putting blankets and pillows over the windows to keep out the street noises. It took me about a month. The Telefunken was broken so I couldn’t do any track bouncing like I’d learned to do with Tony in London. But the defect turned out to be an advantage. I could record my voice and guitar on the left track and listen to it on the right track NOT in delay. Tho the signal was faint with headphones I was able to add another track synchronized with the first. Organ or piano and occasionally (I think) a second voice. I had to keep checking the playback to make sure the two tracks sounded OK combined. It had to be cut as a mono disc. But that suited the sound of the music. Incidentally I never liked my work on those songs and I was ready to toss the tape in the trash. My Belgian friends talked me out of it. I let it go – out into the public. I still don’t like the album and won’t have anything to do with it, tho I just learned that Schwenken will soon re-release it on CD. That’s up to him. I let the tapes go in 1971 and they’ve been none of my business since. I don’t have fond memories about this project and I know I didn’t make a decent album until I went to Paris in 1974 and recorded Over Here in Europe. It took me that long to find my voice and the path I wanted to follow. Ten Songs and the self-titled Black Album were accidents along the way – tests to help me prepare for the songs I really wanted to put out into the world.

JR: Many great spiritual teachers were raised by their grandparents. How do you feel that being raised by your grandmother has influenced your songs?

Tucker Zimmerman: My grandmother indeed raised me and everything she did and said influenced me profoundly. She not only influenced my songs, which were to come later and which she never heard (she died in 1958 when I was seventeen), she shaped my entire view of the world. She was the backbone of the McCullough side of the family. She and grandpa, their three daughters and son left their dirt farm in Farmersville, Illinois during the depression and came to San Francisco. She had been a teacher in Farmersville, a one room school house, for many years. In SF the whole family moved into a two story, 5 bedroom house on 5th avenue in the Sunset, a few blocks down from the U of Cal hospital where I was born. I was the first of the cousins. Everybody – sisters and their husbands and grandpa too – was out working at their jobs and Grandma took care of the house. We were alone during the day for a couple of years and she started to educate me early. We had a blackboard in the kitchen and she taught me to read and write and do simple arithmetic before I went to the 1st grade. She also read aloud to me – books and stories of all kinds. So yes, right there, I got an early sense that words were not supposed to stay glued to the printed page but to be lifted into the air with a voice. (Later in college, I became fascinated with the study of oral poetry and epic literature. I also at the time – my first years in college – was absorbing as much of the so-called beat poetry that was being read in the coffee houses and Back in the joints around SF.) In the third grade I started getting into trouble – stealing, fighting etc – and they threatened to expel me from school and send me to a correctional school in the city (where I would have got an advanced education in theft and murder) but my grandma stepped in and said I needed a change of scenery and I was moved up to Healdsburg where an aunt (one of my mother’s sisters) and uncle who had just moved out of the city had bought a ranch. Grandma went with me. 

Looking back I realize that she made the move for me. That she wanted to be close to me, continue to educate me, guide me, that she knew I had better things to do in my life than get in trouble. I was close to her all the way thru high school. We talked for hours and hours. When I was about 13 she introduced me to Buddhism. I’d already rebelled from going to Sunday school when I was 10 and I don’t think I saw the inside of the family’s protestant church after the age of 12. Incidentally, Grandma never went to church, tho she never told me the reason why. Which is to say we never got into a conversation about Christianity or Jesus Christ who my aunt was so hot for. Grandma believed that the best answers to life were in the teachings of Buddha. She was a pacifist. She was compassionate to all forms of life. She believed in re-incarnation. She knew we’d been together in some previous life and that we would come back together in another. She had a particular focus on Madam Blavatsky and her teachings, but those never rubbed off on me. I still have the books, with her marginal notations, but I still can’t make sense of what Theosophy or the Theosophical Society were all about. 

I also still have her hardbound copy of Emerson’s essays which she read over and over – sometimes aloud to me. Emerson made sense if she explained it – and believe me, she always made a great effort to explain things and made sure I understood. Emerson’s writing led me later to Whitman whose poems were the first I paid any attention to and got me started writing my own. I went back to the city when I was 17 to go to college and grandma died three months later. I’ve always carried the strange thought that she’d lived the last years of her life for me and now that her work was done it was time to move on. I still carry her spirit with me wherever I go and she’s been my guardian angel over all these many years since. Who else could it be?

And that’s my grandmother – which is to say, I’ve scratched the surface talking about her, but that’s all I need to say at this time

JR: You say the songs “write you”…. is this a form of channeling?

Tucker Zimmerman: I don’t know what that means – channeling. I’ll take a guess. If you mean that I feel the creative spirit flow thru me and that I am somewhat of an incidental transformer in the process then I’d have to say yes, I am a channel. I really don’t know where the songs come from. The poems. The stories. The music. And I don’t really want to know. I do not examine this part of my life too closely. So when I say the songs write me, it means that I put down what they want to say and I try not to get in the way. For what it’s worth (and this may anticipate another of your questions) there is a hierarchy of creative priorities that I’ve learned to respect. Songs come first. If I hear even a whisper of a song in my ear I drop no matter what I am doing and find out what the song has to say. Work it out. I don’t always finish a song in one shot, but I can usually get it far enough along so that it takes on a character or personality of its own. If I ignore a potential song its energy backs up on me, gets inside my brain and does a lot of damage. I’ve learned this the hard way. I never ignore a song. 

Next is poetry. Poems are easy anyway and I’m usually in motion when I write them. In the warm months of the year I spend a lot of time on my bicycle – sometimes 6 or 8 hours a day – biking around the network of farm roads in the Hesbaye – which is the plateau north of the Meuse River that extends down into Flanders. I’ve been doing this for about 5 years now. I used to go out for the sunshine and the fresh air, but now I go out mostly for the poems that are waiting for me in the wheat fields. After poems comes my fiction writing. Stories long and short – mostly long.

Last is music. I don’t practice composition any more and I almost never touch a score unless I get a commission or a job writing film music. Music comes to me the most easily. Composition is effortless. Writing words is the hardest. I know almost nothing about writing and that’s why it interests me the most. It’s a challenge and I’m always learning something new. I hope that makes sense to you. It makes sense to me.

JR: You were writing poetry, how did you make the transition into composition and how did that lead to singer/songwriting?

Tucker Zimmerman: I started writing words and music about the same time – in 1958 when I went back to the city to go to college. I was a music major. I had my trombone with me and I saw my future as trombonist in a symphony orchestra or as a studio musician. Then I got involved in theory classes and that led to composing music. That was what I was getting from school in those first two years. At the same time I was out prowling around the city at night, usually down in North Beach, absorbing the poetry and jazz – lots of live jazz. All the best came thru SF in the late 50s and early 60s. I spent many, many hours in the basement of City Lights reading their books. I bought a few books of poetry – Whitman, Ginsberg, Ferlinghetti himself – and like any decent 17-year old creative soul I started imitating. I couldn’t stop. It was a fascinating process – putting words down on paper, fooling around with language. I didn’t start writing songs (apart from a few blues tunes on piano that Pete Petersen and I put together on weekends in 1958 and 59) until Christmas break of 1965. I had a shoebox full of poem-like things by then and I was well past the stage of imitation. Putting these writings to music started this way. During that break a friend dropped off a 6-string classical guitar at my place – a guitar his girlfriend had given him. He was breaking up with the girl and he didn’t want her to take the guitar back. So he hid it at my place. I stuck it up next to my piano and it stayed there for a couple of days. 

One night I’d had enough composing – I was already working on my thesis – the Cantata on Thomas Wolfe – and I uncased the guitar to see what kind of instrument it was. I’d never touched a guitar before. There was a thin booklet in the gadget box and it showed me how the strings should be tuned. They also had printed a few diagrams of where to put your fingers to make chords. I started fooling around with the chords. I loved the sounds that were coming out. I promised myself at that moment that I would NEVER, EVER learn how to play the guitar. By this I mean, I’d studied both the piano and the trombone since I was eight and those instruments had long since lost their magic. Playing them was a chore. The piano especially was a drag (I still feel that familiar lethargy come over me when I sit down at a piano – a vague repulsion). I’ve kept my promise about the guitar, and playing a guitar – any guitar today – tho I don’t “know how to play it”, it is still an exciting, magical experience. It’s more than that sometimes – the guitar can heal, the guitar can pick me up from the floor and get me flying again. 

That night back at the end of 1965 I figured out how to play “Mr. Tambourine Man.” Not too difficult. Easy, in fact. I had some friends – a married couple with two small kids, so I took the guitar over to their place the next day and sang the song for them. Aaron, the oldest boy of 7, went wild. I came home feeling I’d cheated them. I didn’t know how to sing “Mr. Tambourine Man.” I had no business singing it. I dug out my old shoebox and started putting my words and music together. I wrote four songs straight off without going to sleep. I went back to my friends and tried them out. Aaron went wild again and started crying. I came home feeling I was on the right track. I didn’t get much sleep that week. By the end I had 12 songs and I had no idea of what I was supposed to do with them. That’s how it all started. 

Now it’s 40 years later. I don’t remember what my Thomas Wolfe Cantata sounded like and I now have over a thousand songs in my song box. I still don’t know what to do with all of them.

And by the way I still don’t know how to play guitar.

JR: Did sharing the same last name with Bob Dylan create any confusion or expectations?

Tucker Zimmerman: It never created any confusion in me. I didn’t know he existed until about 1964 when my friend Pete came back from the Monterey Folk Festival and said there was a guy in a leather jacket that sounded pretty good. I’d already written a few songs – in fact, Pete and I worked out one in 1958 called “That’s Life” – he’d drive up from Stanford on the weekends and we’d drink a lot of beer and write future hits that would make us famous. I finally recorded the song That’s Life in 2005 on my Chautauqua album. As for Dylan, I didn’t know his last name was the same as mine until sometime in 1971 or 72 when I returned to the continent from England and started touring in Germany. Someone came up after a concert and asked me if I was Bob Dylan’s brother. That was the first time I heard there was another Zimmerman running around on the music scene. It was not to be the first time I was asked that question. I got it almost every night for a few years. I picked up enough information here and there to figure out that it was almost impossible for him to be my brother. We were born three months apart in 1941 and unless our mother was a freak, or our father was skipping around from one wife to another, we couldn’t be brothers. I’ve never met Dylan. I’ve never had the desire to meet him. If we are to meet, he’ll have to look me up. I’m not about to intrude upon his life. He doesn’t need me.

Confusion and expectations in others? No doubt about it. Especially recently. My son tells me

that my website has been getting something like 3,000 hits a month. It took me a while but I finally figured out that most of those hits had to be Dylan fans searching into the most remote corners of the world in case he might be wearing a new mask.

Have I ever been tempted to change my name? It never occurred to me until 1974 when I went to Paris to see if Philips was interested in releasing my Over Here in Europe Album. They were – if I’d change my name. I said I’d change my name if they’d change theirs.

JR: What do you do to relax and recharge after you’ve completed a project?

Tucker Zimmerman: Nothing. I don’t relax. I read a lot, every night before going to sleep mostly. I take off alone from time to time to other cities just to have a change of scenery, but I always take my work with me. I write on the train. I used to read when I was on tour (trains and hotels) in the 70s, but now I always write – plane trips to America, train rides to Amsterdam and Paris. I travel to each of those cities at least once a year – usually two or three times to Amsterdam. But always I’m writing. Poems sneak in and out of my mainstream here and there usually when I’m in motion. 

Last year I finished my Song Poet Cycle – nine books called Journals – about 4000 pages. I put ten years of constant work into this project and somewhere in the middle of it all I saw that it would be my life work – my so-called magnum opus – the one thing that all my other writing has led to. 

Then in November of 2006 while living alone for six weeks in Amsterdam I finished the first draft of the ninth book. That was a strange moment. After coming down, I felt completely empty. I’d never been in that particular place before. For almost 40 years music and words had been flowing from one poem, one song, one story to the next effortlessly. For three months I did nothing but go crazy inside. I never had the sense of recharging. I certainly wasn’t relaxed. I read a lot of books and that kept me somewhat on track. Then I started revising the Cycle. Now it’s over a year later and I’m still revising. Not obsessively and certainly not on a daily basis. I’m just letting all those words – all those scenes – settle in and tell me what needs to be done to connect them more solidly. Somewhere along the line I must have recharged myself, but don’t ask me when or where. 

Two other thoughts that are sort of related. I’ve never had what some call a “writer’s block.” And I’ve never taken a vacation. As I said in my previous letter, for the past three winters I’ve escaped the wet cold of the north and tried to find temporary nests in the south. But while I’m down there I’m always busy. There’s never enough time in a day to get what’s in my head down on paper.

JR: What’s the oldest thing you own?

Tucker Zimmerman: This one baffles me. I can’t figure what you will learn about me from the answer. But that’s OK. I’ve never encountered this question before, that’s all and I’m curious to see what it might mean.

Actually I see two ways of answering this question. If you mean the thing that has come into my possession in my lifetime and which I still have something to do with (which was my initial reaction) then I’d have to say it’s a rug my grandmother made when I was three or four. I remember her working on it – the big hook and string of rags and all the colorful fabrics. It went on the floor by my bed and it stayed there all the way thru high school. Then I moved back to SF and I forgot about it. In January and February of this year, down in Catalonia, where Marie Claire and I stayed for 6 weeks, we had a tile floor that was too cold for my bare feet. Marie Claire went and got the old rug out of the back of the car where she’d been using it as a mat. I’d forgotten it was there. It must have come over from Tacoma, shipped by boat with my mom’s stuff when she came to stay with us for a few months in 1996. And there it was again, under my feet by the side of my bed in 2008 a few thousand miles away from where it had started in 1944. Six squares, each about 16 inches on a side, stitched together – two rows of three. Now it’s back to where it belongs – beside my bed, the last thing I see before I go to sleep, the first thing I see when I wake up. It’s somewhat faded but it still looks pretty good.

OR

 Do you mean the very oldest thing that I’ve got laying around? It’s a book. James Joyce’s Ulysses. Hardbound first edition from 1936. I didn’t know it was a first edition when I bought it in London in 1969. It was just an old book in an old book store and it was cheap. I read it thru at the time (understanding maybe a 10th of what I read) and ended up carrying it around with me whenever we moved. I picked it up again last December and finally re-read it – something I’d been intending to do these many years since. That’s when I took a look at the inside page and saw the date of publication – the Random edition just after the book was cleared of obscenity charges by the Supreme Court. This time around I enjoyed the book and I think I understood more than 10% of it – who knows? – maybe as much as 11 or 12%, “Everything that comes into your house new will someday leave as trash.” I think that includes me too. Grandma said the body is just an envelope that carries the spirit from place to place. She said take care of your body cause you’ll need it, but don’t get too attached. Good advice. And there you are. I hope my answers are clear and that I’ve given you a few things you can use. Once again, I thank you for your interest in my work. – Tucker
