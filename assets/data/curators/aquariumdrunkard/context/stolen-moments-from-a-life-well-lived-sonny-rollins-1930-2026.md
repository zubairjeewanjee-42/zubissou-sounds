## Body

This week, Sonny Rollins passed away at the age of 95 — a titan of 20th century music, a real American hero who shared so much with us over the course of a long life. Decades upon decades in search of some combination of notes and rhythm that passes beyond all understanding. A holy sound.

Such a long career means that there are endless corners to explore and uncover. Go way, way back to 1951 to hear Sonny and Miles Davis cutting their teeth at Birdland on a toasty radio broadcast. Check him out at East River Park in the summer of 1962, playing “The Star Spangled Banner” and more with Don Cherry. Watch an astounding BBC documentary from the late 1960s that follows Sonny from the Williamsburg Bridge to a New Jersey forest to a youth jazz orchestra rehearsal.

See him on the Tonight Show in 1979, playing an awe-inspiring unaccompanied solo that seems to contain the secrets of the universe. Marvel at his inspired contribution to Leonard Cohen’s “Who By Fire” in 1989, Rollins’ moody saxophone adding untold depths to an already deep song. And get blown away all over again by his one-time-only 2010 collab with Ornette Coleman on a remarkable 21-minute “Sonnymoon for Two,” a pair of aging giants still vital, still pushing forward.
